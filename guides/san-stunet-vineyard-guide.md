# San Stunet

## Introduction

San Stunet is one of the officially recognized *menzioni geografiche aggiuntive* (MGAs) in the commune of Neive, part of the greater Barbaresco DOCG zone in Piedmont's Langhe district. Formalized in 2007 when Barbaresco's official MGA list was codified—three years before Barolo implemented a similar system—San Stunet represents a distinct terroir expression within Neive's expanding Nebbiolo landscape.

The historical context of Neive's vineyard hierarchy provides important perspective for understanding San Stunet's position. Unlike Barbaresco proper, where certain sites like Asili, Montestefano, and Rabajà established reputations extending back generations, Neive's viticultural identity developed more recently. Lorenzo Fantini's influential late 19th-century monograph on Piedmontese viticulture identified very few "choice positions" in Barbaresco commune and notably, "none whatsoever in Neive." This absence from historical records doesn't diminish the quality potential of Neive's sites but rather reflects the township's later specialization in Nebbiolo. Neive long identified itself as "the township of four wines"—Moscato, Barbera, Dolcetto, and Nebbiolo—with Nebbiolo only consolidating its position after the Second World War.

## The Neive Context

Understanding San Stunet requires situating it within Neive's dramatic viticultural transformation. The commune has witnessed remarkable growth in Nebbiolo plantings, expanding from 140 hectares in 1995 to 265 hectares by 2019, largely at the expense of Barbera and Dolcetto. Today, Nebbiolo represents the township's most lucrative variety, though Moscato remains significant, comprising roughly half the planted area.

Within Neive, négociants' historical willingness to pay premium prices established a consensus regarding the commune's finest sites: Albesani and Gallina achieved recognition alongside producer-driven vineyards like Santo Stefano, which gained prestige through Bruno Giacosa's exacting work. The 2007 MGA codification formalized many such sites while sensibly retaining historically known vineyards, avoiding the commercial temptation to absorb lesser-known sites into famous names for marketing purposes.

## Terroir Considerations

While specific terroir details for San Stunet are not extensively documented in readily available sources, the site shares fundamental characteristics with Neive's broader geological framework. Barbaresco's soils divide roughly into two types, with calcareous clay from the Tortonian epoch—similar to that found in Barolo's La Morra and Barolo communes—producing perfumed, elegant wines. This soil type influences water regulation and mineral nutrition, key factors in Nebbiolo quality. The most successful Barbaresco sites typically feature well-drained soils that furnish steady but moderate water supply, combined with appropriate mineral nutrition that restrains vegetative growth, particularly before veraison.

The soil's physical characteristics—especially those governing water supply—exert more influence on grape and wine quality than chemical composition, a principle well-established in terroir studies. Such conditions promote smaller berries less prone to bunch congestion and compression, ultimately contributing to concentration and balance in the finished wine.

## The MGA System and San Stunet's Place

The 2007 MGA classification represented a watershed moment for Barbaresco, preceding Barolo's similar system by three years. This official recognition provided legal framework for single-vineyard designation, moving beyond the relatively recent phenomenon of cru-designated Barbaresco—the first being Prunotto's Barbaresco Montestefano in 1961. The MGA system brought clarity to a zone with less firmly established written records of cru designation than Barolo, where documentation extends deeper into history.

For San Stunet, inclusion in the official MGA list confirms its recognition as a distinct terroir unit worthy of individual designation, even if it hasn't achieved the fame of Neive's most celebrated sites or the producer-driven prestige of Santo Stefano.

## Current Status

As a lesser-documented MGA within Neive, San Stunet represents the kind of site that rewards exploration by enthusiasts interested in discovering Barbaresco beyond the most famous names. The expansion of quality-focused viticulture throughout Neive since the 1990s suggests that many sites, including San Stunet, benefit from improved viticultural understanding and winemaking refinement that has elevated the entire Barbaresco zone. The increasing economic incentive to plant Nebbiolo has brought greater attention to site-specific expression throughout the commune, potentially allowing sites like San Stunet to more fully demonstrate their individual character.
