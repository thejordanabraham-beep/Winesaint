# WineSaint Tasting Academy - Training Course Notes

> **Purpose:** These notes are for the Duolingo-style training course being built in Desktop. This is NOT for the reference guide feature.

---

## Philosophy

- Wine tasting is a learnable skill, not an innate gift
- Everyone's palate is different; yours is not wrong
- The goal is understanding and articulation, not correct answers
- Science explains but doesn't diminish the magic
- Expertise is developed through practice, not bestowed through certification
- Quality exists but so does subjectivity; both are true
- This knowledge should be free and accessible to everyone

---

## The Seven Levels

| Level | Name | Core Question | Output |
|-------|------|---------------|--------|
| 1 | **Foundations** | How does MY palate work? | Understanding of your own perception |
| 2 | **Structure** | What holds this wine together? | Ability to assess acid, tannin, alcohol, body, balance |
| 3 | **Aroma** | What am I smelling and why? | Systematic aroma identification and vocabulary |
| 4 | **Quality** | Is this wine good? | Framework for assessing quality (separate from preference) |
| 5 | **Faults** | Is something wrong? | Recognition of common wine faults |
| 6 | **Context** | What affects my perception? | Understanding of temperature, glass, food, bias |
| 7 | **Identification** | What IS this wine? | Blind tasting methodology (advanced, optional) |

**Why this order matters:**
- You can't assess structure if you don't understand your own thresholds (Level 1 → 2)
- You can't identify aromas if you haven't built vocabulary (Level 2 → 3)
- You can't judge quality without understanding structure and aroma (Level 3 → 4)
- Fault detection requires baseline understanding of "correct" (Level 4 → 5)
- Context awareness comes after you have a foundation (Level 5 → 6)
- Identification is the capstone, not the starting point (Level 7)

## Level 1: Foundations
*"Before you can taste wine, you must understand how YOU taste."*

### Content:
- How taste works (the five basic tastes, receptor biology)
- How smell works (olfaction, retronasal pathway, why smell is 80% of "taste")
- The trigeminal sense (heat, cold, astringency, effervescence)
- Perception variability (supertasters, anosmia, threshold differences)
- Your first calibration: testing YOUR sensitivity

### Exercises:
- Threshold testing with solutions (sugar, citric acid, tannin)
- Anosmia check (can you smell common compounds?)
- Supertaster indicator (PROP sensitivity discussion)

### Output:
You understand that your perception is unique, not wrong. Begin developing awareness of what you actually sense vs. what you think you should sense.

---

## Level 2: Structure
*"The architecture of wine: what holds it together."*

### Content:
- **Sweetness**: Residual sugar, perception threshold, dry to sweet spectrum
- **Acidity**: Tartaric, malic, lactic, citric. How to assess. What it does to wine.
- **Tannin**: Phenolic compounds, where they come from, texture vocabulary (silky to grippy)
- **Alcohol**: Perception as heat and weight. Estimating levels.
- **Body**: The combined effect. Light to full spectrum.
- **Balance**: When elements harmonize vs. when one dominates

### The Science:
- Why acid makes you salivate (protective response)
- Why tannins feel drying (protein binding)
- Why alcohol feels hot (TRPV1 activation)

### Exercises:
- Comparative tasting: high acid vs. low acid wines
- Tannin texture exercise: tea steeping times
- Alcohol heat calibration

### Output:
You can describe the structural profile of any wine with precision. Understand why some wines feel "balanced" and others don't.

---

## Level 3: Aroma
*"The vocabulary of smell: primary, secondary, tertiary."*

### Content:
- **Primary aromas**: From the grape itself. Variety-specific markers.
- **Secondary aromas**: From fermentation. Yeast, MLF, carbonic maceration.
- **Tertiary aromas**: From aging. Oak influence, oxidative development, bottle bouquet.
- **The compound connection**: Why wine smells like cherries (benzaldehyde), vanilla (vanillin), pepper (rotundone)
- **Building YOUR vocabulary**: Not memorizing a wheel, but developing genuine recognition

### The Science:
- Volatile compound families
- How esterification creates fruity aromas
- How MLF converts malic to lactic and produces diacetyl (butter)

### Interactive Element:
The WineSaint Aroma Map. Not a wheel (wheels are passive). A compound-linked exploration tool.

### Output:
You can identify aromas systematically and understand their chemical origin. Develop a personal vocabulary that's meaningful to YOU.

---

## Level 4: Quality
*"The hard question: is this wine actually good?"*

### Content:
- **The subjectivity problem**: Quality is not purely objective. But it's not purely subjective either.
- **Defensible quality markers:**
  - Complexity (layers, evolution)
  - Concentration (intensity without heaviness)
  - Length (finish that matters)
  - Balance (structural harmony)
  - Typicity (expression of place and variety)
- **Quality vs. preference**: A wine can be high quality and not to your taste. Both truths coexist.
- **The rating debate**: Points, stars, and their limitations

### The Honesty:
We don't pretend objectivity. We teach you how experts think about quality while acknowledging the constructed nature of "expertise."

### Output:
You can assess wine quality using defensible criteria while remaining aware of your own biases and preferences.

---

## Level 5: Faults
*"When something's wrong: detection and understanding."*

### Content:
- **Cork taint (TCA)**: Cause (2,4,6-trichloroanisole), detection threshold (1-2 ppt), what it smells like, is the wine ruined?
- **Brettanomyces**: The spectrum from "character" to "flaw." Cause, detection, regional tolerance.
- **Oxidation**: Intentional (Sherry, Vin Jaune) vs. accidental. Detection, when it's too much.
- **Volatile acidity**: Acetic acid and ethyl acetate. Threshold, when it's a flaw.
- **Reduction**: Sulfur compounds. Cause, can it be fixed (decanting)?
- **Heat damage**: Cooked fruit, seepage. Visual and olfactory markers.
- **Light strike**: Sulfur compounds from UV exposure. "Skunky" wines.

### Why This Matters:
Most casual drinkers can't identify faults. They drink corked wine and think they don't like the producer. This is practical, essential knowledge.

### Output:
You can identify common faults and understand their chemistry. Know when to send a wine back vs. when to decant and enjoy.

---

## Level 6: Context
*"Everything that affects perception beyond the wine itself."*

### Content:
- **Temperature**: How cold mutes aroma and emphasizes tannin/acid. Optimal serving ranges.
- **Glassware**: Why shape matters (surface area, aroma concentration). What's essential, what's marketing.
- **Decanting**: Oxygen, sediment, temperature adjustment. When it helps, when it doesn't.
- **Food pairing**: The science (tannin + protein, acid + fat, salt + bitter). Beyond rules of thumb.
- **Expectation bias**: How price, label, and context change perception. The blind tasting research.
- **Adaptation**: Why the second sip tastes different. Palate fatigue.

### The Science:
- Why cold suppresses volatile release
- Why tannins soften with air (polymerization)
- Why your brain fills in what it expects

### Output:
You can optimize tasting conditions. Understand why the same wine tastes different in different contexts. Approach wine without being fooled by bias.

---

## Level 7: Identification
*"The advanced game: what IS this wine?"*

### Content:
- **Climate deduction**: Cool vs. warm markers (acid, alcohol, ripeness, aroma profile)
- **Variety markers**: Signature aromatics and structural patterns by grape
- **Regional signatures**: Old World vs. New World. Specific regional tells.
- **Age estimation**: Color evolution, aroma development, tannin softening
- **The deductive process**: Systematic elimination, probability thinking
- **Blind tasting practice**: Methodology for improvement

### Our Position:
This is a skill. It's fun. It's impressive at parties. But it's not the point of tasting wine. CMS has made identification the entire game. We put it where it belongs: an advanced, optional pursuit for those who enjoy it.

### Output:
You can approach blind tasting systematically. Develop identification skills through practice. Or skip this entirely and be no less capable of enjoying and understanding wine.

## The WineSaint Method (Questions Framework)

Not a grid. A set of questions you ask yourself:

1. **What do I see?** → Appearance tells a story
2. **What do I smell?** → First impression, then exploration
3. **What do I feel?** → Structure and texture
4. **What do I taste?** → Flavor confirmation
5. **How does it evolve?** → In glass, on palate, through finish
6. **Is it good?** → Quality assessment
7. **Do I like it?** → Personal response (equally valid)
8. **What might it be?** → Optional identification

Questions invite exploration. Checklists invite completion. We want curious tasters, not box-checkers.
