## Overview

Anjou and Anjou Villages are foundational appellations within the Anjou-Saumur sub-region of the Loire Valley, located in the Maine-et-Loire department. The region begins in earnest when crossing westward from the Loire-Atlantique department, marking the transition into what has become one of the Loire's most dramatically improved wine regions in recent years. Anjou AOC covers a vast area with varied terroir throughout the Anjou-Saumur region, while Anjou Villages represents a more restricted, quality-focused designation for red wines from specifically delineated areas within the broader Anjou zone.

The Anjou-Saumur region is the Loire Valley's largest in both production volume and vineyard area, with 15,463 planted hectares covering remarkably diverse terroir. Unlike many French wine regions, there is no overarching Loire Valley regional appellation; instead, Anjou AOC serves as one of three regional middle Loire appellations alongside Saumur AOC and Touraine AOC. This creates a complex hierarchy where Anjou functions both as a broad regional designation and as a specific quality benchmark, particularly for white wines based on Chenin Blanc.

The relationship between Anjou AOC and Anjou Villages AOC is hierarchical and style-specific. Anjou AOC permits red, white, and rosé wines with relatively generous yields, while Anjou Villages exists exclusively for red wines from designated superior sites, with stricter production requirements and longer mandatory aging before release. This two-tiered system reflects the region's ongoing quality evolution, though the complexity of regulations has led some quality-focused producers to opt out of the appellation system entirely in favor of Vin de France designations.

## Terroir & Climate

The Anjou region experiences a cool climate overall, though its position in the middle Loire places it under moderate maritime influence as the Loire River flows westward toward the Atlantic. The soils throughout Anjou are remarkably complex and varied, derived in part from the ancient Armorican Massif, the same geological foundation that shapes the terroir of Muscadet to the west. This geological diversity means that soil composition can vary significantly even within small areas, contributing to the wide range of wine styles produced across the appellation.

The terroir of Anjou Villages areas tends to feature schist and other metamorphic rocks that provide excellent drainage and contribute to the production of more structured, age-worthy red wines. The diverse soil types across the broader Anjou appellation (including clay, limestone, schist, and various sedimentary formations) allow different grape varieties to express themselves distinctly depending on their specific location. This geological complexity has historically been both an asset and a challenge, as the region's potential has often been obscured by inconsistent quality from less suitable sites.

The cool climate is fundamental to wine character throughout Anjou, promoting freshness, moderate alcohol levels, and bright acidity across all styles. The Loire River itself provides some moderating influence on temperature extremes, though the region is subject to vintage variation. The combination of cool temperatures and extended growing seasons allows Chenin Blanc in particular to develop complexity while retaining its characteristic high acidity, though this same climate can challenge red grape ripening in cooler years.

## Grape Varieties & Wine Styles

Anjou AOC permits multiple wine styles with different varietal requirements. Anjou Blanc must contain a minimum of 80% Chenin Blanc, with other Loire varieties permitted for the remainder. Anjou Rouge requires a minimum of 70% Cabernet Franc and/or Cabernet Sauvignon, with the balance from other approved varieties. The appellation allows maximum yields of 60 hL/ha for both red and white wines, a relatively generous limit that can result in wines of lower flavor intensity when producers prioritize quantity over quality.

Anjou Villages AOC represents a significant quality step up for red wines, requiring 100% Cabernet Franc and/or Cabernet Sauvignon, in practice, these wines are predominantly Cabernet Franc. The maximum yield is restricted to 55 hL/ha, and wines cannot be released until September of the year following harvest, ensuring additional bottle maturation before reaching the market. This mandatory aging requirement distinguishes Anjou Villages from standard Anjou Rouge and signals the appellation's focus on more structured, age-worthy expressions of Loire Cabernet Franc.

The Loire Valley produces 93% of France's Chenin Blanc, and Anjou plays a significant role in this production. While Anjou is not primarily known for sweet wines (those designations belong to more specific appellations like Savennières and Quarts de Chaume) the region produces a diverse range of styles from this versatile variety. Anjou whites display the fresh, light body and simple fruit flavors typical of cool-climate Loire wines at the regional appellation level, though quality-focused producers working with old vines and lower yields achieve considerably more complexity. The region's Cabernet Franc-based reds from Anjou Villages can show good structure and aging potential, developing earthy, herbal complexity with time.

## Notable Producers

The Anjou region has undergone significant quality transformation in recent years, with numerous producers focusing on lower yields, organic and biodynamic viticulture, and minimal intervention winemaking. However, the complexity of the appellation system and the commercial challenges of the Anjou name have led many of the region's most ambitious producers to work outside the traditional appellation framework, labeling their wines as Vin de France rather than claiming Anjou or Anjou Villages designations. This trend is particularly notable among younger winemakers and those pushing quality boundaries.

Despite these challenges, both established estates and emerging producers continue to demonstrate Anjou's considerable potential, particularly from the schist-based soils that characterize the best Anjou Villages sites. The region's transformation reflects broader changes in the Loire Valley, where a new generation of vignerons has embraced lower yields, precise viticulture, and expressive winemaking to unlock terroir potential that was previously obscured by industrial-scale production.

## What to Know

Anjou represents exceptional value for consumers willing to explore beyond the region's historical reputation for simple, high-volume wines. The key is to seek out producers focused on quality rather than quantity, particularly for Anjou Villages reds, which offer structured, age-worthy Cabernet Franc at prices well below comparable appellations. Anjou Blanc from serious producers provides an accessible entry point to Chenin Blanc's versatility, though the appellation's 60 hL/ha yield limit means quality varies considerably. Vintage variation matters in this cool climate, warmer years produce riper, more approachable reds, while cooler vintages emphasize freshness and require more patience. Serve Anjou Blanc at 10-12°C in standard white wine glasses; Anjou Villages reds benefit from slight chilling to 14-16°C and can be served in Burgundy-style stems to emphasize their medium-bodied structure. The region's ongoing quality revolution means that recent vintages often outperform older reputations, making Anjou one of the Loire's most exciting areas for discovering emerging talent and undervalued terroir.
