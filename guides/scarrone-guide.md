# Scarrone

## Introduction

Scarrone is a registered Menzione Geografica Aggiuntiva (MGA) in the commune of Castiglione Falletto, one of the five principal townships producing Barolo DOCG. Unlike the more celebrated crus of Castiglione Falletto, namely Rocche di Castiglione, Villero, and Monprivato, which regularly appear on shortlists of Barolo's finest vineyards (Scarrone represents one of the lesser-documented sites within this prestigious commune. This absence from the traditional canon does not necessarily reflect inferior quality, but rather the historical focus on a handful of sites that have long enjoyed privileged positions in both written and oral traditions of the zone.

The introduction of the official MGA system provided formal recognition to sites like Scarrone that, while perhaps not commanding the highest prices from négociants historically, nonetheless possess distinct identities worthy of single-vineyard designation. Understanding Scarrone requires situating it within the broader context of Castiglione Falletto's unique position in the Barolo landscape.

## The Castiglione Falletto Context

Castiglione Falletto occupies a singular position among Barolo's communes. The vineyards lie on a spur that divides the two principal valleys of the Barolo zone, which are conveniently separated by the Alba-Barolo road running along the valley floor. This geographical positioning places Castiglione Falletto at the intersection of the zone's two major soil types and stylistic expressions.

To the west, La Morra and Barolo are characterized by calcareous marls of the Tortonian epoch) relatively compact and more fertile soils that, depending on vineyard location, tend to produce softer, fruitier, more aromatic wines. To the east, Monforte d'Alba and Serralunga d'Alba feature soils from the Helvetian epoch with a higher proportion of compressed sandstone. These less compact, less fertile soils yield more intense, structured wines that mature more slowly.

The wines of Castiglione Falletto characteristically combine elements of both expressions: the elegant, more forward character associated with the western townships alongside the structure and backbone typical of the eastern zones. This synthesis makes Castiglione Falletto wines particularly compelling, offering both immediate appeal and long-term aging potential.

## Terroir Considerations

Without specific documentation of Scarrone's precise soil composition, elevation, and aspect, it is most accurate to consider the site within the general terroir framework of Castiglione Falletto. The commune's position on the dividing spur means its vineyards may feature varying proportions of both Tortonian marls and Helvetian sandstone, with the specific balance heavily influencing the resulting wine character.

The attributes of soil can change rapidly within a vineyard, both vertically and horizontally. In Barolo, the manner in which roots navigate the complex layering of soil strata dictates the amount of water and nutrients vines can access throughout the growing season. The interplay between topography, soil depth, and composition means that even within a single MGA, variation exists (a reality that skilled viticulturists account for through careful site analysis and management decisions.

## Wine Characteristics

Barolo from Castiglione Falletto, including wines from sites like Scarrone, should express the commune's characteristic duality. At their best, these wines offer the rose petal, red cherry, and subtle spice aromatics that make Nebbiolo so compelling, while maintaining the structural integrity) firm but refined tannins, bright acidity, and mineral persistence (necessary for extended bottle aging.

The color follows Nebbiolo's typical trajectory: ruby in youth, never particularly deep or opaque, evolving relatively quickly toward garnet and brick tones. The aromatic profile develops from fresh cherries and plums toward the more complex tertiary notes of dried cherries, rose petals, tar, and liquorice that define mature Barolo. The finest examples achieve balance between fruit expression and structural elements without favoring one extreme over the other.

The commune's wines generally show well at intermediate stages of aging) neither requiring the decades sometimes needed for the most powerful Serralunga crus nor fading as quickly as some lighter expressions from other zones. This makes them particularly appealing to collectors seeking both aging potential and relative approachability.

## Production Standards and Aging

All Barolo, regardless of specific MGA, must adhere to strict DOCG regulations: maximum yields of 56 hectoliters per hectare and minimum aging of 38 months in total, with at least 18 months in oak. These requirements, among Italy's most stringent, ensure a baseline quality level across the appellation.

Modern viticulture and gentler extraction methods have evolved considerably from the traditional approach of extended maceration and years in large Slavonian oak casks. Today's producers working in Castiglione Falletto employ varied approaches, from traditional large-format oak to smaller barriques, with aging durations calibrated to the specific characteristics of each site and vintage. The key is allowing the wine to develop complexity and integrate its structural components while preserving Nebbiolo's essential elegance and perfume.

## Conclusion

Scarrone represents the broader category of Barolo MGAs that, while officially recognized, remain outside the small circle of historically celebrated crus. This positioning offers both challenges and opportunities: less established market recognition but potentially excellent value for consumers seeking authentic expressions of Castiglione Falletto's distinctive terroir. As understanding of Barolo's diverse vineyard landscape continues to deepen, sites like Scarrone merit attention from those willing to explore beyond the most famous names.
