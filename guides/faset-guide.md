# Faset MGA, Barbaresco

## Introduction

Faset (sometimes spelled Fasét) is an officially recognized menzione geografica aggiuntiva (MGA) within the Barbaresco DOCG, located in the commune of Barbaresco itself. When the official MGA system was introduced in 2007, notably three years before Barolo implemented its own cru classification (Faset was among the single-vineyard designations codified in the appellation's geographic hierarchy. This formalization represented an important step in acknowledging the site-specific character that distinguishes Barbaresco's diverse vineyard landscape.

The MGA system in Barbaresco has been praised for sensibly retaining historically known vineyards rather than absorbing smaller sites into more famous names for commercial reasons. Unlike some of Barbaresco's most celebrated crus) such as Asili, Rabajà, Montestefano, or Montefico (Faset has not achieved the same level of international recognition or historical documentation. This relative obscurity, however, does not diminish its status as a legitimate expression of Nebbiolo from a specific terroir within one of Italy's most prestigious appellations.

## The Barbaresco Context

To understand Faset's place within Barbaresco, it's essential to recognize the appellation's broader characteristics and evolution. Barbaresco itself is a relatively young wine historically, with the name appearing on labels only from 1894 when Domizio Cavazza, professor at the Oenological School of Alba, founded the Barbaresco cooperative. The production zone encompasses just 733 hectares across the communes of Barbaresco, Neive, and Treiso) approximately one-third the size of neighboring Barolo.

Single-vineyard bottlings in Barbaresco are a modern phenomenon, with Prunotto's Barbaresco Montestefano in 1961 marking the first such wine. The region has a less firmly established written record of cru designation than Barolo, with Lorenzo Fantini's late 19th-century monograph on Piedmontese viticulture indicating very few "choice positions" in Barbaresco. The first serious attempts to list and rate the finest sites date from the 1960s with Luigi Veronelli's work, followed by Renato Ratti in the 1970s.

## Barbaresco's Terroir Philosophy

While specific details about Faset's individual terroir characteristics remain less documented than the appellation's most famous sites, any Barbaresco MGA reflects the denomination's fundamental terroir principles. The region's soils and mesoclimates create wines that, while sharing Nebbiolo's signature tannins and acidity, express themselves differently than Barolo.

Scientific understanding of terroir emphasizes that soil's physical characteristics (particularly those governing water supply to the vine) play a crucial role in wine quality alongside climate. The best vineyard sites feature well-drained soils that provide steady but moderate water supply, resulting in restrained shoot growth and smaller berries. These conditions, combined with appropriate vine nutrition, help produce the concentrated, structured wines for which Nebbiolo is renowned.

The comune of Barbaresco, where Faset is located, shares the same marl and sandstone soils (Sant'Agata fossil marl) that characterize much of the appellation, though the specific composition, elevation, aspect, and microclimate of each MGA create distinct expressions of the variety.

## Barbaresco Wine Character

Wines from Barbaresco commune MGAs typically exhibit the elegance and aromatic intensity that distinguish the appellation from Barolo. If Barbaresco is considered lighter-bodied than its more famous neighbor (though wines must reach a minimum alcoholic strength of 12.5% and easily achieve 13.5%) this should not be mistaken for lack of structure. Young Barbaresco possesses the tannins and acidity characteristic of Nebbiolo, with the denomination requiring only 26 months of aging (50 months for Riserva) compared to Barolo's longer minimum aging requirements.

Contemporary Barbaresco displays perfumes of cherry, violets, and with age develops notes of iron, tar, and orange peel. An increasing number of producers have returned to traditional winemaking techniques, including long maceration periods of up to 40 days and aging in large oak casks rather than French barriques, which some argue can suppress Nebbiolo's gentle aromatic profile.

## The Challenge of Documentation

The relative lack of detailed information about Faset compared to Barbaresco's most prestigious crus reflects a broader reality of wine regions: not every recognized vineyard site has been the subject of extensive study or promotion. The fame of sites like San Lorenzo, Tildin, and Martinenga in Barbaresco, or Santo Stefano in Neive, arose from the dedicated work of producers such as Gaja, Giacosa, and Alberto di Gresy. Similarly, négociants' willingness to pay premium prices for grapes from certain vineyards established consensus about elite sites like Asili, Montefico, Montestefano, and Rabajà in the Barbaresco commune.

Faset's lower profile may reflect limited production, fewer producers bottling the site as a single-vineyard wine, or simply the realities of a competitive market where consumer attention gravitates toward established names. For the dedicated Barbaresco enthusiast, however, lesser-known MGAs can offer compelling value and authentic expressions of place.

## Conclusion

Faset represents one piece of Barbaresco's complex mosaic of officially recognized vineyard sites. While it has not achieved the fame of the appellation's most celebrated crus, its inclusion in the 2007 MGA system acknowledges its legitimacy as a distinct terroir within the Barbaresco commune. As with any Barbaresco, wines from this site would be expected to express Nebbiolo's characteristic structure, aromatics, and aging potential within the denomination's elegant, perfumed style. For those seeking to explore beyond Barbaresco's most famous names, investigating lesser-known MGAs like Faset offers an opportunity to discover the appellation's full diversity.
