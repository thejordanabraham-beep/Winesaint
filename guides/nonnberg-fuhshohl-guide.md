# Nonnberg Fuhshohl: The Rheingau's Hidden Hilltop Vineyard

Nonnberg Fuhshohl occupies a distinctive position in the Rheingau's viticultural landscape: a steep, south-facing vineyard site perched above the Rhine where ancient soils and modern ambition converge. The name itself hints at the site's character: "Fuhshohl" suggests a hollow or depression, while "Nonnberg" references the nuns who once tended vines on these slopes. This is not a household name like Berg Schlossberg or Steinberg, but among Rheingau insiders, Fuhshohl represents something increasingly valuable: a site where traditional terroir expression meets contemporary dry Riesling production.

## Geography & Topography

Fuhshohl sits within the Nonnberg complex, positioned on the slopes that characterize the Rheingau's most distinguished vineyard territory. The Rheingau itself occupies a unique geographical position: the Rhine River, which flows predominantly north-south through Germany, takes a sharp westward turn here, creating a south-facing amphitheater of slopes that captures maximum sunlight exposure. This geological quirk places vineyards like Fuhshohl at approximately 50 degrees north latitude (the same parallel that runs through Winnipeg, Canada) yet allows Riesling to ripen consistently and completely.

The vineyard's elevation and slope angle create crucial drainage and heat retention characteristics. Steep Rheingau sites typically range from 15% to 30% gradient, with the most precipitous sections reaching 40% or more. These slopes serve multiple functions: they maximize sun exposure throughout the growing season, facilitate cold air drainage on frost-prone spring nights, and prevent water accumulation that would dilute flavor concentration in the grapes.

The proximity to the Rhine River itself cannot be overstated. The broad waterway acts as a thermal regulator, moderating temperature extremes throughout the year. In spring, the river's cooler water temperatures delay budbreak slightly, reducing frost risk. During summer, it provides cooling breezes that maintain acidity in the grapes. Come autumn, the Rhine's retained warmth extends the growing season, allowing Riesling to hang on the vine well into October or even November in exceptional years.

This riverside positioning also creates the humidity conditions that make Botrytis cinerea a common occurrence in the Rheingau, particularly in vineyards nearest the water. The morning mists that rise from the Rhine and settle into hollows like Fuhshohl create the moisture necessary for noble rot development, though modern Rheingau production (approximately 80% of which finishes with nine grams per liter or less of residual sugar) has shifted away from relying on botrytis as a primary stylistic driver.

## Terroir & Geological Foundation

Understanding Fuhshohl's terroir requires understanding the Rheingau's geological heritage. The region's vineyard soils derive primarily from the Taunus Mountains, the quartzite and slate-rich range that forms the Rheingau's northern boundary. Over millions of years, erosion transported these materials downslope, creating complex soil profiles that vary dramatically even within individual vineyard sites.

The Rheingau's soil composition differs markedly from its eastern neighbor, the Rheinhessen, and its upstream cousin, the Mittelrhein. While slate dominates the Mosel and quartzite appears prominently in certain Rheingau sites, the region's most distinctive vineyards often feature deep loess deposits mixed with weathered rock, clay, and varying proportions of limestone. These soils retain sufficient moisture to support vines through dry summer periods while draining quickly enough to prevent waterlogging: a crucial balance in a region where annual precipitation averages 500-600mm.

Fuhshohl's specific soil profile likely includes significant loess content (that wind-deposited silt that characterizes many of the Rheingau's gentler slopes) mixed with decomposed slate and quartzite fragments. This combination creates what German viticulturists call "warm soils," earth that heats quickly in spring sunshine and retains that warmth overnight, accelerating ripening during the crucial final weeks before harvest.

The geological age of these materials spans hundreds of millions of years. The Taunus Mountains themselves formed during the Variscan orogeny approximately 300-400 million years ago, creating the metamorphic rocks (particularly the Taunus quartzite) that would eventually weather into vineyard soils. The Rhine River's course through this landscape carved the valley and deposited successive layers of alluvial material, creating the complex soil stratification visible in vineyard profiles today.

## Wine Character & Expression

Riesling from Fuhshohl expresses the broader Rheingau style while offering site-specific nuances. The Rheingau built its reputation on Riesling that balances ripe fruit character with structural integrity, wines that show neither the ethereal delicacy of the Saar nor the tropical exuberance of the Pfalz, but rather a middle path of yellow stone fruit, citrus precision, and mineral tension.

The modern Rheingau Riesling, particularly from sites like Fuhshohl, typically presents as bone-dry or nearly so. The shift toward dry production, formalized with the founding of the Charta Association in 1984, replaced the off-dry style that dominated the region through much of the 20th century. This transformation reflected both changing consumer preferences and a renewed focus on terroir expression, residual sugar, after all, can mask the subtle differences between neighboring sites.

Expect wines from Fuhshohl to show classic Rheingau aromatics: white peach, yellow apple, and citrus blossom, often with a distinctive stony or flinty note that speaks to the quartzite and slate in the soil. The acidity (Riesling's signature characteristic) provides both immediate freshness and long-term aging potential. A well-made Rheingau Riesling from a site like Fuhshohl can easily evolve for 10-15 years in bottle, developing the petrol and honey notes that mark mature Riesling without losing its core tension.

The alcohol levels in contemporary dry Rheingau Riesling typically range from 12% to 13.5%, reflecting complete physiological ripeness without the phenolic bitterness that plagues underripe dry Riesling. This represents a careful calibration: the grapes must hang long enough to develop flavor complexity and shed green character, but not so long that they lose the acidity that defines the variety.

The Rheingau's position as a Prädikatswein region means that even dry wines may carry designations like Kabinett, Spätlese, or higher, referring to the must weight at harvest rather than sweetness level. This can confuse consumers unfamiliar with German wine law, but for producers working sites like Fuhshohl, these designations communicate picking date and physiological ripeness, crucial information for understanding the wine's structure and aging trajectory.

## Comparative Context: Fuhshohl Within the Rheingau

To understand Fuhshohl's position within the Rheingau hierarchy, consider the region's most celebrated sites. Schloss Johannisberg, perhaps the Rheingau's most famous vineyard, occupies a hilltop position with 360-degree exposure and deep loess soils. Berg Schlossberg and Berg Rottland in Rüdesheim feature steeper slopes and more prominent slate and quartzite, yielding wines of particular intensity and minerality. Steinberg, the historic Cistercian monopole, sits in a protected bowl with clay-rich soils that produce powerful, age-worthy Rieslings.

Fuhshohl operates in a different register than these iconic sites. It lacks the historical pedigree of Steinberg (where monks cultivated Riesling as early as the 12th century) and the immediate name recognition of Johannisberg. But this relative anonymity creates opportunity: producers working Fuhshohl can focus on pure expression rather than maintaining historical reputation, and consumers can access serious Rheingau Riesling without paying the premium commanded by the region's most famous names.

The site's characteristics place it closer to the Rheingau's middle-slope vineyards than its most extreme sites. Unlike the riverside plots where humidity encourages botrytis and complicates dry wine production, Fuhshohl's elevation provides better air circulation. Unlike the highest slopes where soil depth decreases and water stress can limit yields, Fuhshohl likely offers sufficient soil depth for consistent production even in dry vintages.

## Historical Context & Development

The Rheingau's viticultural history stretches back to Roman times, but Riesling's dominance is more recent. Medieval records show the region was actually red wine country, with Pinot Noir (Spätburgunder) occupying many of the slopes now devoted to Riesling. The transformation began in earnest during the 15th and 16th centuries. Monks at Kloster Eberbach cultivated "Riesslaner" by 1435, and Hieronymus Bock's 1552 Latin herbal documented Riesling in the Mosel, Rheingau, and Rheinhessen.

Noble and clerical authorities mandated Riesling's expansion from the late 17th century onward, recognizing the variety's particular suitability to the region's climate and soils. The Benedictine monks of Johannisberg proved particularly influential, so much so that "Johannisberger" became a New World synonym for Riesling throughout the 20th century.

The Nonnberg area, including Fuhshohl, likely participated in this Riesling expansion, though specific historical documentation for individual vineyard sites remains sparse. The "Nonnberg" designation suggests ecclesiastical ownership at some point, convents and monasteries were major vineyard proprietors throughout the Middle Ages and early modern period.

The 20th century brought dramatic changes to German viticulture. The 1971 German Wine Law consolidated thousands of individual vineyard names into larger Einzellagen (single vineyards) and created the Grosslagen (collective sites) that still confuse consumers today. Many traditional vineyard names disappeared in this consolidation, though sites with strong historical identity or distinctive terroir characteristics maintained their individual designations.

The founding of the Charta Association in 1984 marked another turning point, particularly for sites like Fuhshohl. The association promoted stricter quality standards than the 1971 law required and championed dry Riesling production at a time when German wine remained associated with sweet, low-quality exports. This movement laid the groundwork for the VDP (Verband Deutscher Prädikatsweingüter), the association of elite German estates that created its own classification system based on terroir rather than must weight.

## Classification & Status

Understanding Fuhshohl's status requires navigating Germany's complex classification systems. The official government system, established in 1971, recognizes Fuhshohl as an Einzellage (single vineyard) within the Rheingau Anbaugebiet (growing region). This designation provides legal protection for the name but says nothing about quality. Germany's official system famously lacks the hierarchical terroir classification found in Burgundy or Piedmont.

The VDP, Germany's association of elite producers, created its own four-tier classification to address this gap: Gutswein (estate wine), Ortswein (village wine), Erste Lage (premier cru equivalent), and Grosse Lage (grand cru equivalent). Grosse Lage sites produce Grosses Gewächs (GG) wines, dry wines from the best sites that represent German fine wine's highest expression.

Whether Fuhshohl holds VDP classification depends on whether member estates own parcels within the site and whether the VDP's Rheingau chapter recognizes it as Erste Lage or Grosse Lage quality. The VDP's classification process considers historical reputation, soil characteristics, mesoclimate, and consistent quality over time. Sites without long-documented histories or consistent production from VDP members may lack official classification even if their terroir merits recognition.

This creates an interesting dynamic: Fuhshohl may represent excellent value precisely because it operates outside the most celebrated classifications. Ambitious producers working the site can focus on quality without the burden of grand cru pricing, while consumers can access serious Rheingau Riesling at more accessible price points.

## Key Producers & Approaches

The Rheingau's producer landscape includes historic estates with centuries of family ownership, monastery holdings dating to medieval times, and newer operations bringing contemporary perspectives to traditional sites. Estates like Schloss Johannisberg, Kloster Eberbach (now state-owned), and Georg Breuer have defined Rheingau Riesling for generations, while producers like August Kesseler and Peter Jakob Kühn represent more recent quality-focused initiatives.

Producers working Fuhshohl likely fall into several categories. Established estates with extensive holdings across multiple Rheingau sites may include Fuhshohl parcels in their village-level or estate-level bottlings, blending fruit from various sources to create consistent house styles. More focused producers might bottle Fuhshohl separately, emphasizing the site's distinctive characteristics and building its reputation through single-vineyard wines.

The winemaking approach for Rheingau Riesling has evolved considerably over recent decades. Traditional methods emphasized large old oak casks (Stückfass or Halbstückfass, holding 1,200 or 600 liters respectively) for fermentation and aging. These vessels allowed slow, cool fermentation while imparting minimal oak flavor: the goal was pure fruit and terroir expression, not wood influence.

Contemporary producers increasingly use stainless steel for fermentation, particularly for wines intended to showcase bright fruit and precise minerality. Some employ spontaneous fermentation with ambient yeasts, believing this approach better expresses terroir than cultured yeast strains. Others use temperature-controlled fermentation with selected yeasts to ensure clean, predictable results.

The fermentation process for dry Riesling typically extends for several weeks or even months, as the wine slowly converts sugar to alcohol at cool temperatures. This extended fermentation preserves aromatic compounds that would volatilize at higher temperatures and allows complex flavor development. The wine may rest on its fine lees through winter and spring, gaining texture and complexity before bottling in early summer.

Producers focused on Prädikatswein production from sites like Fuhshohl may harvest multiple times through autumn, selecting grapes at different ripeness levels for different wine styles. Early pickings might produce Kabinett, lighter in alcohol, bright in acidity, potentially with a few grams of residual sugar for balance. Later harvests yield Spätlese and Auslese, whether finished dry (as increasingly common) or with noble sweetness from botrytis concentration.

## Vintage Variation & Climatic Trends

Rheingau Riesling shows pronounced vintage variation, responding to each growing season's particular weather patterns. The region's marginal climate (warm enough for consistent ripening but cool enough to maintain acidity) means that small climatic shifts create significant quality differences.

Cool, wet vintages challenge ripeness, particularly in less favored sites. Grapes may struggle to reach full physiological maturity, resulting in wines with green apple character and aggressive acidity. Producers must decide whether to pick early and preserve acidity at the cost of ripeness, or wait for better flavor development while risking rot or dilution from autumn rains.

Warm, dry vintages present different challenges. Riesling's naturally high acidity provides a buffer against heat, but extreme warmth can reduce freshness and shift the flavor profile toward tropical fruit rather than the stone fruit and citrus that defines classic Rheingau expression. Water stress on steep, shallow-soiled sites can shut down photosynthesis, preventing full ripening despite abundant sunshine.

Ideal conditions combine warm, dry weather from flowering through summer (establishing good fruit set and healthy canopy development) followed by cool, clear autumn weather that allows extended hang time without rot pressure. These conditions allow Riesling to develop complete physiological ripeness while maintaining the high natural acidity that defines the variety.

Climate change has shifted vintage patterns in the Rheingau as throughout Europe. The region now regularly achieves ripeness levels that were exceptional in earlier decades. The 50th parallel, which once marked the northern limit of reliable viticulture, now supports consistent quality even in sites that struggled historically. This warming trend has facilitated the shift toward dry production, grapes that might have required residual sugar for balance in cooler decades now ripen fully enough to finish bone-dry while maintaining structure.

For Fuhshohl specifically, the site's middle-slope position and likely deep soils provide resilience across vintage variation. Higher sites may struggle in cool years, and riverside plots may face rot pressure in wet autumns, but middle-slope sites with good drainage and air circulation can produce quality fruit across a wide range of conditions.
