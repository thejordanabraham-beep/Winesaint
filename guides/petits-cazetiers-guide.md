# Petits Cazetiers Premier Cru: A Complete Guide to Gevrey-Chambertin's Hidden Gem

## Overview & Location

Petits Cazetiers stands as one of Gevrey-Chambertin's most distinguished Premier Cru vineyards, occupying a privileged position on the slopes above this celebrated village in Burgundy's Côte de Nuits. Located in the heart of Burgundy's most prestigious wine region, approximately 12 kilometers south of Dijon, this climat represents the epitome of Burgundian terroir expression and the complexity that has made this region legendary among wine enthusiasts worldwide.

The vineyard sits strategically positioned on the mid-slope of the Côte d'Or escarpment, nestled between the village appellations below and the Grand Cru vineyards that crown the hillside. Petits Cazetiers benefits from an optimal exposition and geological foundation that places it among the most respected Premier Cru sites in Gevrey-Chambertin, a commune already renowned for producing some of Burgundy's most powerful and age-worthy Pinot Noirs.

Within the broader context of the Côte de Nuits, Petits Cazetiers occupies a transitional zone where the limestone-rich soils of the upper slopes begin to intermingle with the clay and alluvial deposits of the lower vineyard sites. This positioning creates a unique terroir that captures both the mineral precision associated with Burgundy's greatest sites and the depth and richness that characterizes Gevrey-Chambertin's distinctive style.

## Size

Petits Cazetiers encompasses approximately 8.5 hectares (21 acres) of vineyard land, making it a medium-sized Premier Cru within Gevrey-Chambertin's extensive classification system. This relatively modest size is typical of Burgundy's fragmented vineyard landscape, where centuries of inheritance laws and land division have created a complex patchwork of small plots owned by multiple proprietors.

The compact size of Petits Cazetiers contributes to its terroir homogeneity, as the entire vineyard shares similar geological foundations, exposure, and microclimatic conditions. However, subtle variations in slope gradient, soil depth, and drainage patterns create nuanced differences across different parcels, allowing individual producers to express their interpretation of this exceptional site's potential.

## Terroir & Geology

The geological foundation of Petits Cazetiers reflects the complex interplay of limestone, marl, and clay that characterizes the Côte d'Or's most distinguished vineyard sites. The underlying bedrock consists primarily of Jurassic limestone from the Bathonian period, specifically the Comblanchien limestone formation that provides the mineral backbone for many of Burgundy's greatest wines.

Above this limestone foundation lies a variable layer of brown limestone mixed with iron-rich clay, creating soils that range from relatively shallow (30-50 centimeters) in the upper portions of the vineyard to deeper deposits (80-120 centimeters) in the lower sections. This variation in soil depth significantly influences vine behavior and wine character, with the shallow soils producing wines of greater mineral intensity and the deeper soils contributing additional richness and textural complexity.

The clay content in Petits Cazetiers averages 25-35%, providing excellent water retention during dry periods while maintaining sufficient drainage to prevent waterlogging during wet seasons. This clay component, rich in iron oxides, contributes to the distinctive red-brown coloration of the soil and plays a crucial role in the wine's color development and tannic structure.

The vineyard faces primarily east-southeast, capturing the gentle morning sun while receiving protection from the harsh afternoon heat during summer months. This exposition ensures optimal ripening conditions while preserving the natural acidity that gives Burgundian Pinot Noir its characteristic elegance and aging potential.

Elevation ranges from 280 to 320 meters above sea level, placing Petits Cazetiers in the optimal zone for Pinot Noir cultivation in Burgundy. The gentle slope gradient of 8-15% provides excellent drainage while preventing excessive erosion, creating stable growing conditions that have remained largely unchanged for centuries.

## Climate & Microclimate

Petits Cazetiers benefits from a continental climate moderated by oceanic influences, creating growing conditions that favor the slow, even ripening essential for producing complex Pinot Noir. The vineyard's position on the mid-slope creates a unique microclimate that combines the cooling effects of elevation with protection from extreme temperature fluctuations.

The east-southeast exposition ensures that the vineyard receives optimal sunlight exposure during the crucial morning hours when photosynthesis is most efficient. This early sun exposure helps eliminate excess moisture from dew and light precipitation, reducing disease pressure and promoting healthy grape development.

Air circulation patterns in Petits Cazetiers are particularly favorable, with gentle thermal currents moving up the slope during the day and cooler air settling into the valley during evening hours. This natural ventilation system helps maintain moderate temperatures during the growing season and provides the diurnal temperature variation that preserves acidity and enhances aromatic development.

The vineyard's position offers natural protection from the prevailing westerly winds that can sometimes bring excessive moisture or temperature extremes. The forest coverage on the hillside above provides additional climatic buffering, moderating temperature swings and creating a stable mesoclimate that rarely experiences the extreme conditions that can compromise vintage quality.

Rainfall patterns in Petits Cazetiers typically follow the regional average of 650-750mm annually, with the vineyard's excellent drainage ensuring that excess moisture moves quickly through the soil profile without creating stagnant conditions that could negatively impact root development or grape quality.

## Viticulture

Pinot Noir represents virtually 100% of plantings in Petits Cazetiers, reflecting both the site's exceptional suitability for this variety and the historical focus on red wine production in Gevrey-Chambertin. The clones most commonly planted include traditional Burgundian selections that have been propagated from the best-performing vines within the region over many generations.

Vine age across Petits Cazetiers varies considerably among different producers, with some parcels containing vines over 60 years old while others feature more recently replanted sections. The older vines, typically 30-50 years of age, often produce the most concentrated and complex wines, as their deep root systems access mineral-rich substrates and their naturally reduced yields result in greater flavor concentration.

Planting density follows traditional Burgundian practices, typically ranging from 10,000 to 12,500 vines per hectare. This high-density planting encourages competition among vines, leading to smaller berries with higher skin-to-juice ratios and more concentrated flavors. The precise spacing varies according to individual producer preferences and the specific characteristics of each parcel.

Viticultural practices in Petits Cazetiers emphasize sustainable and often biodynamic approaches, with many producers adopting organic certification or implementing biodynamic principles without formal certification. Pruning follows the Guyot system, typically utilizing single or double Guyot depending on vine vigor and producer philosophy.

Yield management represents a critical aspect of quality production in Petits Cazetiers, with green harvesting and selective berry removal commonly employed to limit production to optimal levels. Most producers target yields between 35-45 hectoliters per hectare, though exceptional vintages may see slightly higher yields while maintaining quality standards.

Harvest timing requires careful monitoring of both sugar and phenolic ripeness, with picking typically occurring in late September or early October. The vineyard's excellent drainage and optimal exposition generally allow for extended hang time when weather conditions permit, enabling full physiological ripeness while maintaining natural acidity levels.

## Wine Character & Style

Petits Cazetiers produces Pinot Noirs that exemplify the power and complexity for which Gevrey-Chambertin is renowned while maintaining the elegance and finesse that distinguish Premier Cru quality. The wines typically display deep ruby to garnet coloration with excellent concentration and clarity, reflecting both the site's exceptional terroir and the careful winemaking practices employed by its various producers.

Aromatic profiles in Petits Cazetiers wines reveal layers of complexity that evolve dramatically with proper cellaring. Young wines often exhibit intense red and black fruit characteristics, including cherry, raspberry, blackberry, and cassis, complemented by floral notes of violet and rose. As the wines mature, secondary aromas develop, including earth, forest floor, mushroom, and subtle spice elements that reflect both the terroir and the wine's evolution.

The mineral component in Petits Cazetiers wines provides a distinctive backbone that supports the fruit characteristics while adding complexity and length to the finish. This minerality, derived from the limestone-rich soils, manifests as a subtle chalky or stony quality that enhances the wine's overall balance and contributes to its aging potential.

Structural elements in these wines typically include moderate to high tannin levels that provide aging potential while remaining sufficiently refined to allow for enjoyable consumption at various stages of development. The tannins possess a fine-grained quality that reflects both the site's terroir and careful extraction techniques during winemaking.

Acidity levels in Petits Cazetiers wines generally fall within the ideal range for Burgundian Pinot Noir, providing freshness and aging potential while supporting the fruit characteristics. This natural acidity, preserved through optimal harvest timing and careful winemaking, contributes significantly to the wines' food-friendliness and cellar potential.

Textural qualities vary among different producers but typically include a medium to full body with excellent concentration and a silky, refined mouthfeel that develops greater complexity with proper aging. The wines often display remarkable length on the finish, with flavor persistence that can extend for 30-45 seconds or longer in exceptional examples.

## Historical Background & Classification

The history of Petits Cazetiers extends back several centuries, with documented viticulture in this specific climat dating to at least the 17th century. The vineyard's name derives from local terminology related to the small cazettes (small plots or enclosures) that historically characterized this section of the hillside, reflecting the traditional Burgundian practice of detailed vineyard classification and recognition.

The formal classification of Petits Cazetiers as Premier Cru occurred as part of the comprehensive Burgundy classification system established in the 1930s, with official recognition confirmed in 1936. This classification recognized the exceptional quality potential of wines produced from this specific terroir, acknowledging centuries of empirical evidence regarding the site's distinctive characteristics.

Historical records indicate that wines from Petits Cazetiers commanded premium prices relative to village-level appellations even before formal classification, suggesting that local understanding of the site's exceptional quality predated official recognition by many decades. The vineyard's reputation for producing wines of exceptional longevity and complexity contributed to its inclusion among Gevrey-Chambertin's most respected Premier Cru sites.
