# Marcorino

## Introduction

Marcorino is one of the officially recognized menzioni geografiche aggiuntive (MGAs) within the Barbaresco DOCG in Piedmont's Langhe hills. Established as part of the comprehensive MGA system formalized in 2007, Marcorino represents one of the numerous vineyard sites that compose the intricate mosaic of Barbaresco's terroir. Unlike the zone's most celebrated and extensively documented sites—Asili, Rabajà, Montestefano, and Montefico in the commune of Barbaresco proper; Gallina and Santo Stefano in Neive; or Pajorè in Treiso—Marcorino remains among the lesser-publicized MGAs, with limited presence in the historical literature and contemporary wine discourse.

The introduction of the MGA system in Barbaresco preceded similar classification in neighboring Barolo by three years, reflecting a deliberate effort to codify and preserve the region's traditional vineyard nomenclature. The official list "sensibly retained almost all the historically known vineyards, rarely absorbing them in more famous vineyard names for commercial reasons," as noted in documentation of the system's development. Marcorino's inclusion in this official register acknowledges its distinct identity within Barbaresco's viticultural landscape, even if it has not achieved the commercial recognition or critical attention garnered by the zone's most prestigious sites.

## The Barbaresco Context

To understand Marcorino's place within the denomination, one must consider the broader characteristics and evolution of Barbaresco itself. The production zone encompasses the townships of Barbaresco, Neive, and Treiso, northeast and east of Alba, covering approximately 733 hectares as of 2019—roughly one-third the size of Barolo. The region's viticultural identity emerged relatively recently in historical terms, with the Barbaresco name appearing on labels only from 1894, when Domizio Cavazza, professor at the Oenological School of Alba, founded the Barbaresco cooperative.

The emphasis on single-vineyard designation in Barbaresco is itself a modern phenomenon. The first cru-designated Barbaresco, Prunotto's Montestefano, appeared in 1961, and there exists a less firmly established written record of individual vineyard recognition here than in Barolo. Lorenzo Fantini's late 19th-century monograph on Piedmontese viticulture indicated very few "choice positions" in Barbaresco and none whatsoever in Neive. Systematic attempts to list and rate the finest positions date only from the 1960s with Luigi Veronelli and the 1970s with Renato Ratti. 

The consensus regarding Barbaresco's elite sites emerged largely through négociants' willingness to pay premium prices for grapes from specific vineyards. Certain famous vineyards gained their prestige primarily through "the dedicated work and exacting standards of producers such as Gaja, Giacosa, and Alberto di Gresy," whose sustained commitment to particular sites elevated their reputations. This producer-driven model of site recognition contrasts with regions possessing longer documented hierarchies of vineyard classification.

## Terroir Considerations in Barbaresco

While specific terroir data for Marcorino remains elusive in readily available literature, the general principles governing quality in Barbaresco provide essential context. Scientific research on terroir emphasizes that soil's physical characteristics—particularly those governing water supply to the vine—exert the primary influence on grape and wine quality after climate. The best wines emerge from very well-drained soils that furnish steady but moderate, even limiting, water supply to vines. When combined with appropriate restrictive mineral nutrition, this ensures restrained shoot tip growth, especially immediately prior to véraison, and produces smaller berries less liable to congestion and compression within bunches.

Barbaresco's position northeast of Alba places it within a complex geological and topographical landscape where elevation, aspect, soil composition, and mesoclimate vary significantly across short distances. These variations create the foundation for the distinct personalities expressed by different MGAs. The interplay between site-specific characteristics and producer choices regarding plant material, rootstock selection, training systems, and canopy management—what might be termed "managing terroir"—ultimately determines the character of wines from any given site.

The moderate fertility and well-regulated water supply characteristic of Barbaresco's better sites prove particularly suited to Nebbiolo, a variety that produces its most distinguished wines when vine vigor is controlled and berry size limited. The variety's naturally high tannin levels, marked acidity, and complex aromatic profile require careful site selection and management to achieve physiological ripeness and phenolic maturity while maintaining balance.

## Barbaresco Wine Characteristics

Barbaresco wines made from Nebbiolo display a distinctive profile often characterized as more elegant and aromatic compared to the generally more powerful and structured Barolo. Despite Barbaresco's reputation for relative approachability (the wines require 26 months of aging before release compared to Barolo's 38 months, and minimum alcohol of 12.5% though typically reaching 13.5%), the wines are by no means lacking in the tannins and acidity that define the Nebbiolo grape. Young Barbaresco is not inevitably an immediately pleasurable wine; the variety's structural components demand patience.

Well-made Barbaresco exhibits perfumes of cherry and violets in youth, evolving with age to reveal notes of iron, tar, and orange peel—classic markers of mature Nebbiolo. The wines possess muscular structure beneath their aromatic intensity, particularly those produced by adherents of traditional winemaking techniques who increasingly employ organic and biodynamic viticulture. These approaches can yield highly original, long-lived wines that express terroir with particular clarity.

The variation in wine character across Barbaresco's MGAs reflects differences in terroir factors—soil type and depth, elevation, slope angle and orientation, and microclimate—as filtered through producer philosophy and technique. Some sites yield wines of immediate floral charm and relative suppleness; others produce more structured, austere wines demanding extended cellaring. Without specific documentation of Marcorino's characteristics, definitive statements about its typical wine profile remain speculative.

## The MGA System and Lesser-Known Sites

The official MGA list encompasses a substantial number of recognized vineyard sites across Barbaresco's three communes, extending well beyond the handful that dominate critical attention and commercial success. This breadth reflects both the system's inclusiveness and the reality that reputation and recognition in wine regions develop unevenly, influenced by factors beyond intrinsic terroir quality: producer talent and ambition, marketing effectiveness, critical attention, and historical accident all play roles.

Lesser-documented MGAs like Marcorino may produce excellent wines that simply lack the visibility achieved by more famous sites. They may be worked by small family estates that bottle limited quantities sold primarily through local channels or to dedicated clients, without the international distribution that generates critical reviews and market recognition. In some cases, grapes from these sites may be sold to larger négociant houses and blended into communal Barbaresco bottlings rather than vinified and bottled as single-vineyard wines.

The economic realities of Barbaresco production influence these patterns. Approximately 75% of Barbaresco is exported, with primary markets in the United States, Germany, the United Kingdom, and Scandinavia. The rising prices for top Bordeaux and Burgundy have directed increased attention toward prestigious Piedmontese wines, but this attention concentrates on established names and critically acclaimed producers. For an MGA to gain recognition, it typically requires a quality-focused producer willing to invest in site-specific viticulture and vinification while building market awareness—a multi-generational undertaking.

## Historical Development and Current Status

Barbaresco suffered relative commercial obscurity for much of its history, lacking Barolo's connection to the House of Savoy and Turinese nobility. Before the late 19th century, wines from the Barbaresco zone were often blended with Barolo to add freshness. The denomination's emergence as a quality wine region accelerated only in the 1960s, when the efforts of Angelo Gaja and Bruno Giacosa demonstrated Barbaresco's full potential. The work of the Produttori del Barbaresco cooperative, founded in the modern era on the site of Cavazza's original cooperative, further established the zone's reputation through meticulous vineyard-designated bottlings.

The dramatic expansion of vineyard area in Barbaresco—from 484 hectares in the early 1990s to 733 hectares in 2019—reflects both increased global demand for Nebbiolo wines and the economic transformation of the Langhe. Higher prices for Nebbiolo wines have enabled many small growers who historically sold grapes to larger producers to bottle and market their own wines. This shift has increased the diversity of producers and bottlings in the market while also raising questions about the variability in quality across the expanded production area.

Within this evolving landscape, lesser-known MGAs occupy an ambiguous position. They may represent opportunities for discerning consumers to discover compelling wines at prices below those commanded by famous sites. Alternatively, they may indicate vineyards of genuinely lesser quality or suitability for Nebbiolo, included in the official register for reasons of tradition or local politics rather than demonstrated excellence. Without extensive tasting experience across multiple producers and vintages, distinguishing between these possibilities remains difficult.

## Conclusion

Marcorino's limited documentation reflects the realities of wine regions where reputation and recognition concentrate on a small number of celebrated sites while numerous other vineyards remain comparatively obscure. The official MGA system preserves the distinct identity of such sites within Barbaresco's legal and cultural framework, but formal recognition alone cannot generate the market presence or critical attention that might reveal their particular character and quality to a wider audience.

For the curious wine enthusiast or professional, lesser-known MGAs like Marcorino represent both challenge and opportunity: the challenge of limited information and scarcity of available bottlings, and the opportunity to explore Barbaresco's diversity beyond its most famous expressions. As with any vineyard site, quality depends on the intersection of terroir potential with viticulture and winemaking—the "dedicated work and exacting standards" that have elevated other Barbaresco sites to prominence. Whether Marcorino will emerge from relative obscurity depends on the commitment of producers working the site and the receptiveness of critics and consumers to expanding their understanding of Barbaresco's terroir beyond its established hierarchy.
