# Ausario MGA, Barbaresco

## Introduction

Ausario is one of the officially recognized menzioni geografiche aggiuntive (MGAs), or additional geographical mentions, within the Barbaresco DOCG. These single-vineyard designations, introduced in 2007, represent a significant refinement of the Piemontese DOC system and predated similar reforms in Barolo by three years. The MGA system sensibly retained historically known vineyards rather than absorbing them into more famous names for commercial reasons, preserving the region's viticultural heritage and diversity.

Located within the commune of Barbaresco, Ausario represents one of the smaller and lesser-documented sites in this prestigious appellation. While other Barbaresco MGAs—such as San Lorenzo, Tildin, and Martinenga—have gained international recognition through the dedicated work of benchmark producers, Ausario remains more obscure in the contemporary wine literature, with limited production and fewer producers bottling wines from this specific site.

## Terroir Context

Understanding Ausario requires first understanding Barbaresco's broader terroir characteristics. The region's soils can be roughly divided into two principal types, each imparting distinct characteristics to Nebbiolo wines. The first is calcareous clay from the Tortonion epoch, very similar to soils found in the Barolo communes of La Morra and Barolo, which tends to yield more perfumed, fruit-driven wines with aromatic elegance. The second soil type is the more compact Sant'Agata fossil marl, somewhat resembling the soils of Monforte d'Alba and Serralunga d'Alba in Barolo, which typically produces wines with more pronounced tannic structure and grip.

The specific soil composition and exposition of Ausario would determine which stylistic profile the wines from this site express, though detailed geological surveys specific to this MGA remain difficult to access in published literature. What can be said with certainty is that all Barbaresco vineyards benefit from the moderating influence of the Tanaro River, which creates favorable mesoclimates and allows Nebbiolo to ripen more reliably than in some parts of Barolo. Vineyards closest to the river tend to show earlier ripening, contributing to the historical perception of Barbaresco as expressing a more accessible style in youth, though this characterization oversimplifies the considerable variation among individual sites.

## Wine Characteristics

Wines bearing the Ausario designation must adhere to Barbaresco DOCG regulations, which require a minimum of 26 months of aging, with at least 9 months in oak, before release (50 months for Riserva bottlings). This contrasts with Barolo's more stringent requirements of 38 months total aging with 18 in oak, reflecting Barbaresco's generally earlier-maturing character—though the assumption that this makes Barbaresco inherently less ageworthy is misleading.

Barbaresco wines, regardless of specific MGA, share fundamental characteristics derived from Nebbiolo: significant tannins and acidity that can make young wines quite austere and demanding. The minimum alcohol of 12.5% is easily exceeded, with most wines reaching 13.5% or higher. Classic Barbaresco aromatics include cherry, violets, rose petals, and with age, more complex notes of iron, tar, orange peel, and dried herbs.

The stylistic expression of individual MGAs varies based on their specific terroir, and contemporary winemaking approaches add another layer of variation. While the 1970s and 1980s saw a shift toward shorter macerations and aging in French barriques for rounder, more immediately approachable wines, an increasing number of producers have returned to longer macerations—often up to 40 days—and aging in large traditional oak casks (botti), seeking to preserve Nebbiolo's delicate perfume and achieve wines with more structured, long-lived profiles.

## Producers and Market Position

Detailed information about specific producers working the Ausario site remains limited in readily available literature. This likely reflects both the small size of the MGA and the possibility that much of its fruit is bottled under the broader Barbaresco designation rather than as a single-vineyard wine. The economics of MGA bottlings favor sites with established reputations that command premium pricing, and lesser-known sites often remain blended into communal or producer-level cuvées.

The Produttori del Barbaresco, one of Italy's finest cooperatives, has been instrumental in establishing quality benchmarks for the region and produces single-vineyard bottlings from multiple MGAs, though their work with Ausario specifically is not well documented in English-language sources. Independent smaller producers increasingly focus on organic and biodynamic viticulture combined with traditional winemaking techniques, producing highly individualistic wines that express their specific sites with clarity and personality.

## Conclusion

Ausario represents one of many officially recognized vineyard sites that collectively define Barbaresco's remarkable diversity. While it lacks the fame of benchmark MGAs, its recognition in the 2007 classification acknowledges its distinct identity within the appellation's viticultural landscape. For wine enthusiasts, exploring lesser-known MGAs like Ausario offers an opportunity to discover the nuances that make Barbaresco's terroir so compelling, even when detailed historical documentation or critical commentary remains scarce. As the region continues to gain recognition for quality and terroir expression, sites like Ausario may yet reveal their particular character more fully to the broader wine world.
