# Garassino

## Introduction

Garassino is one of the lesser-known menzioni geografiche aggiuntive (MGA) within the Barbaresco appellation, located in the commune of Barbaresco itself. While it may not command the same recognition as storied sites like Asili, Rabajà, or Montestefano, Garassino represents the broader tapestry of single-vineyard designations that were officially codified when Barbaresco introduced its MGA system in 2007—a pioneering move that predated Barolo's similar classification by three years.

The MGA system in Barbaresco was designed to preserve the historic vineyard nomenclature of the region while providing a framework for understanding the appellation's remarkable diversity of terroir. Unlike some wine regions where commercial interests led to the absorption of smaller sites into more famous names, Barbaresco's classification "sensibly retained almost all the historically known vineyards," as noted in contemporary wine literature. Garassino falls into the category of these preserved designations—sites that may not have the lengthy documented history of négociant price premiums dating back to the 19th century, but nonetheless represent distinct parcels with their own terroir signatures.

## Terroir

Garassino shares the fundamental geological characteristics that define Barbaresco's greatest sites: Tertiary marine sediments dominated by calcareous marls deposited when this area lay beneath ancient seas. These Sant'Agata Fossil Marl formations, locally called *marne di Sant'Agata*, provide the foundation for Nebbiolo cultivation throughout the appellation. The soils in Garassino, like those across Barbaresco, offer what research has identified as critical terroir factors: "moderate fertility and well-regulated, moderately sufficient water supply."

The importance of soil in Barbaresco cannot be overstated, though it must be understood in context. As terroir research has demonstrated, "soil characteristics do influence grape quality, yield, and wine individuality," particularly through their governance of water supply to the vine and their influence on soil and ambient temperatures. The calcareous marl soils of sites like Garassino provide excellent drainage while maintaining enough water-holding capacity to sustain the vine through dry periods—a crucial balance in achieving optimal Nebbiolo ripeness without excessive vigor.

The specific exposition and elevation of Garassino contribute to its microclimate, though these details are less thoroughly documented than those of Barbaresco's most celebrated sites. What is certain is that the commune of Barbaresco as a whole benefits from favorable aspects—primarily south and southwest exposures—that allow Nebbiolo to achieve full phenolic ripeness while maintaining the aromatic intensity and structural elegance that distinguish Barbaresco from its more powerful neighbor, Barolo.

The interplay between soil composition, elevation, and aspect creates what researchers describe as significant variation even within small areas. "The attributes of soil can change rapidly within a vineyard, both vertically and horizontally," affecting water access and vine vigor. Understanding these subtle differences is essential for producers working sites like Garassino, where thoughtful vineyard management can maximize the expression of place.

## Wine Characteristics

Wines from Garassino should be understood within the broader context of Barbaresco's character. Barbaresco, made entirely from Nebbiolo, is "firmly structured" yet demonstrates "striking qualities of elegance and aromatic intensity" that distinguish it from Barolo. While minimum alcohol requirements stand at 12.5% (with wines regularly reaching 13.5%), the appellation is defined by "tannins and acidity that mark the Nebbiolo grape."

The classic Barbaresco aromatic profile—"perfumes of cherry, violets, and, with age, iron, tar, and orange peel"—provides the template for understanding wines from individual MGAs. Garassino, being less documented than the appellation's premier sites, does not have an extensive critical record establishing its specific signature within this framework. However, the site's terroir would logically produce wines expressing Barbaresco's characteristic elegance rather than power, with the aromatic purity and refined tannins that define the commune's better exposures.

The aging potential of Barbaresco is substantial, despite the shorter mandatory aging requirements compared to Barolo (26 months minimum, versus Barolo's 38 months, with 50 months for Barbaresco Riserva versus 62 for Barolo Riserva). This "has led to the false assumption that Barbaresco is less ageworthy," but quality Barbaresco from well-situated sites can evolve gracefully for decades. Wines from Garassino would be expected to follow this pattern, developing tertiary complexity with appropriate cellaring.

## Current Context

The evolution of winemaking in Barbaresco has direct implications for how sites like Garassino are vinified today. After a period in the 1970s and 1980s when producers moved toward shorter macerations and French barrique aging, "an increasing number of Barbaresco producers are returning to long maceration, often up to 40 days, and ageing in large oak casks rather than barrique." This philosophical shift reflects a renewed focus on terroir expression and the preservation of Nebbiolo's "gentle perfume."

Garassino remains a minor player in the Barbaresco landscape, with limited commercial presence and documentation. It represents the tier of MGAs that provide valuable vineyard land within the appellation without commanding the prestige—or prices—of historically recognized premier sites. For producers working this land, the challenge and opportunity lie in farming and vinification practices that allow the site's intrinsic qualities to emerge, contributing to the broader understanding of Barbaresco's remarkable diversity of expression within a compact geographic area.
