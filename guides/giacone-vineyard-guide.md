# Giacone MGA, Barbaresco

## Introduction

Giacone is one of the officially recognized menzioni geografiche aggiuntive (MGA) within the Barbaresco DOCG, located in the commune of Neive. When the Barbaresco consorzio established its formal list of single-vineyard designations in 2007—notably three years before Barolo implemented its own MGA system—Giacone was included among the sites deemed worthy of individual recognition. However, unlike some of the more celebrated MGAs in the denomination such as Asili, Rabajà, or Montestefano, Giacone remains among the lesser-documented and less commercially prominent sites within Barbaresco's vineyard hierarchy.

The relative obscurity of Giacone stands in contrast to the trajectory of other Neive-based MGAs. As noted in historical assessments of Barbaresco's finest positions, sites such as Albesani, Gallina, and Santo Stefano in Neive have achieved significant recognition, with Santo Stefano in particular gaining prestige through "the dedicated work and exacting standards of producers such as Gaja, Giacosa, and Alberto di Gresy." Giacone, by comparison, has not benefited from similar high-profile advocacy or the singular focus of a prestigious estate.

## Historical Context and Recognition

The challenge in documenting Giacone reflects the broader historical reality of vineyard classification in Barbaresco. Unlike Barolo, which possessed a more established written record of cru designation dating to the 19th century, Barbaresco's single-vineyard tradition developed later and less comprehensively. Lorenzo Fantini's influential monograph on Piedmontese viticulture from the late 19th century "indicates very few 'choice positions' in Barbaresco (and none whatsoever in Neive)." The systematic identification and rating of Barbaresco's finest sites only began in earnest in the 1960s with Luigi Veronelli's work, followed by Renato Ratti's classifications in the 1970s.

That Giacone appears on the official MGA list established in 2007 indicates its recognition as a legitimate historical vineyard site. The compilers of this list, drawing on sources including Alessandro Masnaghetti's comprehensive cartographic work, "sensibly retained almost all the historically known vineyards, rarely absorbing them in more famous vineyard names for commercial reasons." This suggests Giacone possessed sufficient historical documentation and viticultural legitimacy to warrant inclusion, even if it lacked the commercial renown of more celebrated neighbors.

## The Neive Context

Understanding Giacone requires situating it within the broader context of Neive, the southernmost of Barbaresco's three main communes (alongside Barbaresco and Treiso). Neive's viticultural landscape differs subtly from the other townships. While comprehensive site-specific data for Giacone remains limited in published sources, the wines of Neive generally express the fundamental characteristics of Nebbiolo from Barbaresco: the variety's signature tannins and acidity, aromatics of cherry and violets in youth, and the development of complex tertiary notes—iron, tar, orange peel—with age.

The general principles governing terroir quality in Barbaresco apply to all MGAs, including Giacone. Research by Dr. Gérard Seguin and others has demonstrated that in fine wine regions, "soil characteristics do influence grape quality, yield, and wine individuality," though these effects remain "subsidiary to those of climate, vine variety, and vine management." Of the soil-related factors, "the most important are those governing the supply of water to the vine, probably followed by those influencing temperatures in and above the soil." Within Barbaresco's relatively compact geography, such variations in water regulation and thermal properties create the distinctions between individual MGAs.

## Current State and Future Prospects

The limited commercial presence of Giacone-designated wines reflects both the realities of small production volumes and the absence of a producer who has championed the site specifically. In the contemporary Barbaresco landscape, where "a very high proportion of Barolo and Barbaresco is exported (about 85 and 75 per cent respectively)," commercial success often depends on established reputations built over decades. Many small estates in the region now "bottle and market their own wines" rather than selling grapes to larger négociants, but building recognition for a lesser-known MGA requires sustained effort and exceptional quality.

For serious students of Barbaresco, sites like Giacone represent both a challenge and an opportunity. The challenge lies in the paucity of detailed documentation, critical assessment, and comparative tasting notes. The opportunity exists in the potential for discovery—the possibility that Giacone, properly cultivated and vinified, might reveal distinctive characteristics that have simply not yet been adequately documented or promoted. As the global fine wine market continues to seek alternatives to ever-escalating Burgundy and Bordeaux prices, attention to Barbaresco's deeper roster of MGAs may eventually bring sites like Giacone into clearer focus.

Until such time as more comprehensive analysis and wider commercial bottling illuminate Giacone's specific personality, it remains one of Barbaresco's quieter expressions—officially recognized, historically legitimate, but awaiting its full articulation in bottle and in critical assessment.
