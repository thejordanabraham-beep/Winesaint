# Verdicchio dei Castelli di Jesi: The Marche's White Wine Heartland

Verdicchio dei Castelli di Jesi represents the larger and more accessible of the Marche's two premier Verdicchio zones. While its inland sibling Matelica often commands more attention from collectors, Castelli di Jesi produces the lion's share of Italy's finest expressions of this ancient variety, wines that balance Mediterranean warmth with surprising freshness and longevity.

## Geography and Climate

The appellation sprawls across 3,000 hectares in north-central Marche, radiating outward from the medieval town of Jesi, birthplace of Holy Roman Emperor Frederick II in 1194. This is ten times the planted area of Verdicchio di Matelica, making Castelli di Jesi the dominant force in Verdicchio production.

The vineyards occupy a series of hills flanking the Esino River, with elevations ranging from 100 to 700 meters. Most plantings cluster toward the lower end of this spectrum, rarely exceeding 300 meters. This distinguishes Castelli di Jesi from Matelica, where vineyards routinely reach 500 meters in the high valley of the Alta Vallesina.

The climate here is notably warmer than Matelica's. Positioned between the Adriatic Sea to the east and the Apennine Mountains to the west, Castelli di Jesi benefits from dual moderating influences, maritime breezes temper summer heat, while the mountains provide a rain shadow effect. Yet this same geography creates vulnerability: spring frosts remain a persistent threat, capable of devastating yields in difficult years.

The proximity to the Adriatic (just 30 kilometers at the nearest point) fundamentally shapes the growing season. Where Matelica experiences sharp diurnal temperature swings and a distinctly continental character, Castelli di Jesi's climate trends Mediterranean. This warmth allows for fuller phenolic ripeness but requires careful site selection and canopy management to preserve Verdicchio's signature acidity.

## Terroir

The soils of Castelli di Jesi derive from marine sediments deposited when this region lay beneath ancient seas. Calcareous marl dominates, often mixed with clay and sand in varying proportions. These moderately fertile soils encourage steady vine growth without excessive vigor: a critical factor for Verdicchio, which can easily overproduce if left unchecked.

The rolling topography creates countless mesoclimates. South and southeast-facing slopes capture maximum sunlight, producing rounder, more generous wines. North-facing sites and higher elevations yield more tension and mineral precision, though these cooler exposures risk underripeness in marginal vintages.

The Esino River valley itself creates a natural corridor for air movement, helping to mitigate humidity and fungal pressure during the growing season. This drainage of cool air also contributes to the frost risk that haunts growers each spring.

## The DOCG-DOC Distinction

The appellation operates on two tiers: Verdicchio dei Castelli di Jesi DOC and the more restrictive Castelli di Jesi Verdicchio Riserva DOCG, established in 2010. The DOCG designation applies exclusively to Riserva wines: those aged for a minimum of 18 months before release, including at least six months in bottle.

Many producers strategically use the DOC designation to avoid these aging requirements for their younger, fresher expressions. The DOC also permits sparkling wines (metodo classico and Charmat) and passito styles, neither of which qualify for DOCG status. This creates a practical hierarchy: DOC for early-drinking wines and alternative styles, DOCG Riserva for age-worthy expressions intended to demonstrate Verdicchio's longevity.

## Wine Characteristics

Castelli di Jesi Verdicchio typically shows more immediate fruit character than its Matelica counterpart, ripe citrus, white peach, and almond blossom rather than Matelica's steelier minerality and herb-driven profile. The warmer growing conditions yield wines with slightly lower acidity and fuller body, though quality-focused producers working higher-elevation sites can achieve remarkable tension.

The variety's name derives from "verde" (green), referencing the golden-green hue of the berries at full ripeness. This same greenish cast often appears in the wines themselves, particularly in youth. The almond note (sometimes described as bitter almond or marzipan) represents Verdicchio's most distinctive aromatic signature, present across both appellations but often more pronounced in Castelli di Jesi's riper expressions.

Riserva bottlings demonstrate genuine aging potential. Well-made examples evolve over 10-15 years, developing honeyed complexity, dried fruit notes, and a waxy texture while retaining their core of acidity. This longevity remains underappreciated outside Italy, where Verdicchio still suffers from outdated associations with simple, amphora-shaped bottles and seaside tourism.

## Key Producers

Several estates have elevated Castelli di Jesi's reputation beyond regional curiosity. **Umani Ronchi** produces both accessible DOC bottlings and serious Riserva wines, demonstrating the appellation's range. **Garofoli** maintains extensive holdings and a reputation for consistency across multiple quality tiers. **Bucci** crafts benchmark Riserva wines from the Villa Bucci vineyard, showcasing what extended aging brings to Verdicchio: these are among the appellation's longest-lived expressions.

**Sartarelli** focuses exclusively on Verdicchio, farming 65 hectares and producing a range that spans fresh DOC wines to complex single-vineyard Riservas. Their Balciana bottling represents one of the appellation's most sought-after expressions, combining Castelli di Jesi's fruit generosity with structural backbone.

**Marotti Campi** works organically across 60 hectares, emphasizing site expression through single-vineyard bottlings. Their approach highlights how terroir variation within the broader appellation can produce markedly different wines, even within the same vintage.

## Vintage Considerations

Spring frost represents the primary vintage wildcard. Years with late frosts can devastate production, concentrating remaining fruit but reducing volume dramatically. Conversely, frost-free springs with moderate summer temperatures produce the most balanced wines, sufficient ripeness without the flabbiness that excessive heat can bring.

Harvest timing proves critical. Verdicchio maintains acidity well into ripeness, but waiting too long sacrifices the variety's characteristic freshness for blowsy, overripe character. The best producers pick based on phenolic ripeness and flavor development rather than sugar accumulation alone, typically harvesting in mid-to-late September.

Wet autumns pose minimal risk compared to many Italian white wine zones, as harvest usually concludes before October rains arrive. The greater danger comes from summer drought, which can shut down photosynthesis and create angular, unbalanced wines lacking Verdicchio's typical mid-palate flesh.
