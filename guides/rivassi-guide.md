## Context Within Barolo's MGA System

Understanding Rivassi requires situating it within Barolo's broader evolution from a blended, négociant-dominated tradition to the single-vineyard bottling culture that emerged in the latter half of the 20th century. Historically, most top Barolo producers took pride in blending wines from multiple vineyards, seeking complexity through assemblage (a practice that continues among traditionalists such as Bartolo Mascarello. The movement toward single-vineyard bottlings gained momentum through the work of pioneers like Angelo Gaja and Bruno Giacosa, who championed the expression of individual site characteristics.

The formalization of the MGA system in 2010 represented both recognition of established quality hierarchies and an attempt to bring order to an increasingly fragmented landscape of vineyard designations. Under these regulations, the word "vigna" (vineyard) can only appear on labels when accompanied by the MGA name, creating a more regulated and transparent system than previously existed.

## Terroir Considerations in Barolo

While specific terroir information about Rivassi is not available in the standard viticultural literature, any Barolo MGA operates within the broader pedological and climatic framework that defines the denomination. Barolo's reputation rests fundamentally on its complex interplay of soil types, exposition, elevation, and mesoclimate) factors that can vary dramatically even within short distances.

The physical characteristics of soil (particularly those governing water supply to the vine) represent the most critical terroir factors beyond climate itself. Research has consistently demonstrated that the finest wines emerge from well-drained soils that provide steady but moderate, even limiting, water supply to vines. When combined with appropriately restrictive mineral nutrition, this ensures restrained shoot growth, particularly in the critical period before véraison, and typically produces smaller berries less prone to bunch congestion.

Barolo's vineyard sites range across varied geological substrata, with rapid changes in soil composition both vertically through soil profiles and horizontally across vineyard blocks. These variations, combined with differences in topography and elevation, mean that adjacent parcels can experience markedly different growing conditions despite receiving identical rainfall. Exposition (the angle and direction of slope) further modulates temperature, sunlight exposure, and air drainage, creating the mosaic of mesoclimates that gives Barolo its diverse range of expressions.

## Wine Characteristics

Without specific documentation of wines from Rivassi, one can only reference the general characteristics expected from Barolo DOCG wines produced under the denomination's regulations. All Barolo must be made from 100 percent Nebbiolo and aged for a minimum of three years before release, with at least 18 months in oak. These wines typically display aromas of sour cherries, herbs, and sometimes dried flowers, with full body, high acidity, and substantial tannins in the best years.

Traditional Barolo production involved extended aging in large oak barrels (botti) to soften the variety's famously austere tannins. Contemporary viticulture (with improved canopy management, yield control, and gentler extraction methods) produces wines with smoother, riper tannins from the outset, allowing many producers to employ shorter oak aging or smaller barrels while maintaining structure and ageability. The best Barolos are notably powerful and tannic in youth, typically requiring 10-15 years of cellaring to reach optimal expression.

The specific character any MGA expresses depends on the intricate relationship between site characteristics and winemaking approach. This market dynamic has influenced vineyard management decisions throughout the denomination, with some sites that might historically have contributed to Barolo blends now directed toward other designations.
