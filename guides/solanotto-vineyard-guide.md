## The MGA System Context

What we can establish with confidence is the framework within which Solanotto would operate as an officially designated MGA. The 2010 regulations established three categories of geographical sub-zones within Barolo DOCG:

- Entire villages (such as La Morra)
- Specified single vineyards (like Bussia or Cannubi, historically recognized sites with established reputations)
- Composite MGAs that incorporate multiple traditional vineyard names into a single designation (for example, Via Nuova vineyard was absorbed into the Terlo MGA)

Under current regulations, the term *vigna* (vineyard) may only appear on labels when accompanied by the MGA designation, providing a structured hierarchy for geographical identification within Barolo's complex landscape.

## Historical Context of Single-Vineyard Barolo

The emergence of single-vineyard bottlings in Barolo represents a relatively modern phenomenon overlaid on centuries of tradition. Historically, top producers—including houses operating in the négociant tradition—prided themselves on blending wines from multiple vineyard sources to achieve complexity and balance. This practice continues among certain producers, most notably Bartolo Mascarello, who maintain that blending across sites better expresses the character of Barolo as a whole.

However, specific vineyard sites have commanded premium prices and special recognition for generations, documented in both written sources (from Lorenzo Fantini's late 19th-century work through modern writers including Luigi Veronelli, Renato Ratti, and Alessandro Masnaghetti) and in the oral traditions of the zone. Producers such as Angelo Gaja and Bruno Giacosa championed single-vineyard bottlings in the mid-to-late 20th century, accelerating a trend toward cru-specific wines that the 2010 MGA system ultimately codified.
