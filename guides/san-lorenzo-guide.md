# San Lorenzo

## A Note on Location

San Lorenzo is one of the most celebrated vineyard sites in **Barbaresco**, not Barolo. While both denominations produce Nebbiolo wines in the Langhe hills of Piedmont, San Lorenzo (particularly famous as Gaja's Sorì San Lorenzo) has established its reputation within the Barbaresco DOCG. This guide addresses the San Lorenzo MGA in Barbaresco, as no San Lorenzo MGA exists within the Barolo denomination.

## Introduction

San Lorenzo stands among the most prestigious single vineyards in Barbaresco, a small but influential denomination that produces some of Piedmont's finest Nebbiolo wines. Unlike many historically documented crus in neighboring Barolo, San Lorenzo's reputation is a more modern phenomenon, built largely through the dedicated work and exacting standards of producers who recognized the site's exceptional potential in the latter half of the 20th century.

The vineyard gained international prominence through Angelo Gaja's Sorì San Lorenzo, a single-vineyard Barbaresco that became one of Italy's most sought-after and expensive wines. Along with Tildin and Martinenga in Barbaresco proper, and Santo Stefano in Neive, San Lorenzo represents a category of vineyard whose current prestige derives less from centuries of documented excellence and more from the transformative work of visionary producers who elevated both the site and the broader reputation of Barbaresco itself.

## Historical Context and Recognition

The history of single-vineyard designation in Barbaresco differs markedly from that of Barolo. While Lorenzo Fantini's influential late 19th-century monograph on Piedmontese viticulture documented numerous "choice positions" in Barolo, it indicated very few such sites in Barbaresco and none whatsoever in neighboring Neive. This absence from historical records doesn't necessarily reflect lower quality but rather the later development of Barbaresco as a serious wine zone.

Single-vineyard Barbarescos are a relatively recent phenomenon. The first such wine, Prunotto's Barbaresco Montestefano, appeared only in 1961. The systematic identification and rating of Barbaresco's finest positions began in earnest during the 1960s with Luigi Veronelli's work and continued through the 1970s with Renato Ratti's influential classifications. Against this backdrop, San Lorenzo emerged as a site of distinction through the work of producers willing to invest in site-specific winemaking and to champion the potential of Barbaresco at a time when it remained overshadowed by Barolo.

The vineyard's significance is underscored by the existence of "The Vines of San Lorenzo," a 1992 monograph by E. Steinberg dedicated entirely to this site (a rare honor that speaks to San Lorenzo's cultural and oenological importance.

## The Role of Angelo Gaja

No discussion of San Lorenzo can separate the vineyard from Angelo Gaja, whose Sorì San Lorenzo became one of the iconic wines of modern Italian viticulture. The "Sorì" prefix in Piedmontese dialect refers to a hillside location with favorable sun exposure) a prime south or southwest-facing slope. Gaja's work at San Lorenzo exemplified his broader philosophy of radical quality improvement through meticulous viticulture, lower yields, and modernized winemaking techniques that challenged traditional Barbaresco practices.

In 1999, Gaja made the controversial decision to declassify his most famous wines, including Sorì San Lorenzo, from Barbaresco DOCG to the more generic Langhe Nebbiolo DOC designation. This dramatic move allowed him to blend small percentages of international varieties such as Cabernet Sauvignon into wines previously made from 100% Nebbiolo. The decision shocked the wine world and sparked debate about tradition versus innovation in Piedmont. For nearly two decades, some of Barbaresco's most celebrated vineyard names (San Lorenzo among them) appeared on labels only as Langhe Nebbiolo.

However, under the leadership of Gaja's daughter Gaia, working alongside her siblings Giovanni and Rosella, the estate has restored the Barbaresco denomination to these wines, returning San Lorenzo to its rightful place within the appellation it helped make famous. This restoration reflects both a generational shift and an acknowledgment of Barbaresco's secure position among the world's great wine regions: a position that wines like Sorì San Lorenzo were instrumental in establishing.

## The Barbaresco Context

Understanding San Lorenzo requires understanding Barbaresco's distinctive character within the Nebbiolo universe. The Barbaresco DOCG, at roughly one-third the size of Barolo, encompasses villages east of Alba including Barbaresco itself, Neive, and Treiso. While sharing Barolo's commitment to 100% Nebbiolo, Barbaresco's vineyards typically sit at slightly lower elevations and experience a marginally warmer mesoclimate. These subtle differences translate into harvest dates approximately one week earlier than in Barolo.

This moderate climatic advantage contributes to Barbaresco's generally more approachable character in youth compared to Barolo's famously austere tannic structure. The DOCG's regulations reflect this subtle ripeness, requiring two years and two months of aging from November 1 following harvest, compared to Barolo's longer mandatory aging periods. However, this relative accessibility shouldn't suggest lighter wines (Barbaresco, and particularly wines from elite sites like San Lorenzo, possesses the tannin and acidity characteristic of Nebbiolo, along with the capacity for extended aging.

## Wine Characteristics

While comprehensive tasting notes specific to San Lorenzo are limited in the available documentation, we can contextualize the site within Barbaresco's broader stylistic framework. Barbaresco Nebbiolo typically offers perfumed aromatics of cherry, rose petals, and violets, evolving with age toward more complex notes of iron, tar, leather, and dried orange peel. The wines combine full body with high acidity and substantial tannin) young Barbaresco, despite its reputation for relative approachability, demands patience or careful food pairing.

San Lorenzo, having gained its reputation through Gaja's meticulous viticulture and modern winemaking, likely expresses these Barbaresco characteristics with particular intensity and refinement. The "Sorì" designation suggests favorable sun exposure that would promote full phenolic ripeness, potentially yielding wines with density and structure while maintaining Nebbiolo's essential perfume and elegance.

The site's ability to produce wines that commanded extraordinary prices on the international market speaks to both exceptional fruit quality and reliable performance across vintages. Single-vineyard Barbarescos from top sites develop remarkable complexity with age, and San Lorenzo's track record suggests wines capable of evolution over decades rather than years.

## The MGA System

The introduction of the MGA (Menzioni Geografiche Aggiuntive) system in Barbaresco in 2007 (three years before Barolo implemented its own version in 2010) formalized the denomination's approach to single-vineyard designation. This official recognition of specific vineyard sites, also referred to as crus, sensibly retained almost all historically known vineyards without absorbing them into more famous names for commercial reasons.

San Lorenzo's inclusion in the MGA system validated decades of quality-focused production and secured the site's recognition within Barbaresco's official hierarchy. The MGA designation provides consumers with geographical precision and protects historically significant vineyard names from dilution or misuse.

## Current Status and Recognition

Today, San Lorenzo occupies an established position among Barbaresco's most prestigious sites. While consensus about Barbaresco's top vineyards (historically determined by négociants' willingness to pay premium prices for grapes from specific sites) typically emphasizes vineyards like Asili, Montefico, Montestefano, and Rabajà in Barbaresco proper, along with Albesani and Gallina in Neive and Pajorè in Treiso, San Lorenzo commands respect as a site whose reputation was built through uncompromising quality standards.

The restoration of the Barbaresco denomination to wines from this site represents more than a labeling decision (it acknowledges San Lorenzo's integral role in Barbaresco's identity and the denomination's maturity as a wine region secure in its global standing. The vineyard stands as a testament to how dedicated producers can elevate specific sites from relative obscurity to international recognition within a single generation, fundamentally reshaping perceptions of an entire denomination in the process.

## Conclusion

San Lorenzo exemplifies modern Barbaresco at its finest) a site whose reputation rests not on centuries of documented history but on the transformative work of visionary producers committed to expressing terroir through meticulous viticulture and exacting standards. While lacking the deep historical record of some Barolo crus, San Lorenzo's impact on Barbaresco's evolution and its role in establishing the denomination's reputation among collectors and connoisseurs worldwide cannot be overstated. The vineyard remains synonymous with the excellence and ambition that define contemporary Barbaresco, securing its place among Piedmont's most significant vineyard sites.
