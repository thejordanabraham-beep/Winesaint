# Bricco di Neive

## Introduction

Bricco di Neive is one of the officially recognized MGAs (menzioni geografiche aggiuntive) within the commune of Neive in the Barbaresco DOCG zone. Part of the comprehensive list of single vineyards formalized in 2007—notably three years before Barolo implemented its own MGA system—Bricco di Neive represents one of the many historically cultivated sites that were sensibly retained in the official classification rather than being absorbed into more commercially prominent vineyard names.

Neive itself stands as one of the three principal townships producing Barbaresco, alongside the communes of Barbaresco and Treiso. Self-styled as "the township of four wines" (the others being Moscato, Barbera, and Dolcetto), Neive has witnessed a dramatic transformation in its viticultural landscape over recent decades. Nebbiolo consolidated its position in Neive only after the Second World War, and the commune has experienced significant expansion of Nebbiolo plantings at the expense of Barbera and Dolcetto. From 140 hectares in 1995, Neive's Nebbiolo holdings grew to 265 hectares (654 acres) by the late 2010s, reflecting both the wine's commercial success and critical recognition. Moscato, by contrast, has remained relatively stable, still comprising roughly half the commune's planted area.

## Historical Context and Recognition

The historical documentation of specific vineyard sites in Neive reveals an interesting pattern in the evolution of Barbaresco's cru system. Lorenzo Fantini's influential late 19th-century monograph on Piedmontese viticulture indicated very few "choice positions" in Barbaresco and notably identified none whatsoever in Neive. This stands in marked contrast to the situation in Barolo, where single-vineyard designation had deeper historical roots. The first systematic attempts to list and rate the finest positions in Barbaresco date only from the 1960s through Luigi Veronelli and the 1970s through Renato Ratti's work.

Within Neive, négociants' willingness to pay premium prices for grapes from certain vineyards gradually established consensus about the commune's top sites. Albesani and Gallina emerged as widely recognized reference points for quality in Neive, while Santo Stefano achieved particular prestige through the dedicated work and exacting standards of elite producers such as Gaja, Giacosa, and Alberto di Gresy. These benchmark vineyards helped establish Neive's reputation for distinctive Barbaresco, though the commune's recognition developed later than that of the village of Barbaresco itself, where sites like Asili, Montefico, Montestefano, and Rabajà had earlier recognition.

The introduction of the official MGA system in 2007 formalized many sites that had limited prior documentation but represented genuine historical vineyard identities. Bricco di Neive appears to fall into this category—an authentic geographical designation that may not have achieved the fame of Santo Stefano or Gallina but represents a distinct terroir unit within the commune's viticultural mosaic.

## Terroir and Viticultural Context

Understanding Bricco di Neive requires situating it within the broader terroir framework of Neive and Barbaresco. The soils throughout the Barbaresco production zone can be roughly divided into two principal types. The calcareous clay of the Tortonian epoch—which is very similar to that found in the Barolo communes of La Morra and Barolo—produces wines with perfumed characteristics and relatively early accessibility. The alternative soil type contributes to different structural profiles in the resulting wines.

Neive's viticultural landscape is characterized by the moderate water supply that proves essential to producing high-quality red wines from Nebbiolo. Research has demonstrated that terroir capable of producing exceptional red wines provides only moderate amounts of water to the vines, thereby inducing beneficial water-deficit stress. This regulated water supply, combined with moderate soil fertility and appropriate nitrogen levels, results in controlled vine vigour—a key factor in achieving the balance of fruit concentration, tannin structure, and aromatic complexity that defines fine Barbaresco.

The elevation, aspect, and microclimate variations across Neive's vineyard sites create the diversity that justifies individual MGA designations. These factors influence the critical period of grape ripening, affecting the development of Nebbiolo's characteristic aromatic compounds, phenolic maturity, and acid retention that determines aging potential.

## Wine Characteristics and Style

Wines from Neive MGAs participate in the broader stylistic identity of Barbaresco while expressing site-specific nuances. Barbaresco has often been characterized as a lighter-bodied wine than Barolo, though this characterization requires qualification. While the minimum alcoholic strength requirement stands at 12.5% (with wines frequently reaching 13.5% or higher), and the mandatory aging period is shorter than Barolo's (26 months total aging with nine months in wood for standard Barbaresco, compared to 38 months for Barolo), these regulatory differences do not indicate lesser structure or ageability. The assumption that Barbaresco is inherently less age-worthy represents a persistent misconception that has unfortunately influenced some producers to attempt emulating Barolo rather than honoring Barbaresco's unique character.

Contemporary Barbaresco from quality-focused producers—including those working Neive's various MGAs—demonstrates substantial tannins and the pronounced acidity that characterizes the Nebbiolo grape. Young wines are not inevitably pleasurable; they require patience. An increasing number of Barbaresco producers have returned to extended maceration periods, often up to 40 days, and aging in large traditional oak casks (botti) rather than French barriques, recovering techniques that some abandoned during the 1970s and 1980s in pursuit of rounder, more immediately accessible wines. This shift back to traditional methods has proven particularly appropriate for Nebbiolo, whose delicate perfume can be suppressed by assertive new oak.

The aromatic and flavor profile typical of fine Barbaresco includes cherry, violet, and rose notes in youth, evolving with age toward iron, tar, leather, and orange peel—the classic tertiary characteristics of mature Nebbiolo. The specific expression from individual MGAs like Bricco di Neive reflects the interaction between these varietal signatures and site-specific terroir factors.

## Conclusion

Bricco di Neive represents one thread in the complex tapestry of Neive's viticultural landscape. While it may lack the extensive documentation and fame of the commune's most celebrated sites, its inclusion in the official MGA system reflects genuine geographical and historical identity. As Neive continues to refine its reputation within Barbaresco—building on the foundation established by benchmark sites like Santo Stefano, Gallina, and Albesani—the full potential of its lesser-known MGAs merits continued attention from both producers and consumers seeking to understand the commune's complete expression of Nebbiolo.
