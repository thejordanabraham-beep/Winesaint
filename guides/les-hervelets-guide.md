# Les Hervelets Premier Cru: A Complete Guide to Fixin's Northern Jewel

## Overview & Location

Les Hervelets stands as one of Fixin's most distinguished Premier Cru vineyards, occupying a strategic position in the northernmost reaches of Burgundy's renowned Côte de Nuits. Located within the commune of Fixin, this climat represents the sophisticated conclusion of the Côte de Nuits' northern extremity, where the great slope begins its legendary journey southward through some of the world's most celebrated wine terroirs.

Geographically, Les Hervelets sits approximately 15 kilometers south of Dijon, positioned on the mid-slope of the Côte d'Or's eastern face. The vineyard occupies prime real estate on the hillside, benefiting from the optimal exposure and elevation that characterizes Burgundy's greatest sites. Within Fixin itself, Les Hervelets is situated in the northern section of the commune's Premier Cru vineyards, creating a natural bridge between the village appellations to the north and the more famous Premier and Grand Crus that extend southward through Gevrey-Chambertin.

The climat's position within the broader Côte de Nuits context is particularly significant, as it represents the first serious expression of the geological and climatic conditions that will define the entire slope. Here, the fundamental characteristics of Burgundian terroir (the interplay of limestone, marl, and precise mesoclimate) begin to express themselves with the complexity and nuance that make the Côte de Nuits legendary among wine regions worldwide.

## Size

Les Hervelets encompasses approximately 3.64 hectares (9 acres), making it a modestly-sized Premier Cru typical of Burgundy's fragmented vineyard landscape. This relatively intimate scale reflects the precision of Burgundy's classification system, where individual climats are delineated based on specific terroir characteristics rather than administrative convenience. The compact nature of Les Hervelets ensures homogeneous growing conditions across the vineyard, contributing to the consistency and distinct character that defines this Premier Cru.

The vineyard's modest size also reflects the historical development of Burgundian viticulture, where centuries of inheritance laws, monastic cultivation, and terroir observation have created a mosaic of small, precisely-defined parcels. This fragmentation, while complex from a commercial perspective, has proven instrumental in developing the nuanced understanding of terroir that makes Burgundy unique among wine regions.

## Terroir & Geology

The geological foundation of Les Hervelets represents a classic expression of Côte de Nuits terroir, characterized by the complex limestone formations that define this portion of Burgundy. The vineyard sits atop Middle Jurassic limestone, specifically the Bajocian stage formation that underlies much of the Côte de Nuits' greatest sites. This limestone base provides excellent drainage while maintaining sufficient water retention for vine health during dry periods.

The soil profile of Les Hervelets consists of a thin topsoil layer, typically 30-50 centimeters deep, composed of brown limestone-derived earth mixed with varying proportions of clay and scattered limestone fragments. This shallow soil forces vine roots to penetrate deeply into the underlying bedrock, accessing mineral nutrients and developing the stress conditions that contribute to wine complexity and longevity.

Clay content in Les Hervelets varies across the vineyard but generally remains moderate, providing structure and water retention without creating the heavy, poorly-draining conditions that would compromise wine elegance. The presence of iron-rich deposits, evidenced by occasional reddish coloration in the soil, contributes to the wine's mineral complexity and aging potential.

The vineyard faces predominantly east-southeast, capturing morning sunlight while avoiding the potentially excessive heat of late afternoon sun. This orientation proves particularly beneficial in Burgundy's continental climate, ensuring adequate ripening while preserving the acidity and freshness essential to fine Pinot Noir. The slope gradient averages 8-12%, providing natural drainage while preventing erosion issues that might affect vine health or soil consistency.

Elevation ranges from approximately 280 to 320 meters above sea level, positioning Les Hervelets in the optimal mid-slope zone where drainage, sun exposure, and protection from harsh winds combine most favorably. This elevation places the vineyard above the frost-prone valley floor while remaining below the exposed upper slopes where excessive wind and temperature fluctuations might compromise grape quality.

## Climate & Microclimate

Les Hervelets benefits from a continental climate moderated by its position on the Côte d'Or slope and its specific microclimate characteristics. The vineyard experiences warm, dry summers and cold winters, with spring frost representing the primary climatic risk. The east-southeast exposition ensures optimal morning sun exposure while the slope's elevation provides natural air drainage that reduces frost susceptibility compared to valley floor locations.

The microclimate within Les Hervelets is influenced by several factors that distinguish it from surrounding areas. The vineyard's position creates a natural amphitheater effect, concentrating warmth during the growing season while facilitating cool air drainage during harvest time. This diurnal temperature variation proves crucial for maintaining acidity and developing complex aromatic compounds in the grapes.

Rainfall patterns in Les Hervelets mirror those of the broader Côte de Nuits, with annual precipitation averaging 700-800 millimeters. The vineyard's well-draining soils and limestone subsoil manage water availability effectively, rarely experiencing the water stress that might compromise grape development or the excessive moisture that could promote disease pressure.

Wind patterns play a significant role in the vineyard's microclimate, with prevailing winds from the west and northwest providing air circulation that reduces humidity and disease pressure. The slope's configuration protects Les Hervelets from the most severe weather while maintaining sufficient air movement for vine health.

Compared to surrounding sites, Les Hervelets experiences slightly cooler conditions than south-facing vineyards, contributing to wines with enhanced freshness and aromatic complexity. The site ripens approximately 3-5 days later than the warmest expositions in Fixin, allowing for extended hang time that develops flavor complexity while preserving essential acidity.

## Viticulture

Pinot Noir dominates the plantings in Les Hervelets, as is typical for red wine Premier Crus in the Côte de Nuits. The vineyard is planted exclusively to this noble variety, with various clonal selections represented across different parcels. Traditional Burgundian clones, including selections massales chosen for their adaptation to local conditions, predominate, though some producers have incorporated newer clones selected for specific quality characteristics.

Vine age in Les Hervelets varies considerably depending on individual producer replanting schedules, but many parcels contain vines between 30-50 years old, with some sections approaching 60+ years. These mature vines produce smaller crops of concentrated fruit, contributing to the wine's complexity and aging potential. Younger plantings, typically 10-25 years old, provide additional fruit volume while gradually developing the deep root systems that will eventually contribute to greater wine complexity.

Planting density follows traditional Burgundian practices, typically ranging from 9,000 to 12,000 vines per hectare. This high density encourages competition among vines, limiting individual vine vigor while maximizing fruit quality. The precise density varies according to soil conditions and individual producer philosophy, with some favoring higher densities for increased concentration.

Viticultural practices in Les Hervelets reflect both traditional Burgundian methods and modern quality-focused techniques. Most producers employ sustainable or organic farming practices, recognizing the importance of soil health and biodiversity for long-term vineyard sustainability. Cover crops are increasingly common, particularly in the inter-rows during winter months, contributing to soil structure and reducing erosion on the sloped terrain.

Pruning follows the Guyot system predominantly, with some parcels utilizing Cordon de Royat where appropriate for the specific site conditions and vine age. Canopy management proves crucial in Les Hervelets, with careful leaf removal and shoot positioning necessary to optimize sun exposure while preventing overexposure that might compromise fruit quality.

Harvest timing requires careful consideration in Les Hervelets, as the vineyard's cooler microclimate demands patience to achieve full ripeness while maintaining the freshness and acidity that characterize fine Burgundy. Most producers harvest in late September or early October, depending on vintage conditions, with hand-harvesting universal among quality-focused estates.

Site-specific challenges in Les Hervelets include managing the limestone-rich soils' tendency toward chlorosis in certain vine selections, requiring careful rootstock choice and occasional soil amendments. The vineyard's elevation and exposition can also create uneven ripening in challenging vintages, necessitating selective harvesting or careful vineyard management to ensure optimal fruit quality.

## Wine Character & Style

Wines from Les Hervelets exhibit a distinctive character that reflects both the site's terroir and its position within the Fixin commune. These Pinot Noirs typically display medium to medium-full body with elegant structure, combining power and finesse in a manner characteristic of fine Côte de Nuits Premier Crus.

Aromatically, Les Hervelets produces wines of considerable complexity, with primary fruit characteristics centered on red berry fruits (particularly cherry, raspberry, and strawberry) complemented by subtle dark berry notes in riper vintages. Floral elements, especially violet and rose, frequently appear in young wines, while earthy undertones including forest floor, mushroom, and wet limestone emerge with aging.

The wine's aromatic profile often includes distinctive mineral notes that reflect the limestone-rich terroir, manifesting as chalk, wet stone, and subtle metallic elements that add complexity and terroir expression. Spice characteristics, including white pepper, cinnamon, and subtle herbs, contribute additional layers of complexity, particularly in wines from older vines.

Structurally, Les Hervelets wines demonstrate excellent balance between fruit, acidity, and tannin. The acidity tends toward the higher end of the spectrum for Fixin, contributing to the wine's freshness and aging potential. Tannins are typically well-integrated and refined, providing structure without overwhelming the fruit character. This tannic structure develops from both the grape skins and judicious use of oak aging, creating wines capable of long-term cellaring.

The texture of Les Hervelets wines often exhibits a characteristic silkiness combined with underlying minerality, creating a mouthfeel that is both approachable in youth and capable of developing greater complexity with age. The wine's mid-palate frequently shows excellent concentration without heaviness, maintaining the elegance that distinguishes fine Burgundy.

Color characteristics typically range from medium ruby in lighter vintages to deeper ruby-garnet in more concentrated years. The wine's color evolution follows classic Burgundian patterns, developing brick and orange hues with extended aging while maintaining good depth and vibrancy.

What particularly distinguishes Les Hervelets from other Fixin Premier Crus is its combination of elegance and minerality, with a distinctive limestone-driven character that sets it apart from the more powerful, earth-driven styles found in some neighboring sites. The wine maintains excellent freshness while developing complexity, creating a style that bridges the gap between immediate approachability and long-term aging potential.

## Comparison to Surrounding Crus

Within the Fixin commune, Les Hervelets occupies a distinctive position among the Premier Cru vineyards, each of which expresses different aspects of the area's terroir. Compared to Clos du Chapitre, the commune's largest Premier Cru, Les Hervelets tends to produce wines with greater elegance and mineral precision, while Clos du Chapitre often shows more power and earthiness. This difference reflects the varying soil compositions and expositions of the two sites.

Les Arvelets, another significant Fixin Premier Cru, typically produces wines with more immediate fruit expression and slightly less mineral complexity than Les Hervelets. The soil composition and microclimate differences between these neighboring sites result in Les Arvelets wines that are often more approachable in youth, while Les Hervelets requires additional time to fully express its potential.

Clos Napoléon, the most famous of Fixin's Premier Crus due to its historical associations, produces wines that are generally more structured and tannic than Les Hervelets, with greater aging requirements. Les Hervelets offers a more balanced approach to power and elegance, making it more versatile across different vintage conditions.

When compared to Premier Crus in neighboring Gevrey-Chambertin, Les Hervelets wines typically show less power and concentration but greater elegance and mineral precision. The wines lack the robust earthiness of sites like Gevrey-Chambertin Cazetiers or Combe aux Moines, instead offering a more refined, limestone-driven style that emphasizes finesse over power.

In the broader context of northern Côte de Nuits Premier Crus, Les Hervelets represents an excellent introduction to the style and quality level of this prestigious region, offering complexity and aging potential at a more accessible price point than many more famous sites to the south.

Several distinguished producers craft wines from Les Hervelets, each bringing their individual approach to this terroir. Domaine Bart, a family estate based in Marsannay, holds significant holdings in Les Hervelets and produces wines that emphasize the site's mineral character while maintaining excellent fruit expression. Their approach combines traditional winemaking with modern precision, resulting in wines that faithfully express the terroir.

Vincent et Denis Berthaut, another respected Fixin producer, vinifies Les Hervelets with a focus on preserving the vineyard's natural elegance and complexity. Their parcels, located in the central portion of the climat, benefit from optimal exposition and mature vines that contribute depth and concentration to the final wine.

Domaine Pierre Gelin, one of Fixin's most established estates, has maintained holdings in Les Hervelets for multiple generations. Their long experience with the vineyard allows for nuanced management practices that maximize the site's potential while respecting its natural characteristics. Gelin's Les Hervelets typically displays excellent structure and aging potential.

Several négociant houses also source fruit from Les Hervelets, though specific parcel information varies year to year based on availability and relationships with local growers. These bottlings often provide broader distribution for the climat while maintaining quality standards appropriate to the Premier Cru designation.

The stylistic variations among producers reflect different approaches to viticulture and winemaking rather than significant terroir differences within the vineyard. Some producers favor longer macerations to extract more tannin and color, while others prefer gentler extractions that emphasize the site's natural elegance. Oak usage also varies, with some employing higher percentages of new barrels while others favor older wood to preserve fruit character.

## Historical Background & Classification

Les Hervelets shares the ancient viticultural history common to the Côte d'Or, with evidence of vine cultivation dating back to Roman times. The specific name "Hervelets" likely derives from local geographical or ownership references that have been preserved through centuries of continuous cultivation.

The climat gained formal recognition as a Premier Cru with the establishment of Burgundy's modern appellation system in the 1930s. This classification recognized the site's demonstrated ability to produce wines of superior quality compared to village-level vineyards, based on centuries of empirical observation by local vignerons and wine merchants.

Historical records from the 18th and 19th centuries reference Les Hervelets among Fixin's finest vineyards, though detailed documentation remains limited compared to more famous sites in Gevrey-Chambertin and further south. The vineyard's reputation was built gradually through consistent quality rather than dramatic recognition, reflecting the steady, reliable character of the wines it produces.

The climat survived the phylloxera crisis of the late 19th century through replanting on American rootstock, like all Burgundian vineyards of the period. This replanting provided an opportunity to optimize vine selection and spacing, contributing to the modern vineyard's quality potential.

Throughout the 20th century, Les Hervelets maintained its reputation as a reliable source of quality Premier Cru Burgundy, though it remained somewhat overshadowed by more famous appellations. The late 20th and early 21st centuries have seen renewed attention to the site as wine enthusiasts have sought high-quality alternatives to increasingly expensive Grand Cru and Premier Cru sites from more famous communes.

## Aging Potential & Quality Level

Les Hervelets demonstrates excellent aging potential, typically requiring 5-8 years from vintage to reach initial maturity while continuing to develop for 15-20 years in good vintages. The wine's structure, particularly its acidity and tannin balance, provides the foundation necessary for long-term cellaring, while the fruit concentration ensures sufficient material to sustain extended aging.

In youth, Les Hervelets wines often display primary fruit characteristics with subtle earthy and mineral undertones. The first phase of development, occurring between 3-7 years post-vintage, sees the integration of oak and tannin with emerging secondary characteristics including leather, game, and forest floor notes. The wine's mineral core becomes more prominent during this period, providing structure and complexity.

The mature phase, typically beginning around 8-10 years post-vintage, reveals the full complexity of the terroir. Primary fruit evolves into more complex cherry, plum, and dried berry characteristics, while earthy notes become more pronounced. The wine's texture develops silkiness and depth, with tannins fully integrated and acidity providing freshness and structure.

Quality consistency in Les Hervelets is generally high, though vintage variation affects both immediate appeal and aging potential. Warmer vintages tend to produce wines with greater concentration and longer aging potential, while cooler years may emphasize elegance and earlier accessibility. The vineyard's reliable ripening characteristics help maintain quality across different vintage conditions.

The aging trajectory of Les Hervelets wines follows classic Burgundian patterns, with an initial period of fruit-forward appeal, followed by a somewhat closed phase during which tannin and acid integration occurs, and finally the emergence of full maturity with complex secondary and tertiary characteristics. Properly stored examples from good vintages can continue developing for decades, though peak drinking typically occurs between 10-18 years post-vintage.

From a quality perspective, Les Hervelets consistently produces wines worthy of its Premier Cru status, offering complexity, terroir expression, and aging potential that clearly distinguish them from village-level Fixin. While perhaps not reaching the heights of the greatest Grand Cru sites, Les Hervelets represents an excellent example of high-quality Burgundian terroir at a Premier Cru level, providing both immediate pleasure and long-term collecting potential for serious Burgundy enthusiasts.
