# Blanchot: A Comprehensive Guide to Chablis's Grand Cru of Elegance

## Overview & Classification

### Location within Chablis

Blanchot occupies a privileged position at the western extremity of Chablis's renowned Grand Cru slope, positioned on the right bank of the Serein River. This climat sits adjacent to Les Clos to the east and extends toward the village of Fyé. The Grand Cru vineyards of Chablis form a continuous, amphitheater-like hillside that faces predominantly southwest, and Blanchot marks the northwestern terminus of this extraordinary viticultural belt.

The climat is situated approximately 2 kilometers from the town center of Chablis, accessible via the winding roads that climb the slope above the river valley. This geographical positioning places Blanchot within one of the most prestigious white wine appellations in the world, where every nuance of terroir translates directly into the glass.

### Grand Cru Designation

Blanchot holds the distinction of being one of only seven officially recognized Grand Cru climats in Chablis, representing the pinnacle of the appellation's four-tier classification system (Petit Chablis, Chablis, Premier Cru, and Grand Cru). The Grand Cru designation was formalized in 1938 when the Institut National des Appellations d'Origine (INAO) established the boundaries and regulations governing these exceptional vineyard sites.

The Grand Cru climats of Chablis—Blanchot, Les Clos, Valmur, Grenouilles, Vaudésir, Les Preuses, and Bougros—collectively represent approximately 100 hectares out of Chablis's total 5,800 hectares of vineyard land. This exclusivity, representing less than 2% of total production, underscores the exceptional nature of Blanchot's terroir and the wines it produces.

### Size and Boundaries

Blanchot encompasses approximately 12.7 hectares, making it one of the medium-sized Grand Cru climats. While smaller than Les Clos (26 hectares) or Vaudésir (15.4 hectares), its compact size contributes to its identity and consistency of expression. The climat's boundaries are precisely delimited, running from its junction with Les Clos on the eastern side to the outer limits of the Grand Cru appellation to the west.

The vineyard's borders follow the natural contours of the hillside, with the upper boundary near the plateau edge and the lower boundary where the slope begins to flatten toward the valley floor. These carefully drawn boundaries reflect centuries of empirical observation about where the finest fruit consistently ripens and where the terroir characteristics are most pronounced.

### Historical Significance

The name "Blanchot" likely derives from the pale, almost white appearance of its Kimmeridgian limestone soils when viewed from across the valley, though some historians suggest it may reference an early proprietor or the blanc (white) grapes cultivated there. Documentary evidence of viticulture on this slope dates back to the 12th century, when Cistercian monks from the nearby Abbey of Pontigny recognized the exceptional potential of these hillsides.

Throughout the medieval and modern periods, Blanchot developed a reputation for producing wines of exceptional finesse and elegance. Historical records from the 18th and 19th centuries show that wines from this climat commanded premium prices and were sought after by négociants in Paris. The climat survived the phylloxera crisis of the late 19th century and the subsequent replanting on American rootstock, emerging into the 20th century with its reputation intact.

## Terroir & Geology

### Specific Soil Types

Blanchot's geological foundation is built upon the famous Kimmeridgian marl, a Upper Jurassic sedimentary rock formation approximately 150 million years old. This soil type is the defining characteristic of Chablis Grand Cru terroir and contains a remarkable composition: a gray-blue limestone matrix embedded with countless fossilized oyster shells (*Exogyra virgula*) and other marine organisms from the ancient Jurassic sea that once covered this region.

The Kimmeridgian marl in Blanchot is particularly rich in limestone content, with a clay-limestone balance that tends slightly more toward limestone than some neighboring climats. This composition provides excellent drainage while retaining sufficient moisture during dry periods—a crucial balance for Chardonnay viticulture. The soil depth varies across the climat, typically ranging from 30 to 50 centimeters before reaching the mother rock, though some areas feature deeper soil pockets.

The visible presence of fossil shells in the soil has become iconic, though their direct contribution to wine minerality remains debated among scientists. What is indisputable is that this geological makeup creates a unique growing environment that imparts distinctive characteristics to the wine.

### Vineyard Exposition and Slope

Blanchot enjoys a southwest-facing exposition, slightly more westerly than some of its neighboring Grand Crus. This orientation provides several viticultural advantages: extended afternoon sun exposure, protection from cold northeastern winds, and optimal heat accumulation during the growing season. The southwest aspect means the vines receive warming sunlight from late morning through evening, crucial for ripening in this northern climate.

The slope gradient in Blanchot averages between 15-25%, creating ideal conditions for drainage and sun exposure. The incline is sufficient to prevent water stagnation and frost accumulation while not being so steep as to cause excessive erosion or create viticultural challenges. The middle section of the climat features the steepest gradient, while the upper and lower portions are slightly more moderate.

This slope orientation creates a natural amphitheater effect that concentrates warmth and light, effectively creating a microclimate several degrees warmer than the valley floor below. During the critical ripening period in September and early October, this additional warmth can be decisive in achieving optimal phenolic maturity.

### Altitude and Drainage

Blanchot's vineyards span an altitude range from approximately 150 to 200 meters above sea level. The upper parcels near the plateau edge sit at the higher elevations, while the lower sections approach the Grand Cru's minimum altitude threshold. This vertical variation creates subtle differences in exposition, drainage, and ripening patterns across the climat.

The elevation provides natural protection from valley fogs and improves air circulation, reducing disease pressure and frost risk compared to lower-lying areas. The altitude also contributes to the wine's characteristic freshness and acidity, as cooler nighttime temperatures at higher elevations help preserve aromatic compounds and maintain natural acidity levels in the grapes.

Drainage is exceptional throughout Blanchot due to the combination of slope, limestone-rich soils, and the presence of fissures and cracks in the underlying bedrock. Excess water percolates rapidly through the soil profile, preventing waterlogging while allowing vine roots to penetrate deeply in search of moisture and nutrients. This deep rooting is essential for the wine's mineral complexity and drought resistance.

### Unique Terroir Characteristics

What distinguishes Blanchot from its Grand Cru neighbors is a combination of subtle factors that collectively create its unique personality. The climat's position at the western end of the slope means it receives slightly later morning sun than Les Clos or Valmur, but benefits from extended afternoon light. This pattern influences the diurnal temperature variation and ripening rhythms.

The soil composition in Blanchot tends toward finer-textured limestone with slightly higher clay content in some parcels compared to the more rocky, fractured limestone found in Les Clos. This creates wines with a particular textural refinement and elegant structure rather than the more powerful, muscular profile associated with some Grand Crus.

The mesoclimate within Blanchot is influenced by its proximity to the plateau edge, where air currents create movement that moderates temperature extremes. This circulation helps prevent both frost damage in spring and excessive heat stress in summer, contributing to the climat's reliability and consistency across vintages.

## Climate & Viticulture

### Microclimate within Chablis

Chablis occupies one of the northernmost quality wine regions in France, positioned at approximately 47.8°N latitude. The climate is continental with significant maritime influence, creating cool growing conditions that place the region at the very edge of reliable Chardonnay viticulture. However, Blanchot's elevated position on the Grand Cru slope creates a favorable microclimate that distinguishes it from the broader regional pattern.

The southwest-facing slope acts as a heat trap, capturing and retaining solar radiation throughout the day. Temperatures within Blanchot can be 2-3°C warmer than the valley floor during sunny days, a critical advantage for achieving physiological ripeness in cooler vintages. The slope's angle also promotes air drainage, with cold air flowing downhill away from the vines during clear nights, reducing frost risk compared to flat or depressed areas.

Annual rainfall in Chablis averages 700-750mm, distributed relatively evenly throughout the year though with slightly higher precipitation in spring and autumn. The well-drained limestone soils of Blanchot mean that even in wet years, the vines rarely suffer from excessive water stress, while the deep-rooting capability allows survival during drier periods.

### Frost Risk and Protection Methods

Spring frost represents the most significant climatic threat to Chablis viticulture, and Blanchot is no exception. The region experiences an average of 5-10 frost events per spring, with devastating episodes occurring roughly once every five years. The critical period spans from bud break in early April through late May, when young shoots are vulnerable to sub-zero temperatures.

Producers with holdings in Blanchot employ various frost protection strategies. The most traditional method involves burning bales (chaufferettes)—small oil or paraffin burners placed between vine rows—which create a warming blanket of air when temperatures drop dangerously. On clear, calm nights when radiation frost threatens, the sight of thousands of burning pots across the Grand Cru slope has become an iconic, if expensive, part of Chablis viticulture.

Wind machines (éoliennes) have been installed by some producers, particularly those with larger holdings. These giant fans mix warmer air from above the inversion layer with the cold air settling at vine level, raising temperatures by 1-2°C—often sufficient to prevent damage. Sprinkler systems (aspersion) represent another approach, where water is sprayed onto vines; as it freezes, it releases latent heat that protects the buds within a protective ice coating.

Modern weather forecasting and precision temperature monitoring allow producers to deploy protection measures strategically, though the costs remain substantial. The investment required for frost protection in Grand Cru vineyards is justified by the wine's value and the irreplaceable nature of these terroirs.

### Chardonnay Expressions

Chardonnay is the sole permitted variety in Blanchot, as in all of Chablis. The clones and selections planted in the Grand Cru have been refined over decades, with producers favoring clones that balance yield, disease resistance, and quality. Common selections include older Chardonnay Blanc massale selections alongside newer clones such as 75, 76, 95, and 96, which offer different ripening characteristics and aromatic profiles.

In Blanchot's terroir, Chardonnay expresses itself with particular finesse and aromatic purity. The combination of limestone-rich soils, cool climate, and optimal sun exposure produces grapes with moderate sugar levels (typically 12.5-13.5% potential alcohol), naturally high acidity (often 6-8 g/L total acidity), and complex aromatic precursors. The phenolic maturity achieved in Blanchot allows for extended hang time without excessive sugar accumulation, creating wines of balance and elegance.

The vine age across Blanchot varies considerably, with some parcels planted in the 1950s-1960s and others replanted more recently. Older vines with deeply established root systems tend to produce lower yields of more concentrated, complex fruit, while younger vines contribute freshness and vitality.

### Viticultural Practices

Viticulture in Blanchot adheres to strict Grand Cru standards, with maximum yields limited to 54 hectoliters per hectare (though most quality-focused producers target 40-45 hl/ha or less). Vine density typically ranges from 5,000 to 6,500 vines per hectare, trained using either Guyot simple or Guyot double systems that facilitate hand work and optimize sun exposure.

Canopy management is crucial in Chablis's cool climate, with careful leaf thinning around clusters to improve air circulation and sun exposure while avoiding excessive deleafing that might lead to sunburn or loss of photosynthetic capacity. The timing of these operations requires expertise, balancing disease prevention against the need for adequate shade and temperature moderation.

Sustainable and organic viticulture has gained increasing adoption in Blanchot, with several leading producers farming organically or biodynamically. The limestone soils naturally resist compaction and maintain good soil biology, while the slope's excellent drainage reduces disease pressure. Cover crops, composting, and reduced copper usage represent common practices among quality-conscious domaines.

Harvest timing in Blanchot typically occurs in mid-to-late September, though vintage variation can shift this by two weeks either direction. Producers seek the optimal balance between sugar ripeness, acidity retention, and aromatic maturity—a narrower window than in warmer regions. Hand harvesting remains standard for Grand Cru fruit, allowing selective picking and gentle handling.

## Wine Character & Style

### Typical Flavor Profiles and Aromas

Blanchot produces wines of extraordinary refinement, characterized by a complex aromatic profile that evolves gracefully with age. In youth, the wines display intense primary aromas: white flowers (acacia, hawthorn, white lilac), citrus fruits (lemon zest, lime, grapefruit), green apple, and white peach. The mineral signature is pronounced, often described as wet stone, flint, oyster shell, or sea spray—a saline, iodine-tinged character that speaks directly to the terroir.

As the wines develop, secondary and tertiary aromas emerge: honey, beeswax, toasted almond, hazelnut, dried herbs, and sometimes white truffle or mushroom. The mineral backbone remains constant, providing a through-line that connects youth to maturity. Many tasters describe Blanchot wines as having a particular purity and precision, with aromatics that are focused rather than diffuse, crystalline rather than broad.

On the palate, Blanchot reveals its true character: a wine of texture and nuance rather than power. The attack is typically energetic and fresh, with vibrant acidity that frames and lifts the fruit. The mid-palate shows impressive density without heaviness, a silky, almost creamy texture that coats the mouth. The limestone terroir contributes a chalky, fine-grained tannin-like structure that provides grip and definition.

The finish is long and saline, often leaving a mouthwatering minerality that calls for another sip. The balance between fruit intensity, acidity, and mineral character defines Blanchot's style—neither too austere nor too generous, finding an elegant middle path.

### Comparison to Other Chablis Grand Crus

Within the constellation of Chablis Grand Crus, Blanchot occupies a distinctive position. Compared to Les Clos—often considered the most powerful and structured Grand Cru—Blanchot offers greater immediate approachability and finesse. Where Les Clos can be almost impenetrable in youth, requiring years to reveal its complexity, Blanchot shows more aromatics and texture from the outset while still possessing serious aging potential.

Relative to Valmur, which lies adjacent to the east, Blanchot tends toward greater elegance and less overt richness. Valmur often displays more tropical fruit notes and broader texture, while Blanchot maintains a tighter, more linear profile. The difference reflects subtle variations in soil depth and composition, as well as slightly different sun exposure patterns.

Compared to Vaudésir, known for its generosity and accessibility, Blanchot shows more restraint and mineral definition. Vaudésir typically offers more immediate pleasure with rounder fruit and softer acidity, while Blanchot requires more contemplation to fully appreciate its complexities.

Les Preuses, another neighbor, shares some of Blanchot's finesse but often shows more floral aromatics and lighter body. Blanchot generally demonstrates greater depth and structure while maintaining elegance, making it perhaps the perfect synthesis of power and refinement within the Grand Cru portfolio.

### Minerality, Acidity, Body Characteristics

The concept of minerality in wine remains controversial among scientists, but in Blanchot, the sensory impression is undeniable. The wines possess a stony, saline quality that many tasters perceive as a direct expression of the limestone-rich soils. Whether this stems from specific mineral uptake, yeast metabolism of sulfur compounds, or other factors, the consistent presence of this characteristic across producers suggests a terroir-driven phenomenon.

Acidity in Blanchot is typically high by global standards but beautifully integrated within the wine's structure. Total acidity often measures 6-8 g/L, with pH values around 3.0-3.2. This natural acidity preserves freshness, provides aging potential, and creates the mouthwatering quality that makes Chablis the perfect wine for oysters and seafood. The acidity is rarely aggressive or sharp; instead, it presents as energetic and vivacious, contributing to the wine's overall harmony.

Body and texture represent perhaps Blanchot's most distinctive qualities. These are not lightweight wines despite their elegance; they possess surprising density and presence on the palate. The body is typically medium to medium-full, with alcohol levels around 12.5-13.5%. The texture is particularly noteworthy—a combination of silkiness from lees contact and fine-grained structure from the limestone terroir creates a tactile experience that distinguishes great Chablis from mere good examples.

### How Terroir Shapes the Wine

Every element of Blanchot's terroir contributes to the wine's final character. The Kimmeridgian limestone soils provide excellent drainage, encouraging deep rooting and moderate vine vigor, which concentrates flavors and creates the mineral signature. The clay component retains sufficient water to prevent excessive stress while providing nutrients that support steady, even ripening.

The southwest exposition ensures adequate warmth for full phenolic ripeness while the northern latitude preserves acidity and aromatic freshness. The slope angle promotes air movement and drainage, reducing disease pressure and frost risk. The altitude moderates temperature extremes, creating a longer, more gradual ripening period that develops complexity without losing elegance.

The mesoclimate created by the amphitheater-like Grand Cru slope concentrates warmth and light, creating conditions several degrees warmer than the surrounding region—enough to reliably ripen Chardonnay to Grand Cru quality standards while maintaining the tension and energy that defines great Chablis. In Blanchot, all these factors align to produce wines that are unmistakably of their place, impossible to replicate elsewhere.

## Aging & Evolution

### Aging Potential

Blanchot produces wines with exceptional longevity, regularly aging gracefully for 15-25 years and sometimes much longer in great vintages from top producers. This aging potential stems from the combination of high natural acidity, concentrated flavors, mineral structure, and often careful oak integration that provides antioxidant protection.

The wines typically enter a closed phase 3-5 years after vintage, where the exuberant primary fruit becomes muted and the wine shows more austerity. This dumb phase can last several years, reopening around 8-10 years of age when secondary and tertiary characteristics begin to dominate. Patience is rewarded with complexity that cannot be achieved in youth.

Proper cellaring conditions are essential for realizing Blanchot's aging potential: consistent cool temperatures (10-14°C), high humidity (70-80%), darkness, and minimal vibration. Wines should be stored on their side to keep corks moist. Given the investment in Grand Cru Chablis, proper storage represents crucial protection of value and quality.

### Evolution in Bottle

The evolution of Blanchot in bottle follows a predictable but fascinating trajectory. In the first 1-3 years, the wines display vibrant primary fruit, intense florality, and pronounced minerality. The acidity is prominent, the texture still developing, and oak influences (if present) still integrating. These young wines offer intense pleasure but only hint at future complexity.

Between 3-8 years, the wines typically become more reserved, with primary fruit receding and secondary characteristics not yet fully developed. The mineral backbone becomes more apparent, and the texture gains weight and complexity. This intermediate stage requires discernment—the wines are not at their peak but show fascinating evolution.

From 8-15 years, Blanchot reaches its first plateau of maturity. The aromatics become more complex, showing honey, nut, dried fruit, and mushroom alongside retained citrus notes. The texture is silky and fully integrated, the acidity remains vibrant but softer, and the mineral character provides structure and definition. This is when many enthusiasts prefer to drink their wines.

Beyond 15 years, truly great examples continue to develop, gaining tertiary complexity: truffle, petrol notes (in some vintages), oxidative hints of bruised apple and caramel, and profound mineral depth. The wines become more meditative, less about fruit exuberance and more about nuance and complexity.

### Optimal Drinking Windows

The optimal drinking window for Blanchot depends on vintage character, producer style, and personal preference. As a general guideline:

- **Years 1-3**: Enjoyable but not showing full potential; best for those who prefer vibrant primary fruit and don't mind prominent acidity.
- **Years 3-8**: Often the dumb phase; wines can seem closed and austere, though still pleasurable.
- **Years 8-15**: The first optimal window; wines show beautiful balance of fruit, secondary character, and minerality.
- **Years 15-25**: The second optimal window for top vintages; profound complexity and tertiary development.
- **Years 25+**: Exceptional bottles from great vintages and producers; museum pieces showing full tertiary evolution.

Conservative drinkers might aim for the 8-15 year window, while adventurous collectors can explore beyond 15 years. Some prefer the energy of younger wines (3-8 years), accepting that complexity is still developing in exchange for vibrant fruit and acidity.

### Vintage Variations

Vintage variation in Chablis is significant due to the marginal climate. Warm, sunny vintages (like 2015, 2018, 2019, 2020) produce riper, more generous wines with lower acidity and more immediate appeal, typically drinking well from 5-15 years. Cooler vintages (2013, 2014, 2016) yield more classic, austere wines with higher acidity and greater aging potential, often requiring 8-10 years before showing their best.

Frost years can reduce yields dramatically but may produce concentrated wines from the surviving fruit. Hail damage, though less frequent, can devastate production in affected areas. These vintage variables make knowledge of specific years essential for collectors and consumers.

Some benchmark vintages for Blanchot include: 2002, 2005, 2008, 2010, 2012, 2014, 2017, and 2018—each offering different expressions of the terroir based on seasonal conditions. Consulting vintage charts and producer notes helps navigate this complexity.

## Notable Producers

### Leading Domaines with Holdings in Blanchot

Several prestigious domaines own parcels in Blanchot, each bringing different philosophical approaches and winemaking styles:

**Domaine Laroche** (now owned by Advini) holds significant parcels in Blanchot and produces benchmark bottlings. Their "Les Blanchots" (note the plural form sometimes used) is vinified with a combination of stainless steel and oak, emphasizing purity and elegance while adding subtle complexity.

**Domaine Louis Michel & Fils** represents the purist school of Chablis winemaking, fermenting and aging their Blanchot entirely in stainless steel without any oak influence. Their wines showcase the terroir in its most transparent form, offering crystalline minerality and precise fruit expression.

**Domaine William Fèvre**, one of the largest Grand Cru proprietors in Chablis, produces Blanchot with partial oak aging (typically 30-50% new oak), creating wines that balance terroir expression with subtle toasty complexity. Their approach emphasizes texture and longevity.

**La Chablisienne**, the respected cooperative, offers excellent value in Grand Cru Chablis with its "Château Grenouilles" bottling, which sometimes includes Blanchot fruit. Their wines demonstrate that cooperative production can achieve excellence when fruit quality and winemaking are prioritized.

**Domaine Christian Moreau Père & Fils** produces exceptional Blanchot from old vines, aged in a mix of steel and large old oak. Their wines balance tradition and modernity, showing profound mineral character with excellent aging potential.

**Domaine Billaud-Simon** crafts elegant, terroir-focused Blanchot with minimal oak intervention, emphasizing finesse and precision. Their wines are consistently among the most pure expressions of the climat.

### Benchmark Bottlings

When exploring Blanchot, several bottlings stand out as reference points:

- **Louis Michel Blanchot**: The definitive non-oak expression, showing pure terroir character
- **William Fèvre Blanchot**: Balanced oak integration demonstrating the role of élevage
- **Christian Moreau Blanchot**: Old-vine intensity with classical winemaking
- **Domaine Laroche Blanchots Réserve de l'Obédience**: Premium cuvée showing ultimate potential

These bottlings offer different perspectives on the same terroir, illustrating how winemaking choices interact with site characteristics. Comparative tastings of these producers reveal Blanchot's common threads—minerality, elegance, structure—while showcasing different stylistic interpretations.

### Winemaking Approaches

Winemaking approaches in Blanchot span a spectrum from purist to interventionist, though all serious producers share common quality standards. Harvest decisions prioritize optimal ripeness balanced with acidity retention, typically occurring when sugar levels reach 12.5-13.5% potential alcohol.

Pressing is gentle, usually with pneumatic presses, and juice settling occurs overnight with or without cold stabilization. Fermentation may occur in stainless steel tanks, concrete vessels, or oak barrels (ranging from 228L to 600L), depending on producer philosophy. Temperature-controlled fermentation (16-20°C) preserves aromatics, though some producers allow slightly warmer ferments for texture development.

Malolactic fermentation is typically completed, softening the naturally high malic acidity and adding textural complexity. Extended lees aging (élevage sur lie) for 10-18 months is standard, with regular bâtonnage (lees stirring) employed by some producers to build texture and complexity.

Oak usage varies dramatically: from zero (Louis Michel) to 30-50% new oak (William Fèvre), with most producers falling somewhere in between. The trend in recent years has moved toward larger format oak (400-600L) and lower new oak percentages, allowing terroir to speak more clearly while still benefiting from micro-oxygenation and textural enhancement.

Bottling typically occurs 15-20 months after harvest, often without fining or filtration for top cuvées, though light filtration remains common. The goal across all approaches is preserving the essential character of Blanchot—its elegance, minerality, and aging potential—while imprinting an individual stamp.

## Food Pairing & Service

### Classic Pairings

Blanchot's elegance, acidity, and mineral character make it extraordinarily versatile with food, though certain pairings have achieved classic status:

**Oysters**: The iconic pairing—Blanchot's saline minerality and high acidity perfectly complement raw oysters, especially those from Normandy or Brittany. The wine's subtle citrus notes enhance the bivalves' sweetness while its minerality mirrors their oceanic character.

**Lobster and Crab**: Rich crustaceans find an ideal partner in Blanchot, whose acidity cuts through butter-based preparations while its texture matches the meat's density. Lobster thermidor, crab bisque, or simple grilled preparations all excel.

**Scallops**: Pan-seared scallops with brown butter, ceviche, or carpaccio pair beautifully, as the wine's minerality complements the scallops' sweetness and the acidity balances any richness.

**White Fish**: Turbot, sole, halibut, or sea bass prepared simply (grilled, poached, or with light cream sauces) allow Blanchot to shine. The wine elevates rather than overpowers delicate fish flavors.

**Poultry in Cream Sauce**: Classic French preparations like poulet à la crème, chicken with morel mushrooms, or guinea fowl find harmony with Blanchot's texture and acidity, particularly with wines showing some oak influence.

**Comté and aged cheeses**: Mature Comté, Gruyère, or aged goat cheeses create beautiful pairings with developed Blanchot, the nutty, crystalline cheese flavors mirroring the wine's evolved character.

**Japanese cuisine**: Sushi, sashimi, and tempura work exceptionally well with Blanchot's clean minerality and precise acidity, making it perhaps the best Western wine for high-end Japanese dining.

### Serving Temperature

Proper serving temperature is crucial for experiencing Blanchot's full complexity. The wine should be served at 10-12°C (50-54°F)—cool but not cold. Over-chilling mutes aromatics and makes the wine seem more austere, while serving too warm emphasizes alcohol and reduces freshness.

Remove the bottle from a 6-8°C refrigerator about 20-30 minutes before serving, or place it in an ice bucket for 10-15 minutes. If the wine seems closed upon opening, allowing it to warm gradually in the glass reveals additional layers of complexity.

Younger vintages (under 5 years) can tolerate slightly cooler service (10°C), as the vibrant primary fruit remains expressive even when chilled. Mature wines (over 10 years) benefit from warmer service (12°C) to fully display their developed aromatics and textural complexity.

Large-bowled white wine glasses with a narrower opening work best, concentrating aromatics while allowing the wine to breathe. The shape should direct aromas to the nose while providing enough volume for swirling and aeration.

### Decanting Recommendations

Decanting Blanchot is generally unnecessary for young wines (under 8 years) but can benefit mature bottles in two ways: separating any sediment that may have formed and aerating wines that seem closed or reduced upon opening.

For mature wines (15+ years), careful decanting helps remove crystalline tartrate deposits or other sediment. Stand the bottle upright for 24 hours before opening to allow sediment to settle. Decant slowly using a light source to observe when sediment approaches the bottle neck, stopping before it enters the decanter.

If a wine seems reductive (showing struck match, smoky, or sulfurous notes) upon opening, aggressive decanting with splashing can help dissipate these compounds through oxidation. Pour the wine vigorously into a wide decanter, swirl, and allow 15-30 minutes of air contact before serving.

For wines showing delicate tertiary development, gentler handling is appropriate. Simply opening the bottle an hour before service may provide sufficient aeration without risking over-oxidation. Very old bottles (25+ years) should not be decanted far in advance, as their fragile aromatics can fade quickly with air exposure.

When in doubt, taste upon opening and make decisions based on the wine's presentation. If it's showing beautifully, minimal intervention is best. If it seems tight or showing off-notes, more aggressive aeration may help.

---

Blanchot represents one of Burgundy's most refined expressions of Chardonnay, a climat where the Kimmeridgian terroir speaks with particular elegance and finesse. Understanding this Grand Cru requires patience—both in the cellar as the wines evolve and in the glass as they reveal their complexities. For those willing to invest the time and attention, Blanchot offers rewards that few white wines can match: a perfect balance of power and restraint, fruit and mineral, immediacy and profundity. It stands as testimony to the remarkable diversity within Chablis's compact Grand Cru slope, a unique voice in one of the wine world's most prestigious ensembles.
