# Asili

## Introduction

Asili stands among the most celebrated vineyard sites in Barbaresco, recognized by négociants, producers, and critics alike as one of the appellation's premier *crus*. Located within the township of Barbaresco itself—the heart of this compact DOCG zone northeast of Alba—Asili achieved formal recognition when the *menzione geografica aggiuntiva* (MGA) system was introduced in 2007. This designation allows producers to indicate "Barbaresco Asili" on their labels, signaling wines from this specific registered vineyard site.

The MGA system in Barbaresco, implemented a full three years before Barolo adopted a similar framework, represents a crucial step in codifying the zone's viticultural geography. The list of approved MGAs deliberately retained historically recognized vineyard names rather than absorbing them into more commercially prominent designations. Asili's inclusion among these official MGAs confirmed what decades of higher grape prices and critical acclaim had already established: this is a site capable of producing Barbaresco of exceptional quality and distinctive character.

## Historical Context and Recognition

The concept of single-vineyard Barbaresco is relatively young compared to the region's winemaking history. The first such bottling appeared only in 1961, when Prunotto released its Barbaresco Montestefano. Prior to the late 20th century, there existed no firmly established written record of *cru* designation in Barbaresco comparable to what developed in Barolo. Lorenzo Fantini's influential late 19th-century monograph on Piedmontese viticulture documented very few "choice positions" in Barbaresco and none whatsoever in the neighboring commune of Neive, suggesting that vineyard differentiation came later here than in the more historically prominent Barolo zone.

The first systematic attempts to identify and rate Barbaresco's finest positions emerged in the 1960s through the work of Luigi Veronelli, followed by Renato Ratti's efforts in the 1970s. However, the most reliable indicator of quality proved to be economic rather than critical: négociants' willingness to pay premium prices for grapes from specific sites. This market-driven consensus established a clear hierarchy, with Asili consistently grouped among the elite vineyards of the Barbaresco township alongside Montefico, Montestefano, and Rabajà. Other top sites in the wider DOCG include Albesani and Gallina in Neive, and Pajorè in Treiso.

This commercial recognition distinguishes Asili from another category of famous Barbaresco vineyards—sites such as San Lorenzo, Tildin, and Martinenga—that gained their current prestige primarily through the dedicated work and exacting standards of single producers. Gaja, Bruno Giacosa, and Alberto di Gresy, among others, elevated specific vineyards through decades of meticulous viticulture and winemaking. Asili's reputation, by contrast, appears to have developed more broadly across multiple producers, reflecting inherent qualities of the site itself.

## The Barbaresco Context

Understanding Asili requires situating it within Barbaresco's broader geographical and climatic framework. The DOCG production zone encompasses just 733 hectares as of 2019—roughly one-third the size of Barolo—spread across three main townships: Barbaresco, Treiso (historically part of Barbaresco), and Neive, with a small amount of production in a fraction of Alba itself. This compact zone lies northeast and east of Alba, occupying south-facing slopes that range from 200 to 400 meters in elevation.

These lower altitudes compared to Barolo, combined with the moderating influence of the Tanaro River, create conditions in which Nebbiolo ripens earlier and develops somewhat different characteristics. Barbaresco's wines tend toward greater fruitiness and somewhat less overt perfume than their Barolo counterparts, though they share the same formidable structure of tannin and acidity that defines great Nebbiolo. The minimum alcohol requirement stands at 12.5%, though wines regularly reach 13.5% or higher in warmer vintages.

Crucially, Barbaresco's aging requirements differ from Barolo's—two years from harvest rather than three, with at least nine months in wood. For Riserva bottlings, the requirement extends to four years total. This shorter mandatory aging period has unfortunately led to the misconception that Barbaresco wines are less ageworthy than Barolo, when in fact they possess comparable longevity; the difference lies in style and evolution rather than potential.

## Wine Characteristics

Barbaresco from Asili, like all wines from this appellation, is crafted exclusively from Nebbiolo. The grape's inherent character—marked tannins, bright acidity, and complex aromatic development—provides the foundation, but site-specific factors shape the expression. As one of the most esteemed sites in the township of Barbaresco, Asili produces wines that embody both the appellation's general characteristics and the refinement expected from premier vineyard land.

Classic Barbaresco aromatics include cherry, violets, and floral notes in youth, evolving with age toward more complex tertiary characteristics: iron, tar, orange peel, and the earthy, savory notes that mark mature Nebbiolo. The wines possess a structural intensity that can make young Barbaresco challenging to drink, despite its reputation as the more approachable of Piedmont's two great Nebbiolo appellations. The combination of firm tannins and cutting acidity demands patience, though modern viticulture and gentler extraction methods have produced wines with smoother, riper tannins than in previous eras.

The specific expression from Asili would be influenced by factors common to top Barbaresco sites: well-drained soils that provide steady but moderate water supply to the vines, appropriate sun exposure on south-facing slopes, and the mesoclimate created by elevation and vineyard orientation. The most distinguished vineyard sites share the ability to ripen Nebbiolo fully while maintaining the variety's essential freshness and aromatic complexity—avoiding both the overripeness that can come from excessive heat and the underripeness that produces green, harsh tannins.

## Winemaking and Evolution

Barbaresco's winemaking philosophy has undergone significant evolution over recent decades, shifting from one extreme toward another before finding a middle path. Traditional methods favored prolonged maceration and extended aging in large Slavonian oak casks, sometimes resulting in wines that seemed austere and unyielding in youth. The 1970s and 1980s saw a dramatic shift toward shorter maceration periods and aging in smaller French barriques, responding to international tastes for wines that were rounder and more immediately accessible.

This modernist approach drew criticism from traditionalists who argued that French oak suppressed Nebbiolo's delicate perfume and obscured site-specific character—a particularly important concern for wines from distinguished MGAs like Asili, where terroir expression should theoretically take center stage. Today, an increasing number of Barbaresco producers have returned to longer maceration periods, often extending to 40 days, combined with aging in large oak casks rather than barriques. This synthesis attempts to extract Nebbiolo's full phenolic potential while preserving aromatic purity.

The choice of oak treatment, maceration length, and aging duration remains a matter of producer philosophy rather than prescribed formula. What matters most is that improvements in vineyard management—including the shift toward organic and biodynamic viticulture by numerous smaller producers—have elevated grape quality to the point where excessive intervention becomes unnecessary. Fully ripe, healthy fruit from sites like Asili provides winemakers with exceptional raw material that requires primarily time and restraint rather than technical manipulation.

## Producers and the Cooperative Tradition

While specific producer information for Asili is limited in the available documentation, the site benefits from Barbaresco's strong tradition of quality-focused production. The Produttori del Barbaresco cooperative stands as one of Italy's finest, demonstrating that collective winemaking can achieve world-class results when members maintain rigorous viticultural standards and the organization bottles vineyard-designated wines from its members' best sites. This cooperative model has been instrumental in establishing Barbaresco's reputation and providing a quality benchmark for the appellation.

Individual producers such as Angelo Gaja and Bruno Giacosa transformed Barbaresco's international standing beginning in the 1960s, demonstrating that wines from this zone could rival the greatest expressions of Nebbiolo. Their example inspired subsequent generations of winemakers, including numerous smaller producers who have embraced traditional techniques alongside organic and biodynamic farming to create distinctive, terroir-driven wines with the structure and aromatic complexity that allow extended aging.

## The Significance of Place

Asili's status among Barbaresco's elite MGAs reflects the enduring importance of site specificity in Piedmontese viticulture. In a region where Nebbiolo reigns as the sole variety for premium wine production, differentiation emerges not from blending different grapes but from the infinite variations that specific parcels of land impart to a single variety. The MGA system, by codifying these distinctions, provides both a quality signal to consumers and an incentive for producers to maintain the exacting viticultural standards that such designations demand.

As the Barbaresco appellation continues to gain recognition alongside its more famous neighbor Barolo, sites like Asili serve as reference points for understanding what makes this zone distinctive. The combination of favorable mesoclimate, appropriate soils, and generations of accumulated viticultural knowledge creates conditions in which Nebbiolo can achieve both power and elegance—wines that reward patient cellaring with the complex, haunting aromatics and textural refinement that define great Barbaresco.
