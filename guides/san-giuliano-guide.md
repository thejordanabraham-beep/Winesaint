# San Giuliano

## Introduction

San Giuliano is one of the officially designated MGAs (menzioni geografiche aggiuntive) in the commune of Neive, one of three primary townships (alongside Barbaresco and Treiso) that produce Barbaresco DOCG. Established as part of the 2007 MGA codification that formalized Barbaresco's single-vineyard system, San Giuliano represents part of Neive's significant contribution to the appellation's 733 hectares of production.

Unlike some of Neive's more historically documented sites such as Gallina, Albesani, and Santo Stefano, San Giuliano does not appear prominently in the historical literature that tracked the emergence of single-vineyard Barbaresco. Lorenzo Fantini's influential late 19th-century monograph on Piedmontese viticulture noted very few "choice positions" in Barbaresco and, notably, none whatsoever specifically in Neive. This absence from early viticultural documentation places San Giuliano among the MGAs whose reputation has been built more recently, shaped by the modern era of quality-focused viticulture rather than by centuries of recorded prestige.

## The Neive Context

Understanding San Giuliano requires understanding Neive's particular place within Barbaresco. The commune calls itself "the township of four wines," producing not only Nebbiolo for Barbaresco but also Moscato, Barbera, and Dolcetto. This polyculture reflects a different viticultural history than the village of Barbaresco itself. Nebbiolo only consolidated its position in Neive after the Second World War, expanding dramatically from 140 hectares in 1995 to 265 hectares by the second decade of the 21st century. This relatively recent dominance of Nebbiolo (achieved largely at the expense of Barbera and Dolcetto plantings) means that many of Neive's MGAs, including San Giuliano, represent viticultural sites whose modern character has been defined in living memory.

The expansion came as the commercial value of Barbaresco increased and as négociants demonstrated willingness to pay premium prices for grapes from quality sites. Yet even as Neive's Nebbiolo plantings expanded, Moscato maintained its position, still representing roughly half the planted area in the commune, a testament to the economic importance of diverse production in this township.

## Terroir and General Characteristics

While specific documentation of San Giuliano's individual terroir characteristics remains limited, the site shares the broader soil composition found throughout Neive and the larger Barbaresco zone. Barbaresco's soils divide roughly into two types. The calcareous clay of the Tortonion epoch, very similar to that found in the Barolo communes of La Morra and Barolo, produces wines of particular perfume and elegance. The physical characteristics of these soils (particularly those governing water supply to the vine) represent the most significant influence on grape and wine quality after climate itself.

The best sites in Barbaresco feature very well-drained soils that furnish a steady but moderate, even limiting, water supply to the vines. This controlled water stress, combined with appropriate mineral nutrition, ensures that shoot growth remains restrained, especially before veraison. The result is smaller berries with more concentrated flavors and less susceptibility to bunch compression (viticultural outcomes that directly influence wine quality.

Temperature regulation in and above the soil also plays a crucial role in grape development. The interaction of elevation, aspect, and soil composition creates the mesoclimates that give individual MGAs their distinctive characteristics, though specific data for San Giuliano's elevation and orientation is not extensively documented in the historical literature.

## Wine Character

Barbaresco from Neive, including wines from San Giuliano, exhibits the hallmark characteristics of Nebbiolo: substantial tannins and pronounced acidity that make young Barbaresco a challenging but rewarding wine. These wines typically achieve a minimum alcoholic strength of 12.5% and frequently reach 13.5% or higher, providing the structural backbone necessary for aging.

The aromatic profile characteristic of Nebbiolo from this area includes red cherry, violet, and rose petal in youth, developing with age into more complex tertiary notes of iron, tar, leather, underbrush, and orange peel. The particular expression of these aromatics varies by site, vintage, and winemaking approach, but the fundamental character remains consistent across Neive's growing sites.

The work of pioneering producers demonstrated that Barbaresco possesses its own identity, neither junior to Barolo nor simply a lighter version of it, but rather a distinct expression of Nebbiolo shaped by its specific terroir.

## Production and Recognition

The formal recognition of San Giuliano as an MGA in 2007 placed it within the official framework that had been evolving since single-vineyard Barbarescos first appeared in 1961 with Prunotto's Montestefano. The MGA system sensibly retained historically known vineyards while also providing formal recognition to sites that had proven their quality through more recent viticultural work. This inclusive approach) avoiding the absorption of smaller sites into more famous vineyard names for purely commercial reasons, has preserved the diversity and authenticity of Barbaresco's vineyard map.

The producers working San Giuliano today are part of a larger movement within Barbaresco that includes both traditional large-scale producers and smaller estates, some of which have embraced organic and biodynamic viticulture. These smaller producers often employ traditional winemaking techniques to produce highly individual, long-lived wines with muscular structure alongside the perfume and elegance that define the appellation.

## Conclusion

San Giuliano represents an MGA whose story is still being written. Without the extensive historical documentation that accompanies sites like Santo Stefano or the consensus prestige of Gallina and Albesani, it nonetheless contributes to the diversity and complexity of Neive's expression of Nebbiolo. As Barbaresco continues to evolve and as more producers focus on site-specific bottlings that reveal individual terroir characteristics, MGAs like San Giuliano offer opportunities to discover less-heralded expressions of this noble variety. The site's inclusion in the official MGA designation indicates recognition of its viticultural merit, even as its full character and reputation continue to develop with each vintage.
