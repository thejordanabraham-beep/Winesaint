# Meursault: The Complete Guide to Burgundy's Premier White Wine Village

## 1. Overview & Location

Meursault stands as one of Burgundy's most celebrated white wine communes, situated in the heart of the Côte de Beaune approximately 8 kilometers south of Beaune. The village occupies a prime position between Volnay to the north and Puligny-Montrachet to the south, with its vineyards extending westward into the hills toward Auxey-Duresses and Monthélie.

The appellation encompasses 395 hectares of vineyards, making it the largest white wine-producing commune in the Côte de Beaune. The vineyard area stretches from an elevation of 230 meters at its lowest eastern boundary near the RN74 road to approximately 380 meters at its highest western slopes. The vineyards face predominantly east and southeast, ensuring optimal sun exposure throughout the growing season while benefiting from protection against harsh western winds.

Meursault's climate reflects the continental character typical of Burgundy, with cold winters, warm summers, and crucial diurnal temperature variation that preserves acidity in the grapes. The eastern-facing slopes capture morning sunlight while avoiding excessive afternoon heat, creating ideal conditions for Chardonnay cultivation. Annual rainfall averages 750mm, distributed relatively evenly throughout the year, though harvest-time precipitation can vary significantly between vintages.

The commune's strategic location within the Côte de Beaune places it at the intersection of several geological formations, contributing to the complexity and diversity of its terroir. This positioning has established Meursault as the archetypal expression of Côte de Beaune white Burgundy, representing the perfect balance between power and elegance that defines this prestigious wine region.

## 2. Historical Background

Meursault's viticultural history traces back to the Roman occupation of Gaul, when the settlement was known as Murisaltus, meaning "mouse's leap" in Latin. However, the true foundation of Meursault's wine reputation began during the medieval period under monastic influence, particularly through the Cistercian monks of Cîteaux Abbey and the Benedictines of Cluny.

The Abbey of Cîteaux acquired substantial vineyard holdings in Meursault during the 12th century, establishing the methodical approach to vineyard management and winemaking that would define Burgundian viticulture. The monks identified the superior vineyard sites that would eventually become today's Premier Cru vineyards, recognizing the subtle differences in soil composition and microclimate that produced wines of exceptional quality.

During the 14th and 15th centuries, Meursault wines gained recognition at the Burgundian ducal court. The Dukes of Burgundy, particularly Philip the Bold and John the Fearless, actively promoted Burgundian wines throughout their extensive European territories. Historical records from this period document substantial wine trade between Meursault and major European cities, establishing the international reputation that endures today.

The French Revolution of 1789 marked a pivotal transformation in Meursault's wine industry. The confiscation and subsequent sale of Church and aristocratic properties fragmented the large monastic holdings into smaller parcels. This division created the complex ownership structure that characterizes Meursault today, where single vineyards often have multiple owners, each contributing to the diversity of styles within individual appellations.

The 19th century brought modernization and expansion to Meursault's wine industry. The completion of the Paris-Lyon-Mediterranean railway in 1851 provided reliable transportation to major markets, while the development of glass bottle production enabled proper aging and distribution. However, the phylloxera crisis of the 1870s devastated Meursault's vineyards, requiring complete replanting on American rootstock.

The 20th century witnessed the establishment of the modern appellation system. Meursault received AOC status in 1937, with its Premier Cru vineyards officially classified in 1943. This legal framework codified centuries of empirical knowledge about the commune's terroir hierarchy, providing the foundation for Meursault's continued evolution as one of Burgundy's premier white wine appellations.

## 3. Terroir & Geology

Meursault's geological foundation consists primarily of Jurassic limestone formations dating from the Middle and Upper Jurassic periods, approximately 150-180 million years old. The dominant geological substrate is Bathonian limestone, characterized by its high calcium carbonate content and excellent drainage properties. This limestone base provides the mineral backbone that defines Meursault's distinctive wine character.

The soil composition varies significantly across the appellation, creating distinct micro-terroirs that influence wine style and quality. The Premier Cru vineyards located mid-slope feature soils with 60-70% limestone content mixed with brown clay and decomposed limestone marl. This combination provides optimal drainage while retaining sufficient moisture during dry periods, creating ideal growing conditions for Chardonnay.

The eastern vineyards near the village contain deeper alluvial soils with higher clay content, typically ranging from 30-40% clay mixed with limestone fragments and sandy components. These richer soils produce more voluptuous, immediately appealing wines with greater body and lower acidity compared to the mineral-driven wines from the limestone-dominant slopes.

The western hillside vineyards feature thin soils over solid limestone bedrock, with clay content rarely exceeding 20%. These challenging growing conditions stress the vines appropriately, concentrating flavors and producing wines of exceptional longevity and complexity. The reduced soil depth forces vine roots to penetrate deep into the limestone bedrock, accessing mineral nutrients that contribute to the wines' distinctive terroir character.

Slope angles throughout Meursault range from gentle 2-3% gradients in the eastern sectors to steep 12-15% inclines on the western hillsides. The varied topography creates diverse drainage patterns and sun exposure angles, contributing to the stylistic diversity found within the appellation. The Premier Cru vineyards typically occupy the optimal slope positions between 250-300 meters elevation, where drainage, exposure, and soil composition combine most favorably.

The appellation's microclimate benefits from the Morvan Hills to the west, which provide protection from excessive rainfall and strong winds. Morning fog from the Saône Valley frequently blankets the lower vineyards, moderating temperatures during hot summer days and extending the growing season. This climatic moderation preserves the natural acidity essential to Meursault's aging potential while allowing full phenolic ripeness.

Limestone's influence extends beyond soil chemistry to water management and root development. The porous limestone allows excess water to drain quickly, preventing root rot and dilution of flavors, while its water-retention properties provide consistent moisture availability during drought conditions. This natural irrigation system enables consistent quality across varying weather patterns, contributing to Meursault's reputation for reliability.

## 4. Vineyard Classification System

Meursault operates within Burgundy's hierarchical classification system, though notably without any Grand Cru vineyards. The appellation's quality pyramid consists of three distinct levels: Premier Cru, Village, and regional Bourgogne appellations. This structure reflects the historical assessment of terroir quality while acknowledging that even Meursault's Premier Cru sites, despite their exceptional quality, do not quite achieve the ultimate Grand Cru status reserved for Burgundy's most legendary vineyards.

The Premier Cru level encompasses 17 named vineyards covering approximately 132 hectares, representing roughly one-third of the total appellation area. These vineyards occupy the most favorable mid-slope positions where soil composition, drainage, and exposition combine optimally. The Premier Cru sites are concentrated in two main sectors: the northern section adjacent to Volnay and the southern portion bordering Puligny-Montrachet.

Meursault's most prestigious Premier Cru vineyard, Les Perrières, covers 13.7 hectares on the commune's steepest slopes. The vineyard name derives from "pierre" (stone), referencing the abundant limestone fragments in its thin, mineral-rich soils. Perrières consistently produces Meursault's most long-lived and complex wines, often rivaling Grand Cru quality from neighboring communes.

Les Genevrières, encompassing 16.5 hectares, represents the largest and most commercially significant Premier Cru. The vineyard's name references the juniper bushes that once grew wild on these slopes. Genevrières produces wines combining power with elegance, displaying the archetypal Meursault character of rich texture balanced by limestone-driven minerality.

Les Charmes, covering 31 hectares across two separate parcels (Charmes-Dessus and Charmes-Dessous), derives its name from the hornbeam trees that historically bordered the vineyard. This Premier Cru produces wines noted for their immediate appeal and generous fruit character, though they may lack the aging potential of Perrières or Genevrières.

La Goutte d'Or occupies 5.3 hectares on clay-rich soils that produce distinctively opulent wines. The vineyard name translates to "drop of gold," referencing both the wine's color and its precious quality. Goutte d'Or wines display greater body and lower acidity than other Meursault Premier Crus, reflecting their terroir's higher clay content.

The Blagny sector, shared with Puligny-Montrachet, includes several Premier Cru vineyards that can be labeled as either Meursault-Blagny or Puligny-Montrachet Premier Cru, depending on the producer's preference and marketing strategy. These hillside vineyards produce particularly mineral-driven wines with exceptional aging potential.

Village-level Meursault encompasses approximately 263 hectares of vineyards that, while not achieving Premier Cru status, still produce wines of considerable quality and character. Many Village vineyards occupy favorable sites with terroir quality approaching Premier Cru level, offering exceptional value for consumers seeking authentic Meursault character at accessible prices.

The classification system reflects centuries of empirical observation regarding site-specific quality potential. Premier Cru vineyards consistently demonstrate superior aging ability, complexity, and terroir expression compared to Village sites, justifying their elevated status and premium pricing within the marketplace.

## 5. Wine Styles & Characteristics

Meursault produces exclusively white wines from Chardonnay, with rare exceptions for small quantities of red wine from Pinot Noir in the Blagny sector. The appellation's signature style represents the quintessential expression of Côte de Beaune white Burgundy: wines combining substantial body and texture with mineral precision and aging complexity.

Classic Meursault displays a distinctive golden color with greenish highlights in youth, evolving to deeper gold with amber tones as it matures. The wine's visual density often exceeds that of other Burgundian whites, reflecting its characteristic richness and concentration. Premier Cru examples typically show greater color intensity and slower evolution compared to Village wines.

The aromatic profile of Meursault centers on a complex interplay between fruit, floral, and mineral elements. Young wines typically display white orchard fruits (particularly apple and pear) combined with citrus notes of lemon and lime. Floral components include acacia, hawthorn, and white flowers, while mineral expressions range from wet limestone to more complex chalky and saline notes.

As Meursault ages, the aromatic spectrum expands dramatically. Honeyed notes develop alongside toasted nuts, particularly hazelnuts and almonds. Butter and brioche characteristics emerge from malolactic fermentation and careful oak aging, while tertiary aromas include dried fruits, spices, and complex mineral expressions that can include gunflint and wet stone.

The textural characteristics of Meursault set it apart from other white Burgundies. The wines display substantial body and weight, with a creamy, almost viscous mouthfeel that coats the palate. This texture derives from the limestone-rich soils' influence on grape metabolism, concentrated flavors from appropriate yields, and skilled winemaking techniques that preserve the wine's natural richness.

Acidity in Meursault varies by vineyard site and vintage but typically ranges from moderate to moderate-plus levels. The limestone soils help preserve natural acidity while the continental climate's diurnal temperature variation maintains freshness. Premier Cru sites generally show higher acidity levels than Village wines, contributing to their superior aging potential.

The mineral expression in Meursault reflects its limestone-dominant terroir through chalky, saline, and wet stone characteristics. These mineral notes provide the backbone that supports the wine's fruit and texture, preventing excessive richness while adding complexity and length. The finest examples achieve perfect balance between richness and minerality.

Alcohol levels typically range from 12.5% to 14%, with Premier Cru wines often reaching the higher end of this spectrum due to their superior exposition and ripening conditions. The alcohol integration is generally seamless, providing body and warmth without overwhelming the wine's delicate flavor components.

The oak influence in quality Meursault is typically subtle and well-integrated. Traditional producers use varying proportions of new French oak barrels, usually ranging from 10-30% for Village wines and 20-40% for Premier Crus. The oak contributes vanilla, spice, and toasted notes while enhancing the wine's texture without masking its terroir characteristics.

## 6. Comparison to Neighboring Villages

Meursault's position within the Côte de Beaune creates natural comparisons with its neighboring white wine communes, each expressing distinct terroir characteristics despite their geographic proximity. These stylistic differences reflect variations in soil composition, microclimate, and winemaking traditions that have evolved over centuries.

Compared to Puligny-Montrachet immediately to the south, Meursault produces wines with greater body and richness but often less mineral precision and aging complexity. Puligny-Montrachet's higher limestone content and superior Grand Cru sites generate wines of greater finesse and longevity, while Meursault's style emphasizes immediate appeal and textural generosity. The contrast represents the difference between power (Meursault) and precision (Puligny-Montrachet).

The relationship with Chassagne-Montrachet reveals different stylistic priorities. Chassagne produces both red and white wines, with its whites often showing more rustic character and earthier mineral expressions compared to Meursault's polished elegance. Chassagne's red clay soils contribute to wine structure and aging potential but can reduce the immediate charm that characterizes Meursault.

Beaune's white wines, produced primarily from vineyards on the northern extension of the Côte de Beaune slope, display lighter body and more delicate fruit character than Meursault. The different geological formations around Beaune produce wines with higher acidity and more restrained texture, appealing to consumers preferring subtlety over power.

The comparison with Auxey-Duresses, located in the hills west of Meursault, illustrates elevation's influence on wine style. Auxey-Duresses vineyards' higher altitude and cooler microclimate produce wines with greater acidity and more austere mineral character, requiring longer aging to achieve the approachability that Meursault displays in youth.

Pernand-Vergelesses, positioned in the northern Côte de Beaune, produces whites with distinctly different character from Meursault. The cooler climate and different soil composition create wines with higher acidity, leaner texture, and more pronounced mineral expression, representing a more northern style compared to Meursault's southern generosity.

Within the broader Côte de Beaune context, Meursault occupies a unique stylistic position. Its wines combine the accessibility and generous fruit character that appeals to diverse palates while maintaining sufficient complexity and aging potential to satisfy serious collectors. This balance explains Meursault's commercial success and widespread recognition as the archetypal white Burgundy.

The terroir distinctions between neighboring villages reflect geological variations across the Côte de Beaune. Meursault's particular combination of limestone and clay, combined with its favorable exposition and elevation, creates conditions that produce wines of distinctive character that cannot be replicated elsewhere, even in adjacent communes with seemingly similar growing conditions.

## 7. Notable Vineyards

### Les Perrières
Les Perrières represents Meursault's most prestigious vineyard, covering 13.7 hectares on the commune's steepest slopes between 280-320 meters elevation. The vineyard's thin soils over limestone bedrock create challenging growing conditions that produce wines of exceptional concentration and longevity. Perrières consistently demonstrates quality levels approaching Grand Cru status, with many critics considering it Meursault's finest terroir.

The vineyard's exposition faces due east, capturing optimal morning sunlight while avoiding excessive afternoon heat. The steep slope angle of 10-15% ensures excellent drainage while forcing vine roots deep into the limestone substrate. Soil depth rarely exceeds 40 centimeters, with limestone fragments comprising 70-80% of the total composition.

Wines from Perrières display distinctive mineral precision combined with concentrated fruit character. The limestone influence creates complex chalky and saline notes that distinguish Perrières from other Meursault Premier Crus. These wines require 8-10 years to achieve peak maturity and can age gracefully for 20-25 years in exceptional vintages.

### Les Genevrières
Les Genevrières encompasses 16.5 hectares of prime mid-slope vineyard land, representing Meursault's largest Premier Cru by production volume. The vineyard's slightly deeper soils contain 50-60% limestone mixed with brown clay, creating conditions that balance mineral expression with textural richness.

The vineyard's gentle southeast exposition provides consistent ripening conditions while preserving natural acidity. Soil depth varies from 30-60 centimeters, allowing for more vigorous vine growth than Perrières while maintaining appropriate stress levels for quality fruit production.

Genevrières wines epitomize classic Meursault character, combining substantial body with elegant mineral expression. The wines display excellent balance between power and finesse, making them accessible in youth while developing additional complexity with age. Peak maturity typically occurs between 6-12 years, with top examples aging gracefully for 15-20 years.

### Les Charmes
Les Charmes covers 31 hectares across two separate parcels (Charmes-Dessus (upper Charmes) and Charmes-Dessous (lower Charmes)) each expressing slightly different terroir characteristics. The vineyard's varied soil composition creates stylistic diversity within the single Premier Cru designation.

Charmes-Dessus occupies higher elevation slopes with thinner soils and greater limestone content, producing wines with more mineral expression and aging potential. Charmes-Dessous features deeper, clay-rich soils that generate more immediately appealing wines with greater body and lower acidity.

The combined Charmes production represents Meursault's most commercially significant Premier Cru, offering consumers access to Premier Cru quality at relatively moderate prices. The wines display classic Meursault richness with appealing fruit character, typically reaching peak maturity between 5-10 years.

### La Goutte d'Or
La Goutte d'Or occupies 5.3 hectares of clay-rich vineyard land that produces distinctively opulent wines. The vineyard's higher clay content creates growing conditions that favor body and texture over mineral precision, resulting in wines with unique character within the Meursault range.

The vineyard's southeast exposition and moderate slope angle create favorable ripening conditions, while the clay-rich soils retain moisture during dry periods. This terroir combination produces grapes with high sugar levels and rich flavor concentration.

Goutte d'Or wines display exceptional body and texture, often showing lower acidity than other Meursault Premier Crus. The wines appeal to consumers preferring richness over austerity, typically reaching peak maturity between 4-8 years while maintaining quality for 12-15 years.

### Blagny Sector Premier Crus
The Blagny sector includes several Premier Cru vineyards located on the hillside commune boundary with Puligny-Montrachet. These high-elevation sites produce particularly mineral-driven wines with exceptional aging potential, representing Meursault's most austere and long-lived expressions.

The Blagny vineyards' thin soils over limestone bedrock create growing conditions similar to Perrières but with even greater exposure to wind and weather extremes. These challenging conditions produce wines requiring extended aging to achieve full potential but ultimately delivering exceptional complexity and longevity.

Blagny wines display intense mineral character with restrained fruit expression, appealing to consumers preferring precision over power. The wines typically require 10-12 years to reach peak maturity and can age gracefully for 25+ years in exceptional vintages.

## 8. Leading Producers

### Domaine des Comtes Lafon
Domaine des Comtes Lafon represents Meursault's most prestigious producer, with vineyard holdings spanning the appellation's finest terroirs. The estate controls significant parcels in Perrières, Genevrières, and Charmes, producing wines that define modern Meursault excellence.

Under Dominique Lafon's direction since 1983, the domaine has pioneered biodynamic viticulture in Meursault while maintaining traditional winemaking methods. The estate's approach emphasizes terroir expression through minimal intervention, allowing each vineyard's unique characteristics to shine through.

Lafon's wines display exceptional purity and precision, combining Meursault's characteristic richness with remarkable aging potential. The domaine's Perrières consistently ranks among Burgundy's finest white wines, often surpassing Grand Cru quality levels. Production remains limited, with strong international demand creating significant pricing pressure.

### Domaine Coche-Dury
Jean-François Coche-Dury has achieved legendary status among Burgundy producers, creating wines of extraordinary concentration and complexity from relatively modest vineyard holdings. The domaine's meticulous approach to viticulture and winemaking has elevated every wine in their portfolio to exceptional levels.

Coche-Dury's style emphasizes intense fruit concentration balanced by precise mineral expression. The wines require extended aging to achieve full potential but reward patience with remarkable complexity and longevity. Even the domaine's Village Meursault achieves quality levels exceeding many producers' Premier Crus.

Limited production and exceptional quality have created cult status for Coche-Dury wines, with prices reflecting their rarity and desirability. The domaine represents the pinnacle of artisanal Burgundy production, demonstrating how exceptional vineyard work and winemaking can transcend terroir limitations.

### Domaine Pierre-Yves Colin-Morey
Pierre-Yves Colin-Morey has rapidly established himself among Meursault's elite producers since founding his domaine in 2005. The estate sources fruit from exceptional vineyard sites throughout the Côte de Beaune, producing wines that combine power with elegance.

Colin-Morey's approach emphasizes precision and purity, using minimal new oak to preserve terroir expression. The wines display remarkable consistency across different vineyard sites, reflecting the producer's skilled approach to site-specific winemaking.

The domaine's rapid rise to prominence demonstrates the continued evolution of Burgundy's producer hierarchy. Colin-Morey wines have achieved strong critical acclaim and market recognition, establishing the estate among Meursault's most sought-after producers.

### Domaine Guy Roulot
Domaine Guy Roulot, now managed by Jean-Marc Roulot, represents traditional Meursault winemaking at its finest. The estate's holdings include prime parcels in Perrières, Charmes, and other Premier Cru sites, producing wines that epitomize classic Meursault character.

Roulot's winemaking philosophy emphasizes balance and elegance over power, creating wines that age gracefully while remaining approachable in youth. The estate's consistent quality across multiple vineyard sites demonstrates masterful understanding of Meursault's diverse terroirs.

The domaine maintains strong international distribution, making their wines more accessible than some cult producers. Roulot wines offer exceptional quality-to-price ratios within the premium Meursault market, representing reliable choices for serious collectors.

### Traditional vs. Modern Approaches
Meursault's leading producers represent both traditional and modern winemaking philosophies, each achieving excellence through different approaches. Traditional producers like Antoine Jobard emphasize time-honored techniques including extended lees aging and minimal intervention, creating wines that express classic Meursault character.

Modern producers have embraced innovations in viticulture and winemaking while respecting terroir expression. Biodynamic farming, precision fermentation control, and selective oak usage have enhanced quality potential without sacrificing authenticity.

The diversity of successful approaches in Meursault demonstrates the appellation's flexibility and quality potential. Both traditional and modern methods can achieve exceptional results when applied with skill and understanding of each vineyard's unique characteristics.

## 9. Aging Potential & Evolution

Meursault's aging potential varies significantly based on vineyard classification, producer quality, and vintage characteristics. Premier Cru wines from top producers can age gracefully for 15-25 years, developing remarkable complexity while maintaining freshness and vitality. Village wines typically peak between 5-10 years but exceptional examples can evolve positively for 12-15 years.

The evolution pattern in Meursault follows a predictable trajectory that reflects the wine's terroir characteristics and winemaking style. Young wines display primary fruit aromas of apple, pear, and citrus, combined with floral notes and subtle mineral undertones. The texture in youth shows the characteristic Meursault richness but may appear somewhat closed or restrained in the finest examples.

During the intermediate aging phase (3-8 years), Meursault develops secondary characteristics that enhance complexity without overwhelming the primary fruit. Honeyed notes emerge alongside toasted nuts and brioche flavors from malolactic fermentation and oak aging. The texture becomes more integrated and silky, while mineral expression gains prominence and definition.

Mature Meursault (8-15 years) displays the full spectrum of tertiary development that distinguishes great white Burgundy. Complex mineral expressions including wet limestone, chalk, and saline notes dominate the palate, while fruit character evolves toward dried fruits and exotic spices. The texture achieves perfect integration between richness and precision, creating wines of remarkable complexity and length.

Extended aging potential in exceptional Meursault examples reveals the true quality of superior terroirs and vintages. Wines from Perrières, Genevrières, and other top Premier Cru sites can continue evolving positively for 20-25 years, developing profound mineral complexity while maintaining structural integrity and freshness.

Vintage variation significantly influences aging potential and evolution patterns. Cool, high-acidity vintages typically produce wines with greater longevity but slower development, requiring patience to achieve peak quality. Warm, ripe vintages create immediately appealing wines that may mature more quickly but can lack the structural foundation for extended aging.

Storage conditions critically impact Meursault's evolution and ultimate quality. Consistent cool temperatures (50-55°F) and high humidity levels preserve the wine's delicate balance while preventing premature oxidation. Excessive temperature fluctuations or warm storage accelerates aging and can damage the wine's complex flavor development.

The decision regarding optimal drinking windows depends on personal preference for wine style and development phase. Consumers preferring fresh fruit character and vibrant acidity should consume Meursault within 5-8 years, while those seeking complex tertiary development should cellar wines for 10-15 years or longer.

Producer style significantly influences aging potential within the same vineyard sites. Producers emphasizing concentration and structure create wines requiring longer cellaring to achieve peak quality, while those favoring immediate accessibility produce wines that peak earlier but may not develop the same long-term complexity.

## 10. Market Position

Meursault occupies a unique position within the global white Burgundy market, representing the perfect balance between quality, prestige, and relative accessibility compared to the most sought-after appellations. The appellation's substantial production volume (significantly larger than Puligny-Montrachet or Chassagne-Montrachet) provides greater market availability while maintaining premium positioning and pricing.

Pricing structures within Meursault reflect the established quality hierarchy between Village and Premier Cru wines. Village Meursault from reputable producers typically trades at 30-50% premium to generic Côte de Beaune blanc, while Premier Cru examples command 2-3 times Village prices. The most prestigious Premier Cru sites like Perrières can achieve pricing levels approaching Grand Cru wines from other regions.

The international market demonstrates strong demand for Meursault across diverse consumer segments. The appellation's accessible style and reliable quality attract both novice Burgundy consumers and experienced collectors, creating broad-based demand that supports premium pricing. Key markets include the United States, United Kingdom, Japan, and emerging Asian markets where white Burgundy consumption continues expanding.

Collectibility varies significantly by producer and vineyard site within Meursault. Wines from legendary producers like Coche-Dury, Comtes Lafon, and other cult domaines achieve exceptional pricing and appreciate significantly over time. More commercially oriented producers offer excellent quality at moderate prices, providing accessible entry points into premium Meursault.

Investment potential in Meursault reflects broader trends in fine wine markets, with top producers showing consistent appreciation over extended periods. Limited production from prestigious domaines creates scarcity value that supports price appreciation, while the appellation's established reputation provides market stability during economic downturns.

Auction market activity for Meursault demonstrates strong collector interest, particularly for wines from top producers and exceptional vintages. Premier Cru wines from legendary producers regularly achieve prices exceeding original release levels by 200-400%, reflecting strong secondary market demand and limited availability.

The restaurant market represents a significant distribution channel for Meursault, with sommeliers appreciating the appellation's food-friendly character and reliable quality. Many establishments feature Meursault as their premium white Burgundy selection, creating consistent demand that supports producer pricing strategies.

Distribution patterns favor established importers and négociant houses with strong Burgundy relationships. Direct domaine purchases remain limited due to small production volumes and strong domestic French demand, creating challenges for international consumers seeking access to top producers.

Market trends indicate continued growth in Meursault demand, driven by expanding global wine markets and increasing appreciation for white Burgundy quality. Climate change concerns regarding other white wine regions may enhance Burgundy's comparative advantage, supporting long-term demand growth for premium appellations like Meursault.

The appellation's market position benefits from consistent quality delivery across multiple price points, making Meursault an attractive choice for diverse consumer segments. This broad appeal provides market stability and growth potential that distinguishes Meursault from more narrowly focused luxury appellations with limited commercial reach.
