# Secondine

## Introduction

Secondine is one of the officially recognized menzioni geografiche aggiuntive (MGAs) in the Barbaresco DOCG, located within the commune of Barbaresco itself. Included in the official MGA list established in 2007, three years before Barolo codified its own cru system. Secondine represents part of the careful cartographic work that preserved historically known vineyard sites while avoiding the commercial temptation to absorb smaller sites into more famous names.

Unlike the celebrated MGAs that dominate discussion of Barbaresco. Asili, Rabajà, Montestefano, Montefico, and the producer-defined sites like San Lorenzo and Martinenga (Secondine remains among the lesser-documented vineyard sites in the appellation. This relative obscurity should not be mistaken for lack of quality, but rather reflects the historical reality that comprehensive documentation of Barbaresco's finest sites developed later and less systematically than in Barolo.

## Historical Context and Documentation

The challenge in discussing Secondine lies in the documentary record itself. Lorenzo Fantini's late 19th-century monograph on Piedmontese viticulture, the foundational text for understanding historical vineyard hierarchies, indicated remarkably few "choice positions" in Barbaresco compared to Barolo, and made no mention of Neive's vineyards whatsoever. The systematic listing and rating of Barbaresco's finest sites only began in earnest in the 1960s with Luigi Veronelli's work, followed by Renato Ratti's efforts in the 1970s.

What established consensus about Barbaresco's top vineyards was not ancient prestige or comprehensive ampelographic study, but rather négociants' willingness to pay premium prices for grapes from certain sites, combined with the dedicated work of producers who championed specific vineyards. Angelo Gaja transformed San Lorenzo and Tildin into internationally recognized names through exacting standards and marketing prowess. Bruno Giacosa did likewise with his selections from prestigious sites. The Produttori del Barbaresco, Italy's finest cooperative, systematically bottled single-vineyard wines that helped establish a quality hierarchy based on transparent, repeated evidence rather than historical reputation alone.

Secondine did not benefit from this kind of producer-driven advocacy that elevated other sites to fame. It remains a legally recognized MGA without the extensive documentation, critical attention, or producer focus that characterizes Barbaresco's first-tier sites.

## The Barbaresco Context

To understand Secondine's place in Barbaresco's landscape requires understanding the appellation itself. The production zone covers just 733 hectares as of 2019) one-third the size of Barolo (spread across the townships of Barbaresco, Treiso, and Neive, with small portions in Alba. This is a young appellation by Piedmontese standards; the name Barbaresco only began appearing on labels in 1894 when Domizio Cavazza, professor at Alba's Oenological School, founded the Barbaresco cooperative. Before that, these wines were often blended with Barolo to add freshness.

The region's vineyards occupy south-facing slopes at elevations between 200 and 400 meters, lower than Barolo's heights. The Tanaro River's proximity creates a moderating influence that allows Nebbiolo to ripen earlier than in Barolo. The result is wine that expresses Nebbiolo's essential character) high acidity, substantial tannin, and aging potential (with a profile traditionally described as more immediately fruity and approachable than Barolo, though this generalization has been challenged by modern winemaking approaches that often seek to emphasize structure and longevity.

## Wine Characteristics and Barbaresco Style

Without specific documentation of Secondine's wine profile, what can be said with confidence is that any wine from this MGA must conform to Barbaresco DOCG regulations: 100% Nebbiolo, minimum 12.5% alcohol (though 13.5% is easily achieved in most vintages), minimum two years of aging before release (four years for Riserva, with 18 months in wood). The wines must display Nebbiolo's hallmark characteristics: pronounced acidity, firm tannins, and the grape's complex aromatic spectrum spanning cherry, violet, rose, and with age developing notes of tar, iron, truffle, and orange peel.

The broader evolution of Barbaresco winemaking has moved through distinct phases. The 1970s and 1980s saw a shift toward shorter macerations and aging in French barriques, pursuing rounder, fruitier wines for modern markets. Traditional producers resisted, arguing that French oak suppressed Nebbiolo's delicate perfume. Today, a return to longer macerations) often up to 40 days (and aging in large Slavonian oak casks (botti) has become increasingly common, even fashionable, particularly among younger, quality-focused producers practicing organic and biodynamic viticulture.

Where Secondine fits within this stylistic spectrum, and how its terroir might predispose wines toward particular expressions of Nebbiolo, remains undocumented in available literature. The specific soil composition, precise elevation, exact exposition, and microclimate variations that would allow meaningful discussion of the site's terroir signature have not been comprehensively recorded in major references on Barbaresco.

## The MGA System and Secondine's Place

The 2007 MGA system represented a crucial moment for Barbaresco, providing legal recognition and protection for single-vineyard designations. The list sensibly retained almost all historically known vineyards, resisting pressure to consolidate smaller sites into famous names for commercial advantage. This preservation of viticultural diversity and historical continuity serves the long-term interests of the appellation, even if it means some MGAs remain obscure.

Secondine's inclusion in the official MGA list confirms its recognition as a distinct site worthy of legal protection, even without the commercial success or critical attention that has elevated other vineyards to fame. In Barbaresco's hierarchy of sites, it occupies the position of many smaller MGAs: legally recognized, potentially distinctive, but lacking the producer advocacy, critical commentary, or market presence that would generate detailed documentation.

## Conclusion

Secondine stands as a reminder that within prestigious appellations like Barbaresco, not all legally recognized sites enjoy equal fame or documentation. The MGA system protected Secondine's identity as a distinct vineyard site, but the absence of dedicated producer champions or extensive critical attention has left it without the detailed terroir analysis and wine profiling that characterizes more celebrated neighbors.

For the serious student of Barbaresco, sites like Secondine represent both a challenge and an opportunity) a challenge because comprehensive information remains elusive, and an opportunity because undiscovered or underappreciated sites sometimes offer exceptional quality-to-value relationships. Whether Secondine produces wines of interest remains a question best answered not through historical documentation or critical consensus, but through direct tasting experience with the few bottles that may carry its name.

The broader lesson is that wine region hierarchies are not fixed or complete. They evolve through the dedicated work of producers who champion specific sites, the attention of critics and writers who document quality, and the commercial mechanisms that reward excellence. Secondine may yet find its advocate.
