# Pora MGA, Barbaresco

## A Note on Available Information

While the Barbaresco denomination implemented its official MGA (menzione geografica aggiuntiva) system in 2007—notably before Barolo followed suit in 2010—the research data available does not contain specific documentation about the Pora site. This absence from the primary reference materials consulted suggests either that Pora is among the lesser-documented MGAs within the Barbaresco denomination, or that detailed terroir analysis and producer information for this specific site has not been widely published in the standard reference works on the region.

## Context Within Barbaresco's MGA System

The Barbaresco MGA system was designed to recognize and protect historically significant vineyard sites across the denomination's three main communes: Barbaresco, Neive, and Treiso. According to the regulations, the official list "has sensibly retained almost all the historically known vineyards, rarely absorbing them in more famous vineyard names for commercial reasons." This careful approach preserved the historical vineyard nomenclature of the region rather than consolidating sites under more commercially recognizable names.

The most celebrated and well-documented Barbaresco MGAs include Asili, Montefico, Montestefano, and Rabajà in the Barbaresco commune; Albesani and Gallina in Neive; and Pajorè in Treiso. Additionally, sites such as San Lorenzo, Tildin, and Martinenga in Barbaresco, and Santo Stefano in Neive achieved prominence through the dedicated work of renowned producers like Gaja, Giacosa, and Alberto di Gresy.

## The Challenge of Barbaresco's Historical Record

Unlike Barolo, Barbaresco has "a less firmly established written record of cru designation." Single-vineyard bottlings appeared relatively recently, with Prunotto's Barbaresco Montestefano in 1961 marking the first such wine. Lorenzo Fantini's late 19th-century monograph on Piedmontese viticulture noted "very few 'choice positions' in Barbaresco (and none whatsoever in Neive)," and systematic attempts to list and rate the finest sites only emerged in the 1960s with Luigi Veronelli and in the 1970s with Renato Ratti.

This historical context helps explain why some Barbaresco MGAs remain less thoroughly documented than others, particularly those that may not have attracted the sustained attention of marquee producers or négociants willing to pay premium prices for their fruit—the mechanism by which consensus about the finest sites has typically been established in the region.

## Further Research Recommended

For those seeking detailed information about Pora specifically, Alessandro Masnaghetti's *Barbaresco MGA: The Barbaresco Great Vineyard Encyclopedia* (2016) would be the most authoritative source, offering comprehensive cartographic analysis and terroir documentation for all recognized MGAs within the denomination. This reference work remains the definitive guide to understanding the individual characteristics, boundaries, and histories of Barbaresco's officially recognized vineyard sites.
