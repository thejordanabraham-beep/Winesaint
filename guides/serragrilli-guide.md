# Serragrilli

## Introduction

Serragrilli is one of the officially recognized *menzioni geografiche aggiuntive* (MGA) within the Barbaresco DOCG, part of the comprehensive list of single-vineyard designations formally established in 2007. Located in the commune of Barbaresco itself, Serragrilli represents one of the many historically significant sites that were incorporated into the official MGA system, which sensibly retained almost all traditionally known vineyards rather than absorbing them into more commercially famous names.

Unlike the most celebrated Barbaresco crus. Asili, Montefico, Montestefano, and Rabajà, Serragrilli has maintained a lower profile in both historical documentation and contemporary wine discourse. This relative obscurity should not be mistaken for lack of quality, but rather reflects the reality that négociants and critics have historically focused their attention and premiums on a select group of sites, while many other legitimate MGAs have quietly continued producing wines of distinction.

## Historical Context and Recognition

The challenge in documenting Serragrilli lies partly in the comparatively recent phenomenon of single-vineyard designation in Barbaresco itself. As Lorenzo Fantini's late 19th-century monograph on Piedmontese viticulture indicates, there were very few "choice positions" formally recognized in Barbaresco at that time, with systematic attempts to list and rate the finest sites only emerging in the 1960s through Luigi Veronelli's work and the 1970s with Renato Ratti's classifications.

The first single-vineyard Barbaresco, Prunotto's Montestefano, appeared only in 1961, establishing a precedent that would eventually encompass dozens of distinct sites. Serragrilli, like many MGAs, may have been known locally to growers and négociants but lacked the commercial promotion that accompanied vineyards developed by high-profile estates. ## Barbaresco's Terroir Framework

To understand Serragrilli's potential, it must be viewed within the broader context of Barbaresco's terroir. The denomination occupies an area northeast and east of Alba, totaling 733 hectares as of 2019, spread across the communes of Barbaresco, Neive, and Treiso. The region's soils and mesoclimates vary considerably even within short distances, creating the complex patchwork of *crus* that defines the appellation.

Barbaresco's terroir is characterized by calcareous marls of Tortonian and Serravallian origin, with specific sites showing variations in soil depth, clay content, and drainage characteristics. These variations, combined with differences in elevation (typically ranging from 180 to 400 meters), aspect, and exposure, create distinct expressions of Nebbiolo across the denomination's many MGAs.

## Current Status and Producers

The limited documentation of Serragrilli in contemporary wine literature suggests that either few producers are bottling vineyard-designated wines from this site, or those who do are small-scale operations without significant export presence or critical attention. This is not unusual within the Barbaresco denomination, where many legitimate MGAs are vinified as part of larger blends or bottled in quantities too small to generate international recognition.

The 2007 codification of MGAs was designed to preserve historical vineyard names and provide a framework for future recognition, even for sites not currently enjoying premium status. As the market for Barbaresco continues to evolve and consumers develop more granular knowledge of individual *crus*, sites like Serragrilli may emerge from obscurity, particularly if quality-focused producers invest in showcasing their distinctive characteristics.

## Conclusion

Serragrilli stands as one of Barbaresco's many officially recognized MGAs, a designation that confirms its historical legitimacy while also highlighting the gaps in our documented knowledge of the denomination's full range of sites. For wine enthusiasts and professionals, it represents an opportunity, wines from lesser-known MGAs like Serragrilli may offer the essential qualities of Barbaresco terroir without the premium pricing commanded by the most famous *crus*. As the denomination continues to mature and more producers bottle site-specific wines, a clearer picture of Serragrilli's distinctive character may yet emerge.
