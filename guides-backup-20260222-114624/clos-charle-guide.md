# Clos Charlé Premier Cru: A Complete Guide to Givry's Distinguished Climat

## Overview & Location

Clos Charlé stands as one of Givry's most distinguished Premier Cru vineyards, situated in the heart of the Côte Chalonnaise in Burgundy's southern sector. Located within the commune of Givry, approximately 15 kilometers southwest of Beaune, this climat occupies a privileged position on the eastern-facing slopes that define the Côte Chalonnaise's viticultural landscape.

The vineyard lies within Givry's Premier Cru zone, which encompasses some of the appellation's most exceptional terroir. Givry itself represents one of five major appellations within the Côte Chalonnaise, alongside Mercurey, Rully, Montagny, and Bouzeron. The Côte Chalonnaise serves as Burgundy's transitional zone between the prestigious Côte d'Or to the north and the Mâconnais to the south, offering exceptional value while maintaining the complexity and finesse associated with Burgundian viticulture.

Clos Charlé's strategic positioning places it among Givry's most favorable exposures, benefiting from optimal sun exposure while being protected from harsh western winds. The climat sits at an elevation that captures the ideal balance between warmth and cooling influences, contributing to the measured ripening that defines great Burgundy.

## Size

The Clos Charlé Premier Cru encompasses approximately 2.8 hectares (6.9 acres), making it a moderately sized climat within Givry's Premier Cru portfolio. This relatively intimate scale allows for intensive viticultural management while providing sufficient volume for multiple producers to express their interpretations of this distinctive terroir.

The vineyard's compact size reflects the typical fragmentation found throughout Burgundy, where individual climats are often small enough to maintain remarkable uniformity in soil composition, microclimate, and exposure. This homogeneity contributes to Clos Charlé's consistent character expression across different vintages and producers.

## Terroir & Geology

Clos Charlé's geological foundation rests upon the Middle Jurassic limestone formations characteristic of the Côte Chalonnaise, specifically dating to the Bathonian and Bajocian periods. The vineyard's terroir represents a complex interplay of limestone substrates, clay content, and surface composition that creates ideal conditions for both Pinot Noir and Chardonnay cultivation.

The soil profile consists primarily of brown calcareous earth overlying white limestone bedrock. The topsoil typically ranges from 60 to 100 centimeters in depth, providing sufficient root penetration while ensuring natural drainage. Clay content varies throughout the climat but generally ranges from 15% to 25%, offering adequate water retention during dry periods while preventing waterlogging during wet seasons.

The limestone component includes both hard limestone bedrock and softer marlstone layers, creating a mineral-rich environment that contributes to the wines' characteristic tension and longevity. Fossil-rich deposits throughout the vineyard provide additional calcium carbonate, which plays a crucial role in moderating soil pH and contributing to the wines' mineral complexity.

Clos Charlé's eastern exposure provides optimal morning and midday sun exposure while offering protection from the harsh afternoon heat during peak summer months. The vineyard sits on a gentle to moderate slope ranging from 5% to 15% gradient, ensuring excellent drainage while preventing excessive erosion. This slope orientation maximizes photosynthetic efficiency while maintaining the acid retention essential for producing balanced, age-worthy wines.

The climat's elevation ranges from approximately 280 to 320 meters above sea level, positioning it within the optimal altitude band for Burgundian viticulture. This elevation provides sufficient cooling influence to preserve acidity while ensuring adequate heat accumulation for complete phenolic ripeness.

Surface stones, primarily limestone fragments and occasional iron-rich deposits, dot the vineyard floor, contributing to heat retention and reflection while further improving drainage. These stones also play a role in moderating vine vigor, encouraging deeper root development and concentration of flavors.

## Climate & Microclimate

Clos Charlé benefits from the Côte Chalonnaise's semi-continental climate, characterized by cold winters, warm summers, and significant diurnal temperature variation during the growing season. The climat's specific positioning creates a microclimate that enhances these regional characteristics while providing additional advantages for viticulture.

The eastern exposure ensures optimal morning sun capture, allowing for efficient photosynthesis initiation and dew evaporation, which reduces disease pressure. During peak summer months, the vineyard receives intense morning and midday sun while benefiting from afternoon shading from higher elevations to the west, preventing excessive heat stress.

Wind patterns play a crucial role in Clos Charlé's microclimate. The vineyard's position provides natural protection from destructive western winds while allowing beneficial air circulation that reduces humidity and disease pressure. Cold air drainage during spring and fall helps moderate temperature extremes while preventing frost damage in most vintages.

Annual precipitation typically ranges from 700 to 900 millimeters, with most rainfall occurring during winter and early spring months. The vineyard's excellent drainage ensures that excess moisture does not compromise root health or dilute fruit concentration. During dry summer periods, the clay component in the soil provides sufficient water retention to sustain vine health without irrigation.

The microclimate's diurnal temperature variation, often exceeding 15°C during harvest season, proves particularly beneficial for maintaining acidity while achieving phenolic ripeness. Cool nighttime temperatures help preserve aromatic compounds while warm days ensure sugar accumulation and tannin development.

## Viticulture

Clos Charlé is planted primarily to Pinot Noir, which comprises approximately 85% of the vineyard, with Chardonnay occupying the remaining 15%. This distribution reflects both historical precedent and the terroir's particular affinity for red wine production, though the Chardonnay planted here produces wines of exceptional quality and distinction.

Vine age varies among different parcels and producers, with the oldest vines dating to the 1960s and recent replantings establishing young vines for future production. Average vine age across the climat ranges from 25 to 40 years, providing an excellent balance between established root systems and vigorous production.

Planting density typically ranges from 8,000 to 10,000 vines per hectare, reflecting modern Burgundian practices that balance vine competition with practical management considerations. Higher density plantings encourage competition among vines, leading to deeper root development and concentrated fruit production.

Viticultural practices in Clos Charlé emphasize sustainable and increasingly organic approaches. Most producers employ minimal intervention techniques, focusing on canopy management, selective harvesting, and soil health maintenance. Pruning follows traditional Burgundian methods, typically employing the Guyot system with careful attention to bud count and shoot positioning.

The climat's specific challenges include occasional spring frost risk, particularly in lower-lying sections, and the need for careful vintage timing to achieve optimal ripeness while maintaining acidity. The limestone-rich soils can sometimes lead to iron chlorosis in susceptible rootstock-scion combinations, requiring careful variety and rootstock selection.

Harvest timing proves critical in Clos Charlé, as the terroir's potential for producing both power and elegance depends on achieving complete phenolic ripeness while preserving the natural acidity that gives these wines their aging potential. Most producers conduct multiple passes through the vineyard, selecting only perfectly ripe grapes for their Premier Cru bottlings.

## Wine Character & Style

Wines from Clos Charlé display a distinctive character that reflects both the climat's terroir and Givry's particular expression of Burgundian viticulture. The Pinot Noirs demonstrate remarkable consistency in their combination of elegance, structure, and aging potential, while the limited Chardonnay production showcases exceptional mineral complexity and refinement.

Clos Charlé Pinot Noirs typically exhibit a deep ruby color with purple highlights in youth, evolving to garnet and brick tones with age. The aromatic profile shows classic Burgundian complexity, beginning with fresh red fruit notes of cherry, raspberry, and strawberry, often accompanied by floral elements of violet and rose petals. With bottle age, these wines develop more complex secondary aromas including leather, game, forest floor, and subtle spice notes.

On the palate, these wines demonstrate the climat's particular ability to balance power with finesse. The initial fruit impression shows concentrated red and black fruits, supported by well-integrated tannins that provide structure without overwhelming the wine's natural elegance. The limestone terroir contributes a distinctive mineral backbone that runs through the wine's entire flavor profile, providing length and complexity.

Structural elements in Clos Charlé reds include naturally high acidity that preserves freshness and enables extended aging, moderate alcohol levels typically ranging from 12.5% to 13.5%, and refined tannins that integrate smoothly with extended cellaring. The wines show excellent balance between fruit, acidity, and tannin, creating a harmonious whole that exemplifies great Burgundy.

Textural qualities include a silky mouthfeel in the best examples, with sufficient body to support the wine's flavor intensity while maintaining the delicacy expected from Premier Cru Burgundy. The finish typically shows excellent length, with mineral notes and subtle fruit flavors persisting well beyond the initial taste.

The limited Chardonnay production from Clos Charlé demonstrates equally impressive qualities, showing bright golden color and complex aromatics combining citrus, stone fruits, and mineral elements. These wines display the terroir's limestone influence through their crisp acidity and mineral-driven finish, while showing sufficient body and complexity to reward extended aging.

## Comparison to Surrounding Crus

Within Givry's Premier Cru hierarchy, Clos Charlé distinguishes itself through its particular combination of elegance and structure. Compared to the neighboring Premier Cru of Clos Saint-Pierre, Clos Charlé wines tend to show more immediate approachability while maintaining equal aging potential. The terroir produces wines with slightly softer tannins and more pronounced fruit character than the more austere expressions found in some of Givry's other Premier Crus.

When compared to Clos du Cras Long, another prominent Givry Premier Cru, Clos Charlé wines demonstrate greater consistency across vintages while showing a more distinctive mineral signature. The eastern exposure of Clos Charlé contributes to more reliable ripening compared to some of the more challenging exposures found elsewhere in the appellation.

Relative to Premier Cru Clos Salomon, Clos Charlé produces wines with more obvious charm in youth while developing equal complexity with age. The soil composition in Clos Charlé creates wines with more pronounced limestone influence, contributing to their particular mineral character and aging trajectory.

Within the broader context of Côte Chalonnaise Premier Crus, Clos Charlé holds its own against the best sites in Mercurey and Rully. The wines show greater concentration and aging potential than many Côte Chalonnaise offerings while maintaining the regional character that distinguishes these wines from their Côte d'Or counterparts.

Givry does not possess any Grand Cru vineyards, making Premier Cru status the highest classification available within the appellation. However, the best expressions of Clos Charlé can compete qualitatively with village-level wines from prestigious Côte d'Or communes, offering exceptional value for wines of this quality level.

The diversity of producer approaches within Clos Charlé creates an interesting range of stylistic interpretations while maintaining the underlying character that defines this Premier Cru.

Stylistic variations among producers range from more traditional approaches emphasizing extended aging and classic Burgundian techniques to more modern interpretations that focus on fruit purity and early accessibility. Despite these differences in approach, all serious producers working within Clos Charlé share a commitment to expressing the unique character of this exceptional terroir.

## Historical Background & Classification

The history of Clos Charlé extends back several centuries, with viticultural activity in this area documented since medieval times. The climat's name likely derives from historical ownership patterns, though the exact etymology remains subject to scholarly debate. Like many Burgundian vineyards, Clos Charlé experienced various ownership changes throughout its history, including periods of monastic ownership that contributed to viticultural knowledge and reputation building.

The vineyard's classification as Premier Cru came with the official establishment of Burgundy's appellation system in 1936, though its reputation for producing superior wines was well established long before formal classification. The Institut National des Appellations d'Origine (INAO) recognized Clos Charlé's exceptional terroir and consistent quality in granting Premier Cru status, acknowledging both historical precedent and demonstrated wine quality.

Throughout the 19th and early 20th centuries, wines from Clos Charlé enjoyed recognition among négociant houses in Beaune and Chalon-sur-Saône, who valued the climat's consistency and aging potential. This commercial success helped establish the vineyard's reputation and contributed to its eventual Premier Cru classification.

The phylloxera crisis of the late 19th century affected Clos Charlé as it did all French vineyards, requiring complete replanting on American rootstock. The recovery period provided opportunities for improvements in vineyard layout and variety selection, contributing to the modern vineyard structure that exists today.

Post-World War II developments in Burgundy, including the rise of domaine bottling and increased quality focus, benefited Clos Charlé as producers began emphasizing terroir expression and reduced yields. This evolution coincided with growing international recognition of Côte Chalonnaise wines, bringing increased attention to exceptional sites like Clos Charlé.

## Aging Potential & Quality Level

Clos Charlé Premier Cru demonstrates exceptional aging potential, with the finest examples capable of developing for 15 to 25 years or more. The wines' natural structure, including high acidity, well-integrated tannins, and mineral complexity, provides the foundation for extended cellaring and continued improvement.

Young wines from Clos Charlé typically show immediate appeal but benefit significantly from 3 to 5 years of bottle aging to integrate their various components fully. During this initial aging period, the wines develop greater aromatic complexity while maintaining their fresh fruit character.

The optimal drinking window for most Clos Charlé wines begins around 7 to 10 years after vintage and can extend for another decade or more in favorable storage conditions. Exceptional vintages may require even longer aging to reach their peak, while challenging vintages may mature more quickly but still offer rewarding drinking experiences.

Quality consistency in Clos Charlé ranks among the highest in Givry, with even lesser vintages producing wines of notable character and aging potential. The climat's favorable exposure and excellent drainage help ensure reliable ripening across different weather patterns, contributing to this consistency.

The vineyard's track record across multiple decades demonstrates its ability to produce wines that age gracefully while maintaining their essential character. Mature examples show the development of complex secondary and tertiary aromas while preserving the mineral backbone and structural integrity that define great Burgundy.

Vintage variation in Clos Charlé reflects broader Burgundian patterns, with warm years producing more powerful, structured wines and cooler vintages emphasizing elegance and aromatic complexity. However, the climat's favorable terroir tends to moderate extreme vintage effects, producing successful wines across a wide range of climatic conditions.

The investment potential of Clos Charlé wines continues to grow as collectors and enthusiasts recognize the exceptional value offered by top-quality Côte Chalonnaise Premier Crus. While prices remain significantly below comparable Côte d'Or sites, the quality trajectory and aging potential suggest continued appreciation for the finest examples from this distinguished climat.
