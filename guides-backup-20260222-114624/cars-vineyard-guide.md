# Cars

## Introduction

Cars is one of the officially recognized menzioni geografiche aggiuntive (MGAs) in the Barbaresco DOCG, located within the township of Barbaresco itself. The MGA system, introduced in 2007, represents a significant milestone in the formal recognition of Barbaresco's single-vineyard sites—notably preceding Barolo's similar classification by three years. This official list of what are colloquially referred to as crus has, according to Alessandro Masnaghetti's comprehensive work on Barbaresco's vineyard geography, "sensibly retained almost all the historically known vineyards, rarely absorbing them in more famous vineyard names for commercial reasons."

While Cars does not feature among the most celebrated and historically documented sites in Barbaresco—such as Asili, Rabajà, Montestefano, or Montefico—its inclusion in the official MGA list confirms its distinctive character and contribution to the denomination's remarkable diversity of terroir expression.

## Understanding Barbaresco's Terroir Complexity

To appreciate Cars within its proper context requires understanding the fundamental principles that govern quality and distinction in Barbaresco. The production zone lies northeast and east of Alba, covering just one-third the area of Barolo across the townships of Barbaresco, Neive, and Treiso. Despite its compact size—733 hectares as of 2019—Barbaresco displays remarkable terroir variation that has established it as a wine of "striking qualities of elegance and aromatic intensity."

Contemporary terroir research, as conducted by Dr. Gérard Seguin at the University of Bordeaux and others, has demonstrated that while a wide range of soil types can produce high-quality wines, certain characteristics prove most influential. The most important are those governing water supply to the vine, followed by factors influencing soil and ambient temperatures. In Barbaresco, these variables interact with the Nebbiolo grape's exacting requirements to produce wines of distinct personality from site to site.

The interplay between soil composition, elevation, aspect, and microclimate creates what researchers describe as a "hierarchical organization" of terroir factors. Climate operates at the regional level, establishing Barbaresco's fundamental character. Within this framework, topography and soil characteristics create meso-climates and micro-climates that define individual vineyard sites. The choice of plant material (rootstock and vine variety) and management practices—from vineyard floor management to training systems and canopy work—allow producers to optimize what might be described as "managing terroir" within each particular environment.

## The Character of Barbaresco Nebbiolo

Wines from Barbaresco, regardless of specific MGA, share fundamental characteristics derived from Nebbiolo grown in this particular zone. Long considered "very much the junior of Barolo," Barbaresco emerged from that shadow in the 1960s through the efforts of producers like Angelo Gaja and Bruno Giacosa, who demonstrated the wine's distinctive qualities.

The perception of Barbaresco as lighter-bodied than Barolo stems partly from aging requirements—26 months minimum versus Barolo's 38, with 50 months for Riserva versus 62—but this has unfortunately "led to the false assumption that Barbaresco is less ageworthy, which in recent past has unfortunately spurred some producers to attempt to emulate Barolo, ignoring Barbaresco's unique style."

Modern Barbaresco, while requiring minimum alcoholic strength of 12.5% and easily reaching 13.5%, possesses the tannins and acidity that mark the Nebbiolo grape. Young Barbaresco is "by no means an inevitably pleasurable glass of wine," demanding patience from those who seek immediate gratification. The work of quality-focused producers, including the renowned Produttori del Barbaresco cooperative and numerous smaller estates converting to organic and biodynamic viticulture, has established a range of styles—from those using traditional long macerations (often up to 40 days) and large oak casks to those favoring shorter extractions and French barriques.

The signature aromatics include cherry, violets, and with age, more complex notes of iron, tar, and orange peel. These wines demonstrate considerable aging potential, producing "highly original, long-lived wines with a muscular structure" that belie any simplistic characterization of Barbaresco as merely a lighter alternative to Barolo.

## The Evolution of Single-Vineyard Recognition

Understanding Cars requires appreciating the relatively recent phenomenon of single-vineyard bottlings in Barbaresco. The first, Prunotto's Barbaresco Montestefano, appeared only in 1961, and "there is a less firmly established written record of cru designation here than in Barolo." Lorenzo Fantini's late 19th-century monograph on Piedmontese viticulture indicated "very few 'choice positions' in Barbaresco (and none whatsoever in Neive)." Serious attempts to list and rate the finest positions date only from the 1960s with Luigi Veronelli and the 1970s with Renato Ratti.

Over time, négociants' willingness to pay premium prices for grapes from certain vineyards established consensus about elite sites. Some vineyards—San Lorenzo, Tildin, and Martinenga in Barbaresco, Santo Stefano in Neive—gained prestige through "the dedicated work and exacting standards of producers such as Gaja, Giacosa, and Alberto di Gresy" rather than through centuries-old reputation.

The 2007 MGA system formalized this evolution, creating an official framework that balances historical recognition with practical viticultural reality. Sites like Cars benefit from this systematic approach to classification, which acknowledges the genuine diversity of Barbaresco's landscape while maintaining standards that give the denomination meaning and credibility.

## Conclusion

Cars represents one thread in Barbaresco's complex tapestry of terroir. While it may lack the extensive documentation and commercial prominence of the denomination's most celebrated sites, its recognition as an MGA confirms its legitimate place within a region that has proven capable of producing wines of extraordinary elegance and longevity. As Barbaresco continues its evolution—with vineyard area expanding and quality consciousness deepening—the full potential of sites like Cars awaits fuller exploration and documentation by committed producers and the wine enthusiasts who seek to understand this remarkable denomination's complete expression.
