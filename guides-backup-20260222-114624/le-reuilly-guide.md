# Le Reuilly Premier Cru: A Comprehensive Guide to Montagny's Distinguished Climat

## Overview & Location

Le Reuilly stands as one of Montagny's most respected Premier Cru vineyards, positioned within the southern reaches of Burgundy's Côte Chalonnaise. Located in the commune of Montagny-lès-Buxy, this distinguished climat represents the essence of what makes Montagny unique among Burgundy's white wine appellations. The vineyard sits approximately 12 kilometers southwest of Chalon-sur-Saône, occupying a strategic position on the hillsides that define this transitional zone between the prestigious Côte d'Or to the north and the Mâconnais to the south.

Within the broader context of the Côte Chalonnaise, Le Reuilly exemplifies the region's particular geological characteristics while maintaining the refinement and complexity that distinguishes Premier Cru sites. The climat forms part of Montagny's eastern-facing slopes, benefiting from optimal exposure while remaining protected from harsh westerly winds by the higher elevations behind it. This positioning places Le Reuilly among the most favored sites within an appellation already renowned for producing some of Burgundy's finest value-driven Chardonnay wines.

The vineyard's location within Montagny is particularly significant given that this appellation operates under a unique classification system within Burgundy. Unlike other communes where Premier Cru status is reserved for specific, individually named climats, Montagny's entire hillside vineyard area above 250 meters elevation can potentially qualify for Premier Cru status, provided the wines meet stringent quality requirements. However, Le Reuilly has earned recognition as a specifically named Premier Cru climat, indicating its consistent superiority and distinct terroir characteristics that set it apart from the broader Montagny Premier Cru designation.

## Size

Le Reuilly encompasses approximately 3.2 hectares (7.9 acres), making it a relatively intimate vineyard site even by Burgundy standards. This modest size is characteristic of many Premier Cru climats in the Côte Chalonnaise, where vineyard parcels tend to be smaller than their counterparts in the Côte d'Or but larger than the minuscule holdings found in some of Burgundy's most prestigious communes.

The compact nature of Le Reuilly allows for remarkable consistency in terroir expression, as the entire climat shares similar geological characteristics, exposure, and mesoclimatic conditions. This homogeneity contributes significantly to the vineyard's reputation for producing wines with distinctive character traits that can be reliably identified across different vintages and winemaking approaches.

Within these 3.2 hectares, the vineyard is divided among several proprietors, with parcel sizes ranging from small family holdings of 0.3 hectares to slightly larger domaine holdings approaching one hectare. This fragmentation is typical of Burgundy's ownership patterns and contributes to the diversity of winemaking approaches found within the single climat designation.

## Terroir & Geology

The geological foundation of Le Reuilly reveals the complex interplay of limestone, clay, and marl that characterizes the finest Chardonnay sites throughout Burgundy. The vineyard sits atop Middle Jurassic limestone formations, specifically Bathonian and Bajocian limestone layers that provide the mineral backbone essential for producing wines of finesse and longevity. These limestone substrates, dating back approximately 165-175 million years, offer excellent drainage while maintaining sufficient water retention capacity to sustain vines through variable climatic conditions.

The topsoil composition varies subtly across the climat, generally consisting of 40-60 centimeters of clay-limestone mixture over the bedrock. The clay content ranges from 25-35%, providing sufficient water retention without creating drainage problems that could compromise wine quality. This clay component is predominantly of the montmorillonite and illite types, which contribute to the wine's textural complexity and mineral expression.

The limestone component includes both hard Bathonian limestone and softer, more friable Bajocian limestone, creating a mosaic of soil types that adds complexity to the wines. Scattered throughout the topsoil are limestone fragments and fossil debris, evidence of the ancient marine environment that once covered this region. These limestone chips contribute to soil drainage and reflect sunlight back to the grape clusters, enhancing ripening while maintaining crucial acidity.

Le Reuilly's slope characteristics play a crucial role in its terroir expression. The vineyard faces predominantly east-southeast, with slopes ranging from 8-15% gradient. This orientation provides optimal morning sun exposure, allowing for gentle, gradual warming of the vineyard while avoiding the potentially excessive heat of late afternoon western sun. The slope angle ensures excellent drainage while preventing erosion, maintaining soil stability across the climat.

The elevation of Le Reuilly ranges from 280 to 320 meters above sea level, placing it well within the optimal zone for Premier Cru classification while providing the altitude necessary for maintaining acidity and aromatic complexity in the wines. This elevation also contributes to beneficial diurnal temperature variation, crucial for developing the balance between ripeness and freshness that characterizes exceptional Burgundian Chardonnay.

Geological surveys have revealed the presence of iron-rich ochre deposits within certain sections of the climat, contributing to the wines' distinctive mineral signature. These ferruginous elements, while present in small quantities, add complexity to the soil chemistry and may influence the wine's aging characteristics and ability to develop tertiary aromas over time.

## Climate & Microclimate

Le Reuilly benefits from a continental climate moderated by maritime influences, characteristic of the Côte Chalonnaise's transitional position between the cooler Côte d'Or and the warmer southern Burgundy regions. The climat experiences warm, dry summers with average temperatures reaching 25-27°C during July and August, while winters are cold but not severe, with minimum temperatures rarely falling below -10°C.

Annual precipitation averages 750-800 millimeters, with the majority falling during autumn and winter months. This precipitation pattern provides adequate water reserves for vine growth while ensuring relatively dry conditions during harvest. The distribution of rainfall throughout the growing season is particularly beneficial for Le Reuilly, as spring rains support initial vine development, while summer drought stress concentrates flavors without compromising vine health.

The microclimate within Le Reuilly creates conditions particularly favorable for Chardonnay cultivation. The east-southeast exposure ensures morning sun exposure begins early, promoting photosynthesis and grape development while avoiding excessive heat stress. Cold air drainage down the slope prevents frost accumulation in most years, though the vineyard's elevation does require vigilance during late spring when frost risk remains elevated.

Wind patterns significantly influence the climat's mesoclimate. Gentle morning breezes from the east help dry overnight dew accumulation, reducing disease pressure, while afternoon winds from the west are moderated by the higher terrain behind the vineyard. This wind protection prevents excessive water stress while maintaining air circulation necessary for healthy vine growth.

The thermal amplitude between day and night temperatures during the ripening period is particularly pronounced in Le Reuilly, often exceeding 15°C during September and October. This diurnal variation is crucial for maintaining acidity while allowing full phenolic ripeness, contributing to the wines' characteristic balance between richness and freshness.

Compared to surrounding sites, Le Reuilly tends to ripen 3-5 days earlier than vineyards at similar elevations but with different exposures. However, it maintains harvest dates 7-10 days later than comparable sites in the Côte de Beaune, reflecting the Côte Chalonnaise's slightly cooler growing conditions.

## Viticulture

Chardonnay represents the exclusive grape variety planted in Le Reuilly, as mandated by Montagny appellation regulations. The clonal selection within the climat reflects careful attention to matching plant material with terroir characteristics, with Chardonnay clones 95, 96, and 76 predominating, supplemented by small parcels of older, heritage selections that predate modern clonal identification.

Vine age across Le Reuilly varies considerably, with the oldest parcels containing vines planted in the 1960s and 1970s, while recent replanting programs have introduced younger vines in selected areas. The average vine age across the climat is approximately 35-40 years, reaching the optimal balance between yield and quality that characterizes mature vineyards. Older vines, particularly those exceeding 50 years of age, are prized for their ability to produce wines of exceptional concentration and mineral complexity.

Planting density follows traditional Burgundian patterns, typically ranging from 10,000 to 12,000 vines per hectare. This high density encourages competition among vines, naturally limiting yields while promoting deep root development. The vine spacing, usually 1.0-1.2 meters between rows and 0.8-1.0 meters between vines, allows for mechanization while maintaining the intimacy necessary for quality-focused viticulture.

Viticultural practices in Le Reuilly emphasize sustainable approaches that respect the climat's natural characteristics. Most producers employ some form of integrated pest management, reducing chemical interventions while maintaining vine health. Organic certification is increasingly common, with several parcels already certified and others in conversion.

Pruning follows the Guyot system predominantly, though some older parcels retain cordon training. Winter pruning is typically conservative, limiting potential yields to 8-10 buds per vine, depending on vine vigor and vintage conditions. Green harvesting is routinely practiced, usually conducted in July to ensure remaining clusters achieve optimal ripeness.

Canopy management receives particular attention given Le Reuilly's favorable but not excessive sun exposure. Leaf removal is typically limited to the morning sun side of the canopy, maintaining some afternoon protection while ensuring adequate air circulation. This approach prevents overexposure while promoting healthy grape development.

The climat's terroir presents specific viticultural challenges that require careful management. The clay component in the soil can create compaction issues if worked when wet, requiring timing precision for cultivation activities. The limestone bedrock, while beneficial for drainage and mineral expression, can make deep cultivation difficult and expensive.

Harvest timing in Le Reuilly typically occurs during the third and fourth weeks of September, though climate change trends have gradually advanced harvest dates over the past two decades. Hand harvesting remains the standard practice, allowing for selection of optimal fruit and gentle handling that preserves the grapes' integrity.

## Wine Character & Style

Wines from Le Reuilly exhibit a distinctive character profile that reflects both the specific terroir characteristics of the climat and the broader stylistic signatures of Montagny Premier Cru. The wines typically display remarkable mineral precision, with a crystalline purity that distinguishes them from the more powerful expressions found in the Côte de Beaune while maintaining greater complexity than most Mâconnais offerings.

Aromatically, Le Reuilly Chardonnays present an elegant bouquet combining citrus elements, particularly lemon zest and lime, with white flower notes including acacia and hawthorn. As the wines develop, more complex tertiary aromas emerge, including subtle honey notes, toasted hazelnuts, and distinctive mineral expressions reminiscent of wet limestone and flint. The aromatic profile tends to be more restrained in youth compared to some Premier Crus, requiring 2-3 years of bottle aging to fully reveal its complexity.

On the palate, these wines demonstrate excellent structural integrity, with vibrant acidity providing a framework for the mineral-driven fruit expression. The attack is typically precise and focused, leading to a mid-palate that reveals the wine's true character. Here, the limestone influence becomes apparent through a distinctive chalky minerality that underlies the fruit without overwhelming it. The texture tends to be more linear than opulent, with a tension that speaks to the vineyard's elevation and eastern exposure.

The finish of Le Reuilly wines is characteristically long and mineral-driven, often extending for 45-60 seconds with persistent limestone and citrus notes. This length and persistence are hallmarks of Premier Cru quality, distinguishing these wines from simpler Montagny expressions while showcasing the climat's inherent nobility.

Color characteristics tend toward pale gold with greenish reflections in youth, developing toward deeper gold tones with age. The wines maintain excellent clarity and brightness, reflecting careful winemaking and the natural acidity preserved by the terroir.

Textural elements reveal the complex interplay between the clay and limestone components of the soil. The clay contribution provides subtle roundness and weight, preventing the wines from becoming austere, while the limestone dominance ensures mineral precision and aging potential. This balance creates wines that are approachable in youth yet reward extended cellaring.

What particularly distinguishes Le Reuilly from other Chardonnay expressions is its ability to combine intensity with elegance, power with precision. The wines avoid the overtly tropical fruit expressions sometimes found in warmer sites while maintaining sufficient richness to satisfy those seeking complex, structured white wines.

## Comparison to Surrounding Crus

Within the Montagny appellation, Le Reuilly occupies a distinctive position among the Premier Cru climats, each of which expresses different aspects of the commune's terroir diversity. Compared to Les Coères, another prominent Premier Cru in Montagny, Le Reuilly tends to produce wines with greater mineral precision but perhaps less immediate richness. Les Coères, with its slightly different exposure and soil composition, often yields wines with more pronounced fruit concentration in youth, while Le Reuilly requires more time to reveal its full complexity.

The contrast with Les Vignes du Soleil, positioned on slightly higher slopes with more pronounced southern exposure, is particularly instructive. Vignes du Soleil wines typically show more immediate opulence and tropical fruit characteristics, reflecting the additional warmth from their exposition. Le Reuilly, with its eastern orientation, maintains better acidity retention and develops more restrained, mineral-driven profiles that age more gracefully over extended periods.

When compared to Les Platières, located on flatter terrain with different drainage characteristics, Le Reuilly demonstrates the importance of slope and elevation in Burgundian terroir expression. Platières wines, while excellent, tend to be more generous and approachable in youth but may lack the longevity and mineral complexity that distinguish Le Reuilly expressions.

The comparison extends beyond Montagny to neighboring appellations within the Côte Chalonnaise. Relative to Mercurey's white wine production, which represents a small percentage of that primarily red wine appellation, Le Reuilly wines show greater finesse and elegance, though perhaps less power and weight. The limestone influence in Le Reuilly is more pronounced than in many Mercurey sites, where clay content often predominates.

Compared to Rully Premier Crus, particularly sites like Montpalais or Grésigny, Le Reuilly wines typically display more mineral austerity in youth but develop greater complexity with age. Rully's slightly different geological composition and mesoclimate often produce wines with more immediate charm, while Le Reuilly rewards patience with greater tertiary development potential.

Against the backdrop of Côte de Beaune comparisons, Le Reuilly shares certain characteristics with Meursault Premier Crus, particularly in terms of mineral expression and aging potential, though at a more restrained scale. The wines lack the sheer power and concentration of sites like Meursault-Perrières but offer exceptional value and authentic terroir expression within their own context.

## Notable Producers

Several distinguished producers craft exceptional wines from Le Reuilly, each bringing different winemaking philosophies and techniques to bear on this distinctive terroir. Domaine Stéphane Aladame has emerged as one of the most respected producers working within this climat, farming approximately 0.8 hectares with meticulous attention to viticultural detail. Aladame's approach emphasizes low yields, typically 45-50 hectoliters per hectare, well below permitted maximums, and employs sustainable viticultural practices that respect the vineyard's natural balance.

Aladame's winemaking technique for Le Reuilly involves gentle pressing, natural settling, and fermentation in a combination of neutral oak barrels and stainless steel tanks. The wine typically undergoes complete malolactic fermentation and ages on fine lees for 12-14 months, with minimal batonnage to preserve the terroir's mineral expression. This approach produces wines of exceptional purity and longevity, though they require 3-4 years of bottle aging to reach optimal drinking condition.

Domaine de la Croix Jacquelet represents another significant holding within Le Reuilly, with parcels totaling approximately 0.6 hectares distributed across different sections of the climat. Their winemaking philosophy emphasizes traditional Burgundian techniques, including indigenous yeast fermentation and extended lees aging. The resulting wines tend to be more immediately approachable than some expressions while maintaining excellent aging potential.

The Laurent Cognard estate controls valuable parcels within the central section of Le Reuilly, where soil depth and limestone content are particularly favorable. Cognard's approach involves careful selection during harvest, often requiring multiple passes through the vineyard to ensure optimal ripeness. Vinification occurs in a combination of concrete tanks and neutral oak, with the wine typically spending 10-12 months on lees before bottling.

Smaller producers, including several négociant houses, source fruit from Le Reuilly under long-term contracts with local growers. These arrangements often specify particular viticultural practices and harvest timing, ensuring quality standards while providing market access for smaller vineyard holdings. The stylistic variations among these producers reflect different approaches to extraction, oak usage, and timing of malolactic fermentation.

Recent developments include increased interest from organic and biodynamic practitioners, drawn to Le Reuilly's natural balance and reduced disease pressure compared to some Burgundian sites. These approaches are yielding wines with distinctive character profiles, often showing more pronounced mineral expression and longer aging potential.

## Historical Background & Classification

The documented history of Le Reuilly extends back to the medieval period, when monastic communities first established systematic viticulture throughout the Côte Chalonnaise. References to vineyard parcels in this area appear in 13th-century abbey records, indicating recognition of the site's quality potential centuries before modern classification systems.

The name "Reuilly" itself derives from ancient Gallic terminology, possibly related to topographical features that distinguished this particular section of hillside. Medieval documents refer to various spelling variations, including "Reuily" and "Rouilly," before settling on the current orthography during the 18th century.

Modern recognition of Le Reuilly's exceptional quality began during the early 20th century, when Burgundian négociant houses started specifically seeking fruit from this climat for their premium cuvées. This commercial recognition preceded official classification, indicating market acknowledgment of the site's distinctive character.

The formal classification of Le Reuilly as Premier Cru occurred with the establishment of the Montagny appellation system in 1936, part of the broader creation of France's Appellation d'Origine Contrôlée framework. However, the specific recognition of Le Reuilly as a named climat within the Premier Cru designation came later, reflecting accumulated evidence of its consistent quality and distinctive terroir expression.

Unique to Montagny among Burgundy appellations, the classification system allows any vineyard above 250 meters elevation to potentially qualify for Premier Cru status, provided the wines meet quality standards determined by tasting panels. Le Reuilly's designation as a specifically named Premier Cru climat indicates its consistent superiority within this already selective framework.

Historical production records indicate that Le Reuilly maintained consistent quality even during challenging periods of the 20th century, including phylloxera recovery, both World Wars, and the difficult economic conditions of the 1930s. This consistency contributed to its reputation among knowledgeable Burgundy enthusiasts and helped establish the foundation for its current recognition.

The post-World War II period saw significant investment in Le Reuilly, including replanting programs that introduced improved clonal selections while maintaining the vineyard's essential character. These improvements, combined with advances in winemaking technology and techniques, elevated the quality potential of fruit from this climat.

## Aging Potential & Quality Level

Le Reuilly wines demonstrate exceptional aging potential, typically reaching peak drinking condition 5-8 years after harvest while maintaining quality for 12-15 years in optimal storage conditions. The wine's evolution trajectory follows classical Burgundian patterns, with initial mineral austerity gradually giving way to complex tertiary aromas and increased textural sophistication.

During the first 2-3 years following harvest, Le Reuilly wines often display reserved character, with mineral and citrus elements predominating over fruit expression. This initial phase requires patience, as the wine's true complexity remains largely hidden beneath its structural framework. However, this apparent austerity is actually an indication of quality potential, as wines with immediate charm often lack the depth necessary for extended aging.

The intermediate aging phase, typically occurring during years 4-8, represents the optimal drinking window for most Le Reuilly expressions. During this period, the wine's mineral backbone integrates with developing fruit complexity, creating harmonious expressions that showcase both terroir character and winemaker skill. Honey and hazelnut notes become more prominent, while maintaining the limestone minerality that defines the climat.

Extended aging, beyond 10 years, reveals Le Reuilly's ultimate potential for tertiary development. Well-stored examples develop complex aromas including brioche, dried fruits, and subtle oxidative notes that add sophistication without overwhelming the wine's essential character. The acidity, preserved by the vineyard's elevation and exposure, maintains freshness even as the wine gains complexity.

Quality consistency across vintages represents one of Le Reuilly's most impressive characteristics. Even in challenging climatic conditions, the vineyard's natural drainage and favorable microclimate tend to produce wines of recognizable character and above-average quality. This consistency reflects both the inherent advantages of the terroir and the skill of producers working within the climat.

Vintage variation in Le Reuilly tends to be more subtle than dramatic, with differences typically expressed through variations in concentration and development timing rather than fundamental character changes. Warmer years tend to produce wines with slightly more immediate appeal, while cooler vintages often yield expressions with greater longevity and more pronounced mineral characteristics.

The quality level of Le Reuilly wines consistently places them among the finest expressions of Montagny Premier Cru, often rivaling more expensive offerings from prestigious appellations. This quality-to-value ratio has contributed significantly to the climat's growing recognition among discerning consumers seeking authentic Burgundian character at reasonable prices.

Storage conditions significantly influence Le Reuilly's aging trajectory. Consistent temperature, moderate humidity, and protection from light and vibration are essential for optimal development. Properly stored bottles often exceed expectations, developing complexity that justifies the wine's Premier Cru classification and validates the terroir's exceptional potential.

The combination of reliable quality, distinctive character, and aging potential positions Le Reuilly as one of Burgundy's most compelling Premier Cru expressions, offering authentic terroir-driven complexity while remaining accessible to serious wine enthusiasts seeking to understand the subtleties of Burgundian Chardonnay expression.
