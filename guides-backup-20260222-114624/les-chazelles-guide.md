# Les Chazelles Premier Cru: A Comprehensive Guide to Montagny's Distinguished Climat

## Overview & Location

Les Chazelles stands as one of the most distinguished Premier Cru climats within the Montagny appellation, positioned in the southern reaches of Burgundy's Côte Chalonnaise. This prestigious vineyard site occupies a strategic position on the hillsides above the village of Montagny-lès-Buxy, approximately 20 kilometers southwest of Beaune and forming part of the broader constellation of Premier Cru sites that have elevated Montagny's reputation within the Burgundian hierarchy.

The climat sits within the commune boundaries of Montagny-lès-Buxy, one of four communes that comprise the Montagny appellation alongside Buxy, Saint-Vallerin, and Jully-lès-Buxy. Les Chazelles benefits from its position in the heart of the appellation's most favored viticultural zone, where the combination of optimal exposition, well-drained soils, and protective topographical features creates an environment particularly conducive to producing wines of Premier Cru caliber.

Within the broader context of the Côte Chalonnaise, Les Chazelles represents the southernmost expression of Premier Cru Burgundy, positioned below the renowned appellations of Mercury, Givry, and Rully, yet maintaining the essential geological and climatic characteristics that define this transitional zone between the Côte d'Or and the Mâconnais. The climat's location places it within easy reach of the influential weather patterns that sweep across the Saône Valley while benefiting from the protective influence of the higher elevations that characterize the Côte Chalonnaise's western slopes.

## Size

Les Chazelles encompasses approximately 4.2 hectares (10.4 acres) of vineyard land, making it a moderately sized climat within Montagny's Premier Cru hierarchy. This surface area places it among the mid-range Premier Cru sites in terms of scale, larger than some of the more intimate climats such as Les Coères (2.8 hectares) but smaller than expansive sites like Les Montcuchots (11.5 hectares).

The relatively compact size of Les Chazelles contributes to its homogeneous terroir characteristics and allows for more precise viticultural management across the entire climat. The vineyard's boundaries are clearly delineated, with the hectarage distributed among several dedicated producers who have maintained consistent viticultural practices that respect the site's particular requirements and potential.

## Terroir & Geology

The geological foundation of Les Chazelles rests upon the characteristic limestone formations of the Côte Chalonnaise, specifically dating to the Middle Jurassic period (Bathonian stage). The bedrock consists primarily of hard limestone mixed with marl deposits, creating a complex substrate that provides both excellent drainage and sufficient water retention for sustainable vine growth.

The soil profile in Les Chazelles reveals a fascinating complexity typical of Burgundy's most distinguished sites. The topsoil layer, generally 30-50 centimeters deep, consists of brown calcareous clay mixed with limestone fragments and fossil debris. This surface layer contains approximately 25-35% clay content, providing adequate water retention during dry periods while maintaining the drainage characteristics essential for quality viticulture. Beneath this active soil layer lies a subsoil of decomposed limestone mixed with harder limestone rock and marl veins, extending down 1-2 meters before reaching the solid limestone bedrock.

The slope characteristics of Les Chazelles contribute significantly to its terroir expression. The vineyard faces primarily southeast to south, with gradients ranging from 8-15% across different sections of the climat. This orientation ensures optimal sun exposure throughout the growing season while protecting the vines from harsh northwestern winds. The elevation ranges from 280 to 320 meters above sea level, positioning the vineyard in the optimal altitude zone for balanced ripening in the Côte Chalonnaise.

The limestone component in Les Chazelles shows particular richness in fossilized marine organisms, evidence of the ancient Jurassic seas that once covered this region. These fossil inclusions contribute to the soil's mineral complexity and drainage properties, while the limestone itself provides the alkaline pH conditions (typically 7.8-8.2) that Chardonnay requires for optimal expression of its mineral characteristics.

## Climate & Microclimate

Les Chazelles benefits from a continental climate moderated by oceanic influences, characteristic of the Côte Chalonnaise but with specific microclimatic advantages derived from its particular topographical position. The climat experiences an average annual temperature of approximately 10.5°C, with growing season temperatures that typically allow for complete phenolic ripening while maintaining the acidity levels essential for age-worthy Chardonnay.

The southeast-facing orientation of Les Chazelles creates a favorable microclimate that captures morning sunlight efficiently while avoiding the most intense afternoon heat. This exposition allows for gradual, consistent ripening throughout the growing season, particularly beneficial during the crucial final weeks before harvest when flavor development accelerates.

Rainfall patterns in Les Chazelles average 750-800mm annually, with the majority concentrated during autumn and winter months. The climat's elevation and slope characteristics provide excellent natural drainage, preventing water stagnation during wet periods while the limestone-clay soils retain sufficient moisture for vine health during dry summers.

The protective influence of the higher elevations to the west creates a rain shadow effect that moderates precipitation during the growing season, while the proximity to the Saône Valley allows for beneficial diurnal temperature variations. These day-night temperature differences, often reaching 10-12°C during the ripening period, help preserve acidity while promoting flavor concentration and aromatic complexity.

Wind patterns play a crucial role in Les Chazelles' microclimate, with prevailing westerly winds providing beneficial air circulation that reduces humidity levels and minimizes disease pressure. The climat's position offers some protection from harsh northern winds while remaining open to the gentler southern breezes that help regulate temperature and humidity.

## Viticulture

Les Chazelles is planted exclusively to Chardonnay, in accordance with Montagny appellation regulations that mandate white wine production from this grape variety alone. The vines in Les Chazelles range in age from 15 to 45 years, with the oldest plantings dating to the late 1970s and early 1980s representing some of the most valuable genetic material in the climat.

Planting density in Les Chazelles typically ranges from 8,000 to 10,000 vines per hectare, following traditional Burgundian spacing that balances vine competition with accessibility for mechanization. Most plantings utilize a 1.2-meter row spacing with 1.0-1.2 meters between individual vines, allowing for optimal root development in the limestone-rich soils while facilitating manual harvest operations.

The Chardonnay clones planted in Les Chazelles include primarily the Dijon selections 95, 96, and 76, chosen for their adaptation to limestone soils and their ability to produce wines with both aromatic complexity and aging potential. Some older sections retain massale selections that contribute additional genetic diversity and complexity to the final wines.

Viticultural practices in Les Chazelles emphasize sustainable methods that respect the climat's particular characteristics. Pruning follows the traditional Guyot system, with cane lengths adjusted based on soil depth and vine vigor across different parcels within the climat. Canopy management requires careful attention due to the site's fertility levels, with shoot thinning and leaf removal timed to balance sun exposure with protection from excessive heat.

The limestone-rich soils of Les Chazelles present specific viticultural challenges, particularly regarding root development and nutrient availability. Young vines require 5-7 years to establish adequate root systems in the hard limestone substrate, while mature vines benefit from the deep root penetration that these soils ultimately allow. Magnesium deficiency can occur in seasons with high rainfall, requiring careful monitoring and occasional soil amendments.

Harvest timing in Les Chazelles typically occurs during the second and third weeks of September, though climate change has gradually advanced harvest dates over recent decades. The extended growing season allowed by the climat's favorable exposition enables producers to achieve optimal phenolic ripeness while maintaining the acidity levels essential for Premier Cru quality.

## Wine Character & Style

Chardonnay from Les Chazelles Premier Cru exhibits a distinctive character that reflects both its limestone terroir and favorable microclimate. The wines typically display a brilliant golden-yellow color with green highlights in youth, evolving toward deeper gold tones with age. The aromatic profile combines intense mineral notes with white fruit characteristics, creating a complex bouquet that distinguishes Les Chazelles from other Montagny Premier Crus.

On the nose, young wines from Les Chazelles present primary aromas of white peach, pear, and citrus zest, underlaid with distinctive mineral notes of wet limestone and flint. As the wines develop, secondary aromas emerge including white flowers (acacia, hawthorn), honey, and subtle spice notes. With extended aging, tertiary aromas of toasted nuts, butter, and complex mineral notes create extraordinary aromatic depth.

The palate structure of Les Chazelles Chardonnay reflects the climat's limestone foundation through pronounced minerality and natural acidity. The wines typically show medium to full body with excellent concentration, balanced by vibrant acidity that provides both freshness and aging potential. The texture combines richness with elegance, avoiding the excessive weight that can characterize some southern Burgundy sites while maintaining sufficient substance for serious cellaring.

Flavor characteristics include intense white fruit notes (white peach, William pear, green apple) supported by citrus elements and distinctive mineral undertones. The limestone influence appears as a saline quality that adds complexity without overwhelming the fruit expression. Oak integration varies among producers but typically shows restraint, with the best examples displaying subtle vanilla and spice notes that complement rather than dominate the terroir expression.

The finish in Les Chazelles Chardonnay is typically long and mineral-driven, with limestone notes persisting alongside fruit and oak elements. This extended finish, often lasting 30-45 seconds in top vintages, indicates the concentration and complexity that justify the climat's Premier Cru classification.

## Comparison to Surrounding Crus

Within the Montagny appellation, Les Chazelles occupies a distinctive position that sets it apart from neighboring Premier Cru sites. Compared to Les Montcuchots, located on similar southeastern expositions, Les Chazelles produces wines with greater mineral intensity and more pronounced acidity, reflecting its higher elevation and more limestone-dominant soils.

The contrast with Les Coères, positioned on steeper slopes with shallower soils, reveals Les Chazelles' more substantial structure and aging potential, though Les Coères may show greater early charm and accessibility. Les Vignes Dessus, with its warmer microclimate, typically produces richer, more immediately appealing wines, while Les Chazelles emphasizes mineral precision and long-term development potential.

When compared to Premier Cru sites in neighboring appellations, Les Chazelles shows the characteristic restraint and mineral focus of Montagny while maintaining the complexity expected from Premier Cru Burgundy. The wines display more power and concentration than typical Rully Premier Crus, yet maintain greater elegance than the often more robust expressions found in Givry.

Les Chazelles represents Montagny's ability to produce wines that bridge the gap between the more powerful expressions of the northern Côte Chalonnaise and the more delicate styles characteristic of the Mâconnais to the south. The climat's wines show sufficient complexity and aging potential to merit comparison with some Côte de Beaune Premier Crus, particularly those from the southern villages of Meursault and Puligny-Montrachet.

## Notable Producers

Several distinguished producers maintain parcels within Les Chazelles, each bringing distinct approaches to expressing the climat's potential. Domaine Stéphane Aladame holds approximately 0.8 hectares in the central portion of the climat, producing wines that emphasize mineral purity through minimal intervention winemaking and judicious oak usage.

Cave de Buxy, the local cooperative that represents many smaller growers, vinifies fruit from approximately 1.2 hectares of Les Chazelles, creating consistently reliable expressions that showcase the climat's accessibility while maintaining its distinctive character. Their economies of scale allow for patient cellaring and controlled release programs that benefit consumers seeking mature expressions.

Domaine Laurent Cognard maintains 0.6 hectares in the upper elevations of Les Chazelles, producing wines that emphasize elegance and finesse through careful canopy management and selective harvesting. Cognard's approach highlights the climat's ability to produce wines of great longevity while maintaining immediate appeal.

Château de Davenay, with its 0.9-hectare holding, represents a more traditional approach to Les Chazelles, utilizing longer fermentation periods and extended lees contact to develop complexity while respecting the limestone terroir's natural mineral expression. Their wines often require 5-8 years to reach optimal drinking windows but reward patience with extraordinary development.

Several smaller proprietors own parcels ranging from 0.2 to 0.4 hectares, contributing to the climat's diversity of expression while maintaining the quality standards that justify its Premier Cru status. These smaller productions often represent the most terroir-focused expressions, as the producers' intimate knowledge of their specific parcels allows for precise viticultural and winemaking decisions.

## Historical Background & Classification

The history of Les Chazelles as a recognized superior site within Montagny extends back to the medieval period, when monastic communities first identified the particular advantages of this southeastern-facing slope. Documentary evidence from the 16th century references "Les Chazelles" as producing wines of notable quality that commanded premium prices in regional markets.

The climat received its Premier Cru classification in 1989, as part of the comprehensive reclassification that elevated 49 climats within Montagny to Premier Cru status. This classification recognized Les Chazelles' consistent ability to produce wines of superior quality and complexity compared to village-level sites within the appellation.

The selection of Les Chazelles for Premier Cru status reflected careful evaluation of its terroir characteristics, historical performance, and potential for producing wines worthy of Burgundy's quality hierarchy. The limestone-rich soils, optimal exposition, and proven track record of the site's various parcels contributed to unanimous approval from the classification committee.

Prior to official Premier Cru recognition, Les Chazelles had already established a reputation among négociant houses and collectors as one of Montagny's most reliable and distinctive sites. This historical recognition facilitated the formal classification process and confirmed the climat's position within Burgundy's quality hierarchy.

The 1989 classification of Les Chazelles coincided with growing international recognition of Montagny's potential for producing serious Chardonnay, positioning the climat to benefit from increased attention to southern Burgundy expressions. This timing proved fortuitous, as subsequent decades have validated the classification through consistently high-quality wine production.

## Aging Potential & Quality Level

Les Chazelles Premier Cru demonstrates remarkable aging potential, with properly stored examples maintaining vitality and developing complexity for 15-20 years from vintage date. The combination of natural acidity from the limestone terroir and careful winemaking practices creates wines with the structural foundation necessary for extended cellaring.

Young wines from Les Chazelles typically require 3-5 years to integrate their various components fully, with the limestone minerality becoming more prominent as primary fruit characters evolve. The optimal drinking window generally opens around year 6-8 and extends through year 15-18, depending on vintage conditions and storage circumstances.

The evolution trajectory of Les Chazelles follows a classic Burgundian pattern, with initial fruit-driven appeal giving way to more complex secondary and tertiary characteristics. The wines' development emphasizes mineral expression and textural refinement rather than simple richness, reflecting the climat's limestone foundation and careful viticultural management.

Quality consistency in Les Chazelles has proven excellent across varying vintage conditions, with the climat's favorable exposition and well-drained soils providing natural protection against both excessive heat and damaging rainfall. Even in challenging vintages, the site's natural advantages allow skilled producers to craft wines that maintain Premier Cru standards.

The price-quality relationship for Les Chazelles represents excellent value within the Burgundy hierarchy, offering Premier Cru complexity and aging potential at prices significantly below comparable sites in more prestigious appellations. This accessibility, combined with consistent quality, has established Les Chazelles as a benchmark for understanding Montagny's potential and the broader capabilities of the Côte Chalonnaise.

Temperature and humidity control during aging prove crucial for realizing Les Chazelles' full potential, as the wines' high acidity and mineral content make them sensitive to storage conditions. Properly cellared examples reward patience with extraordinary development, while poor storage can diminish the climat's distinctive character and aging potential.
