# La Volta

## Introduction

La Volta is a Menzioni Geografiche Aggiuntive (MGA) within the Barolo DOCG, part of the official system of sub-zones introduced in 2010 to formally recognize and codify the region's most distinguished vineyard sites. The MGA system represents a significant evolution in Barolo's regulatory framework, bringing official recognition to sites that had long been valued in both written and oral traditions of the zone, with historical documentation dating back to Lorenzo Fantini in the late 19th century and continuing through modern cartographers such as Renato Ratti and Alessandro Masnaghetti.

The establishment of the MGA system formalized three types of geographical designations within Barolo: entire villages (such as La Morra), specified single vineyards (such as Bussia or Cannubi), and consolidated MGAs that incorporate multiple previously named vineyards into a single designation. Under current regulations, the word *vigna* (vineyard) can only appear as a labeling term if the name of the MGA is also stated, ensuring clarity and protecting the integrity of these official geographical indications.

## The MGA System Context

Understanding La Volta requires understanding the broader shift toward single-vineyard designation in Barolo. Historically, most top producers prided themselves on blending wines from multiple vineyards to achieve complexity—a practice that continues today with traditionalists like Bartolo Mascarello. Négociant houses, dealing in large quantities, necessarily blended wines from different provenances into a house Barolo, much like their counterparts in Burgundy. When skillfully executed, this approach created balanced and harmonious wines exemplifying Barolo's general characteristics.

However, certain privileged positions have long enjoyed greater prestige and produced more distinctive wines. Significant producers who championed single-vineyard wines in Barolo include Angelo Gaja and Bruno Giacosa, helping to establish the modern understanding that terroir specificity can express something that blending, despite its merits, cannot capture. The MGA system represents the official recognition of this philosophy, codifying distinctions that négociants had long acknowledged by paying higher prices for grapes and wines from particular sites.

## Barolo Terroir Fundamentals

The terroir factors that distinguish individual MGAs within Barolo operate at multiple scales. Contemporary terroir research, pioneered by Dr. Gérard Seguin at the University of Bordeaux, demonstrates that while soil characteristics influence grape quality, yield, and wine individuality, their effects are generally subsidiary to those of climate, vine variety, and vine management. Of the influential soil characteristics, the most important are those governing water supply to the vine, followed by those influencing temperatures in and above the soil.

Within Barolo, soil types vary considerably and contribute significantly to the character distinctions between MGAs. The calcareous clay of the Tortonian epoch, found prominently in the communes of La Morra and Barolo village, typically results in more perfumed, fruit-driven styles. These soils share characteristics with the best terroirs of Barbaresco. In contrast, the more compact Sant'Agata fossil marl, more prevalent in Monforte d'Alba and Serralunga d'Alba, tends to yield more structured, tannic wines requiring extended aging.

Elevation and aspect play crucial roles in determining microclimate, affecting ripening patterns, diurnal temperature variation, and ultimately the balance between fruit ripeness and the preservation of Nebbiolo's essential acidity and tannic structure. The subtle interplay between these factors—soil composition, drainage, elevation, aspect, and mesoclimate—creates the distinctive personality of each MGA.

## Wine Characteristics

Barolo, regardless of its specific MGA origin, must be produced from 100% Nebbiolo and meet minimum aging requirements of 38 months (of which 18 must be in oak) for standard releases, or 62 months for Riserva bottlings. These wines typically show medium ruby color with garnet tinges, even in youth, and are characterized by high acidity, firm tannins, and complex aromatics.

The Nebbiolo grape expresses itself through a distinctive aromatic profile that evolves considerably with age. Young Barolo often displays floral notes of rose and violet alongside red fruit—cherry, raspberry, and strawberry. With maturation, the wines develop tertiary characteristics including tar, leather, tobacco, dried herbs, truffle, and autumn leaves. Despite aromatic delicacy, these are structured, powerful wines that can be quite closed and tannic in youth, typically requiring 10-15 years of cellaring to reach their optimal drinking windows.

The specific expression of La Volta, like any MGA, would be shaped by its particular terroir variables—the precise soil composition at the site, its elevation and aspect, and how these factors interact with vine age and viticulture practices employed by individual producers.

## Contemporary Significance

The MGA system represents more than administrative reorganization; it reflects an evolving understanding of terroir as "managing terroir"—the concept that producers select appropriate plant material and adapt management choices to optimize wine quality in each particular environment. Modern precision viticulture techniques, employing global navigation satellite systems and geographical information systems, allow differential management of vineyard blocks to maximize quality, addressing terroir effects that are largely due to soil differences.

As the Barolo production zone continues to refine its understanding of site-specific quality markers, MGAs like La Volta contribute to the broader tapestry of this historically significant wine region, offering wine enthusiasts the opportunity to explore the remarkable diversity contained within Barolo's relatively compact geographical boundaries.
