# Gallina

## Introduction

Gallina stands among the most distinguished vineyard sites in Neive, the southernmost commune of the Barbaresco appellation. Recognized alongside Albesani as one of Neive's finest menzioni geografiche aggiuntive (MGAs), Gallina has earned its reputation through the consistent quality of its Nebbiolo and the willingness of négociants to pay premium prices for its grapes. This market-driven recognition, established over decades of commercial practice, speaks to the site's ability to produce Barbaresco of distinctive character and notable aging potential.

The vineyard's prominence is particularly significant given Neive's relatively late arrival to single-vineyard recognition. While the village calls itself "the township of four wines"—referencing its production of Moscato, Barbera, Dolcetto, and Nebbiolo—it consolidated its focus on Nebbiolo only after the Second World War. Today, Neive cultivates 265 hectares of the variety, more than doubling the 140 hectares planted in 1995, with Gallina occupying a prestigious position within this expanding landscape.

## Historical Context and Recognition

The story of Gallina's recognition reflects the broader evolution of Barbaresco's single-vineyard culture. Lorenzo Fantini's influential monograph on Piedmontese viticulture from the late 19th century documented very few "choice positions" in Barbaresco and none whatsoever in Neive. This absence from early viticultural literature stands in stark contrast to the more firmly established cru system that developed in Barolo during the same period.

Systematic attempts to identify and classify Barbaresco's finest sites emerged only in the 1960s with Luigi Veronelli's work and continued through the 1970s with Renato Ratti's classifications. Gallina's elevation to the top tier of Neive vineyards occurred through practical commercial channels rather than historical documentation. The willingness of négociants to pay higher prices for Gallina fruit established a market consensus about quality that predated official recognition. This pragmatic path to prestige reflects a certain meritocracy—the vineyard proved its worth through the wines it produced rather than through historical pedigree or aristocratic patronage.

The formalization of Barbaresco's MGA system in 2007 codified what the market had already determined. The list sensibly retained historically known vineyards like Gallina while avoiding the commercial temptation to absorb smaller sites into more famous names. This approach preserved the appellation's viticultural geography with unusual integrity.

## Terroir and Vineyard Characteristics

Understanding Gallina's terroir requires examining Neive's position within Barbaresco's geological framework. Barbaresco's soils divide roughly into two principal types, both of marine sedimentary origin. The calcareous clay of the Tortonian epoch resembles the soils found in La Morra and Barolo commune in the Barolo zone, typically producing perfumed, fruit-driven wines. The second type, Sant'Agata fossil marl, is more compact and similar to soils in Monforte d'Alba and Serralunga d'Alba, yielding wines with more pronounced tannic structure.

While specific geological data for Gallina is not extensively documented in historical viticultural literature, Neive's sites generally exhibit characteristics that distinguish them from vineyards in Barbaresco proper and Treiso. The commune's position in the southern reaches of the appellation influences both soil composition and mesoclimate, factors that contribute to distinctive wine profiles.

Nebbiolo ripening patterns in Barbaresco vary by proximity to the Tanaro River and by elevation and exposure. Certain Barbaresco vineyards, particularly those closest to the river, ripen earlier than sites in Barolo, a factor that has historically contributed to perceptions of Barbaresco as producing lighter wines. However, this characterization oversimplifies the complexity within the appellation, and Neive's premier sites like Gallina demonstrate that Barbaresco can achieve both elegance and substantial structure.

## Wine Characteristics

Barbaresco from Gallina expresses the classic characteristics of Nebbiolo while displaying the particular stamp of its terroir. These wines exhibit the variety's signature aromatics—cherry, violets, and rose petal in youth, evolving toward more complex notes of tar, iron, leather, and orange peel with age. The tannins, while always present given Nebbiolo's phenolic abundance, integrate with a balance that allows the wines to show well across their aging curve.

The acidity that defines Nebbiolo, essential for both food compatibility and longevity, manifests clearly in Gallina bottlings. This structural backbone, combined with moderate alcohol levels (typically 13.5% and above, meeting Barbaresco's minimum requirement of 12.5%), creates wines that require patience but reward those who provide it. Young Barbaresco from any site, Gallina included, is rarely an immediately pleasurable experience. The variety demands either age or careful food pairing to tame its assertive structure.

Barbaresco's aging requirements—a minimum of 26 months with at least 9 months in oak, extending to 50 months for Riserva—are less stringent than Barolo's 38-month minimum (18 in oak) and 62-month Riserva requirement. This regulatory difference has unfortunately fostered misconceptions about Barbaresco's aging potential and encouraged some producers to pursue unnecessary extraction and concentration. The finest expressions from sites like Gallina demonstrate that elegance and longevity are not mutually exclusive.

## Production and Producers

The commercial recognition that Gallina receives from négociants indicates its status as a sought-after source of fruit. This demand reflects not only the site's quality but also the professionalism of the growers who farm it. The dramatic expansion of Nebbiolo plantings in Neive since the 1990s occurred largely at the expense of Barbera and Dolcetto, as the economics of viticulture increasingly favored the more lucrative noble variety.

The work of Barbaresco's finest producers, from the exemplary Produttori del Barbaresco cooperative to individual estates pursuing both traditional and more contemporary approaches, has elevated the entire appellation. A number of smaller producers have embraced organic and biodynamic viticulture, producing wines that prioritize terroir expression over formulaic winemaking. These efforts have been particularly important for sites like Gallina, where distinctive character might otherwise be masked by heavy-handed cellar techniques.

## Conclusion

Gallina's position among Neive's premier MGAs rests on decades of demonstrated quality rather than centuries of documented history. This relatively recent recognition does not diminish the site's significance; rather, it reflects Barbaresco's evolution from Barolo's shadow into its own distinct identity. As the appellation continues to refine its understanding of its individual vineyard sites, Gallina stands as evidence that terroir recognition can emerge from practical experience and market validation as legitimately as from historical records. For those seeking to understand Neive's contribution to Barbaresco, Gallina remains an essential reference point.
