## 3. Terroir & Geology

The geological foundation of En Remilly reflects the complex sedimentary history that defines the Côte de Beaune. The vineyard sits upon layers of limestone and marl that were deposited during the Jurassic period, creating the calcareous-clay matrix that has proven ideal for Chardonnay cultivation. The soil composition demonstrates the subtle variations that distinguish one Premier Cru site from another, with En Remilly exhibiting particular characteristics that contribute to its distinctive wine profile.

The topsoil in En Remilly consists primarily of brown limestone-rich earth with varying proportions of clay and decomposed limestone fragments. This composition provides excellent drainage during periods of excessive rainfall while retaining sufficient moisture during dry spells to prevent vine stress. The presence of limestone actively contributes to wine character, providing the mineral backbone that defines great Chassagne-Montrachet whites and influencing pH levels in ways that promote wine longevity.

Beneath the topsoil lies the crucial bedrock formation that ultimately defines En Remilly's terroir. The underlying geology consists of Middle Jurassic limestone formations, specifically Bathonian limestone that provides both structural support for proper drainage and a steady supply of mineral elements that vines access through their deep root systems. This geological foundation creates the conditions necessary for vines to develop the complex root networks that contribute to wine complexity and terroir expression.

The soil depth in En Remilly varies across the vineyard, with some areas featuring relatively shallow topsoil over limestone bedrock, while other sections offer deeper soil profiles. These variations contribute to the complexity found in wines from this climat, as vines growing in different soil depths develop varying stress levels and nutrient access patterns that influence fruit character and concentration.

The presence of fossilized marine organisms within the limestone substrate provides additional mineral complexity, contributing trace elements that subtly influence wine character. The slow weathering of limestone over millennia has created the fine-grained soil particles that give En Remilly its distinctive texture and drainage characteristics, while the continuous geological processes ensure ongoing mineral availability for vine nutrition.

## 4. Climate & Microclimate

En Remilly benefits from the continental climate that characterizes the Côte de Beaune, with distinct seasonal variations that promote proper vine development and fruit maturation. The vineyard's eastern exposure ensures optimal morning sun exposure while providing some protection from the intense afternoon heat that can stress vines during the warmest summer periods.

The microclimate within En Remilly reflects its specific topographical position and elevation. The vineyard sits at an altitude that captures the ideal balance of temperature moderation and sun exposure, typically ranging from 250 to 280 meters above sea level. This elevation places En Remilly within the optimal viticultural zone where temperatures remain warm enough to ensure complete fruit maturation while avoiding the excessive heat that can lead to overripe or cooked fruit characters.

Air circulation patterns within En Remilly contribute significantly to vine health and fruit quality. The vineyard's position allows for natural air movement that helps prevent fungal diseases while moderating temperature extremes. The slope gradient facilitates cold air drainage during cool nights, preventing frost damage while maintaining the diurnal temperature variations that promote aromatic complexity and acidity retention in the developing fruit.

Rainfall patterns affect En Remilly similarly to other Côte de Beaune sites, with annual precipitation typically ranging from 700 to 800 millimeters. The vineyard's drainage characteristics become crucial during wet periods, as the limestone-rich soils efficiently channel excess water away from vine roots while retaining adequate moisture for vine needs. The timing of rainfall throughout the growing season significantly impacts vintage character, with spring moisture supporting vine growth and summer drought stress concentrating flavors.

The frost risk in En Remilly remains manageable due to the vineyard's elevation and slope position, though spring frosts can occasionally affect bud break and flowering. The site's natural air drainage helps minimize frost damage compared to lower-lying areas, contributing to more consistent annual production levels.

## 6. Wine Character & Style
