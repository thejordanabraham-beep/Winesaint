# Martinenga

## Introduction

Martinenga stands among the most celebrated MGAs (menzioni geografiche aggiuntive) in Barbaresco, representing the pinnacle of what this distinguished appellation can achieve. Located within the township of Barbaresco proper, Martinenga has earned its place alongside the denomination's most prestigious sites through decades of dedicated viticulture and winemaking rather than centuries of documented history. Unlike some Barbaresco crus whose reputations emerged organically through négociant demand and historical record, Martinenga's ascent to fame is inextricably linked to the vision and exacting standards of the producers who have championed it, most notably the estate of Alberto di Gresy (now Marchesi di Gresy).

The vineyard exemplifies an important phenomenon in Barbaresco's relatively recent quality revolution: certain sites have achieved their current prestige not through ancient pedigree but through the meticulous work of individual producers who recognized their potential and dedicated themselves to expressing it. This distinguishes Martinenga from historically acknowledged sites like Asili, Montefico, Montestefano, and Rabajà, which had established reputations through négociant pricing by the mid-20th century. Instead, Martinenga belongs to that select group of vineyards (including San Lorenzo and Tildin in Barbaresco, and Santo Stefano in Neive) whose greatness was revealed through the dedicated work of visionary producers such as Angelo Gaja, Bruno Giacosa, and Alberto di Gresy.

## Historical Context and Recognition

The story of single-vineyard Barbaresco is relatively brief when compared to the centuries-old traditions of France's grand crus. The first single-vineyard Barbaresco (Prunotto's Montestefano) appeared only in 1961, marking the beginning of cru designation in the appellation. This late start reflects broader historical patterns: Lorenzo Fantini's monograph on Piedmontese viticulture from the late 19th century documented very few "choice positions" in Barbaresco and none whatsoever in neighboring Neive. The systematic attempts to identify and rate the finest sites emerged even later, beginning with Luigi Veronelli's efforts in the 1960s and Renato Ratti's influential work in the 1970s.

Within this context, Martinenga's prominence developed through a different trajectory than the consensus sites identified through négociant pricing. The vineyard's reputation was built largely through the commitment of the di Gresy family, who acquired the property and dedicated themselves to showcasing its distinctive character. This producer-driven model of cru development proved that exceptional terroir combined with uncompromising standards could establish a vineyard's reputation within a generation.

The formalization of Barbaresco's MGA system in 2007 represented a watershed moment for the appellation, preceding even Barolo's official cru delineation by three years. The official list sensibly retained almost all historically known vineyards, resisting the temptation to absorb lesser-known sites into more famous names for commercial advantage. Martinenga's inclusion in this official register acknowledged both its quality credentials and its established place in Barbaresco's hierarchy of sites, cementing a reputation that had been built over the previous decades.

## The Barbaresco Context

To understand Martinenga, one must first appreciate Barbaresco's position within Piedmont's Nebbiolo landscape. The appellation lies northeast and east of Alba, covering approximately 733 hectares as of 2019, roughly one-third the size of Barolo. The production zone encompasses three main townships: Barbaresco, Neive, and Treiso, with a small portion in Alba itself. Martinenga's location within the commune of Barbaresco proper places it at the historical heart of the appellation, in the same township where Domizio Cavazza founded the Barbaresco cooperative in 1894 and first began bottling wine under the Barbaresco name.

The Barbaresco denomination has long struggled against misperceptions about its character relative to Barolo. The shorter minimum aging requirement, 26 months total with 9 in wood for regular Barbaresco, compared to 38 months with 18 in wood for Barolo, led to the false assumption that Barbaresco is inherently less ageworthy or less structured. This misconception has unfortunately prompted some producers to emulate Barolo's power rather than honor Barbaresco's unique personality. The finest Barbaresco sites, Martinenga among them, demonstrate that the denomination can produce wines of extraordinary longevity and complexity while maintaining a distinct identity.

## Terroir and Viticultural Character

While the specific geological and climatic details of Martinenga reflect the broader patterns of the Barbaresco township, the site benefits from the particular combination of factors that define the appellation's finest vineyards. Barbaresco's terroir is characterized by calcareous marl soils of varying composition, favorable elevations that balance ripeness with acidity, and aspects that optimize sun exposure for the late-ripening Nebbiolo grape. The sites within the township of Barbaresco itself tend to produce wines that express the appellation's characteristic elegance and aromatic complexity, often showing slightly more restraint than the powerful expressions from certain Neive sites while offering greater immediate charm than some of Treiso's more austere bottlings.

The marl soils common to Barbaresco's finest sites provide excellent drainage while maintaining sufficient water retention to support measured vine growth through the growing season. This balance is crucial for Nebbiolo, a variety that demands extended hang time to achieve full phenolic ripeness while preserving the variety's signature acidity and perfume. The interplay between soil composition, drainage, aspect, and mesoclimate determines each site's distinctive expression: the subtle variations that distinguish one MGA from another and make the study of Barbaresco's crus so compelling.

## Wine Characteristics

Barbaresco produced from Martinenga reflects both the general characteristics of well-sited Nebbiolo from the denomination and the specific qualities that have earned the vineyard its reputation. Like all great Barbarescos, wines from this site display Nebbiolo's characteristic aromatic complexity: red cherry, rose petal, violet, and subtle herbal notes in youth, evolving toward more complex expressions of tar, leather, dried flowers, iron, and orange peel with age. The tannin structure shows Nebbiolo's firm but fine-grained quality, requiring time to integrate fully but promising extended aging potential.

What distinguishes exceptional Martinenga bottlings is the combination of power and refinement, structure and aromatic purity. These are wines that must meet Barbaresco's minimum alcohol requirement of 12.5% but easily reach 13.5% or higher in warm vintages, demonstrating that the site can achieve full ripeness. Yet this concentration never comes at the expense of the elegance and perfume that define great Barbaresco. The finest examples show remarkable precision and clarity of expression: the transparency of terroir that marks truly distinguished vineyard sites.

The aging trajectory of Martinenga follows the pattern of Barbaresco's most structured crus. Young wines can show considerable austerity, their tannins firm and their aromatic profile still closed. With 5-10 years of bottle age, the wines typically enter an initial phase of harmony, the tannins beginning to soften and tertiary complexity emerging. With 15-20 years or more, the greatest vintages reveal the full depth and nuance that Nebbiolo can achieve, developing the haunting aromatics and silky texture that make aged Barbaresco so compelling.

## Producers and Benchmark Bottlings

The Marchesi di Gresy (formerly Alberto di Gresy) estate has been the dominant force in establishing and maintaining Martinenga's reputation. The family's dedication to the site, their willingness to make significant viticultural and commercial investments, and their commitment to quality across decades have been instrumental in revealing the vineyard's potential. Their approach exemplifies how individual producers can elevate sites through consistent excellence and clear vision.

The estate's work with Martinenga has included both single-vineyard bottlings and, at times, designations of specific parcels within the larger MGA, reflecting the site's internal complexity and the family's intimate knowledge of its varied sections. This granular approach to site expression (bottling different portions of a larger vineyard separately when their characters warrant it) represents a sophisticated understanding of terroir that has become increasingly common among Barbaresco's quality-focused producers.

Beyond the di Gresy family's flagship bottlings, Martinenga's reputation has been reinforced by its inclusion in discussions of Barbaresco's elite sites alongside names like Asili, Rabajà, and Montestefano. The vineyard appears in critical assessments of the appellation's hierarchy and features in comprehensive studies of Barbaresco's cru landscape, including Alessandro Masnaghetti's detailed cartographic and analytical work on the denomination's MGAs.

## Contemporary Significance

Today, Martinenga occupies a secure position within Barbaresco's established cru hierarchy. The vineyard's recognition reflects broader changes in how Italian wine regions understand and communicate terroir. The 2007 introduction of the MGA system provided official acknowledgment of what producers and consumers had already recognized: that specific sites within Barbaresco produce distinctive wines worthy of individual designation and study.

The site's prominence also illustrates an important aspect of terroir development, that great vineyards can emerge from dedicated stewardship and quality-focused viticulture as much as from centuries of historical documentation. While some wine regions lean heavily on ancient records and traditional hierarchies, Barbaresco's more recent evolution has allowed for a merit-based assessment of sites, with vineyards like Martinenga earning recognition through the consistent quality of the wines they produce.

For collectors and enthusiasts, Martinenga represents an essential reference point for understanding Barbaresco's range of expression. The wines offer a window into what distinguished sites within the Barbaresco township can achieve: the combination of structure and elegance, power and perfume, immediate appeal and long-term aging potential that defines great Barbaresco. As the appellation continues to refine its understanding of its cru landscape and as producers increasingly focus on site-specific expression, Martinenga's status as one of Barbaresco's benchmark vineyards appears firmly established.
