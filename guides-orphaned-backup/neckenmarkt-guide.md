# Neckenmarkt: Red Slate and the Identity of Mittelburgenland Blaufränkisch

Neckenmarkt stands apart in Mittelburgenland. While neighboring municipalities work with clay and loam, Neckenmarkt possesses something geologically distinct: red slate. This is not a subtle distinction. The presence of slate fundamentally alters both viticultural conditions and wine character, creating some of the most structured and mineral-driven Blaufränkisch in Austria.

The municipality sits in the northern section of Mittelburgenland, positioned to receive warm autumn winds pushed from Lake Neusiedl. Yet the slate beneath the vines provides a counterbalance, excellent drainage, heat retention during cool nights, and a mineral signature that distinguishes Neckenmarkt wines from the richer, more opulent expressions found just kilometers away in Deutschkreutz or Horitschon.

## Geography & Terroir

### The Slate Factor

Red slate is rare in Burgenland. Its presence in Neckenmarkt represents a geological anomaly in a region otherwise dominated by sedimentary soils. Slate is a metamorphic rock formed through the compression of clay and volcanic ash under heat and pressure: a process that creates the characteristic layered structure that fractures into thin, flat pieces.

This structure matters vitally for viticulture. Slate's layered composition allows vine roots to penetrate deeply while providing exceptional drainage. Water moves quickly through the fractured planes, forcing roots downward in search of moisture and nutrients. The result: vines that develop extensive root systems and produce smaller, more concentrated berries.

The red coloration comes from iron oxide content within the slate. These iron traces contribute not just color but also specific mineral characteristics to the wines. Winemakers in Neckenmarkt frequently describe a ferrous quality in their Blaufränkisch, not overtly metallic, but a kind of stony grip that anchors the fruit.

### Elevation and Aspect

Neckenmarkt's vineyards range from approximately 200 to 350 meters in elevation. The best sites occupy mid-slope positions with southeastern to southern aspects, capturing morning sun while avoiding the most intense afternoon heat. This orientation proves critical for Blaufränkisch, which requires warmth to develop phenolic ripeness but can lose its characteristic acidity and spice in excessively hot conditions.

The slope gradient in premier sites typically ranges from 10 to 20 percent, enough to ensure good air drainage and frost protection, but not so steep as to cause excessive erosion of the thin topsoil layer above the slate bedrock.

### Climate Influence

Lake Neusiedl, located approximately 25 kilometers to the northeast, exerts measurable influence on Neckenmarkt's climate. The lake acts as a thermal regulator, moderating temperature extremes and extending the growing season into autumn. Warm winds from the lake prove particularly valuable in September and October, when Blaufränkisch completes its phenolic maturation.

Despite this lake influence, Neckenmarkt remains fundamentally a continental climate zone. Summers are hot and dry, with daytime temperatures regularly exceeding 30°C in July and August. Nights cool significantly, particularly at higher elevations where the slate radiates accumulated heat slowly. This diurnal temperature variation preserves acidity while allowing sugars and phenolics to develop: the essential balance for structured red wines.

Annual precipitation averages approximately 600-650 millimeters, concentrated in spring and early summer. By harvest, conditions are typically dry, reducing disease pressure and allowing extended hang time when vintages permit.

## Wine Character

### Structure and Minerality

Neckenmarkt Blaufränkisch is defined by structure. The slate imparts a vertical architecture to these wines: a sense of tension and linearity that distinguishes them from the rounder, more immediately generous wines from clay-based sites in neighboring Deutschkreutz or Lutzmannsburg.

Tannins are firm and fine-grained rather than broad and coating. The texture resembles graphite more than velvet. Acidity remains prominent even in warm vintages, providing the backbone necessary for extended aging. The best examples require five to seven years to begin resolving their structural components into harmony.

The mineral signature manifests as a stony, almost saline quality that emerges on the mid-palate and extends through the finish. This is not the chalky minerality of limestone sites, nor the flint-strike character of certain Loire Sauvignon Blancs. Instead, it presents as a kind of iron-tinged stoniness, subtle but persistent, anchoring the fruit without overwhelming it.

### Fruit Expression

Despite the structural emphasis, Neckenmarkt Blaufränkisch is not austere. The fruit tends toward dark berries (blackberry, black cherry, and black currant) rather than the red fruit spectrum. In cooler vintages or from higher-elevation sites, notes of sour cherry and cranberry appear, adding complexity and lift.

The characteristic Blaufränkisch spice (black pepper, clove, and dried herbs) integrates seamlessly with the fruit. Oak, when used judiciously, contributes structure and aging potential without dominating. The best producers employ large format barrels (500-liter puncheons or larger) or a combination of barrel sizes to preserve the variety's inherent character while adding textural complexity.

### Aging Potential

Neckenmarkt Blaufränkisch ranks among Austria's most age-worthy red wines. The combination of natural acidity, firm tannins, and mineral structure provides the framework for extended cellaring. Well-made examples from strong vintages can develop for 15 to 20 years, evolving from primary fruit toward secondary notes of leather, tobacco, forest floor, and dried flowers while maintaining their core of minerality.

The wines typically enter an awkward adolescent phase around three to five years after vintage, when the fruit begins to recede but tertiary complexity has not yet emerged. Patience proves essential.

## Hochberg: The Reference Point

The Hochberg vineyard represents Neckenmarkt's most celebrated site. Located at the upper elevation range for the municipality, Hochberg combines red slate bedrock with a thin layer of loamy topsoil. The site's southeastern exposure and elevation provide ideal conditions for slow, even ripening.

Hochberg Blaufränkisch demonstrates the full potential of Neckenmarkt's terroir: pronounced minerality, firm but refined tannins, vibrant acidity, and concentrated dark fruit. The wines require extended aging but reward patience with remarkable complexity and persistence.

The site has achieved recognition as one of Mittelburgenland's premier rieden, standing alongside Hochäcker in Horitschon and Goldberg in Deutschkreutz as a reference point for the region's quality potential.

## Comparison to Neighboring Sites

### Deutschkreutz: Clay and Power

Deutschkreutz, located immediately to the west, works primarily with clay and loam soils. The resulting Blaufränkisch tends toward greater power and opulence, broader shoulders, richer fruit, and softer acidity. The Goldberg vineyard in Deutschkreutz produces wines of considerable concentration and aging potential, but with a fundamentally different structure than Neckenmarkt's slate-based expressions.

Where Neckenmarkt emphasizes verticality and tension, Deutschkreutz offers width and generosity. Neither approach is superior; they represent different expressions of Blaufränkisch shaped by geological reality.

### Horitschon: Limestone and Elegance

Horitschon, to the south, occupies higher elevations where limestone becomes more prominent in the soil profile. Some sites even contain traces of iron, adding complexity. Horitschon Blaufränkisch typically displays more floral aromatics and a finer tannin structure than either Neckenmarkt or Deutschkreutz.

The limestone influence creates wines with less overt minerality than Neckenmarkt's slate-based expressions but with notable elegance and aromatic complexity. Hochäcker, Horitschon's premier site, produces Blaufränkisch of extraordinary refinement, structured but not severe, concentrated but not heavy.

### Lutzmannsburg: Sand and Accessibility

Lutzmannsburg, the southernmost municipality in Mittelburgenland, features sandy loam soils that produce the most immediately accessible wines in the region. The sandy component provides excellent drainage but less heat retention than slate or clay. Lutzmannsburg Blaufränkisch tends toward red fruit, softer tannins, and earlier drinking windows.

These wines offer charm and approachability but generally lack the aging potential and structural complexity of Neckenmarkt, Deutschkreutz, or Horitschon expressions.

## Key Producers

### The Founding Generation

Albert Gesellmann and Hans Igler were instrumental in establishing Mittelburgenland's reputation for quality Blaufränkisch in the 1980s and 1990s. Their work demonstrated that the region could produce red wines of genuine complexity and aging potential, not merely simple, fruity expressions for immediate consumption.

This founding generation focused on yield management, site selection, and appropriate oak aging, fundamental quality principles that transformed Mittelburgenland from a bulk wine source into a serious red wine region. Their influence extended beyond their own estates, inspiring younger winemakers to pursue excellence.

### Contemporary Approaches

Recent decades have witnessed an increasing emphasis on single-vineyard expressions and terroir-specific bottlings. Producers now differentiate between sites based on soil type, elevation, and aspect, creating wines that express specific vineyard characteristics rather than blended regional styles.

This evolution reflects both growing market sophistication and winemaker confidence in Mittelburgenland's terroir diversity. Neckenmarkt's distinctive slate-based wines have benefited particularly from this trend, as the geological differences become more apparent when sites are vinified separately.

Modern winemaking in Neckenmarkt balances tradition with precision. Extended maceration extracts color, tannin, and flavor compounds, but with careful attention to avoiding harsh extraction. Oak aging remains standard for premium wines, but with increasing use of large format barrels and reduced new oak percentages to preserve varietal character and site expression.

Some producers have adopted biodynamic farming practices, seeking to enhance soil health and vine balance through natural methods. The dry climate and well-drained slate soils prove conducive to organic and biodynamic approaches, as disease pressure remains relatively low.

## Mittelburgenland DAC and Classification

Mittelburgenland DAC, established in 2005, provides a regional classification system focused exclusively on Blaufränkisch. The appellation recognizes three quality tiers:

**Gebietswein** (regional wine): Entry-level Blaufränkisch emphasizing fruit purity and varietal character. These wines must show at least 12% alcohol and may not display overt oak influence. They offer accessibility and value.

**Ortswein** (village wine): Site-specific wines from a single municipality, showing greater concentration and complexity. Minimum alcohol increases to 12.5%, and oak aging is permitted provided it does not dominate varietal character.

**Riedenwein** (vineyard wine): Single-vineyard expressions from designated premier sites. These wines must achieve at least 13% alcohol and may not be released until two years after harvest, allowing time for integration and development. Riedenwein represents the appellation's quality apex.

Hochberg qualifies as a recognized ried within the Mittelburgenland DAC system, permitting producers to label single-vineyard wines from this site as Riedenwein. This classification acknowledges Hochberg's status as one of Neckenmarkt's (and Mittelburgenland's) premier sites.

The DAC system provides clarity for consumers while encouraging producers to pursue quality and site expression. The mandatory aging period for Riedenwein proves particularly important for Neckenmarkt's structured, mineral-driven wines, which require time to resolve their tannins and reveal their complexity.

## The Blaufränkisch Challenge

Blaufränkisch is not an easy variety. It ripens late, making it vulnerable to autumn weather. It requires warmth for phenolic ripeness but loses its characteristic acidity and spice in excessively hot conditions. Yields must be managed carefully; overcropping produces wines with green, herbaceous characters and insufficient concentration.

When properly cultivated, however, Blaufränkisch produces some of Austria's most age-worthy and intense red wines. The variety's natural acidity provides structure for extended aging, while its flavor profile (dark fruit, black pepper, dried herbs) offers complexity without heaviness.

Neckenmarkt's slate soils address several of Blaufränkisch's challenges. The excellent drainage naturally limits yields by stressing vines. The heat retention extends the effective growing season, helping achieve phenolic ripeness even in cooler vintages. The mineral influence adds complexity and structure, creating wines that transcend simple fruit expression.

## Historical Context

Mittelburgenland's emergence as a quality red wine region is relatively recent. For much of the 20th century, the area produced bulk wine for the Austrian and German markets, simple, light reds without particular distinction or aging potential.

The quality revolution began in the 1980s, driven by producers like Gesellmann and Igler who recognized the region's potential for serious Blaufränkisch. Their work coincided with broader changes in Austrian wine culture following the 1985 antifreeze scandal, which devastated the country's wine reputation but ultimately led to stricter quality controls and a renewed focus on terroir and authenticity.

Neckenmarkt participated in this transformation, with producers increasingly recognizing the distinctive character imparted by the municipality's red slate soils. The establishment of Mittelburgenland DAC in 2005 formalized the region's quality focus and provided a framework for communicating terroir differences to consumers.

Today, Neckenmarkt stands as one of Mittelburgenland's most distinctive municipalities, proof that geological variation creates meaningful wine differences even within a relatively compact region.
