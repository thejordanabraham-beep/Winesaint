# Pajè MGA, Barbaresco

## Introduction

Pajè is a recognized menzione geografica aggiuntiva (MGA) within the commune of Barbaresco in the Barbaresco DOCG. The MGA system, officially introduced in 2007 for Barbaresco (three years before Barolo implemented its own cru classification) represents a formalization of the region's historic single-vineyard sites. This classification sensibly retained almost all the historically known vineyards, avoiding the commercial temptation to absorb smaller sites into more famous vineyard names.

While Pajè does not appear among the most frequently cited Barbaresco MGAs in historical literature, its inclusion in the official 2007 classification acknowledges its distinct terroir and wine-producing potential within the broader Barbaresco landscape. The vineyard sits within the commune of Barbaresco itself, one of three principal communes (alongside Neive and Treiso) that comprise the 733-hectare Barbaresco production zone as of 2019.

## Barbaresco Context and Terroir Principles

Understanding Pajè requires situating it within Barbaresco's broader terroir framework. The Barbaresco production zone lies northeast and east of Alba, covering approximately one-third the area of neighboring Barolo. Despite its smaller size, Barbaresco has gained recognition for producing wines of striking elegance and aromatic intensity from the Nebbiolo grape, wines that have emerged from Barolo's shadow since the 1960s through the dedicated work of producers like Angelo Gaja and Bruno Giacosa.

The concept of terroir in Barbaresco, as throughout Piedmont's Nebbiolo-growing regions, encompasses the complex interaction of soil composition, elevation, aspect, microclimate, and human intervention. Research into terroir factors demonstrates that while soil characteristics influence grape quality and wine individuality, these effects operate in concert with climate, vine variety, and vineyard management. Of the influential soil characteristics, those governing water supply to the vine prove most important, likely followed by factors influencing soil and above-soil temperatures.

The manner in which vine roots navigate the complex layering of soil strata dictates the amount of water and nutrients the plant can access throughout the growing season. Soil attributes can change rapidly within a vineyard, both vertically and horizontally, creating variation in vine vigor even within small parcels. This variation underscores the legitimacy of recognizing individual MGAs like Pajè as distinct terroir units.

## Wine Characteristics and Barbaresco's Expression

Wines from Barbaresco, including those from Pajè, are produced entirely from Nebbiolo, a grape that yields firmly structured wines marked by significant tannins and acidity. The DOCG requires a minimum alcoholic strength of 12.5%, though wines easily reach 13.5% or higher. Despite Barbaresco's reputation for greater elegance compared to Barolo (a perception partly attributable to the shorter minimum aging requirement) young Barbaresco from any MGA is rarely an immediately pleasurable experience, demanding patience and understanding of Nebbiolo's evolution.

The characteristic aromatic profile of Barbaresco encompasses perfumes of cherry and violets in youth, developing with age into complex notes of iron, tar, and orange peel. The tannin structure, while formidable in youth, integrates over time to reveal the wine's inherent elegance and aromatic intensity. The terroir variations between different MGAs create subtle but meaningful differences in how these characteristics express themselves.

The influence of specific soil composition, drainage patterns, elevation, and exposition shapes each MGA's particular expression. Moderate fertility and well-regulated, moderately sufficient water supply have been shown to be common factors in soils capable of producing high-quality wines, allowing the vine to achieve optimal physiological balance during grape ripening.

## Historical Development and Recognition

The formal recognition of Barbaresco's single-vineyard sites represents a relatively recent development in the region's long winemaking history. Single-vineyard bottlings emerged as a phenomenon only in 1961, when Prunotto released the first Barbaresco Montestefano. This stands in contrast to Barolo, where a more firmly established written record of cru designation existed.

Lorenzo Fantini's late 19th-century monograph on Piedmontese viticulture indicated very few "choice positions" in the commune of Barbaresco and none whatsoever in Neive, suggesting that the hierarchy of sites has evolved considerably over the past century. The first systematic attempts to list and rate the finest positions emerged in the 1960s through the work of Luigi Veronelli and in the 1970s through Renato Ratti's efforts.

A consensus about the top-tier sites gradually developed through négociants' willingness to pay premium prices for grapes from certain vineyards. By the late 20th century, this market-driven recognition had established Asili, Montefico, Montestefano, and Rabajà as the most celebrated MGAs in the Barbaresco commune. Other famous vineyards (San Lorenzo, Tildin, and Martinenga) gained their prestige through the dedicated work and exacting standards of individual producers like Gaja and Giacosa, demonstrating how single-estate commitment can elevate a site's reputation.

The 2007 MGA classification formalized this evolution, creating a comprehensive map of recognized single vineyards that balanced historical reputation with territorial integrity. Pajè's inclusion in this system places it within the official hierarchy of Barbaresco's diverse terroir mosaic.

## Production and Market Context

The broader Barbaresco appellation has experienced significant growth, with vineyard area expanding from 484 hectares in the early 1990s to 733 hectares by 2019. This expansion reflects growing international recognition and demand for Barbaresco wines. Approximately 75 percent of Barbaresco production is exported, with principal markets including the United States, Germany, the United Kingdom, and Scandinavia.

The region has maintained its character as an area of small family-owned farms, with relatively little consolidation of vineyard holdings compared to other leading Italian regions like Tuscany. While small growers historically sold their grapes to larger producers such as Fontanafredda, the substantial increase in Nebbiolo wine prices has enabled many small estates to bottle and market their own wines. This shift has contributed to greater diversity in how individual MGAs like Pajè are interpreted and presented to the market.

Rising prices for top-quality Bordeaux and Burgundy since 2010 have directed fine wine merchants, collectors, and buyers toward Piedmont, further elevating interest in understanding the distinctions between Barbaresco's various MGAs. This attention has encouraged both traditional and emerging producers to explore how different sites express Nebbiolo's potential, with some converting to organic and biodynamic viticulture and employing traditional winemaking techniques to produce highly original, long-lived wines with muscular structure.

## Conclusion

Pajè represents one thread in the complex tapestry of Barbaresco's terroir, a formally recognized MGA within the commune of Barbaresco that contributes to the appellation's diversity of expression. While it may not hold the historical prominence of sites like Asili or Rabajà, its inclusion in the 2007 MGA classification acknowledges its distinct character and potential for producing wines of quality and individuality. As with all Barbaresco MGAs, wines from Pajè reflect the fundamental characteristics of Nebbiolo grown in this unique corner of Piedmont: firm structure, aromatic complexity, and the capacity for extended aging that reveals layers of nuance over time.
