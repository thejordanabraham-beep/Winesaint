## REVISED DESCRIPTION (less flowery, more factual)

Pinot Noir is one of the oldest and most genetically unstable grape varieties, with documented cultivation in Burgundy dating to at least the 14th century. The variety produces wines ranging from light and perfumed to complex and age-worthy, depending on climate, clone, and winemaking. Its thin skin yields wines with relatively light color and moderate tannins but high aromatic intensity. Pinot Noir is notoriously difficult to grow and vinify, requiring specific climatic conditions and careful handling at every stage.

---

## Origins & History

Pinot Noir originated in Burgundy, France, with the earliest reliable references appearing in records from the 1370s. DNA analysis has confirmed Traminer as one parent, though the other remains unknown. The name likely derives from the French words for "pine" (pin) and "black" (noir), referencing the variety's tight, pinecone-shaped clusters.

Cistercian monks in Burgundy were instrumental in developing Pinot Noir cultivation, systematically mapping vineyard sites and observing how the grape expressed differently across parcels. This work laid the foundation for Burgundy's climat system. By the 17th century, Pinot Noir had spread throughout northeastern France and into Germany, where it became known as Spätburgunder.

The variety arrived in the New World in the 19th century but struggled until the late 20th century, when cooler-climate regions in Oregon, New Zealand, and Tasmania demonstrated that quality Pinot Noir could be produced outside Burgundy.

## Genetic Lineage

### Parents
- **Traminer** - Confirmed through DNA analysis by researchers at UC Davis
- **Unknown** - Second parent has not been identified

### Offspring
Pinot Noir has an unusually large number of offspring due to its genetic instability and long cultivation history:
- **Chardonnay** (with Gouais Blanc)
- **Gamay** (with Gouais Blanc)
- **Aligoté** (with Gouais Blanc)
- **Melon de Bourgogne** (with Gouais Blanc)
- **Auxerrois** (with Gouais Blanc)

### Mutations
Pinot Noir's genetic instability has produced several color mutations that are now cultivated as distinct varieties:
- **Pinot Gris** - Pink/grey-skinned mutation, produces fuller-bodied white wines
- **Pinot Blanc** - White-skinned mutation, lighter and crisper than Gris
- **Pinot Meunier** - Possibly a mutation or close relative, used extensively in Champagne

## Clonal Selection

Pinot Noir has more identified clones than almost any other variety, with over 50 officially recognized in France alone. Clone selection significantly impacts wine style.

### Burgundian Heritage Clones
- **Dijon 115** - Small clusters, intense color and flavor, low yields. Widely planted in Oregon and cooler New World regions.
- **Dijon 667** - Medium clusters, good color, slightly higher yields than 115. Balances quality and quantity.
- **Dijon 777** - Very small berries, high acidity, loose clusters reducing rot pressure. Favored in cooler vintages.
- **Dijon 828** - Larger clusters, more structured wines. Less common but valued for blending.

### Traditional Clones
- **Pommard** - Originated in Burgundy's Pommard village. Larger berries, more tannic wines with darker color. Common in California.
- **Wadenswil** - Swiss origin, brought to Oregon in the 1960s. Produces aromatic, lighter-bodied wines.
- **Mariafeld** - Swiss clone, larger clusters, higher yields. Used where quantity matters.

### Regional Preferences
- **Burgundy:** Mix of heritage selections, often field blends of multiple clones
- **Oregon:** Primarily Dijon clones (115, 667, 777) plus Pommard and Wadenswil
- **California:** Pommard dominant, with increasing Dijon clone plantings
- **New Zealand:** Dijon clones, particularly in Martinborough and Central Otago

## In the Vineyard

### Climate Requirements
Pinot Noir requires a narrow temperature band. Too warm and the grapes lose acidity and aromatic complexity; too cold and they fail to ripen. The variety performs best where average growing season temperatures fall between 14-16°C (57-61°F). It buds early, making it vulnerable to spring frosts, and ripens early to mid-season.

### Terroir Interactions

**Limestone/Chalk:**
The classic pairing. Burgundy's limestone soils produce Pinot Noir with pronounced minerality, tension, and aging potential. Champagne's chalk provides similar effects. The high calcium content is thought to influence acidity retention.

**Clay:**
Heavier clay soils produce fuller-bodied, more powerful wines with deeper color. Common in Pommard and parts of the Côte de Beaune.

**Volcanic:**
In Oregon's Willamette Valley and parts of New Zealand, volcanic soils (Jory in Oregon) yield Pinot Noir with distinctive iron-tinged minerality and firm structure.

**Sandy/Alluvial:**
Lighter soils produce more aromatic, earlier-drinking wines with less structure. Common in Alsace and parts of Germany.

### Disease Susceptibility

Pinot Noir's thin skin and tight clusters make it particularly vulnerable to:

- **Botrytis (Grey Rot)** - HIGH susceptibility. Tight clusters trap moisture, encouraging rot. Requires careful canopy management and often green harvesting to loosen clusters.
- **Powdery Mildew** - HIGH susceptibility. The thin skin provides less protection. Sulfur applications are standard prevention.
- **Downy Mildew** - MODERATE susceptibility. Less problematic than powdery mildew but still requires monitoring.
- **Phylloxera** - MODERATE susceptibility. Like most vinifera, requires grafting to resistant rootstocks.

The variety's disease pressure is a primary reason Burgundy has lower yields than other French wine regions.

## Wine Styles

### Still Red Wines

**Burgundy Style (Cool Climate):**
Light to medium body, high acidity, delicate tannins. Aromatics emphasize red fruits (cherry, raspberry, strawberry), florals (rose, violet), and with age, earth, mushroom, and forest floor. Best examples age 10-30+ years. Serve slightly cool (14-16°C).

**New World Style (Moderate Climate):**
Medium body, ripe fruit character, softer acidity. More emphasis on darker fruits (black cherry, plum) and oak influence (vanilla, toast). Generally approachable younger. California, parts of New Zealand.

**Germanic Style (Spätburgunder):**
Historically lighter and simpler, but modern German Pinot Noir can rival Burgundy in quality. Tends toward elegance over power, with notable mineral character.

### Sparkling Wines

Pinot Noir is essential to Champagne production, providing body, structure, and red fruit character. Used for:
- **Blanc de Noirs** - 100% Pinot Noir (and/or Pinot Meunier), white sparkling wine
- **Rosé Champagne** - Short skin contact adds color and red fruit character
- **Traditional blends** - Typically blended with Chardonnay and Pinot Meunier

Also used for quality sparkling wines in England, Tasmania, and the US.

### Rosé

Increasingly popular, particularly from:
- **Sancerre** (Loire Valley)
- **Marsannay** (Burgundy)
- **California and Oregon**

## Regional Expressions

### France

**Burgundy - Côte de Nuits:**
The northern half of the Côte d'Or. Produces the most structured, longest-lived Burgundies. Key villages: Gevrey-Chambertin, Morey-Saint-Denis, Chambolle-Musigny, Vougeot, Vosne-Romanée, Nuits-Saint-Georges.

**Burgundy - Côte de Beaune:**
Southern Côte d'Or. Generally softer, more approachable wines with red fruit emphasis. Key villages: Pommard, Volnay, Beaune, Savigny-lès-Beaune.

**Champagne:**
Planted extensively in the Montagne de Reims and Côte des Bar. Rarely made as still wine (exceptions: Coteaux Champenois).

### United States

**Oregon - Willamette Valley:**
Closest climate to Burgundy in the US. Volcanic Jory soils and Dijon clones produce wines with bright acidity and earthy complexity. Sub-AVAs include Dundee Hills, Eola-Amity Hills, and Chehalem Mountains.

**California - Sonoma Coast:**
Cool, fog-influenced sites produce Pinot Noir with more structure than inland regions. Russian River Valley and the true Sonoma Coast AVA are key areas.

**California - Santa Barbara:**
Sta. Rita Hills and Santa Maria Valley produce distinctive, often powerful Pinot Noir with dark fruit and firm acidity.

### New Zealand

**Martinborough:**
Small region with gravelly soils and a dry climate. Produces concentrated, structured Pinot Noir.

**Central Otago:**
The world's southernmost wine region. Continental climate with dramatic day-night temperature swings yields Pinot Noir with intense fruit and firm structure.

### Germany

**Baden and Pfalz:**
Warmest German regions, producing riper, fuller styles.

**Ahr:**
Small northern region specializing in Pinot Noir despite its cool climate. Produces elegant, Burgundy-like wines.

### Australia

**Tasmania:**
Cool maritime climate produces the country's most Burgundian expressions.

**Yarra Valley and Mornington Peninsula:**
Victoria's cooler sites yield high-quality Pinot Noir with elegance and complexity.
