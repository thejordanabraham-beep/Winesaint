**Question 98**
A guest who drinks serious white Burgundy — premier cru Meursault, Corton-Charlemagne — is considering trying Germany for the first time. They are specifically looking for great dry white wine from a classified single vineyard. What is the precise German equivalent of what they are already drinking, and how do you explain the parallel?

- A) Recommend a Mosel Kabinett — the lightest German style has the most delicate character, closest to white Burgundy's finesse
- B) The German equivalent is a Grosses Gewächs (GG) from a VDP Grosse Lage vineyard — Germany's privately classified grand cru dry white wine. The parallel: Grosse Lage is the vineyard (like Corton-Charlemagne or Les Perrières); GG is the dry wine from that vineyard (like a Corton-Charlemagne blanc). The VDP classification, while private rather than legally mandated, was explicitly modeled on Burgundy's grand cru hierarchy. GG must be dry (below 9 g/L RS), must come from a classified Grosse Lage site, must be from a VDP member estate, and is released no earlier than September of the year following harvest. For this guest, cite Keller Kirchspiel GG (Rheinhessen, limestone terroir), Dönnhoff Hermannsberg GG (Nahe, slate), or Egon Müller Scharzhofberg GG (Saar) as specific recommendations — each representing the GG category at its most accomplished
- C) Recommend any Spätlese with the Trocken designation — German wine law requires all Trocken wines to come from classified single vineyards, making the Trocken designation functionally equivalent to a grand cru designation
- D) The closest German equivalent to premier cru white Burgundy is a Pfalz Grauburgunder from a cooperative producer — the grape variety similarity (both are Pinot family) makes this the most accurate stylistic comparison

**Correct: B**

**Question 99**
The Großlage system created by the 1971 Wine Law damaged Germany's export reputation. The VDP classification was the private-sector response. Explain precisely why the VDP eagle on a capsule matters more than a Prädikat designation alone when assessing quality from an unfamiliar producer.

- B) A Prädikat designation — Spätlese, Auslese — tells you only the ripeness at harvest, not who grew the grapes, where, or under what production standards. A Spätlese from an industrial Großlage producer and a Spätlese from a great classified single vineyard are legally equivalent. The VDP eagle, however, confirms: the grapes were estate-grown, the producer met membership standards voted on by peer estates, the vineyard classification reflects real quality assessment rather than legal minimum compliance, and the wine was subject to internal VDP tasting review. The eagle adds provenance, vineyard quality, and production standard information that the Prädikat alone cannot provide. That is why it is the fastest reliable quality signal on any German label
- C) The VDP eagle guarantees the wine is bone dry — all VDP member estates are required to produce exclusively trocken wines by the organization's charter
- D) The VDP eagle is primarily useful for identifying wines made without chaptalization; the Prädikat designation covers all other quality information
