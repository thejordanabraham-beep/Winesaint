- A) High-quality Provence rosé is deep salmon-pink in color with strong strawberry and cherry fruit from 48-72 hours of cold maceration before pressing; the direct press method refers to pressing grapes at lower-than-usual pressure to extract only the lightest color
- B) High-quality Provence rosé — particularly from appellations like Bandol and Côtes de Provence — is pale (often pale salmon or "oeil de perdrix"), fresh, and delicate; the direct press method means red-skinned grapes (primarily Grenache, Cinsault, and Mourvèdre) are pressed without any skin maceration, extracting only the faintest color from the juice immediately adjacent to the skin; this minimal extraction produces wines of striking delicacy — red fruit, citrus, Provence herbs, and a saline, mineral character — ideal for warm-weather drinking and Mediterranean cuisine
- C) Provence rosé achieves its pale color through centrifuge technology that removes pigmentation after fermentation; the "direct press" designation is a marketing term with no technical meaning
- D) High-quality Provence rosé must be aged a minimum of 12 months in new oak; the wood contact creates the characteristic vanilla and toast aromas that distinguish it from other French rosé regions

**Correct: B**

**Question 91**
Bandol is Provence's most serious red wine appellation. What makes Bandol red distinctive among Provence wines, and what grape is required to dominate the blend?

- A) Bandol red must contain a minimum 50% Grenache Noir, making it structurally similar to Châteauneuf-du-Pape; it is aged in new oak barrels for a mandatory 24 months
- B) Bandol red must contain a minimum 50% Mourvèdre — a late-ripening variety that struggles to achieve ripeness in most of Provence's coastal climate but finds its ideal conditions in Bandol's sun-drenched amphitheater of limestone and clay soils facing the Mediterranean; the result is Provence's most age-worthy red — dark, structured, iron-inflected, with notes of garrigue, meat, and black fruit that develop extraordinary complexity over 10–20+ years
- C) Bandol red contains 100% Syrah — AOC regulations prohibit all other varieties; the single-variety requirement produces wines comparable to Northern Rhône Syrah in structure and aging potential
- D) Bandol red is notable for its Carignan content — a minimum 70% Carignan requirement produces wines of unusual freshness and high acidity compared to other Provençal reds

**Correct: B**

A sommelier is building a wine list for a Mediterranean-themed restaurant. The chef specializes in bouillabaisse, grilled branzino, ratatouille, and lamb chops with herbes de Provence. Which French regional pairing strategy best serves this menu?

- B) Center the by-the-glass program around Provence rosé (for the seafood and vegetable dishes) and Bandol red (for the lamb), supplemented by Cassis white (Provence's coastal white appellation, predominantly Clairette and Marsanne, with a salinity that mirrors the sea) for the bouillabaisse; this regional approach provides an authentic through-line that reinforces the restaurant's identity and creates a "what grows together, goes together" story that guests appreciate

**Question 93**
Les Baux-de-Provence is an appellation within Provence known for its commitment to organic and biodynamic farming. What geographic feature defines its character and how does it influence its wine style compared to the coastal Côtes de Provence?

- A) Les Baux-de-Provence is a coastal appellation directly on the Mediterranean; its marine influence produces the lightest, most saline rosé in all of Provence
- B) Les Baux-de-Provence sits inland, at the foot of the Alpilles limestone range — a dramatic, arid environment with extreme heat, rocky limestone soils, and the Mistral wind that desiccates the canopy and naturally reduces disease pressure (making organic farming viable); the resulting wines have more concentration, structure, and garrigue intensity than coastal Côtes de Provence, with a wild, mineral earthiness that reflects the harshness of the landscape

**Question 94**
Cahors is famous for producing the original "black wine" of Southwest France. What grape variety is used, what is its minimum required percentage in Cahors AOC blends, and why does the wine carry such a dramatically dark character?

- A) Tannat; minimum 70%; Tannat's extraordinary tannin levels and dark pigmentation produce wines of almost impenetrable color and structure; the tannin compounds in Tannat are among the highest recorded in any commercially produced wine
- B) Malbec (locally called Côt or Auxerrois); minimum 70%; Malbec's thick skins produce wines of deep violet-black color and concentrated dark fruit; the combination of the Lot Valley's clay-limestone soils, continental climate, and old-vine Malbec creates wines of exceptional density, often requiring 10+ years of aging before they fully reveal their character
- C) Syrah; minimum 80%; Cahors AOC regulations require Syrah as the primary variety because its arrival from the Northern Rhône in the 17th century is considered the historic origin of the "black wine" reputation
- D) Cabernet Sauvignon; minimum 60%; Cahors Cabernet achieves unusually dark color because the continental climate produces late-ripening fruit with maximum phenolic development

**Correct: B**

**Question 95**
Gascony and Bergerac are Southwest French regions that produce wines often overshadowed by their neighbor Bordeaux. What commercially useful distinction can a floor professional make when positioning a Bergerac or Monbazillac to a Bordeaux-focused guest?

- A) Bergerac and Bordeaux are legally the same appellation — both fall under the broader "Sud-Ouest" AOC, so there is no meaningful distinction to make
- B) Bergerac uses the same grape varieties as Bordeaux (Cabernet Sauvignon, Merlot, Sauvignon Blanc, Sémillon) on similar soils directly east of the Bordeaux boundary; wines are often stylistically comparable to entry-level Bordeaux at 20–40% lower prices — a legitimate value position; Monbazillac uses Sémillon and Sauvignon Blanc with botrytis in the same manner as Sauternes and offers comparable sweet wine character at dramatically lower prices — it is the floor professional's most honest Sauternes alternative for budget-conscious guests who want that style
- C) Bergerac is stylistically closer to the Loire Valley than to Bordeaux; it primarily uses Chenin Blanc and Cabernet Franc, making it inappropriate to position as a Bordeaux alternative
- D) Monbazillac produces dry wine only — the "sweet" reputation is a historical artifact; modern Monbazillac is fermented to complete dryness in the same manner as Graves white

**Correct: B**

**Question 96**
Armagnac is Southwest France's most important brandy — produced in Gascony and considered by many connoisseurs to be more characterful than Cognac. Which statement best positions Armagnac versus Cognac for a guest exploring French spirits?

- A) Armagnac is younger and less prestigious than Cognac — it received its appellation designation 50 years after Cognac and lacks Cognac's international distribution
- B) Armagnac is France's oldest designated brandy region; it uses single continuous distillation (the alambic armagnacais) rather than Cognac's double distillation, producing a rougher, more characterful spirit that retains more congeners and grape-derived flavor; it is also more frequently sold as vintage-dated spirits from single distilleries, making it of particular interest to guests who appreciate artisan, single-origin spirits; while Cognac excels in polished consistency, Armagnac rewards those seeking personality and terroir expression in their brandy
- C) Armagnac and Cognac are legally identical products with no production method differences; the only distinction is geographic — Armagnac is from Gascony, Cognac is from Charentes

**Question 100**
A new sommelier on the floor asks you — their mentor — to summarize the single most important conceptual framework for communicating French wine to guests in a way that builds confidence, drives sales, and distinguishes a knowledgeable professional from an order-taker. What is your answer?
