## Section 1: Harvest Decisions and Fruit Processing

### Physiological vs. Technical Ripeness

The harvest decision is the first and most consequential choice of the winemaking year. The question is not simply "when are the grapes ripe?" but "ripe by which measure?"

**Technical ripeness** is measurable: Brix (sugar content, measured with a refractometer; each degree Brix roughly equals 0.55% potential alcohol after fermentation), pH (acidity intensity; typically harvest range is 3.1-3.6), and TA (titratable acidity, meaning total acid concentration in grams per liter).

**Physiological (phenolic) ripeness** is the ripeness of seeds, skins, and stalks, the structural elements. Green, unripe tannins are harsh, drying, and vegetal. Fully ripe tannins are silky, plush, and integrating. Seeds transition from green and bitter to brown and nutty. Stems transition from green and punishing to brown and tea-like as they ripen. Physiological ripeness often trails technical ripeness: a grape can be technically overripe (very high sugar) while tannins remain phenologically unripe, or vice versa.

The challenge in warm climates: by the time tannins are physiologically ripe, Brix may be at 27-29° (potential alcohol 15-16%) and acidity has fallen sharply. Winemakers in Napa Valley, Barossa, and Priorat increasingly face this dilemma. Solutions include early picks for freshness, water back addition (controversial, illegal in many EU regions), acid addition, and reverse osmosis for alcohol adjustment.

### Hand Harvest vs. Machine Harvest

**Hand Harvest:** Allows precise selection at the vine, diseased or underripe clusters can be discarded, individual berries can be selected (as in Sauternes' multiple passes, or tris). Fruit arrives at the winery in whole clusters, undamaged, with minimal oxidation and no juice run. Essential for whole-cluster fermentation, prestige sparkling wine (to preserve CO2 and prevent phenolic extraction), and any winery committed to maximum quality and selectivity.

Disadvantages: Labor-intensive, slow, expensive, and limited by available crew size during compressed harvest windows. When a storm is approaching, a large estate may not be able to complete manual harvest quickly enough.

**Machine Harvest:** Mechanical harvesters straddle vine rows and shake berries free. Fast, cost-efficient, and able to harvest at night (important in warm climates, since harvesting cool fruit preserves aromatics and reduces oxidation risk). Large estates in Bordeaux, the Languedoc, and California increasingly use machines for all but their top cuvées.

Disadvantages: Produces a mix of berries, juice, stems, and leaves; whole-cluster fermentation is not possible; damaged berries begin oxidizing immediately; unsuitable for steep slopes (Mosel, Douro, steep Côte d'Or) or for very old, fragile vine structures.

### Sorting

Fruit quality control post-harvest:

**Hand Sorting:** Workers at a sorting table remove damaged clusters, underripe berries, leaves, and MOG (material other than grapes). The level of selectivity reflects commitment to quality and is expensive in labor.

**Vibrating Sort Tables:** Mechanical vibration causes irregular (underripe, damaged) berries to roll off the table while round, plump berries remain. Relatively low cost, high throughput.

**Optical Sorting:** High-technology cameras and air jets scan each berry and remove those that don't meet defined parameters (color, size, surface integrity). Extremely precise but expensive. Used by top estates in Burgundy, Napa, and Bordeaux. Can sort by color intensity, which is useful for removing underripe red berries from a Pinot Noir lot.

> **Pro Tip:** When evaluating new producers or unfamiliar wines for your list, sorting philosophy is a useful due diligence question. "Do you sort, and at what level?" tells you a great deal about the producer's quality commitment and production costs. A producer doing optical sorting at €30,000+ per machine plus operating costs is making a very different quality investment than one relying on a single pass by part-time seasonal workers.

## Section 2: Fruit Processing and Fermentation Vessels

### Destemming and Crushing

The decision to destem (remove berries from their stems) or retain whole clusters (stems intact) is one of the most debated in modern winemaking, particularly for Pinot Noir and Syrah.

**Destemmed fermentation:** Standard practice for most red wines. Berries are separated from stems and either lightly crushed or kept whole. Results in a fruit-forward, clean fermentation profile. Without stems, tannin and structure come entirely from skins and seeds.

**Whole-cluster fermentation:** Entire clusters, berries and stems alike, are placed in the fermentation vessel uncrushed. The stems must be physiologically ripe (brown, lignified) or they contribute harsh, green tannins and vegetal character. When ripe, whole-cluster fermentation adds: spice (cinnamon, clove), structural tannin, a cool, almost "crunchy" texture, and aromatic complexity. Carbon dioxide produced by intracellular fermentation within intact berries (a minor carbonic maceration component) adds purple florality. Used by a significant proportion of top Burgundy producers (de Vogüé, Rousseau, Leroy use varying proportions). Syrah in the Northern Rhône (particularly Cornas and Côte-Rôtie) classically includes a proportion of whole clusters; in Côte-Rôtie, up to 20% whole white Viognier grapes may be co-fermented.

**Crushing:** Light crushing ruptures berry skins to release juice, starting the fermentation process. Most modern wineries destem and then lightly crush; some top estates prefer to ferment entirely whole berries within a destemmed lot (Pommard method) for fruit purity and gentle extraction.

### Pressing

**Pneumatic Press:** The modern standard. An inflatable bladder gently presses grapes with even, controlled pressure. Multiple press cycles at increasing pressure; juice from each cycle can be kept separately. The gentlest, most controllable method for high-quality pressing. Used for virtually all top white wine production and sparkling wine base wines.

**Basket Press:** Traditional wooden or stainless basket press, using vertical plate pressure. Slower than pneumatic; produces wine with slightly more phenolic extraction but also, in the hands of skilled operators, wines of remarkable texture and complexity. Preferred by some artisan producers in Burgundy and natural wine circles for the texture and micro-oxygenation it provides during pressing.

**Free-Run Juice:** The juice that flows from the grape mass before any mechanical pressure is applied, the most delicate and aromatic fraction, lowest in phenolics and tannin. In sparkling wine production, *cuvée* is the first fraction of free-run juice; the *taille* is subsequent press fractions with higher phenolic content. In still wine, free-run is typically kept separately and re-integrated with press fractions at the winemaker's discretion.

### Cold Soak (Pre-Fermentative Maceration)

Before alcoholic fermentation begins, crushed red grapes can be held at cold temperatures (5-10°C) for 3-10 days. At these temperatures, yeast is inactive and no fermentation occurs, but the cold juice extracts color compounds (anthocyanins) and aromatic compounds from skins through diffusion without extracting significant tannin. Tannin extraction requires ethanol as a solvent; without alcohol, the cold soak extracts color and aroma selectively. Used by many Pinot Noir and Gamay producers seeking maximum color and aroma without tannin harshness.

### Fermentation Vessel Materials

**Stainless Steel:** The dominant vessel in modern winemaking. Temperature-controlled (jacketed tanks allow precise cooling or heating), clean, neutral (no flavor contribution), easy to clean, and reusable indefinitely. Preserves primary fruit character and aromatics. Used for virtually all aromatic white wines (Riesling, Sauvignon Blanc, Pinot Grigio, Albariño), many red wines, and rosé.

**Concrete:** Experiencing a significant revival. Concrete is neutral in flavor (unlike oak), porous at a micro level (provides gentle oxygen exchange similar to a large-format barrel), thermally stable (concrete's mass moderates temperature swings during fermentation without active temperature control), and alkaline (which some believe buffers acidity). The concrete egg shape (developed by Nomblot) creates natural convection currents during fermentation that keep lees in suspension without mechanical stirring. Used for both whites (Sauvignon Blanc, Chardonnay) and reds. Associated with texture, roundness, and honest varietal character.

**Oak:** Barrels contribute flavor (vanilla, coconut, toast, spice, from oak lactones and guaiacol), tannin (from oak polyphenols), and controlled oxidation (micro-oxygenation through wood staves). Discussed in detail in Section 4. New oak is essentially a flavoring agent; used oak is primarily an oxygen-management vessel.

**Amphora and Qvevri:** Ancient clay vessels, where amphoras are above-ground and qvevri (qvevri is the Georgian spelling; also written as kvevri) are buried below ground, maintaining stable cool temperatures. Both are porous, providing micro-oxygenation. Qvevri are sealed with beeswax and used in traditional Georgian winemaking; some qvevri are millennia old as a technology. Extended skin contact in amphora or qvevri produces "orange wine," meaning white wines with skin contact tannins and amber color. Used by natural wine producers in Italy (Friuli, where Josko Gravner and Stanko Radikon pioneered modern amphora use), Slovenia, and Georgia.

**Plastic/Polyethylene:** Used primarily for carbonic maceration in Beaujolais, in large plastic tanks filled with whole clusters under CO2. Cost-effective for high-volume production. Not associated with quality elevation beyond its specific purpose.

> **Pro Tip:** The vessel question has become a sophisticated guest conversation topic. Guests who follow wine media will encounter debates about "concrete egg" Chardonnay or "amphora-aged" Grenache. Being able to explain concretely (pun intended) what each vessel does, and why a producer chose it, is a mark of serious technical depth that creates guest confidence and trust.

## Section 3: Alcoholic Fermentation and Extraction

### The Role of Yeast

Alcoholic fermentation is the conversion of sugar to ethanol and CO2 by yeast. This is the central chemical transformation of winemaking.

**Commercial Yeast (Saccharomyces cerevisiae strains):** Hundreds of commercial strains are available, each selected for specific properties: temperature tolerance, fermentation speed, aromatic profile, SO2 tolerance, nutrient requirements, and flocculation characteristics. Commercial yeasts are reliable, predictable, and reduce the risk of stuck fermentation. Critics argue they homogenize wine character across different terroirs and producers.

**Indigenous (Wild/Ambient) Yeasts:** The population of wild yeast and bacteria that naturally colonize the vineyard, grape surfaces, and winery equipment. Indigenous fermentations are typically slower, more unpredictable, and more temperature-sensitive. They can produce wines of greater aromatic complexity and terroir specificity, or they can produce wines with serious volatile acidity, acetic acid, or other off-characters if the indigenous population is dominated by non-*Saccharomyces* species (Candida, Pichia, Dekkera/Brettanomyces). The debate between indigenous and commercial yeast is partly a debate about control versus expression.

**Stuck Fermentation:** A fermentation that stops before all sugar has been converted to alcohol, leaving residual sugar unintentionally. Causes: nutrient deficiency (particularly nitrogen, addressed with DAP, diammonium phosphate, additions), excessively high or low temperature, high initial sugar (above 27-28° Brix), high alcohol toxicity on yeast, sulfur inhibition. Stuck fermentations can cause serious quality problems and spoilage. Management: adding nutrients early, maintaining temperature in the optimal 18-28°C range, inoculating with fresh active yeast cultures.

### Fermentation Temperature

Fermentation temperature is a major stylistic lever:

**Cool fermentation (10-18°C):** Produces aromatic, fresh, light-bodied whites. Sauvignon Blanc, Riesling, Pinot Grigio, and rosé are typically fermented cool to preserve volatile aromatic compounds. The tradeoff: cool fermentations are slow and require careful yeast nutrition management.

**Warm fermentation (20-32°C):** Produces wines with greater phenolic extraction, body, and complexity. Red wine fermentation typically runs 25-30°C; some winemakers push to 32-35°C for intense extraction. Warm fermentation speeds yeast activity and can risk stuck fermentation or volatile acidity issues if not managed.

### Chaptalisation and Acidification

**Chaptalisation:** Adding sugar to the must before or during fermentation to increase final alcohol level. Named after Jean-Antoine Chaptal, Napoleon's agriculture minister. Legal in cooler climates (Burgundy, Bordeaux, Champagne, Germany at lower quality levels, most of France) and strictly prohibited in warm climates (California, Spain, Southern Italy, Australia). The addition of up to 2% potential alcohol is standard in lean Burgundy vintages; over-chaptalisation is a quality problem producing alcoholic wines without the fruit concentration to support the alcohol.

**Acidification:** In warm climates, grapes often arrive with dangerously low TA. Tartaric acid addition (the most common, producing a firm, clean acid profile) or citric acid (less common; metabolized by bacteria, can cause problems) is permitted in most New World countries and in warmer EU regions under strict limits. Illegal in Burgundy and Champagne for still wines. The use of acid additions in top Napa Cabernet is not discussed openly but is an open secret.

**De-Acidification:** In cold climates or very cool vintages, must acidity may be excessively high. Calcium carbonate addition (precipitates tartrate), double-salt deacidification, or encouraging malolactic fermentation address this.

### Extraction Techniques for Red Wine

**Pump-Over (Remontage):** Juice is pumped from the bottom of the tank and sprayed over the cap of skins and seeds floating at the top. Provides oxygen to the yeast (stimulating fermentation), mixes the cap (preventing desiccation and bacterial growth), and extracts color, tannin, and flavor compounds from the skins. The standard extraction technique for Bordeaux varieties and most large-production reds. Frequency and intensity of pump-overs is a key winemaking variable; more pump-overs mean more extraction.

**Punch-Down (Pigeage):** A plate, plunger, or worker's feet pushes the cap down into the fermenting juice. Gentler than pump-overs (less aeration, less mechanical disruption of berry skins), better for fragile, thin-skinned varieties. The traditional technique for Pinot Noir in Burgundy. Hand punch-downs in open-top fermenters are labor-intensive and still practiced by top producers (Domaine de la Romanée-Conti, Rousseau, Dujac).

**Rack and Return (Délestage):** The fermenting juice is completely drained from the tank, the cap falls and compacts, and the juice is then pumped back over the cap from the top. Intensive extraction, significant aeration. Used for varieties requiring maximum extraction or in years with underripe tannins that benefit from more extended contact.

**Carbonic and Semi-Carbonic Maceration:** Discussed separately in Section 5.

**Extended Maceration:** Post-fermentative skin contact, meaning the wine is left on its skins for weeks or months after alcoholic fermentation is complete. As alcohol levels are high during post-fermentative maceration, harsh green tannins gradually polymerize into longer, softer, more complex tannin chains. Used for traditionally styled Barolos (the Old School approach, as opposed to modern shorter-maceration rotary fermenters), traditional Bordeaux, and Rioja Gran Reserva. The result: wines with extraordinary tannin structure and longevity but requiring significant aging before approachability.

## Section 4: Malolactic Fermentation and Élevage

### Malolactic Fermentation (MLF)

MLF is a bacterial conversion, not a true fermentation, performed by lactic acid bacteria (LAB), primarily *Oenococcus oeni*. The sharp dicarboxylic malic acid (tart, green apple) is converted to the softer monocarboxylic lactic acid (soft, creamy, yogurt-like) plus CO2.

**In red wine:** MLF is encouraged, even required, in virtually all dry red wine production. The reduction in total acidity softens the wine, and the conversion from malic to lactic acid adds complexity and stability. Wines retaining significant malic acid are vulnerable to spontaneous MLF in bottle, causing CO2, haze, and off-flavors in warm storage conditions.

**In full-bodied whites:** Deliberate MLF is a stylistic choice for wines where richness and body are priorities, including Chardonnay (White Burgundy style, California Chardonnay, White Bordeaux). Full MLF in Chardonnay produces butter (diacetyl from LAB activity), cream, and yogurt notes alongside reduced fresh fruit acidity. Many winemakers allow partial MLF, stopping the conversion partway through to retain some freshness while adding texture.

**In aromatic whites:** MLF is blocked. Riesling, Sauvignon Blanc (certain styles), Albariño, Grüner Veltliner, and Muscadet for young drinking all retain their primary acidity by preventing MLF. Blocking methods: early and sufficient SO2 addition (sulfur inhibits LAB); lowering fermentation and aging temperature below 12°C (LAB are temperature-sensitive); sterile filtration to remove bacteria; rapid bottling to prevent MLF starting post-bottling.

### Oak Barrel Aging in Depth

**Barrel Size:** The size of the vessel determines the ratio of wine surface area to volume in contact with oak. A 225-liter Bordeaux barrique (the standard Bordeaux barrel) has the highest oak-to-wine contact ratio; a 500-liter demi-muid has less; a 600-liter puncheon has even less; a 20-hectoliter foudre (large oval cask) imparts minimal oak flavor. Burgundy uses the 228-liter pièce Bourguignonne, almost identical to the Bordeaux barrique in its contact ratio. As new oak intensity becomes less fashionable, larger-format vessels (demi-muids, foudres, concrete eggs) are increasingly used to provide oxygen management without oak flavor.

**New vs. Used Oak:** New oak contributes maximum oak flavor compounds (vanillin, lactones, eugenol, guaiacol from toasting) and the most tannin. Second-year oak (barrique already used for one vintage) has approximately half the new oak impact. Third-year and older oak is essentially a neutral vessel, providing micro-oxygenation without oak flavor. Top Burgundy producers typically use 20-30% new oak for village wines, 30-50% for Premier Cru, and up to 100% for Grand Cru (though the trend is moving toward lower new oak proportions even at the top).

**Oak Origin:** French oak (from the forests of Allier, Tronçais, Vosges, Nevers, Limousin) is tight-grained, with fine tannins and subtle spice notes, and is the dominant choice for Burgundy, Bordeaux, and the Rhône. American oak (*Quercus alba*) is wider-grained, less expensive, and releases flavor compounds faster and more intensely, with pronounced vanilla, coconut, and dill notes. Historically used in Rioja (still traditional in some wineries) and in Australian Shiraz. Hungarian oak, Slovenian oak, and other Eastern European oaks are increasingly used as cost-effective alternatives to French.

**Toasting:** During the barrel manufacturing process, the staves are toasted over an open fire. Light toast preserves more of the raw oak's tannin and woody flavor. Medium toast, the most common, balances flavor compounds with tannin. Heavy toast adds smoke, chocolate, coffee, and roasted notes while burning off some of the harsh oak tannins.

### Lees Aging and Bâtonnage

Lees are the solid sediment remaining after fermentation, primarily dead yeast cells (yeast lees), but also grape pulp, tartrate crystals, and other compounds. Aging wine on its lees (sur lie) provides:

**Autolysis:** As yeast cells die and break down (autolysis), they release mannoproteins, polysaccharides, and fatty acids that add texture (creaminess, viscosity), complexity (brioche, toast, bready notes), and protection against oxidation. Champagne's extended lees aging (minimum 15 months for non-vintage; 3 years for vintage) is the most extreme example of autolytic development in wine.

**Bâtonnage:** Stirring the lees with a stick (*bâton*) to keep them in suspension and in contact with the wine. Most commonly used for white Burgundy and other barrel-fermented whites. Weekly or biweekly bâtonnage during the first months of élevage adds body, creaminess, and yeast autolysis characters. Some winemakers prefer not to bâtonner, allowing the lees to settle and providing cleaner wines with less reductive risk.

### Blending

Blending is one of the winemaker's highest skills. It operates at multiple levels:

**Lot blending:** Wines from different parcels, varieties, or clones are tasted and assembled. Different lots contribute different elements: one might have aromatic intensity, another structural tannin, a third mid-palate weight.

**Barrel selection:** Individual barrels of the same wine taste differently (micro-terroir of the barrel itself, including oak grain, position in cellar, and slight temperature variation). The top selection from across barrels forms the Grand Vin; second and third selections are declassified to second or third wines.

**Assemblage:** The traditional Bordeaux term for the blending of Cabernet Sauvignon, Merlot, Cabernet Franc, Petit Verdot, and Malbec in varying proportions each vintage. The assemblage decision is made in spring following the harvest after an intensive tasting process comparing varieties, parcels, and lots.

### Stabilization and Fining

**Cold Stabilization:** Chilling wine to near-freezing (-4°C) for 1-2 weeks causes tartrate crystals (potassium bitartrate, the "wine diamonds" sometimes seen in wine glasses or on corks) to precipitate from solution. The alternative is tartrate stabilization with KHT inhibitors or electrodialysis. Many quality-focused producers skip cold stabilization, accepting the visual appearance of crystals in bottle.

**Protein Stabilization:** White wines prone to protein haze (caused by grape proteins reacting with tannins at high temperatures) require treatment with bentonite, a charged clay that binds and precipitates proteins. Bentonite strips aromatic and textural compounds along with proteins; the amount and timing of addition matters for quality.

**Fining:** Adding a coagulant that binds to specific compounds (tannins, proteins, color) and falls out of suspension, taking the bound compounds with it. Common fining agents:

- *Egg whites (albumin):* Traditional for red wine; binds harsh tannins selectively; the gentlest red wine fining method. Standard practice in Burgundy, with multiple egg whites per barrel.
- *Isinglass (fish bladder gelatin):* Traditional for white wine; very gentle; produces wine of fine clarity. Not vegan.
- *Gelatin:* Protein-based; effective for tannin reduction.
- *Bentonite:* As above, strips protein; used for whites.
- *Casein (milk protein):* Used for some whites; not vegan; effective for color stabilization.
- *PVPP (polyvinylpolypyrrolidone):* Synthetic; binds oxidation products; acceptable for vegan wines.

**Filtration:** Removal of particles and microorganisms through filtration membranes. *Rough filtration* (diatomaceous earth / cellulose pad) removes gross sediment. *Sterile filtration* (0.45 micron membrane) removes all yeast and bacteria, producing a microbiologically stable, shelf-stable wine. The quality debate centers on whether filtration strips aromatic and textural compounds alongside undesirable particles; many quality producers prefer to fine lightly and bottle without filtration, accepting the risk of bottle variation.

> **Pro Tip:** Unfiltered and unfined wines are increasingly a point of pride for producers and a guest communication opportunity. Understanding what fining and filtration do, and what their absence means (potential sediment, potential bottle variation, greater texture and complexity in theory), allows you to set guest expectations correctly and prevent returns or complaints based on perceived defects that are actually a sign of minimal intervention.

## Section 5: Natural Wine, Specific Techniques, and the Debate Over Intervention

### Natural Wine Philosophy

Natural wine is not a defined legal category, not a certification, and not a consistent style. It is better understood as a spectrum of minimal-intervention winemaking practices united by a philosophical commitment to avoiding additions and manipulation.

Core natural wine practices:
- Organic or biodynamic farming as a prerequisite
- Indigenous (wild) yeast fermentation only, with no commercial yeasts
- No addition of sulfur (SO2) or minimal addition at bottling only (typically below 20-30 mg/L total SO2)
- No fining agents
- No filtration
- No acidification, chaptalisation, concentration, or other additions
- No micro-oxygenation

Resulting wine characteristics: Higher textural interest (no filtration), sometimes greater aromatic complexity and site-specificity, and often significant bottle-to-bottle variation. The risks: volatile acidity (ethyl acetate, with a nail polish remover aroma), brett (Brettanomyces, producing barnyard, band-aid, and smoke notes), refermentation in bottle (residual yeast activity on residual sugar or malic acid), and oxidation.

The quality spectrum is wide. Producers like Jean-Pierre Rietsch in Alsace, Thierry Allemand in Cornas, and Catherine and Pierre Breton in Bourgueil make extraordinary, stable natural wines with genuine terroir expression. The lower end of natural wine production, particularly very low SO2 or zero-SO2 wines, carries real fault risk that a thoughtful wine director must evaluate on a bottle-by-bottle basis before adding to a program.

> **Pro Tip:** Natural wine is one of the most customer-facing debates in modern wine. Your approach as a director should be empirical: taste widely, know each wine in your program intimately, and be able to speak fluently about natural wine philosophy without either dismissing it ideologically or embracing it uncritically. The best stance: "We select natural wines that are technically sound and express their terroir clearly. We taste everything before we pour it."

### Carbonic Maceration

Carbonic maceration is an intracellular fermentation technique, meaning fermentation begins inside intact, undamaged berries rather than in extracted juice.

**Process:** Whole intact berry clusters are loaded into a sealed tank filled with CO2. In the CO2 atmosphere, enzymes within each berry initiate fermentation intracellularly. The berry converts a small amount of sugar to alcohol (1-2%) and produces glycerol, primary aromatic compounds (isoamyl acetate, ethyl acetate in trace amounts), and color without extracting tannin. Eventually, the berry's cell wall breaks down under its own weight and the accumulated liquid; the remaining sugar is fermented in the normal way by yeast. The tank is then pressed.

**Result:** Wines with brilliant purple-red color, intense red fruit (kirsch, raspberry, strawberry), low tannin, high glycerol (soft, juicy mouthfeel), very fresh acidity, and minimal structure for aging. Beaujolais Nouveau is the most famous product.

**Semi-Carbonic Maceration:** In Beaujolais and other regions, the natural process involves whole clusters placed in an open tank without deliberate CO2 flushing. The lower berries are crushed by the weight above them and begin conventional yeast fermentation, which produces CO2, providing a CO2 atmosphere for the upper intact berries. This is the traditional Beaujolais method and produces wines with slightly more tannin and structure than pure carbonic maceration.

### The Solera System

The solera is a fractional blending system used to produce wines of consistent style across multiple vintages. It is the foundation of Sherry, Madeira (some), and certain aged Tawny Ports.

**Structure:** A solera consists of multiple rows (scales/*criaderas*) of barrels stacked or arranged in succession. The oldest wine is in the lowest row (the *solera* proper). When wine is drawn off for bottling, no more than one-third of each barrel is removed. The drawn portion is replaced with wine from the next oldest scale, which is in turn topped from the scale above, which is replenished with the most recent vintage. The oldest barrels may contain traces of wine from many decades back, creating a continuity of style across vintages.

**For Sherry:** The solera system is essential because Sherry's style is defined not by vintage variation but by the consistent expression of the house style developed over generations. Soleras for Fino, Amontillado, and Oloroso may run 20-50+ years deep. The biological aging under *flor* yeast (discussed in Module 18) continues in the barrel as wine is replenished.

### Appassimento

Appassimento is the drying of harvested grapes, on mats, in drying chambers, or in traditional *fruttai* (drying lofts), to concentrate sugars, acids, and flavor compounds through partial dehydration. As water evaporates, all dissolved solids concentrate.

**Key wines:** Amarone della Valpolicella (Veneto), made from Corvina, Corvinone, and Rondinella grapes dried for 90-120 days, losing 30-40% of their weight, then fermented dry to 14-17% ABV, producing a wine of extraordinary power, dried fruit, chocolate, and tar. Recioto della Valpolicella uses the same process but with fermentation stopped early to retain sweetness. Sforzato di Valtellina (Lombardy) is made from dried Nebbiolo. Passito di Pantelleria (Sicily) is made from dried Muscat.

**Quality considerations:** Slow, careful drying in well-ventilated fruttai produces the finest concentration. The risk is *botrytis* infection during drying (desirable for Recioto, problematic for Amarone, where it produces musty, off-putting notes and is managed through strict fungal control). The best Amarone producers, including Dal Forno Romano, Quintarelli, and Bertani, combine selective harvest with meticulous drying protocols and extended barrel aging.

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| Brix | A measurement of dissolved sugar in grape juice; approximately 1° Brix = 0.55% potential alcohol |
| Physiological Ripeness | The ripeness of seeds, skins, and stems, meaning structural maturity; often lags behind technical (sugar) ripeness |
| Chaptalisation | Addition of sugar to must before or during fermentation to increase final alcohol level; legal in cool climates, prohibited in warm ones |
| Pigeage | Punch-down extraction technique; the floating cap is pushed down into fermenting juice; gentler than pump-overs; traditional for Pinot Noir |
| Remontage | Pump-over extraction; juice pumped from the bottom of the tank and sprayed over the cap |
| Délestage | Rack and return; the fermenting juice is completely drained from the tank, the cap compacts, then juice is returned over the cap for intensive extraction |
| MLF | Malolactic fermentation; bacterial conversion of sharp malic acid to soft lactic acid; reduces total acidity and adds textural complexity |
| Bâtonnage | Stirring of lees with a stick to keep them in suspension and enhance autolytic character; adds texture and creaminess to barrel-aged whites |
| Autolysis | Breakdown of dead yeast cells releasing mannoproteins and polysaccharides; adds bread, biscuit, and cream complexity; essential to Champagne character |
| Free-Run Juice | Juice flowing from the grape mass without mechanical pressure; most delicate fraction, lowest in tannin and phenolics |
| Carbonic Maceration | Intracellular fermentation inside whole intact berries under CO2 atmosphere; produces low-tannin, fruity, fresh wine; classic Beaujolais technique |
| Solera | Fractional blending system drawing wine from the oldest barrels and replenishing from progressively younger ones; used for Sherry and some fortified wines |
| Appassimento | Drying of harvested grapes on mats or racks to concentrate sugars and flavor; used for Amarone, Recioto, and Sforzato |
| Élevage | French term for the raising/aging of wine post-fermentation through barrel aging, lees contact, and blending before bottling |
| Stuck Fermentation | A fermentation that stops prematurely before all sugar is converted to alcohol; caused by nutrient deficiency, temperature extremes, or high initial sugar |
