# Module 15: Viticulture & Terroir

**Level:** 3 — Wine Director Certification
**Audience:** Wine Directors, Head Sommeliers, Beverage Program Directors

**Certification Requirement:** Required. Minimum passing score of 80% on module quiz.

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this module, you will be able to:

- Define and critically evaluate the concept of terroir, including the academic debate surrounding its validity and the tension between terroir and winemaker influence
- Identify and explain the functional anatomy of the grapevine and the complete annual growth cycle, including key risk points at each phenological stage
- Differentiate between major soil types and explain the mechanisms by which soil composition influences vine physiology and wine character
- Analyze climate at the macro, meso, and micro levels and articulate the roles of topography, bodies of water, and aspect in shaping wine style
- Compare and contrast viticulture philosophies — conventional, sustainable, organic, biodynamic, and natural — and evaluate their relevance for program development and guest communication

## Section 1: Terroir — Definition, Debate, and Practical Meaning

### Origins of the Concept

The word terroir derives from the French *terre* (earth, land), but its meaning extends far beyond soil. In its broadest application, terroir describes the complete natural environment in which a vine grows — and, in the more expansive French understanding, the human traditions that have developed within that environment over centuries. It is simultaneously a viticultural concept, a philosophical claim, and a marketing tool.

The idea that place imprints itself on wine is ancient. Monks in Burgundy noted during the medieval period that wine from one plot tasted different from wine grown meters away. The Cistercian monks of Clos de Vougeot famously tasted the soil to understand what a plot might produce. This empirical observation over centuries became codified into the appellation system: the premise that geography can and should be legally protected because it is meaningfully expressed in the wine.

### Components of Terroir

Terroir is not a single variable but a system of interacting factors:

**Soil and Subsoil:** Soil type (limestone, clay, gravel, granite, schist, volcanic) determines drainage, water retention, heat retention, and nutrient availability. Subsoil structure determines root depth and access to water tables. The interaction between surface soil, subsoil, and bedrock shapes vine stress patterns and mineral uptake — though note that scientific consensus on how minerals in soil translate to mineral perception in wine is contested.

**Climate:** Temperature, rainfall, sunlight hours, humidity, and wind patterns at the regional (macroclimate), site (mesoclimate), and vine-row level (microclimate). Climate shapes the ripening window, disease pressure, and the balance of sugar accumulation versus acid retention.

**Aspect and Altitude:** A south-facing slope in the northern hemisphere receives more direct solar radiation than a flat site — critical in marginal climates like Burgundy or the Mosel. Altitude reduces temperature (approximately 0.6°C per 100 meters), extends the growing season, and increases diurnal temperature variation.

**The Human Element (Savoir-Faire):** The most contested component. Some definitions of terroir include the accumulated human knowledge — pruning choices, training systems, harvest timing, winemaking approach — developed over generations in response to a place. Others argue that including human factors collapses terroir into something too vague to be meaningful. The debate is real and ongoing.

### The Academic Debate

The scientific literature on terroir is divided. Critics point out that there is no confirmed physiological mechanism by which soil minerals are selectively absorbed by vines and translated into perceptible mineral aromas or flavors in wine. The "minerality" perceived in Chablis or Mosel Riesling likely results from sulfur compounds, reducing conditions during fermentation, or residual CO2 — not limestone or slate being expressed in the glass.

Defenders of terroir argue that the empirical evidence is overwhelming at the human perception level, even if the mechanisms are not fully understood. Blind tasters consistently distinguish wines from specific sites. The consistent profile of Chambolle-Musigny versus Gevrey-Chambertin — despite the same grape, same winemakers, same techniques — suggests that place matters.

The honest answer for a wine director: terroir is real in the sense that place consistently shapes wine character. The mechanism is incompletely understood. The concept is invaluable for communicating about wine but should be used with intellectual honesty rather than mysticism.

### Terroir vs. Winemaker Influence

One of the central tensions in fine wine discourse: to what extent does terroir express itself independent of human decisions? A winemaker can suppress terroir through heavy extraction, excessive new oak, concentration techniques, and homogenizing additions. A winemaker can reveal terroir through restraint, low intervention, and site-specific selection.

The debate about "natural wine" is partly a debate about terroir — the claim being that minimal intervention in the cellar allows the vineyard to speak. But this framing is challenged by evidence that some natural wine techniques (ambient yeast fermentations, no SO2 additions) introduce volatility and off-characters that arguably obscure site expression as much as they reveal it.

> **Pro Tip:** When building a list narrative around terroir, be precise about what you mean. "This wine expresses the limestone soils of Chablis" is a more defensible claim if you can articulate what that expression actually is — high acidity, linear texture, saline finish — rather than invoking terroir as a vague intensifier. Guests at the Director level expect nuance.

## Section 2: Vine Anatomy and Taxonomy

### Grapevine Anatomy

Understanding vine anatomy matters for viticulture decisions and for communicating with growers. The vine's structure directly affects how it interacts with soil, climate, and management.

**Root System:** The root system has two primary components. Tap roots grow vertically, often reaching several meters into the subsoil and bedrock in search of water and nutrients. This is the mechanism by which older vines access deeply stored water reserves during drought — the reason old vine wines are often praised for consistency across hot vintages. Lateral roots spread horizontally through the topsoil, absorbing surface water and nutrients. In young vines, the root system is shallow; in old vines, it can penetrate 10 to 15 meters. The composition of subsoil therefore matters more to old vines than young ones.

**Trunk:** The perennial woody structure above the soil line. The trunk stores carbohydrates (as starch) during dormancy, which are mobilized at budburst to fuel early season growth. Trunk diameter increases with vine age; the relationship between old trunk and old roots is part of why age correlates with quality.

**Cordons and Canes:** Cordons are semi-permanent horizontal extensions of the trunk, forming the framework on which spurs or canes are trained. Canes are one-year-old wood — the source of the current season's shoots. In Guyot training, canes are selected annually and tied to wires; in cordon training, spurs are left on permanent cordons. The choice of cane versus spur pruning affects yield and cluster distribution.

**Shoots, Leaves, and Tendrils:** Shoots grow from buds on the cane or cordon, producing leaves, tendrils, and inflorescences (which become grape clusters). Leaves are the photosynthetic engine of the vine — their area, angle, and exposure determine how effectively the vine converts sunlight into sugar. Tendrils anchor the shoot to support wires. Shoot positioning (VSP training) is partly about managing canopy density to optimize light interception.

**Grape Clusters:** Each inflorescence becomes a cluster. Cluster size, berry size, skin-to-pulp ratio, and seed content all affect wine quality. Small berries mean a higher skin-to-pulp ratio and therefore higher tannin and color concentration relative to juice — this is one reason old vines and stressed vines often produce superior wine.

### Grapevine Taxonomy

The genus *Vitis* comprises approximately 60 species, but virtually all fine wine production uses *Vitis vinifera*, the European grapevine. *Vinifera* is thought to have originated in the South Caucasus (modern Georgia, Armenia, Azerbaijan) and spread westward with human civilization.

**American Vitis Species:** Several North American species — *Vitis labrusca* (the "fox grape," used for Concord and Niagara), *Vitis rupestris*, *Vitis berlandieri*, and *Vitis riparia* — are naturally resistant to phylloxera and many fungal diseases. They produce wines with a distinctive "foxy" (ethyl acetate, methyl anthranilate) character considered undesirable in fine wine contexts. Their importance lies in their use as rootstock.

**Phylloxera and Rootstock:** Phylloxera (*Daktulosphaira vitifoliae*) is a microscopic aphid-like insect that attacks *vinifera* root systems, causing vine death within a few years. It devastated European vineyards in the late 19th century. The solution — which remains universal today — is to graft *vinifera* scions onto phylloxera-resistant American rootstocks. Different rootstocks confer different properties: vigor, drought tolerance, soil adaptability, and calcium tolerance vary by rootstock selection. The choice of rootstock is now recognized as part of terroir expression.

Ungrafted *vinifera* vines survive only in a handful of locations: sandy soils (phylloxera cannot move through deep sand — Colares, Portugal; Sables du Golfe du Lion, France), some volcanic islands (Santorini, Chile's coastal regions), and highly isolated areas. Quinta do Noval's Nacional plot in the Douro — producing ungrafted Vintage Port from pre-phylloxera vines — is one of the most famous and highly priced wines in the world, partly because of its ungrafted status.

> **Pro Tip:** The rootstock story is underutilized in sommelier education and almost entirely absent from guest wine conversations. For a sophisticated guest interested in old vines or biodynamic viticulture, introducing the concept of ungrafted vines and phylloxera history adds a layer of narrative that distinguishes your program.

## Section 3: The Vine Growth Cycle and Risk Points

### Dormancy (December–February, Northern Hemisphere)

During winter dormancy, the vine is metabolically inactive. Carbohydrates are stored in the wood. Pruning occurs during dormancy — it is the single most important viticultural decision of the year, setting the framework for yield, canopy structure, and grape quality. Bud count determines potential shoot and therefore cluster number. Over-pruning limits yield too severely; under-pruning results in excessive vigor and dilution.

**Risk:** Winter freeze. Temperatures below -15°C to -20°C can kill buds or even destroy the trunk. This is a significant concern in continental climates (Chablis, Champagne, some parts of Burgundy, Germany's Mosel and Rhine). The winter of 1956 devastated large portions of the Bordeaux region, particularly older vine plantings in Pomerol and St-Émilion.

### Budburst (March–April, NH)

The vine emerges from dormancy when soil temperatures reach approximately 10°C. Buds swell, burst, and produce the first green shoots. This is the most phenologically vulnerable moment in the growing season.

**Risk:** Spring frost. Once buds have burst, exposed green tissue is killed by temperatures below -2°C. Frost events in April and May have caused catastrophic losses in Burgundy (2016, 2021), Chablis, Champagne, and the Loire. Frost mitigation techniques include smudge pots (bougies), wind machines (which invert the temperature inversion layer), and helicopter overflights. Aspersion (spraying water over buds, which forms an insulating ice layer) is effective but water-intensive.

### Shoot and Leaf Growth (April–June, NH)

Rapid vegetative growth follows budburst. Canopy management — shoot positioning, leaf removal, green harvesting — begins. The vine's energy is partly competitive: excessive shoot growth can come at the expense of berry development.

**Risk:** Excessive vigor. Vines on rich soils or with too much water access produce excessive canopy, shading the fruit zone and creating a humid microclimate susceptible to disease (botrytis, powdery mildew, downy mildew). Canopy management through leaf removal and shoot thinning addresses this.

### Flowering and Fruit Set (May–June, NH)

Inflorescences become flowers. Pollination is wind-assisted and largely self-pollinating. After pollination, berries begin to form. The proportion of flowers that successfully set as berries is the "fruit set rate."

**Risk:** Coulure and Millerandage. Coulure is the failure of pollinated flowers to set as berries — caused by cold, wet, or windy weather during flowering. Severe coulure can reduce crop size by 30-60%. Millerandage is the formation of undersized, seedless berries (shot berries) alongside normal berries — also caused by poor flowering conditions. While coulure is a yield catastrophe, millerandage can sometimes produce intensely concentrated shot berries that, if selectively harvested, contribute to wine quality (notably in some Mosel Rieslings and Sauternes).

### Veraison (July–August, NH)

Veraison is the onset of berry ripening. Berries soften, change color (red varieties turn from green to red/black; white varieties turn from green to translucent yellow), and begin accumulating sugar while acid levels begin to decline. Physiologically, the vine transitions from a growth phase to a ripening phase.

**Risk:** Heat stress, sunburn, and uneven ripening. In hot vintages, berries can sunburn (solar exposure causes browning and off-flavors). Extreme heat can halt ripening (vine "shutdown" above 35-40°C). Uneven veraison — where berries in the same cluster ripen at different rates — creates challenges at harvest. Climate change has pushed veraison earlier in many regions, compressing the post-veraison ripening window.

### Harvest (August–October, NH)

Harvest timing is the culminating viticultural decision. It represents the integration of sugar accumulation, acid retention, phenolic ripeness, and flavor development. Early harvest preserves acidity and produces lower-alcohol wines with fresher profiles. Late harvest produces riper, richer, higher-alcohol wines with softer acidity.

**Risk:** Rain and rot. Harvest rain is the perennial enemy in Atlantic-influenced regions. Rain near harvest dilutes juice, promotes gray rot (botrytis cinerea in its undesirable form), and can physically split berry skins. Swift harvest decisions — and access to large hand-picking crews for fast harvest — are the primary defense. The great Bordeaux vintages are partly defined by dry September weather (2000, 2005, 2009, 2010, 2016).

## Section 4: Soil Types and Their Influence on Wine

### Limestone and Chalk

Limestone is calcium carbonate bedrock, formed from compressed marine sediments. Chalk is soft, porous limestone. Both provide excellent drainage (important for vine stress and root depth) while retaining some water within the pore structure. Limestone warms slowly but holds heat well. Its high calcium content and alkaline pH affect nutrient availability and vine metabolism.

**Key regions:** Champagne (chalk — the Côte des Blancs and Montagne de Reims are fundamentally chalk-based); Burgundy's Côte d'Or (limestone and marl — Kimmeridgian limestone at Chablis is particularly notable, the same geological formation that surfaces in the Upper Loire at Sancerre); Pouilly-Fumé; Jerez (albariza chalk); Marlborough, New Zealand (limestone-influenced).

Limestone-grown wines are often associated with high natural acidity, linear texture, and a mineral/saline quality — though the mechanism of this association is debated.

### Clay

Clay soils have small particle size, high water retention, and poor drainage. They warm slowly in spring (delaying budburst, which can be an advantage in frost-prone areas) and remain cool. They are fertile and support vigorous growth — which requires careful canopy management.

**Key regions:** Pomerol (blue clay subsoil beneath gravel and sand — the specific clay geology of the plateau around Pétrus's vineyard, with a high proportion of iron-rich clay, is considered central to Merlot's remarkable richness there); parts of Burgundy's Côte de Nuits; Pessac-Léognan's white wines (clay-gravel mix).

Clay-grown wines are often associated with weight, roundness, and lower tannin fineness — Merlot thrives in clay for these reasons.

### Gravel

Gravel drains rapidly (stressing the vine into producing concentrated fruit), retains solar heat during the day and radiates it back to the vine at night (extending ripening into cooler evenings), and forces roots to penetrate deeply for water. Gravel soils are typically nutrient-poor, limiting vine vigor.

**Key regions:** The Médoc's celebrated gravel mounds (croupes) — deep Günzian gravel at Pauillac (Latour, Mouton, Lafite), Margaux, and St-Julien is considered the physical basis for the Left Bank's Cabernet Sauvignon quality. Graves/Pessac-Léognan (the name means "gravel"). New Zealand's Gimblett Gravels (Hawke's Bay).

### Granite

Granite is an igneous rock — coarse-grained, acidic (in the geological sense), nutrient-poor, and very well-drained. It breaks down into sandy, granular soils. Low nutrients force vine stress and small berry production. The resulting wines are often highly concentrated with excellent natural acidity.

**Key regions:** Northern Rhône (Côte-Rôtie, Condrieu, Hermitage, Crozes-Hermitage — the granite of the Massif Central is the foundation of Syrah's northern expression); Beaujolais (granite crus in the north — Moulin-à-Vent, Fleurie, Morgon — versus lighter sandy granite soils in the south); Alsace (granite in Ribeauvillé and Andlau); Muscadet.

Granite wines are often described as having a steely, stony character with high tension — though as noted, the mechanism of this perception remains debated.

### Volcanic and Basaltic Soils

Volcanic soils derive from lava flows, ash deposits, or pumice. They vary widely by age and composition but are typically dark, heat-absorbing, porous, mineral-rich, and low in organic matter. They are associated with wines of pronounced minerality and high natural acidity.

**Key regions:** Etna (Sicily) — ancient black basalt flows at high altitude; Santorini (Greece) — volcanic ash and pumice, extremely old ungrafted *Assyrtiko* vines trained in basket form (kouloura) to protect against wind; Canary Islands (Lanzarote — vines planted in volcanic ash pits surrounded by low rock walls to collect moisture); Soave (volcanic basalt in the Classico zone); Champagne (traces of volcanic origin in parts of the Côte des Bar).

### Schist and Slate

Schist and slate are metamorphic rocks with layered, plate-like structure. They drain exceptionally well (important on steep slopes), absorb heat during the day, and radiate it back at night — extending the ripening window in cool climates. Their layered structure allows vine roots to penetrate deeply through cracks. Associated with pronounced minerality and sapidity.

**Key regions:** Mosel (grey slate — *Grauschiefer* — and red slate — *Rotschiefer* — with the latter associated with particularly concentrated, peppery Rieslings, most famously on the Mosel's steepest slopes: Bernkasteler Doctor, Wehlener Sonnenuhr, Ürziger Würzgarten); Priorat (licorella — black slate and quartz — the foundation of Garnacha and Cariñena concentration); Duriense/Douro (schist — slate-like rock underlying the Douro Valley, again associated with concentration through vine stress); Wachau (gneiss and primary rock — functionally similar to schist in drainage and heat retention).

### Sand

Sandy soils drain rapidly, warm quickly, and have very low fertility and water retention. They are also — critically — almost impossible for phylloxera to navigate. The phylloxera louse cannot move through deep, dry sand, meaning that ungrafted *vinifera* vines can survive in sandy soils.

**Key regions:** Colares (Portugal) — ungrafted pre-phylloxera vines in deep coastal sand, producing some of the world's most unusual and historically significant wines; Sables du Golfe du Lion (Southern France) — ungrafted vines on Mediterranean beach sand; parts of Pomerol have sand on the plateau's fringes.

> **Pro Tip:** The soil type framework is one of the most useful organizational tools for teaching staff about regional wine character. A simple "soil chart" — limestone/chalk, clay, gravel, granite, volcanic, schist, sand — with two or three key regions and their characteristic wine styles beside each entry is one of the most effective staff training handouts you can create. It gives non-technical servers a defensible, consistent framework for talking about wine at the table.

## Section 5: Climate, Topography, and Vineyard Management

### Climate Levels

**Macroclimate** describes the broad regional climate. Maritime climates (Bordeaux, Burgundy, Loire, Champagne; Willamette Valley; Marlborough) are influenced by proximity to ocean, producing moderate temperatures, significant rainfall, and vintage variation. Continental climates (Alsace, inland Germany, Burgundy's inland sites, parts of Eastern Europe) are more extreme — hotter summers, colder winters, lower rainfall, and greater vintage variability. Mediterranean climates (Southern Rhône, Provence, Southern Italy, California Coast, McLaren Vale) are warm, dry, and sunny — lower disease pressure, higher sugar accumulation, riper styles.

**Mesoclimate** describes the climate of a specific vineyard or hillside slope — the scale at which aspect, slope angle, altitude, and proximity to water matter. The difference between two adjacent slopes in Burgundy is a mesoclimate difference.

**Microclimate** describes the climate within an individual vine canopy or vine row — affected by canopy management, vine spacing, and training system. Winemakers and viticulturalists work at the microclimate level through canopy management decisions.

### The Role of Rivers

Rivers moderate temperatures (large bodies of water heat and cool slowly, buffering extremes); provide morning fog or mist (important for botrytis in Sauternes, humidity management in the Mosel); create corridors for air movement; and influence soil deposition (alluvial gravel transported by rivers forms the gravel banks of the Médoc and Graves).

**Mosel:** The deeply incised valley traps heat, protects from cold north winds, and focuses solar radiation on south-facing slate slopes. The river's curves create microclimatic variation between each bend.

**Loire:** Long river that moderates Atlantic influence and provides different soil exposures along its course — from the granite of the Upper Loire (Pouilly-Fumé, Sancerre) through the tuffeau (limestone) of Touraine (Vouvray, Chinon) to the schist of Anjou (Savennières) and the gneiss of Muscadet country.

**Garonne and Ciron (Sauternes):** The cold Ciron River meets the warmer Garonne in Sauternes, creating the perfect conditions for morning mist that promotes botrytis cinerea development on Sémillon and Sauvignon Blanc grapes.

### Mountains and Rain Shadow

Mountains create rain shadows: prevailing winds drop precipitation on the windward side; the leeward side is drier and often warmer. This is critical for several of the world's finest wine regions:

**Vosges Mountains:** Protect Alsace from Atlantic rainfall. Alsace is one of the driest regions in France as a result, enabling full phenolic ripeness. The same latitude as Champagne, but far warmer and drier.

**Andes (Mendoza):** The Argentine Andes create an extreme rain shadow — Mendoza receives approximately 200mm of rainfall annually. The climate is sunny and dry; irrigation from Andean snowmelt is essential. Altitude (vineyard elevations from 800 to 1500+ meters) provides temperature moderation and diurnal variation critical for maintaining acidity.

**Wachau:** The Danube cuts through the Bohemian Massif; the western mountain ranges moderate the continental climate, while the river moderates temperature and the south-facing granite terraces capture maximum solar radiation.

### Aspect and Slope

A south-facing slope in the Northern Hemisphere (north-facing in the Southern Hemisphere) maximizes solar radiation. On the Côte d'Or, virtually every Grand Cru vineyard faces east or southeast — capturing morning sun to warm grapes after cold nights, without the brutal afternoon heat of a full south-facing exposure. The east-facing aspect at moderate slope angle is close to ideal for Pinot Noir in Burgundy's marginal climate.

Slope also affects drainage — steeper slopes drain faster, reducing water stress modulation, forcing roots deeper, and lowering yields. The steepest Mosel vineyards (Bremmer Calmont, Bernkasteler Doctor) are essentially terraced cliff faces where mechanization is impossible and yield is minimal.

> **Pro Tip:** Understanding aspect and slope allows you to explain why two wines from the same appellation can taste radically different. In conversations with serious wine guests, explaining the difference between a village-level Meursault from a flat, clay-heavy site versus a Premier Cru from a well-drained east-facing limestone slope — using aspect, soil, and drainage as your framework — is the kind of technical depth that differentiates your program.

### Viticulture Philosophies

**Conventional Viticulture** uses synthetic herbicides, pesticides, and fertilizers. It prioritizes consistency, disease control, and yield reliability. Glyphosate (Roundup) has been widely used for weed control under vine rows; this practice is now highly controversial.

**Sustainable Viticulture** rejects a single standard in favor of a principles-based approach: minimizing chemical inputs, protecting soil health, reducing water use, managing carbon footprint. Certification bodies include SIP (Sustainability in Practice — California), LIVE (Low Input Viticulture and Enology — Pacific Northwest), and Sustainable Winegrowing Australia. No prohibition on synthetic chemistry, but strict minimization protocols.

**Organic Viticulture** prohibits synthetic pesticides, herbicides, and fertilizers. Permitted treatments include copper sulfate (against downy mildew — the Bordeaux mixture of copper sulfate and lime; controversial for its long-term soil accumulation effects) and sulfur (against powdery mildew). Certified by Ecocert, AB (Agriculture Biologique — France), USDA Organic, EU Organic logo. Converting from conventional to certified organic requires a three-year transition period. Climate and disease pressure make organic viticulture significantly more challenging in wet regions (Bordeaux, Germany) than in dry ones (Southern France, parts of Italy and Spain).

**Biodynamic Viticulture** goes beyond organic to treat the farm as a self-sustaining ecosystem. Based on the lectures of Rudolf Steiner (1924), formalized for wine through the Demeter (international) and Biodyvin (France) certification programs. Key principles include: the lunar calendar (planting by lunar cycle; root days vs. fruit days vs. flower days for harvesting and tasting; empirically contested but widely practiced); the biodynamic preparations 500-508 (500: cow manure fermented in a cow horn buried over winter, stirred into water dynamically and sprayed on soil to stimulate root and soil microbial activity; 501: silica powder in cow horn, stimulates light and photosynthesis; 502-507: herbal preparations — yarrow, chamomile, valerian, oak bark, dandelion, stinging nettle — applied to compost piles to build soil vitality; 508: horsetail tea, used against fungal disease). Proponents include some of Burgundy's most respected producers — Domaine Leroy, Domaine de la Romanée-Conti, Domaine Leflaive, Pontet-Canet (Pauillac), Zind-Humbrecht (Alsace).

**Natural Wine** has no legal definition and no certification body. As a philosophy, it typically encompasses: organic or biodynamic farming in the vineyard; indigenous (wild) yeast fermentation only; no or minimal sulfur additions; no fining; no filtration; no additions (no acidification, no chaptalisation, no water addition, no concentration, no micro-oxygenation). The spectrum ranges from serious, stable, terroir-expressive wines from skilled practitioners to wines with significant technical faults (volatile acidity, brett, oxidation, refermentation in bottle). As a wine director, building a natural wine program requires rigorous personal evaluation because the absence of a quality standard certification means quality must be assessed bottle by bottle, producer by producer.

### Vine Training Systems

**Guyot (Single and Double):** The dominant training system in Burgundy (single Guyot) and Bordeaux (double Guyot). One or two canes are selected at winter pruning and tied horizontally to a lower wire; shoots grow vertically from the cane, trained up through upper wires (VSP). Allows precise yield control, excellent canopy management, and mechanization.

**Gobelet (Bush Vine):** Free-standing vine with no wires, multiple short arms radiating from the trunk. Traditional in Southern Rhône (Grenache), Beaujolais (Gamay in the Crus), Southern Spain and old-vine Garnacha in Priorat and Calatayud. Suits hot, dry, windy climates — the bush form protects fruit from wind and excessive sun, creating natural shade. Extremely difficult to mechanize. Associated with old vines and concentration.

**VSP (Vertical Shoot Positioning):** The modern standard in cool climates — shoots trained vertically upward through parallel wires. Creates a uniform, manageable canopy; allows leaf removal for sun exposure and air circulation; enables mechanical harvest. Most Guyot-trained vineyards use VSP for canopy management.

**Pergola/Tendone:** High canopy trained overhead on a horizontal framework. Traditional in Vinho Verde (northern Portugal) — the high canopy allows cereal crops to grow underneath and keeps grape-bearing wood away from the humid soil surface, reducing rot pressure in the wet climate. Also used in Trentino (Pergola Trentina). Produces high yields and lighter, acid-forward wines.

**Lyre and GDC (Geneva Double Curtain):** Canopy-divided systems that split the vine canopy into two parallel walls, increasing leaf-to-fruit ratio and ventilation. Developed for humid climates with excessive vigor issues. Not common in fine wine production but used in warm, humid regions where disease management is challenging.

### Old Vines

There is no legal definition of "old vine" in any major wine country, though Australia's Barossa Valley Grape and Wine Association has proposed a tiered system (25+, 70+, 100+ years). General consensus in the trade acknowledges "old vine" meaningfully at 35-50+ years.

Why old vines matter: root systems penetrate deeper into the soil profile, accessing ancient geological layers and stored water; yield is naturally lower as vine capacity decreases; berry size is smaller; sugar and phenolic concentrations are higher. Old vine wines often show greater complexity, a more profound sense of place, and longer aging potential.

The world's oldest vines are remarkable historical survivors: Maribor's Stara Trta in Slovenia (over 400 years old, still producing); Australia's Barossa Valley has documented pre-1880 Grenache, Shiraz, and Mourvèdre plantings; Lodi, California has Century Vine Zinfandel plantings from the 1880s.

### Climate Change

Climate change is reshaping viticulture globally at an accelerating pace. Key documented impacts:

**Earlier Harvest Dates:** Burgundy harvest dates have moved forward by approximately three weeks since the 1970s. The early 21st century has seen a series of exceptional but very early harvests (2003, 2015, 2018, 2019, 2020, 2022).

**Higher Alcohol:** As grapes ripen at higher sugar levels, fermented alcohol levels rise. Burgundy Pinot Noir that was 12.5% ABV in the 1980s is routinely 13.5-14.5% today. This has driven techniques like harvesting earlier, water addition (controversial and often illegal), and must concentration reversal.

**Loss of Acidity:** Warm nights in particular reduce acid retention during ripening. Tartaric acid — the primary fixed acid in wine — is consumed as a respiratory substrate at high temperatures. Low-acid wines age poorly and taste flat.

**New Regions:** England's sparkling wine industry (Nyetimber, Chapel Down, Ridgeview, Hambledon) is now producing Champagne-quality sparkling wine on chalk soils in Sussex, Kent, and Hampshire — the same geological formation as Champagne, now warm enough to ripen Chardonnay and Pinot Noir. Southern Scandinavia is producing experimental plantings. Southern Ontario and Nova Scotia are expanding.

**Established Regions Under Pressure:** Napa Valley, Barossa Valley, and Southern Spain face harvest dates so compressed and temperatures so extreme that wine styles are fundamentally changing. The response includes higher altitude planting (Mendoza moving above 1500m), coastal sites (Sonoma Coast vs. Napa Valley floor), and variety switching (heat-tolerant Spanish and Southern Italian varieties gaining traction in Australian and Californian plantings).

> **Pro Tip:** Climate change provides exceptional guest conversation content. Being able to articulate what 2003 meant for Burgundy (the hottest vintage in recorded history — wines of power but often lack acid; rapid early drinking), what 2022 means for Champagne (ripe, unusual, early-drinking for the region), and why you're seeing English sparkling wine on your list positions you and your program as forward-thinking and relevant. Don't wait for guests to ask — build these narratives into your list notes and server training.

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| Terroir | The complete natural environment of a vineyard — soil, subsoil, climate, aspect, altitude, microclimate — and, in some definitions, the human traditions of a place |
| Phylloxera | Root louse (*Daktulosphaira vitifoliae*) that devastated European vineyards in the late 19th century; controlled by grafting *vinifera* onto resistant American rootstocks |
| Budburst | The emergence of green shoots from dormant buds in early spring; the most frost-vulnerable point in the growth cycle |
| Coulure | Failure of pollinated flowers to set as berries, due to poor weather at flowering; causes significant yield loss |
| Millerandage | Formation of undersized, seedless berries (shot berries) alongside normal berries; caused by poor conditions at fruit set |
| Veraison | The onset of ripening in midsummer — berries soften, change color, begin accumulating sugar while acidity drops |
| Gobelet | Free-standing bush vine training system with no wires; traditional in hot, dry climates; associated with old vines |
| Biodynamic | Farming philosophy based on Rudolf Steiner's lectures; treats the farm as a self-sustaining ecosystem; includes lunar calendar and herbal preparations |
| Albariza | The white chalk soil of the Jerez region in Andalusia; key to Sherry's character |
| Schist | Metamorphic rock with layered structure; key soil in the Mosel, Priorat, and Douro; heat-absorbing and well-draining |
| Diurnal Variation | The difference between daytime high and nighttime low temperatures; large diurnal range preserves acidity and aromatic intensity |
| Macroclimate | The broad regional climate of a wine zone |
| Mesoclimate | The climate of a specific hillside, slope, or vineyard |
| Microclimate | The climate within an individual vine canopy or row |
| VSP | Vertical Shoot Positioning — modern training standard in cool climates; shoots trained upward through parallel wires |
