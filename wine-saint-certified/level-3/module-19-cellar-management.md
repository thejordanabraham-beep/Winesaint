## Section 1: The Wine Director's Role

### Scope and Responsibilities

The Wine Director title represents a significantly broader mandate than the traditional sommelier role. A sommelier serves wine on the floor; a Wine Director builds and manages the system through which wine is selected, purchased, stored, presented, sold, and replenished, and trains the team that operates within it.

The core responsibilities:

**Inventory Management:** Knowing precisely what is in the cellar at all times: quantity, bin location, purchase price, current retail value, and aging status. For a restaurant with a meaningful cellar program, inventory may represent USD 100,000 to several million dollars in stock. This is significant capital; managing it responsibly requires systems, discipline, and regular physical audit.

**Wine List Design and Maintenance:** The wine list is the primary commercial document of the program. It reflects the director's expertise, the restaurant's concept, and the guest's expectations. It requires continuous updating: wines sell out, vintages change, producers are added or removed. It also requires thoughtful design that makes navigation intuitive.

**Staff Training:** The Wine Director is responsible for the wine knowledge of everyone who interacts with guests around wine: servers, bartenders, backwaiters, and managers. A wine director who cannot translate their expertise into effective, accessible training produces a floor team that cannot sell.

**Trade Relationships:** Managing relationships with the distributor network, importers, and in some cases directly with producers. These relationships determine access to allocations, early information about new releases, and the quality of support for wine events and staff education.

**Cost Management:** Beverage cost targets, markup policies, pour cost on BTG wines, inventory shrinkage, and financial reporting. The wine program must be financially productive.

**Guest Relations:** The wine director is often the face of the wine program to serious wine guests: recommending selections, building vertical experiences, sourcing specific bottles, and creating wine events that become part of the restaurant's identity.

> **Pro Tip:** The most common failure mode of talented sommeliers moving into a Wine Director role is the inability to delegate the floor. A director who spends every night personally opening every special bottle has not built a functioning program; they have become a dependency. Building a floor team capable of executing the program at a high level is more important, and more difficult, than personal tableside performance. Measure success by the program's quality when you're not present.

## Section 2: Wine List Design Philosophy

### Breadth vs. Depth

The central tension in wine list design: a list that covers everything broadly versus one that builds significant depth in selected areas.

**Broad list:** Covers all major regions and styles; something for every type of guest; excellent for restaurants with diverse guest demographics and cuisine that spans flavors. Risk: can become superficial. A few bottles of everything means nothing truly distinguished or compelling. Large lists (500+ SKUs) are expensive to maintain, require significant staff knowledge, risk slow-moving inventory, and create decision paralysis for guests.

**Focused list:** Builds genuine depth in areas of strength, perhaps 10-15 producers in Burgundy rather than 40 bottles spread across all of France. The focused approach signals genuine expertise, enables better staff training (fewer wines to learn deeply), reduces dead stock risk, and creates a more distinctive identity. Trend: the industry has moved meaningfully toward focused lists in the 2015-2025 period, with many respected programs reducing SKU count while deepening quality.

The right answer depends on the restaurant concept. A French bistro benefits from depth in Burgundy, the Loire, and the Rhône. A multi-concept global restaurant needs broader coverage. A steakhouse needs depth in Cabernet-based reds and Chardonnay, and meaningful Champagne selection.

### List Organization

How a list is organized directly affects sales patterns and guest experience:

**By region:** The traditional approach. Professional wine guests navigate it effectively. Challenges: requires geographic knowledge from guests; obscures style-based decision-making; long regional lists can be exhausting.

**By grape variety:** Growing in popularity, particularly in modern casual-fine dining. Intuitive for the growing segment of wine-educated younger guests who think in varieties rather than regions. Risk: loses the story of regional specificity.

**By style/weight:** "Light and Fresh → Medium-Bodied → Full and Rich" frameworks are highly accessible and empower guests who don't know varieties or regions but understand their palate preference. Used successfully by casual fine dining programs.

**By price:** Generally avoided as the primary organization; it feels commercial and reduces the perceived curation of the list. May be appropriate for very simple, accessible programs.

**Best practice:** A hybrid approach: primary organization by region (for traditional context) with secondary cues about style and food pairing within each section. Page or section headers that convey the flavor territory ("Lively, Mineral Whites of the Loire") alongside the geographic identifier.

### List Format: Digital vs. Printed

**Printed lists** create a physical artifact of the program; they feel curated and authoritative. A beautifully designed printed list signals investment and seriousness. Limitations: expensive to reprint, rapidly outdated as bottles sell out, inflexible for day-to-day adjustments.

**Digital lists** (iPad, QR code, or dedicated wine app) can be updated instantly, include photographs, provide additional information (producer notes, pairing suggestions, vintage scores), and do not become outdated between reprints. Used effectively in many serious programs. Risk: loss of the tactile, curated experience; technology failures at the table.

**Best practice:** A printed list for the overall program with a digital supplement for current inventory and nightly specials. Some programs keep a tight printed list and use a digital menu for the full vintage archive.

> **Pro Tip:** List size is a financial decision as much as an editorial one. Every SKU on the list is a capital commitment. A 600-bottle list with average inventory of 12 bottles per SKU is USD 500,000-1,000,000+ in stock depending on price points. Before expanding a list, calculate the working capital cost of carrying the additional inventory. The most productive lists are not the largest; they are the most precisely selected.

## Section 3: Pricing, Cost Management, and BTG Economics

### Beverage Cost of Goods (COGS)

Beverage cost, the cost of the wine itself as a percentage of its selling price, is the foundational financial metric of the wine program.

**Industry benchmarks:**
- Wine by the glass: 20-28% beverage cost (markup of 3.5-5x the pour cost)
- Wine by the bottle (mid-range, USD 50-150 selling price): 30-40% beverage cost
- High-end bottles (USD 150+): Typically a flat dollar markup over a percentage markup is more common at the top, adding USD 75-150 per bottle rather than 3x, which would make premium wines unattainably expensive

**The flat vs. percentage markup debate:** Applying a consistent 3x or 3.5x markup across all price points is simple to administer but produces anomalies: a USD 10 wholesale bottle sells for USD 30-35, which is reasonable; a USD 200 wholesale bottle sells for USD 600-700, which many guests find exploitative and will simply not purchase. The best practice is a tiered markup structure: higher percentage markup at low price points (to maintain adequate absolute margin); declining percentage as wholesale cost rises; flat dollar markup at the premium end (often USD 75-200 per bottle above cost for wines over USD 100 wholesale).

**The "retail plus" method:** Some directors set pricing at retail price plus a set markup (USD 20-40 for moderately priced bottles; flat fee for premium) rather than cost-based markup. This creates an easily communicated guest rationale ("our markup is X above retail") but requires detailed retail price tracking.

### By-the-Glass Program Design

The BTG program is the most financially significant section of the wine list. Most guests in most restaurant categories drink wine by the glass. A well-designed BTG program drives beverage revenue, introduces guests to wines they'll order by the bottle on return visits, and provides a vehicle for staff training (you can taste it every day).

**BTG economics:** A standard pour (5 oz in the US) yields approximately five pours per 750mL bottle. If the bottle cost is USD 15 and the BTG price is USD 14-16 per glass, the program generates approximately USD 70-80 per bottle at cost of USD 15, a 78-82% profit margin. The BTG program is the highest-margin section of the wine list.

**Key design principles:**
- Cover the full style spectrum (light/sparkling; light white; rich white; light red; medium red; rich/full red; sweet/dessert) so every guest's preference is served
- At minimum one Champagne or quality sparkling option by the glass; sparkling is the highest-margin BTG offering and has the highest guest satisfaction impact
- Price point ladder: 3-4 price tiers from accessible to premium to enable upselling and accommodate the full guest range
- Rotate selections seasonally: lighter styles (crisp whites, rosé, Beaujolais) in summer; richer reds, fuller whites in winter
- Feature producers you're developing relationships with; the BTG list is your best tool for introducing new producers to your guest base

**Pour cost management:** Track BTG pour cost weekly. A 5 oz pour at USD 15 bottle cost produces USD 3 cost per pour; at USD 14 selling price, that's 21.4% cost. When tracking, account for: the host glass (bartenders pouring themselves a small taste to verify freshness, representing 1 oz per bottle if done once), spillage (estimated 1-2%), and waste from overnight oxidation on open bottles not consumed. Coravin use for premium BTG offerings reduces waste on high-value wines significantly; it is worth the equipment investment for any serious BTG program.

### Financial Analysis of the Wine Program

Track these metrics monthly:

- **Beverage revenue as % of total revenue:** Most fine dining operations target 25-35% of total revenue from beverages; casual dining typically 15-25%
- **Beverage cost % (overall and by category):** BTG vs. bottle vs. Champagne vs. spirits; category-level data identifies where cost control opportunities exist
- **Revenue per seat:** Useful for identifying whether certain sections of the room or certain service periods have lower wine attachment rates
- **Slow-moving inventory:** Any wine that has not had a purchase in 90 days deserves attention, either a staff training push, a BTG conversion, or a markdown
- **Dead stock identification:** Wines that will not sell before the program or vintage no longer suits the list; manage through BTG conversion, markdown, or trade return where possible

> **Pro Tip:** The most common financial error in wine program management is carrying too much inventory in slow-moving premium bottles. Capital tied up in unsold USD 400 bottles is not earning return. Build a quarterly dead stock review into your program calendar: anything without movement in 90 days gets BTG treatment, staff incentive, or a direct sales conversation with your top wine-enthusiast regulars. Proactive inventory management prevents the cycle of year-end markdown disasters.

## Section 4: Working with the Trade

### The Distributor Ecosystem

In the US three-tier system (see Module 17), distributors are the essential intermediary between producers/importers and restaurants. Building productive distributor relationships, rather than transactional purchase orders, is one of the most important long-term investments a Wine Director can make.

**What excellent distributor relationships provide:**
- Early access to new releases before general market availability
- Allocations of sought-after, limited-production wines
- Producer representative visits and education opportunities
- Winery travel invitations (valuable for program building and personal education)
- Flexibility on payment terms for large orders
- Staff training support (producer-led tastings, sample provision)
- Market intelligence about competitor programs, new producers, and upcoming releases

**What you can offer distributors in return:**
- Commitment and volume; distributors prioritize accounts that buy consistently and grow
- Floor placement and BTG listing of new releases; a BTG listing is the highest-value placement a restaurant can offer
- Willingness to try new producers; being a launch account is genuinely valuable for distributors introducing a new import
- Introductions to your network; referrals to other restaurants or contacts

**Managing distributor relationships at scale:** A Wine Director at a major property will have relationships with dozens of distributors. Weekly rep visits, constant sample flow, and ongoing communication can become overwhelming. Build a structured relationship management system: a scheduled weekly slot for distributor tastings rather than ad-hoc visits; a clear policy about sample acceptances and evaluation timelines; a tracking document showing which distributor handles which producers.

### Allocations

The most sought-after wines in any market are allocation-based, with quantities so limited that distributors have more demand than supply and must allocate between accounts based on purchasing history, relationship depth, and strategic value.

**Key allocation strategies:**
- Buy consistently across a portfolio, not just cherry-pick the famous names; distributors track purchase breadth, and accounts that only want the trophy wines get the smallest allocations
- Be a reliable, paying customer; accounts with clean AP (accounts payable) records get preferential treatment
- Participate in portfolio tastings, winery events, and education programs; presence signals engagement and earns goodwill
- Build a personal relationship with the senior portfolio manager for key categories; the people who make allocation decisions are people, not algorithms
- Offer meaningful floor placement; a restaurant that features a producer prominently on the BTG list or gives them dedicated shelf space in the wine room gets rewarded

**Direct-from-Winery Relationships:** For some programs, particularly at the ultra-premium level, developing direct relationships with small producers is valuable. In states where DTC (direct-to-consumer) or direct purchasing by licensed accounts is permitted, this can provide access unavailable through distribution. More commonly, the direct relationship creates preferential positioning when the winery contacts importers about restaurant allocations.

### En Primeur and Futures Purchasing

For programs with serious cellar programs, en primeur purchasing is a relevant sourcing channel. A wine director purchasing Bordeaux en primeur in spring following the harvest will receive the wine 18-24 months later and must have the capital and storage ready. The primary value: accessing wines that will be unavailable (or only available at significant secondary market premiums) once bottled and released.

**Practical considerations:** Who in the three-tier chain is the en primeur channel? In the US, typically an importer with a direct château relationship. Know which importers handle Bordeaux en primeur (Martine's Wines, Vinifera Imports, and large-format importers are key sources). For Burgundy, the négociant system (Jadot, Drouhin, Faiveley, Louis Latour) offers futures through their US importers; small domaine allocations come through specialized importer-négociant relationships (Becky Wasserman, KLWM/Kermit Lynch, Louis/Dressner).

## Section 5: Cellar Organization, Staff Training, and Guest Relations

### Physical Cellar Management

**Storage Conditions:** The non-negotiables for a professional wine cellar:
- Temperature: 12-14°C (54-57°F); stable rather than fluctuating. A wine stored at a constant 18°C ages faster than ideal but consistently; a wine cycling between 10°C and 22°C is damaged by thermal expansion and contraction.
- Humidity: 60-70%; too dry and corks dry out and allow oxidation; too humid causes label and capsule deterioration (cosmetic but affects resale value).
- Vibration: None. Constant vibration disrupts the slow chemical evolution of aging wine. Storage near elevators, compressors, or high-traffic service corridors is suboptimal.
- Light: No UV exposure. Light catalyzes oxidation through photodegradation. Brown/amber glass bottles (traditional for Burgundy and Champagne) provide some UV protection; clear glass bottles require complete light exclusion.
- Position: Cork-sealed wines must be stored horizontally to keep the cork moist and maintain the seal. Crown cap and screwcap closures (increasingly common in New Zealand, Australia, and some French producers) do not require horizontal storage.

**Bin System and Organization:** A bin system assigns a fixed location in the cellar to each wine. Every bottle has a home; the inventory system references bin numbers. Organization options: by region (standard and intuitive for a cellar team), by price tier, by drinking window (current drinkers in accessible locations; long-term aging wines in a secure, less-accessed section). A good bin system enables fast, accurate pulling during service, which is critical when a table needs a specific bottle brought up in three minutes.

**Inventory Software:** Several platforms are designed for restaurant cellar management:
- *Bevager:* Specifically designed for restaurant beverage programs; integrates with POS systems for consumption tracking; supports bottle receiving and depletion
- *Uncork'd:* Wine-specific cellar management with list generation features
- *WineDirect:* More winery-focused but used by some large restaurant programs
- *Provi:* Ordering and invoice management platform; integrates with major US distributors for streamlined purchasing
- *Excel/Google Sheets:* Simple, free, and widely used by smaller programs; workable if maintained rigorously

**FIFO and Inventory Discipline:** First In, First Out (FIFO) is standard practice: new bottles of a wine go behind existing bottles on the shelf, ensuring that older stock is served first. Critical for wines with a defined optimal drinking window. LIFO (Last In, First Out) is appropriate only in specific scenarios such as aging programs where you intentionally hold older vintages for later release.

**Cellar Audits:** Physical inventory counts should be conducted quarterly at minimum for high-value cellar programs, with a full physical reconciliation annually. Variance between the inventory system and physical count identifies breakage, theft, or administrative errors. Unexplained variance is a compliance and financial risk; any variance above 1-2% deserves investigation.

### Staff Training and Development

A wine program's quality in the guest's experience is determined by the person who interacts at the table, often a server, not the wine director. Staff training is therefore the Wine Director's most leveraged activity.

**Weekly Tasting Program:** Short (15-minute) pre-service tastings of wines on the current BTG list or new releases. Servers taste the wine, discuss food pairings with an example or two, and leave service with a specific recommendation to make. Over a year, this builds real knowledge; over six months, it measurably increases wine attachment rates.

**Role-Playing Guest Scenarios:** Working through standard guest situations, such as "I'd like something dry and crisp under USD 60"; "We're having the ribeye; what do you recommend?"; "I don't like heavy, tannic wines," gives servers a practical vocabulary and decision framework. Role-playing is more effective than lecture for building tableside confidence.

**Cheat Sheets:** A one-page guide to the BTG wines, with a brief flavor description, food pairing suggestion, and one conversation point per wine, placed in the server notebook or at the service station. The goal is not to replace knowledge but to support it under service pressure.

**Wine Education Incentives:** Competitions within the staff team (monthly wine quiz, best guest feedback nomination), financial incentives for certified wine education (WSET Level 2 or 3, Court of Master Sommeliers Introductory or Certified), and public recognition create a culture of ongoing learning. Programs that invest in server education see higher wine sales, higher check averages, and lower staff turnover.

**Team Sommelier / Wine Captain:** In programs large enough to support it, having a designated floor sommelier or wine captain, a trained team member who supports wine service during all shifts but doesn't carry a full table section, substantially improves program consistency when the Wine Director is not present.

### Guest Relations

**The Regular Guest Wine Journey:** Returning guests who are wine enthusiasts represent the highest-value relationship in a restaurant's guest universe. Tracking their preferences (a simple CRM note: "prefers Burgundy; interested in natural wine; birthday in March") enables a personalized experience that creates deep loyalty. "I remembered you enjoyed the Jadot Puligny last time; we have a beautiful Meursault you should try tonight" is the single most effective guest loyalty tool in fine dining wine service.

**Wine Dinners and Vertical Tastings:** Curated wine events, such as a vertical of Barolo producers across five vintages or a six-course dinner paired by the Wine Director with commentary, are differentiation tools, revenue generators, and loyalty builders simultaneously. A USD 150-250 wine dinner with 20-30 guests generates USD 3,000-7,500 in food and beverage revenue for one evening, at high margin, with the highest engagement guests in the database.

**Private Cellar Storage Programs:** Some urban fine dining programs offer private locker storage for regular guests. Guests purchase wine for their own account, store it in the restaurant's temperature-controlled cellar, and bring it to their tables with a relatively modest corkage fee. The program creates a captive guest relationship (they need to come to access their wine), generates ancillary revenue (corkage, food sales), and provides the director with insight into guests' wine tastes that enables better recommendations.

## Section 6: Sustainability, Ethics, and Financial Sophistication

### Sustainable and Ethical Sourcing

**Organic and Biodynamic on the List:** An increasing segment of wine-educated guests ask about farming philosophy before making a selection. Annotating the list with organic/biodynamic certification icons (or a simple "Farmed Organically" or "Biodynamic Estate" note) serves these guests efficiently and signals the program's values.

**Supporting Small Producers:** A program built around small-production, artisan producers is both an ethical position and a commercial differentiator. Small producers rarely have the marketing budgets of large négociants; their wines need floor-level advocacy to succeed. When a sommelier says "This is a 500-case production Grenache Blanc from a family who's been farming this hillside for four generations, and you won't find it anywhere else in town," that recommendation is both true and compelling.

**Packaging and Footprint:** Glass bottle weight is a significant environmental factor; heavy prestige bottles use more raw material and more fuel for transport. Some programs specify preference for lighter glass production runs from suppliers. Alternatives to traditional closures (screwcap, DIAM technical cork) offer sustainability benefits and in many cases quality benefits (screwcap eliminates TCA cork taint entirely). The conversation about alternative closures has matured: dismissing screwcap as a quality indicator is no longer intellectually defensible in 2026.

**Wine by the Glass Waste Reduction:** Gas preservation systems (Coravin, Repour, VacuVin) reduce BTG oxidation waste. A Coravin system for three premium BTG offerings at USD 20+ per glass, reducing daily waste from half-bottle to zero, can save USD 5,000-10,000 in annual waste on a high-volume program. The capital investment pays back within months.

### Seasonal Adjustments and Slow-Moving Stock

Wine programs require seasonal recalibration. Summer shifts consumption toward lighter whites, rosé, and sparkling; winter toward rich reds and fuller whites. Failing to adjust the BTG program seasonally is leaving money on the table: a Côtes du Rhône red as a BTG option in August will underperform the same slot filled by a crisp Albarizo or a Provençal rosé.

**Slow-moving bottle management:** When a bottle hasn't sold in 90 days, the options are: (1) move it to BTG, which immediately increases exposure to new guests; (2) feature it in a server recommendation push or a "Wine Director's Pick" callout on the table; (3) use it in a wine dinner or staff training; (4) offer it at a modest markdown to regular guests; (5) if truly unsound (oxidized, corked, or off-vintage), return to distributor or write off. The worst option: leave it on the list at full price while capital sits inert.

> **Pro Tip:** One of the most powerful financial management moves available to a Wine Director is creating a standing monthly "Dead Inventory Meeting" with the general manager or beverage manager, reviewing any wine without movement in the past 60 days and making an active decision about each. This habit prevents the accumulation of dead stock, creates regular inventory discipline, and signals financial accountability to the management team. It takes 30 minutes and saves thousands annually.

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| COGS | Cost of Goods Sold: the cost of the wine itself as a percentage of its selling price; the primary financial metric of beverage program profitability |
| Pour Cost | The cost of a single pour (e.g., 5 oz glass) expressed as a percentage of its selling price |
| BTG | By the Glass: wine sold in individual pours rather than by the bottle; the highest-margin segment of the wine program |
| Beverage Cost % | Total beverage cost (wine, spirits, beer) as a percentage of total beverage revenue; the primary operational metric for beverage financial performance |
| FIFO | First In, First Out: inventory management method ensuring oldest stock is sold first |
| Allocation | A limited quantity of sought-after wine assigned by a distributor or producer to a specific account based on purchasing history and relationship |
| Dead Stock | Bottles that have not had a sale within a defined period (typically 60-90 days); represent tied-up capital and aging risk |
| Coravin | A wine access system that extracts wine through the cork using a needle without removing the cork; extends the useful life of open premium BTG bottles |
| Bin System | A numbered location system assigning fixed storage positions to each wine in the cellar for inventory tracking and efficient picking |
| Markup | The amount added to the cost of a wine to determine its selling price; expressed as a multiplier (3x cost) or percentage above cost |
| SKU | Stock Keeping Unit: each distinct wine on the list is a separate SKU; SKU count is a proxy for list size and inventory complexity |
| En Primeur | Wine purchased as futures before bottling; a relevant sourcing channel for directors building programs around collectible wines |
| Three-Tier System | The US alcohol distribution framework: producer/importer → distributor → retailer/restaurant |
| Bevager | Inventory management software platform designed for restaurant beverage programs |
