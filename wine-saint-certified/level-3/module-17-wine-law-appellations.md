## Section 1: The Purpose of Wine Law and the EU Framework

### Why Wine Law Exists

Wine law serves a cluster of related purposes that are sometimes in tension with each other:

**Consumer protection:** Labels must accurately represent what is in the bottle. Without regulation, "Champagne" could mean any sparkling wine. "Burgundy" could mean any Pinot Noir. Consumers making purchasing decisions based on region of origin, grape variety, or vintage year require legally enforced accuracy.

**Producer protection:** Established regions with long reputations have economic interests in controlling who can use their names. The value of the name "Champagne" or "Barolo" is the accumulated reputation of generations of producers — a legal framework prevents free-riding by others using these names without meeting the standards.

**Defining quality:** Appellation law defines minimum standards — permitted varieties, maximum yields, minimum alcohol levels, required aging periods — that theoretically exclude sub-standard products from prestigious designations.

**Maintaining regional identity:** Wine regions are cultural artifacts. Appellation law is partly about preserving agricultural heritage, traditional varieties, and viticultural practices — protecting them from economic pressures that might otherwise replace them with more productive but less characterful alternatives.

**Enabling international trade:** Trade agreements increasingly incorporate GI (Geographical Indication) protections, enabling wine-producing countries to protect their regional names in export markets.

### The EU Framework: PDO and PGI

The European Union reorganized wine law in 2009 (EC Regulation 479/2008) into two broad categories:

**PDO (Protected Designation of Origin) / AOP (Appellation d'Origine Protégée):** The highest EU wine category. All qualities of the wine must be essentially attributable to its geographical origin. This encompasses the finest national designations: France's AOC, Italy's DOC and DOCG, Spain's DO and DOCa, Germany's Qualitätswein and Prädikatswein, Portugal's DOC. Labels may show either the EU PDO designation or the national equivalent (AOC, DOCG, etc.).

**PGI (Protected Geographical Indication) / IGP (Indication Géographique Protégée):** A broader category requiring only that at least 85% of grapes come from the named region. Encompasses France's IGP (replacing Vin de Pays), Italy's IGT (Indicazione Geografica Tipica — home of the Super Tuscans), and Spain's Vino de la Tierra.

**Vin/Vino de France/Table Wine:** The most basic category — no geographical indication, no vintage, no variety required on the label. The floor of the pyramid. In France, a recent regulatory shift allows *Vin de France* wines to display grape variety and vintage on the label — a change that has enabled some serious producers to use *Vin de France* as a vehicle for wines outside AOC rules (varietal wines, unusual blends).

> **Pro Tip:** The PDO/PGI reorganization created some practical labeling complexity. A wine listed as "AOC Burgundy" on your list is technically "PDO Burgundy" under EU nomenclature. Both are accurate; the national term is more useful for guest communication. What matters practically for a director: understanding that PDO wines have more restrictive rules of origin and production than PGI wines, and that a "Super Tuscan" IGT wine can be more expensive and prestigious than many DOCG wines despite its lower classification tier.

## Section 2: France's AOC/AOP System

### INAO and the Cahier des Charges

France's Appellation d'Origine Contrôlée (AOC) system, now aligned with the EU's AOP designation, is the oldest, most complex, and most influential appellation system in the world. It is administered by INAO (Institut National de l'Origine et de la Qualité), a government body under the Ministry of Agriculture.

Each AOC is defined by a **cahier des charges** — a specification document that legally defines everything about what may be produced under that appellation:

- Geographical delimitation of the production zone (mapped to the parcel level for Burgundy Grand Crus)
- Permitted grape varieties (and maximum and minimum proportions in blended appellations)
- Maximum permitted yields (in hectoliters per hectare; yields above this threshold require declassification to a lower appellation)
- Minimum natural alcohol levels at harvest (ensuring physiological ripeness before enrichment)
- Permitted winemaking practices (including whether chaptalisation, acidification, or concentration techniques are permitted)
- Aging requirements (minimum barrel and bottle aging for specific appellations)
- Tasting panel requirements (a blind tasting panel must approve each wine's typicité — its conformity to the appellation's expected character — before it may be labeled with the AOC)

The tasting panel requirement is significant and underappreciated. A wine can fail its AOC tasting and must be declassified. In practice, panels are inconsistent and the threshold for failure is highly variable.

### The French Quality Hierarchy

**Vin de France (formerly Vin de Table):** No geographic indication required. May now show grape variety and vintage (recent regulatory change). The category of last resort for declassified wines and for unconventional producers working outside AOC rules.

**IGP (Indication Géographique Protégée, formerly Vin de Pays):** Broader geographic identity (typically regional — IGP d'Oc covers the entire Languedoc). Permitted varieties are wider than AOC; yields are higher. Some serious producers use IGP deliberately — for example, Mas de Daumas Gassac, which could theoretically qualify for Languedoc AOC, has historically classified as Vin de Pays de l'Hérault as a statement of independence from appellation rules.

**AOC/AOP:** The apex. Over 400 AOCs in France. The system provides a geographical hierarchy within regions — Burgundy's four-level classification (Grand Cru → Premier Cru → Village AOC → Regional AOC) is the most elaborate; Bordeaux uses the 1855 Classification and equivalents as a reputational hierarchy within the broader appellation structure.

### Recent Controversies

**St-Émilion Classification Litigation:** The St-Émilion classification (revised periodically, unlike the static 1855 Médoc classification) was revised in 2012 and again in 2022. Both revisions were challenged in French courts. The 2012 reclassification was overturned. The 2022 revision, which elevated Château Figeac and Château Pavie Macquin to Premier Grand Cru Classé A status alongside Ausone, Cheval Blanc, Angélus, and Pavie, was contested by demoted estates. As of early 2026, legal challenges were ongoing. This litigation illustrates the enormous financial stakes of classification decisions and the limits of appellation law as an objective quality arbiter.

**Burgundy Premier Cru Boundary Disputes:** Several villages in Burgundy have petitioned INAO to reclassify specific plots from Village to Premier Cru status, or to expand existing Premier Cru boundaries. These decisions are contentious because Premier Cru status can triple or quadruple the market value of wine from a specific parcel.

## Section 3: Italy's DOC/DOCG System

### Origins and Reforms

Italy's appellation system was established by Presidential Decree 930 in 1963 (DPR 930/1963), which created the Denominazione di Origine Controllata (DOC) designation. The law was reform-driven by the experience of Italian producers watching France's AOC system define international quality benchmarks while Italian wine was sold in fiaschi (straw-covered flasks) or generic blends.

The **1992 Goria Law** (named for Agriculture Minister Giovanni Goria) introduced critical reforms:
- Created the DOCG (Denominazione di Origine Controllata e Garantita) — a higher tier with more restrictive rules, mandatory government tasting panels, and neck-seal approval labels
- Created the IGT (Indicazione Geografica Tipica) — a broader geographic category that paradoxically became the legal home of some of Italy's finest and most expensive wines (see Super Tuscans below)
- Strengthened enforcement mechanisms

The **2009 EU reform** aligned the Italian system with the EU PDO/PGI framework, as in France.

### The Super Tuscan Paradox

The Super Tuscans are wines produced in Tuscany that deliberately violate the DOC rules of their origin — either by using non-traditional varieties (Cabernet Sauvignon, Merlot, Syrah) in blends that DOC regulations prohibited, or by abandoning the Sangiovese grape requirements entirely. The most famous examples: Sassicaia (Bolgheri — 100% Cabernet Sauvignon; began as Vino da Tavola in 1968; now has its own DOC as Bolgheri Sassicaia), Ornellaia (Bolgheri Superiore), Tignanello (Antinori — Sangiovese with Cabernet Sauvignon, first 1971 vintage), Solaia (Antinori — Cabernet dominant), Masseto (pure Merlot, unclassified by choice).

Because these wines fell outside DOC rules, they were initially labeled as Vino da Tavola or IGT Toscana — the lowest classification, carrying no quality guarantee. Yet they commanded prices far above any DOCG wine, demonstrating that critical reputation and market pricing are entirely independent of appellation classification. The Super Tuscan phenomenon exposed the limitation of any regulatory system that equates classification tier with quality.

### DOCG vs. DOC Requirements

**DOCG** requirements include: mandatory government tasting panel approval (the "Garantita" in the designation); neck seal certification labels; lower maximum yields than equivalent DOC; longer minimum aging requirements. Italy has approximately 77 DOCGs as of 2026. Examples: Barolo, Barbaresco, Brunello di Montalcino, Amarone della Valpolicella, Chianti Classico, Soave Superiore, Franciacorta, Gattinara.

**The role of Consorzi:** Most DOC and DOCG wines are governed by a Consorzio — a producers' association that manages marketing, sets voluntary rules within the legal framework, and in some cases enforces standards. The Consorzio del Vino Brunello di Montalcino (established 1967) is one of the most powerful in Italy. The Consorzio Barolo e Barbaresco governs those two great Piedmontese DOCGs.

## Section 4: Spain's DO/DOCa System and Germany's 2021 Reform

### Spain's Appellation Hierarchy

Spain has over 70 Denominaciones de Origen (DO), plus a small number of higher-tier designations and a significant number of regional PGI wines.

**DO (Denominación de Origen):** The standard appellation tier covering most named Spanish wine regions. Rioja, Ribera del Duero, Priorat, Rías Baixas, Jerez, Cava (geographic but primarily a production method designation) are all DOs.

**DOCa (Denominación de Origen Calificada):** The highest tier, requiring sustained quality demonstration and stricter production rules. Only two DOs have been elevated to DOCa: **Rioja** (1991 — the first DOCa) and **Priorat** (2009 — formally *DOQ Priorat* in Catalan).

**Vinos de Pago:** The newest and most specific tier — single-estate appellations covering a specific winery's vineyards, theoretically the most precise geographic designation in Spanish wine law. Currently approximately 20 recognized Pagos. Examples: Dominio de Valdepusa (Marqués de Griñón, Castilla-La Mancha — Cabernet Sauvignon, Syrah, and Petit Verdot); Pago de Arínzano (Navarra).

**Vino de la Tierra (VdlT):** Spain's equivalent of IGP/PGI — regional wines with fewer restrictions than DO. Some prestigious producers use VdlT as a framework for unconventional wines.

### Germany's Evolving Wine Law

Germany's wine law has historically been based on must weight (sugar content at harvest) rather than geography — an unusual approach reflecting the country's climatic challenges and the emphasis on residual sweetness as a quality indicator. The traditional system created the Prädikat categories based on harvest ripeness:

**Prädikat levels (ascending sugar concentration):**
- Kabinett: The lightest; grapes harvested at normal ripeness; typically low alcohol, delicate, often off-dry
- Spätlese: Late harvest; riper grapes; more body and concentration
- Auslese: Selected harvest; clusters selected for full ripeness or botrytis influence
- Beerenauslese (BA): Individual berry selection; significant botrytis; naturally sweet, luscious
- Trockenbeerenauslese (TBA): Individual selection of shriveled, dried, botrytized berries; intensely sweet; one of the world's great sweet wine categories
- Eiswein: Grapes frozen on the vine and pressed while frozen; concentrated sugar and acidity; made in years without sufficient botrytis for TBA

The **2021 reform** aligned Germany's national wine law more closely with the VDP's voluntary quality classification:

**New quality hierarchy:**
- Qualitätswein: Entry-level quality wine; may use chaptalisation
- Prädikatswein: The Prädikat categories above; no chaptalisation permitted
- The 2021 reform also formalized **Grosses Gewächs (GG)** (the VDP's top dry single-vineyard wine designation) and **Erste Lage** (premier cru equivalent) within the national system

**The VDP (Verband Deutscher Prädikatsweingüter):** Germany's private producer association has operated its own four-tier classification system — Gutswein (estate) → Ortswein (village) → Erste Lage (premier cru, formerly Erste Gewächs) → Grosses Gewächs/Grosse Lage (grand cru) — since 2012. The VDP classification maps to specific vineyard sites (*Einzellagen*) reviewed and approved by the VDP. It coexists with and overlaps the legal system but is more specific in its geographic claims and more rigorous in its quality benchmarks.

**The Einzellage vs. Grosslage Problem:** The 1971 wine law bundled hundreds of small individual vineyard names (*Einzellagen*) into larger collective vineyard names (*Grosslagen*) that could be used by any producer within the group. This created enormous consumer confusion — the name "Niersteiner Gutes Domtal" covers 3,000 hectares and is associated with ordinary commercial wine, while "Niersteiner Hipping" is a specific premier cru vineyard of extraordinary quality. The VDP's ongoing initiative to clarify Einzellagen and retire Grosslagen names from quality wines is the practical mechanism for improving label clarity.

> **Pro Tip:** Germany's wine law complexity is one of the most significant barriers to German wine program development. The VDP classification is the most reliable quality guide — but it requires knowing which estates are VDP members and what their specific vineyard holdings are. For your program, consider building a focused German section around two or three key producers with VDP membership and annotating the list with the VDP classification tier, rather than attempting to explain the full Prädikat system in a list note.

## Section 5: The United States Labeling System and Label Requirements

### TTB Regulation and the AVA System

The United States wine regulatory framework is administered by the TTB (Alcohol and Tobacco Tax and Trade Bureau), a bureau of the Treasury Department. All wine labels must receive TTB approval (COLA — Certificate of Label Approval) before commercial release.

**AVA (American Viticultural Area):** The US geographic designation system. There are 250+ approved AVAs, ranging from the enormous (California AVA encompasses the entire state) to the hyper-specific (True Rockpile Road AVA in Sonoma, approximately 160 acres).

Critical distinction from European systems: An AVA defines only geographic boundaries — not permitted grape varieties, not yields, not winemaking methods, not minimum aging requirements. Any variety can be grown in any AVA; any winemaking method is permitted. The AVA is a purely geographic and climatic designation.

**Label Requirements for AVA Claims:** To use an AVA name on a label, at least 75% of the wine must be derived from grapes grown within that AVA. To use a county name, 75% county-grown fruit is required. To use a state name, 75% in-state fruit is required (except for multi-state appellations with specific rules).

**Varietal Labeling:** To name a grape variety on the label, at least 75% of the wine must be made from that variety (except in Oregon, where state law requires 90% for most varieties).

**Vintage Labeling:** At least 95% of the wine must derive from the stated vintage year (for AVA-designated wines; 85% for wines with only state or county designations).

### The Three-Tier System

The US alcohol distribution system — established after Prohibition (21st Amendment repeal, 1933) — is a three-tier structure:

**Tier 1 — Producers/Importers:** Wineries and importers who obtain federal and state permits to produce or import wine.

**Tier 2 — Distributors/Wholesalers:** Licensed intermediaries who purchase from producers and sell to retailers/restaurants. In most states, a winery cannot sell directly to a restaurant without going through a licensed distributor.

**Tier 3 — Retailers/Restaurants/Bars:** Licensed establishments that sell to the end consumer.

The three-tier system has significant practical implications for wine directors: you typically cannot buy directly from a winery (except in states with DTC laws or self-distribution allowances); your price is distributor-set (plus your markup); your access to small-production or foreign wines depends entirely on which distributors carry them in your state.

**Direct-to-Consumer (DTC) and Reciprocal Shipping Laws:** States vary widely. Approximately 40 states permit some form of DTC wine shipment; others (including major markets) still prohibit or strictly limit it. For a wine director, understanding DTC as a sourcing channel for rare wines or personal program development (not always commercial) requires knowing your state's specific rules.

### Australia's Geographic Indication System

Australia's GI (Geographic Indication) system is administered by IP Australia and the Geographical Indications Committee. Like the US AVA, it defines geographic zones and regions without prescribing varieties or winemaking methods. The **Label Integrity Program (LIP)** requires that claims of variety (85% minimum), vintage (85% minimum), and region (85% minimum) are verifiable through wine company records auditable by the AWBC.

Multi-regional blending is common and legal in Australia — a wine may blend grapes from multiple GI regions, in which case "South Eastern Australia" (the umbrella GI covering most of the country) is used. This enables large-volume brands like Yellow Tail (Jacob's Creek and others) to blend consistently regardless of regional variation, but it limits geographic specificity.

## Section 6: Labeling, Intellectual Property, and Responsible Service

### What Must and May Appear on a Wine Label

**Required on all wine labels (US):**
- Brand name
- Class/type designation (wine, sparkling wine, dessert wine)
- Country of origin
- Net contents (volume in milliliters)
- Alcohol content (stated as % ABV, with a 1.5% variance tolerance for wines below 14% ABV; 1% variance for wines above 14%)
- Sulfite declaration ("Contains Sulfites" if total SO2 exceeds 10 mg/L)
- Health warning statement (DHHS/surgeon general warning — required since 1989)
- Name and address of bottler/importer

**Optional on US labels:**
- Vintage year (subject to 95% rule)
- Grape variety (subject to 75% rule)
- AVA or geographic designation (subject to 75% rule)
- Estate bottled designation (100% estate-grown and produced)
- Vineyard designation
- Production method claims (barrel-fermented, oak-aged, etc.)

**EU label differences:** EU wine labels require allergen information (sulfites, milk proteins, egg products — relevant if fined with egg whites or casein); a statement if the wine is organic; the EU emblem for PDO/PGI wines. From 2023, EU regulations mandate a QR code or web link providing full ingredient and nutritional information, replacing the previous requirement for printed nutritional panels on the label.

### Intellectual Property in Wine

**The Champagne Question:** "Champagne" is a protected geographical indication under EU law — only sparkling wine produced in the Champagne region of France using the *méthode champenoise* (or *méthode traditionnelle*) and specific permitted varieties may be labeled "Champagne" within the EU and in countries with bilateral trade agreements protecting EU GIs. The United States, however, grandfathered the use of "Champagne" as a semi-generic term: US producers who used the term before 2006 may continue to use it (with a mandatory US geographic qualifier) — hence "California Champagne" from producers like Cook's and Korbel technically remains legal in the US market, though declining in use.

**Other Protected Names:** Chablis, Burgundy, Claret, Rhine, Sauternes, and Sherry were historically used as generic terms for wine styles in the US, UK, and Australia. The EU has systematically negotiated GI protection in trade agreements to retire these uses. In Australia, the Wine Agreement with the EU (2010) phases out the use of these names. In the UK (post-Brexit), transition provisions apply. This is an ongoing geopolitical process.

**Trademark vs. GI:** Producers sometimes hold trademarks that conflict with protected GIs. The "Gallo" family of wines and their use of regional European names in certain markets; the contested use of "Prosecco" (now a PDO) in markets where it was previously used generically — these disputes illustrate the interface of trademark law and GI protection.

### Responsible Service and Alcohol Law

Wine directors bear legal and ethical responsibilities in service:

**Legal Drinking Age:** 21 in the United States — one of the highest globally. 18 in most of Europe, 19 in Canada.

**Dram Shop Liability:** In most US states, licensed establishments can be held legally liable if they serve alcohol to a visibly intoxicated guest who subsequently causes harm — most commonly a DUI injury accident. The standard of care requires staff to recognize signs of intoxication and refuse service or stop service.

**Over-Service Liability:** Training staff to recognize intoxication signs, maintain accurate counts of servings per guest, and refuse service when appropriate is not just ethical but a legal risk management issue. Some states impose mandatory Responsible Beverage Service (RBS) certification for servers and managers.

**Program-Level Protocols:** A wine director's responsibility includes establishing service protocols that manage liability: standardized pour sizes (5oz for wine by the glass is standard in the US), training staff on recognizing intoxication, clear refusal protocols, and management escalation paths. These protocols are program design issues as much as they are legal compliance issues.

> **Pro Tip:** The most common wine law error in wine program management is inaccurate list descriptions — describing a wine as "Reserva" when it does not meet the legal aging requirements, or listing a vintage year that does not match the actual bottle. These are label integrity errors with potential legal and certainly reputational consequences. Make it a practice to verify every wine list entry against the actual label before publication.
