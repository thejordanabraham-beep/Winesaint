## Section 1: The 1855 Classification — Full Detail

### Origins

The 1855 Classification was commissioned by Napoleon III for the Paris Universal Exhibition. Bordeaux wine brokers (courtiers) ranked the top châteaux of the Médoc — plus one property from Graves — based on price history and prestige. It has remained almost entirely unchanged for 170 years.

### The Five Growths (Crus Classés)

**Premier Grand Cru Classé (First Growths):**
- Château Lafite Rothschild (Pauillac)
- Château Latour (Pauillac)
- Château Margaux (Margaux)
- Château Haut-Brion (Pessac-Léognan/Graves) — the only non-Médoc property included in 1855
- Château Mouton Rothschild (Pauillac) — elevated from Second to First Growth in 1973 by decree of the French government; the only change to the classification since 1855

**Deuxièmes Crus (Second Growths — 14 châteaux):** Included Mouton Rothschild (until 1973), Cos d'Estournel (Saint-Estèphe), Montrose (Saint-Estèphe), Ducru-Beaucaillou (Saint-Julien), Léoville Las Cases, Léoville Barton, Léoville Poyferré (all Saint-Julien), Pichon Longueville Baron, Pichon Longueville Comtesse de Lalande (both Pauillac), Gruaud Larose (Saint-Julien), Lascombes, Brane-Cantenac, Durfort-Vivens, Rauzan-Ségla (all Margaux).

**Troisièmes, Quatrièmes, Cinquièmes Crus (Third through Fifth Growths — 46 total châteaux):** Range from Saint-Estèphe to Margaux. Noteworthy Fifth Growths include Château Lynch-Bages (Pauillac) and Château Pontet-Canet (Pauillac) — both widely considered to perform above their classification.

> **Pro Tip:** When a guest asks about the "best" Bordeaux, avoid anchoring to classification alone. Lynch-Bages is a Fifth Growth that comfortably outperforms many Second Growths in blind tastings and trades at near-Second Growth prices. Knowing these "over-performers" makes you indispensable to a guest building a cellar or buying at auction.

### Haut-Brion's Inclusion

Haut-Brion was the only château from outside the Médoc included in the 1855 Classification. Located in Pessac (now Pessac-Léognan, a sub-appellation of Graves), it was granted First Growth status because of its long-established international reputation — it was already famous in 17th-century England. Its inclusion reflects how dominant its reputation was at the time. It is the only First Growth that produces a significant amount of white wine (Château Haut-Brion Blanc), though not classified under the 1855 system.

### Mouton Rothschild's 1973 Elevation

Baron Philippe de Rothschild spent decades lobbying to elevate Mouton from Second to First Growth. The effort paid off in 1973 when the French Minister of Agriculture signed the decree. Baron Philippe's response became legendary: "Premier je suis, Second je fus, Mouton ne change." ("First I am, Second I was, Mouton does not change.") The elevation remains the only formal modification to the 1855 Classification.

> **Pro Tip:** This story is a great table conversation piece. Guests who buy First Growths love the background — it reinforces the history and prestige without sounding like a lecture.

### The Sauternes Classification of 1855

The 1855 also included a classification of Sauternes and Barsac:

- **Premier Cru Supérieur (sole):** Château d'Yquem — the only wine at this rank, above all others
- **Premiers Crus:** Château Climens (Barsac), Château Guiraud, Château Lafaurie-Peyraguey, Château Rieussec, Château Sigalas Rabaud, Château Suduiraut, Clos Haut-Peyraguey, Château Coutet (Barsac), Château Rayne Vigneau, Château Rabaud-Promis
- **Deuxièmes Crus:** 14 properties including Château d'Arche, Château Doisy-Daëne, Château Doisy-Védrines, Château Filhot, and others

## Section 2: The Other Classification Systems

### St-Émilion Classification

Unlike the 1855, the St-Émilion Classification is **periodically revised** — originally every 10 years, now more frequently. This revisability has caused significant controversy.

**Three tiers:**
- **Premier Grand Cru Classé A (PGCCA):** The apex. Currently: Château Pétrus is NOT in this system (Pomerol has no classification). Genuine PGCCA wines are: Château Ausone (now declassified by choice), Château Cheval Blanc, Château Angélus, Château Pavie. Note: Ausone and Cheval Blanc long held this status alone; Angélus and Pavie were promoted in 2012, triggering significant controversy and legal challenges from other châteaux.
- **Premier Grand Cru Classé B (PGCCB):** Approximately 14 châteaux, including Château Figeac, Château Canon, Clos Fourtet, Château Valandraud, and others
- **Grand Cru Classé:** A broader tier of approximately 64 châteaux

**The Controversy:** The 2012 revision promoted Angélus and Pavie to PGCCA status and demoted several established estates. Multiple châteaux filed legal challenges. The 2022 revision was even more contentious — Ausone and Cheval Blanc voluntarily withdrew from the classification entirely rather than participate in a system they viewed as compromised. This was a major story in Bordeaux and remains ongoing.

> **Pro Tip:** If a guest asks why Cheval Blanc isn't "classified," explain the voluntary withdrawal and frame it as a statement of independence — like a Michelin-starred chef returning their stars. It's a differentiated selling story, not a deficiency.

### The Graves / Pessac-Léognan Classification

Created in 1959 (not 1855), this classification covers both red and white wines from Graves — a rarity in Bordeaux where most classifications are red-only. **16 châteaux** are classified for red wine, **9 for white**. Key properties:

- **Both red and white:** Château Haut-Brion, Château La Mission Haut-Brion, Château Laville Haut-Brion (now Château La Mission Haut-Brion Blanc), Château Pape Clément, Domaine de Chevalier, Château Carbonnieux
- **White only:** Château Couhins, Château Couhins-Lurton, Château Malartic-Lagravière (also red)

Unlike the Médoc classification, there are no growth tiers — classified châteaux are simply "Cru Classé de Graves."

### Pomerol — No Classification

Pomerol has never had a formal classification, yet it contains some of the most sought-after and expensive wines in the world. The appellation is tiny (approximately 800 hectares), dominated by clay soils over iron-rich subsoil (crasse de fer), and planted almost entirely to Merlot with small amounts of Cabernet Franc.

**Key estates:**
- **Château Pétrus:** The undisputed king of Pomerol. Nearly 100% Merlot on a unique plateau of blue clay. Harvested when the clay dries in the afternoon to avoid diluting juice — sometimes picking only in the afternoon of a single day. Production is tiny (approximately 2,500–3,000 cases per year). Price-wise it trades with and often above the First Growths.
- **Le Pin:** Only ~500 cases per year. A micro-estate created in 1979 by the Thienpont family. Its scarcity makes it one of the most expensive wines in the world — sometimes surpassing Pétrus at auction. A garage wine before the term existed.
- **Château Lafleur:** Approximately 1,200 cases. A blend of roughly equal parts Merlot and Bouchet (Cabernet Franc), giving it a more structured profile than Pétrus. Often considered the second wine of Pomerol in terms of prestige.
- **Vieux Château Certan (VCC):** One of the oldest estates in Pomerol. Produces a more Cabernet Franc-dominant blend than most Pomerol properties, giving it a more austere, intellectual style.

## Section 3: Left Bank Appellations in Depth

The **Left Bank** refers to the west bank of the Gironde estuary and Garonne River. Soils are predominantly gravel over clay, perfect for **Cabernet Sauvignon**, which is the dominant variety in all Left Bank blends.

### Médoc vs. Haut-Médoc

The broad "Médoc" appellation covers the northern tip of the peninsula. "Haut-Médoc" is the higher-quality designation covering the southern portion, which contains the six major communal appellations. Wines labeled "Haut-Médoc" without a communal name are typically from properties that don't qualify for a commune-level appellation.

### The Six Major Appellations

**Margaux:**
The southernmost of the six major communes. Margaux soils are the thinnest and most gravelly in the Médoc, producing wines that are typically the most **aromatic, perfumed, and feminine** of the Left Bank. Cabernet Sauvignon dominates. Key châteaux: Château Margaux (First Growth), Château Palmer (Third Growth, performs at second growth level or above), Rauzan-Ségla, Château Cantenac Brown, Château Brane-Cantenac.

**Pauillac:**
Home to **three of the five First Growths** (Lafite, Latour, Mouton). Generally considered the most prestigious commune in Bordeaux. Pauillac wines are known for their power, structure, blackcurrant and cedar character, and exceptional aging potential. Soils are deep gravel. Cabernet Sauvignon often exceeds 70–80% of blends.

**Saint-Estèphe:**
The most northerly of the major communes. Heavier clay content in the soil produces wines with more tannin, higher acidity, and a more robust, sometimes rustic character when young. Best examples are long-lived. Key châteaux: Cos d'Estournel (Second Growth — with its distinctive Indo-Chinese pagoda label), Montrose (Second Growth), Calon-Ségur (Third Growth), Phélan Ségur.

**Saint-Julien:**
No First Growths but considered the most consistently excellent commune. Produces wines with a middle ground between Pauillac's power and Margaux's elegance. Dominated by Second Growths: Léoville Las Cases, Léoville Barton, Léoville Poyferré, Ducru-Beaucaillou, Gruaud Larose, Talbot (Fourth Growth), Beychevelle (Fourth Growth).

**Moulis:**
A smaller appellation producing wines of value. Château Chasse-Spleen is the standout — technically a Cru Bourgeois but widely respected as performing at Cru Classé level.

**Listrac:**
Adjacent to Moulis, the highest elevation in the Médoc. More Merlot in blends due to cooler, heavier soils. Less well-known but can offer value.

> **Pro Tip:** For guests on a budget who want "real Bordeaux," Saint-Julien Cru Classés and Moulis/Listrac estates are your moves. Chasse-Spleen is a name that impresses without the First Growth price tag.

## Section 4: Right Bank Appellations and Sauternes

### Right Bank Appellations

**Saint-Émilion:**
The ancient Roman town of Saint-Émilion gives its name to a large appellation (~5,500 ha) built on **limestone plateau and clay-limestone slopes**. Merlot and Cabernet Franc dominate. The best estates sit on the limestone plateau around the town (Cheval Blanc, Ausone) or on the côtes (slopes). Beyond the classified properties, Saint-Émilion Grand Cru (without "Classé") is simply a marketing designation requiring lower yields — many producers use it without meeting any additional quality bar.

**Pomerol:** (covered in Section 2)

**Fronsac and Canon-Fronsac:**
Often called the "sleeping giants" of Bordeaux. Located west of Pomerol on limestone hills, these two appellations produce Merlot-dominant wines of real quality at a fraction of Pomerol prices. Canon-Fronsac is the superior sub-appellation. Key producers: Château Canon de Brem, Château Moulin Haut-Laroque, Château Fontenil (Michel Rolland's estate).

> **Pro Tip:** Fronsac is one of the best value plays in serious Bordeaux. A guest who wants Right Bank complexity but isn't ready for Pomerol prices — Fronsac is your answer.

### Sauternes and Barsac

**Noble Rot (Botrytis cinerea):**
The foundation of Sauternes is **botrytis cinerea**, a beneficial mold that dehydrates and concentrates grapes while preserving and transforming acidity and flavor. The conditions required are unique: morning mist (from the Ciron River meeting the warmer Garonne) followed by warm, sunny afternoons. This allows botrytis to develop without destroying the fruit. Without this microclimate, Sauternes cannot be made.

**Botrytized grapes are picked by hand in multiple passes (tries) through the vineyard** — sometimes up to 10 or 12 tries per vintage, taking weeks. A single vine may yield only one glass of wine. This is why great Sauternes is expensive.

**Grape varieties:** Sémillon (dominant — most susceptible to botrytis and provides richness and body), Sauvignon Blanc (adds acidity and freshness), Muscadelle (small percentages for aromatics).

**Barsac** is a sub-appellation of Sauternes producing wines with a slightly more mineral, "drier" style. Producers may label their wine as either Barsac or Sauternes.

**Key producers:**
- **Château d'Yquem:** Premier Cru Supérieur. The only wine at this classification. Owned by LVMH. Produces wines of extraordinary concentration and longevity — great vintages age for 50–100 years. A half-bottle of 2001 d'Yquem on a list is a conversation-stopper.
- **Château Climens (Barsac):** Often considered the finest Barsac. More Sémillon-dominant than many, producing wines with extraordinary purity and a drier perceived finish. Biodynamic.
- **Château Rieussec:** First Growth Sauternes. Owned by the Rothschild family (same as Lafite). Rich, opulent, approachable young but ages beautifully.
- **Château Suduiraut:** First Growth. Produces some of the most lush, hedonistic Sauternes. 2001 is legendary.
- **Château Coutet (Barsac):** First Growth. Produces a special cuvée in exceptional years called "Cuvée Madame" — one of the rarest wines in Bordeaux.

> **Pro Tip:** Sauternes is the most undersold category in most restaurants. Guests think of it as dessert wine and pass. Reframe it: "This is what happens when you drop everything and just let the vineyard do something extraordinary for three months. It pairs with foie gras, blue cheese, or on its own at the end of a meal." The right story sells it.

## Section 5: Second Wines, En Primeur, and Winemaking

### Second Wines

Major Bordeaux châteaux produce a "second wine" from younger vines, less optimal parcels, or lots that don't make the grand vin. Second wines offer the DNA of the estate at lower prices and often drink earlier. They should not be dismissed — the winemaking is identical, the selection is simply stricter for the first wine.

| Grand Vin | Second Wine |
|---|---|
| Château Lafite Rothschild | Carruades de Lafite |
| Château Mouton Rothschild | Petit Mouton |
| Château Latour | Les Forts de Latour |
| Château Margaux | Pavillon Rouge du Château Margaux |
| Château Cheval Blanc | Le Petit Cheval |
| Château d'Yquem | Y (Ygrec) — a dry wine, not a second wine in the traditional sense |
| Château Léoville Las Cases | Clos du Marquis |
| Château Pichon Comtesse | Réserve de la Comtesse |
| Château Cos d'Estournel | Les Pagodes de Cos |

> **Pro Tip:** Second wines are a sommelier's secret weapon for guests who want the brand but not the price. "Les Forts de Latour drinks like a Second Growth Pauillac at a fraction of the first wine's price" is a real selling sentence.

### En Primeur (Bordeaux Futures)

**What it is:** En primeur, or "futures," is the system by which Bordeaux châteaux sell wine while it is still aging in barrel — approximately 18 months before bottling and release. Buyers pay upfront, typically receiving the wine 2–3 years later.

**How it works:**
1. After harvest (October), the trade descends on Bordeaux for barrel tastings (usually March–April of the following year)
2. Châteaux set an opening price (prix de sortie)
3. Négociants buy from the château, then sell to international merchants through the **Place de Bordeaux** (the Bordeaux marketplace system)
4. Consumers buy from merchants, often at a premium

**The Place de Bordeaux:** A traditional three-tier system. Châteaux sell exclusively through courtiers (brokers) to négociants, who sell to importers and merchants worldwide. This system gives châteaux market reach without managing international distribution themselves.

**Pros of buying en primeur:**
- Potential to secure wines at lower prices in great vintages before the market moves
- Access to allocation of top estates that may be near-impossible to find at retail post-release
- Locking in quantities of high-demand wines (DRC, Pétrus, Le Pin — though these are rarely sold en primeur at accessible prices)

**Cons:**
- Capital tied up for 2–3 years with no guaranteed return
- Quality cannot be fully assessed from barrel samples — wines change dramatically
- If the vintage disappoints post-release, buyers may have overpaid
- Storage and insurance during the intervening years
- Not all merchants honor contracts if they go under

> **Pro Tip:** If a collector guest brings up en primeur, acknowledge that 2009, 2010, 2015, 2016, and 2022 were the recent vintages where buying futures clearly made financial sense. For many other years, the secondary market provides better value because château opening prices have risen sharply. Be honest — this earns long-term trust.

### Key Vintages

| Vintage | Character | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| 2000 | Excellent | Left Bank superlative. Right Bank also exceptional. The "millennial vintage." |
| 2005 | Outstanding | Powerful, structured. Some suggest the greatest balanced vintage of the modern era. Long aging curve. |
| 2009 | Excellent | Rich, ripe, hedonistic. More immediately approachable than 2005. Both banks excellent. |
| 2010 | Outstanding | Many consider it the greatest modern Bordeaux vintage. Exceptional structure, acidity, and concentration. |
| 2015 | Excellent | Ripe, generous, both banks superb. |
| 2016 | Outstanding | Near-perfect Right Bank. Left Bank exceptional especially Pauillac and Saint-Julien. Long-aging. |
| 2018 | Excellent | Rich, powerful, high alcohol. Ripe and accessible. |
| 2019 | Outstanding | Elegant and fresh. Many consider this the finest since 2010, especially Right Bank. |
| 2020 | Excellent | Early, concentrated vintage. High quality, especially Pomerol and Saint-Émilion. |
| 2022 | Outstanding | Exceptional growing season. Early results suggest one of the greatest decades-spanning vintages. Both banks. |

### Winemaking: Oak, Blending, and Bank Philosophy

**New oak:** First Growths and top Second Growths typically age in **100% new French oak** barrels (225L barriques) for 18–24 months. As quality level decreases, new oak percentages drop: Crus Classés might use 50–80%, Crus Bourgeois might use 30–50%. New oak contributes structure, tannin, and vanilla/spice notes but can overwhelm if the wine lacks the concentration to absorb it.

**Blending philosophy — Left vs. Right Bank:**
- **Left Bank:** Cabernet Sauvignon dominant (typically 60–90%), with Merlot for softness, and small amounts of Cabernet Franc, Petit Verdot, and occasionally Malbec for complexity. Petit Verdot adds deep color and violet aromatics; it's only used in small proportions but valued for its impact.
- **Right Bank:** Merlot dominant (often 70–95% in Pomerol, 60–80% in Saint-Émilion), with Cabernet Franc as the key secondary grape (especially at Cheval Blanc and Ausone). Less Cabernet Sauvignon.

**Why the difference?** Soil. Cabernet Sauvignon needs deep, warm, well-drained gravel to ripen fully. The Left Bank's Médoc gravel provides this. The Right Bank's clay and limestone soils stay cooler and moister — perfect for Merlot and Cabernet Franc, which ripen earlier and thrive in these conditions.

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| 1855 Classification | The ranking of Médoc (and Haut-Brion) châteaux into five growths, commissioned for the Paris Exhibition |
| Premier Grand Cru Classé | Top tier of the St-Émilion classification; or (with "A") the apex tier |
| En primeur | Bordeaux futures system — buying wine while still in barrel, 18+ months before release |
| Négociant | A merchant who buys wine from châteaux and resells it; central to the Place de Bordeaux system |
| Botrytis cinerea | Noble rot — a fungus that dehydrates and concentrates grapes, essential to Sauternes production |
| Grand Vin | The flagship, first wine of a Bordeaux château |
| Second Wine | A château's second-label wine from younger vines or less optimal lots |
| Petit Verdot | A minor Bordeaux variety used in small proportions for color and violet aromatics |
| Crasse de fer | Iron-rich subsoil in Pomerol, key to Pétrus's unique terroir |
| Tries | Multiple selective harvest passes through a vineyard to pick botrytized grapes at optimal concentration |
| Place de Bordeaux | The traditional three-tier distribution system: château → courtier → négociant → world trade |
| Cru Bourgeois | A category below Cru Classé but above generic Haut-Médoc for quality-tier properties |
| Monopole | A wine produced entirely from a single estate with no multiple-owner plots (more relevant in Burgundy but applicable to Bordeaux single-owner estates) |
