# Module 12: Blind Tasting Fundamentals

**Level:** 2 — Advanced
**Audience:** Sommeliers, Beverage Managers, Senior Front-of-House Staff
**Duration:** ~60 minutes
**Certification Requirement:** Pass quiz with 80% or higher. Required for Wine Saint Certified Level 2 designation.

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this module, you will be able to:

- Execute a systematic, step-by-step deductive tasting method from sight through to conclusion
- Identify the key variety markers for major red and white wine grapes by aroma and palate profile
- Apply color, fruit condition, and structural cues to narrow region, climate, and vintage window
- Distinguish between Old World and New World wine styles using a defined checklist of indicators
- Translate technical tasting observations into confident, guest-friendly language on the floor

---

## Section 1: Why Blind Tasting Matters Professionally

Blind tasting is not a party trick. It is the most disciplined form of sensory training available to a wine professional, and the skills it builds directly improve how you perform on the floor every day.

**The three professional purposes of blind tasting:**

**1. Palate calibration.** Tasting without the label removes confirmation bias. When you know you're tasting a $200 Burgundy, your brain fills in the gaps. Blind, you only perceive what's actually there. This sharpens your ability to assess wine objectively, which in turn makes your recommendations more accurate and your guests more satisfied.

**2. Building a sensory library.** Each blind tasting session adds data points to your internal reference map. Over time, you build an unconscious archive of how Riesling TDN (petrol note) smells at different ages, how Syrah from the Rhône differs in texture from Syrah from Barossa, what genuine volcanic mineral character smells like versus oak-derived smokiness. This library cannot be built any other way.

**3. Guest communication.** When you can articulate *why* a wine smells like cassis and graphite rather than just saying "it's classic Cabernet," you become a more compelling and credible communicator. Guests respond to specificity and confidence. Blind tasting trains both.

A sommelier who has tasted 10,000 wines blind is a fundamentally different professional than one who has tasted 10,000 wines with the label showing, even if the total number of wines consumed is identical.

## Section 2: The Systematic Deductive Method

The deductive tasting method is a structured protocol for gathering evidence about a wine before making a conclusion. Its value is that it prevents you from jumping to conclusions (which is the source of most tasting errors) and ensures you've gathered all available data.

Work in this order every time: **Sight → Nose → Palate → Conclusion.**

---

### SIGHT

Pour approximately 1–1.5 oz into a clean, appropriate glass. Evaluate on a white background (a napkin or white tablecloth).

**Color Hue:**
The hue tells you about grape variety, age, and winemaking. Look at the edge of the wine (the rim), not the center, for the most accurate color reading.

*Reds:*
- **Purple/blue-red** = young; Syrah, young Malbec, Merlot
- **Ruby/red** = mid-age; Pinot Noir, Sangiovese, young Cabernet
- **Garnet** = developing age; Cabernet, Tempranillo, Nebbiolo
- **Brick/orange-red at rim** = significant age or warm climate; aged Burgundy, Rioja Reserva, mature Barolo

*Whites:*
- **Pale straw/green-tinged** = cool climate, young, unoaked; Riesling, Muscadet, Pinot Grigio
- **Straw/gold** = warmer or slightly older; Chardonnay, Viognier, Grüner Veltliner
- **Deep gold/amber** = age, botrytis, significant oak, or extended skin contact (orange wine); aged white Burgundy, Sauternes, orange wine
- **Copper/salmon** = rosé, or orange wine

**Color Depth/Intensity:**
- **Pale/light** = cool climate grapes (Pinot Noir, Gamay, Nebbiolo), or light extraction
- **Deep/opaque** = warm climate, thick-skinned grapes (Syrah, Cabernet Sauvignon, Malbec), or long maceration

**Clarity:**
A bright, clear wine indicates clean winemaking. A slight haze in an unfiltered wine is not a fault. Cloudiness in a wine that should be clear may indicate a flaw (refermentation in bottle, microbial instability).

**Viscosity/Legs (Tears):**
Thick, slow-moving legs indicate higher alcohol and/or higher residual sugar. Thin, fast legs suggest lower alcohol, drier wine. Note: legs are not a quality indicator; they are a data point for alcohol and sweetness levels.

> **Pro Tip:** Don't stare at the legs and proclaim the wine "high quality." Guests sometimes believe this myth. Redirect: "The legs are telling us about the alcohol level and body, let me take you through the nose and palate."

---

### NOSE

The nose provides the richest source of data. Evaluate in two passes.

**Pass 1: First Impression (Clean vs. Faulty)**
Before anything else, ask: is this wine clean? Common faults detectable by nose:
- **Cork taint (TCA):** Damp cardboard, musty basement, newspaper
- **Reduction:** Struck match, burnt rubber, garlic (usually dissipates with air)
- **Volatile acidity (VA):** Nail polish remover, sharp vinegar
- **Oxidation:** Flat, bruised apple, sherry-like (when unexpected)
- **Brettanomyces (Brett):** Barnyard, horse stable, leather, band-aid (low levels can be pleasant and intentional)

**Pass 2: Aroma Analysis**

*Fruit Condition; the single most important climate indicator:*
- **Fresh, tart, primary fruit** (green apple, lime, sour cherry, cranberry) = cool climate, young wine
- **Ripe, round fruit** (peach, ripe plum, blackberry, tropical) = warm climate or riper vintage
- **Dried or cooked fruit** (fig, raisin, prune, stewed plum) = very warm climate, old vines, or fortified/late-harvest wine

*Fruit Type, narrows the grape variety:*
- **Citrus** (lemon, lime, grapefruit): Riesling, Sauvignon Blanc, Assyrtiko, Albariño
- **Stone fruit** (peach, apricot, nectarine): Viognier, Chardonnay (warmer), Pinot Gris
- **Tropical** (mango, pineapple, passion fruit): warm-climate Sauvignon Blanc, Chardonnay
- **Red fruit** (strawberry, raspberry, red cherry): Pinot Noir, Grenache, Sangiovese, Gamay
- **Dark fruit** (blackberry, cassis, black plum): Cabernet Sauvignon, Syrah, Malbec, Mourvèdre
- **Dried fruit** (prune, fig, raisin): warm-climate reds, aged wines, sweet wines

*Non-Fruit Aromas:*
- **Floral:** Viognier (apricot blossom), Gewürztraminer (rose, lychee), Muscat (orange blossom), Nebbiolo (dried rose, violet), Tempranillo (violet)
- **Earth/mineral:** Pinot Noir (forest floor, wet earth), Burgundy (terroir-driven), Riesling (slate/petrol), Chablis (chalky/flinty), Assyrtiko (volcanic salt)
- **Oak indicators:** Vanilla, toast, cedar, smoke, baking spice, dill/coconut (American oak specifically); if these are present alongside fruit, oak aging is confirmed
- **Secondary aromas (yeast contact):** Bread, brioche, lees, cream; typical in Champagne, white Burgundy, lees-aged wines
- **Tertiary aromas (bottle age):** Leather, tobacco, earth, dried flowers, truffle, mushroom, petroleum (Riesling TDN), coffee, cedar box; indicates significant age

*Intensity:*
Rate the nose: light, moderate, pronounced. A very pronounced, expressive nose generally suggests a more aromatic variety (Gewürztraminer, Viognier) or significant age development. A subtle, restrained nose may indicate Chablis, Muscadet, or a young Riesling.

---

### PALATE

Confirm and expand what you found on the nose. Evaluate in this order:

**Sweetness:** Dry, off-dry, medium-dry, medium-sweet, sweet, or luscious. Detect at the tip of the tongue within the first second of entry.

**Acidity:** Evaluate the salivation response and the sensation along the sides of the tongue. High acid = profuse salivation, "cut" sensation, refreshing finish (Riesling, Sauvignon Blanc, Sangiovese, Nebbiolo). Low acid = flat, soft, broad texture (Viognier, warm-climate Grenache, Malbec).

**Tannin** (red wines only): Evaluate the drying sensation on gums, cheeks, and the inside of your lips. Fine-grained tannin suggests premium fruit and/or extended maceration (Pinot Noir, good Cabernet Sauvignon). Coarse, drying tannin suggests aggressive extraction or young, undeveloped wine (young Barolo, young Baga, young Tannat). Silky tannin = Merlot, St. Laurent, mature Pinot Noir.

**Alcohol:** Detect via a warm, sometimes burning sensation at the back of the throat. Low alcohol (under 12%) = Riesling, Mosel whites, German Kabinett, Vinho Verde. High alcohol (14.5%+) = Zinfandel, Amarone, Châteauneuf-du-Pape Grenache, Napa Cabernet, Shiraz.

**Body:** Light, medium-light, medium, medium-full, full. A function of alcohol, residual sugar, and extract.

**Flavor Profile:** Should mirror the nose. If it doesn't, take note. Discrepancies between nose and palate can indicate winemaking intervention (significant chaptalization, acidification, or added tannin) or can be a clue to the wine's overall integration.

**Finish Length:** Count mentally. Less than 5 seconds = short (simple wine). 5–10 seconds = medium. 10–15 seconds = long. 15+ seconds = very long. A persistent, evolving finish is a hallmark of great wine.

---

### CONCLUSION

You have gathered all your evidence. Now synthesize it:

1. **Grape Variety:** State your best guess and your reasoning. "The high acid, pale ruby color, red cherry/earth nose, and silky tannin suggest Pinot Noir."
2. **Old World or New World:** Use the checklist in Section 3 below.
3. **Climate:** Cool, moderate, or warm, based on fruit condition and alcohol.
4. **Region:** Narrow it down using all available structural data.
5. **Vintage Window:** Young (0–5 years), developing (5–10), mature (10–15+), or past peak.
6. **Quality Assessment:** Rate the wine honestly: simple, good, very good, outstanding.

## Section 3: Reading Color and Fruit as Climate Indicators

### Color as Clue

| Visual Cue | Likely Inference |
|---|---|
| Pale ruby, translucent | Cool climate Pinot Noir, young Gamay, Nerello Mascalese |
| Deep purple-black, opaque | Young Syrah, Malbec, Cabernet Sauvignon |
| Garnet with orange/brick rim | Age: 8–15+ years depending on variety |
| Pale straw with green tint | Cool white: Riesling, Muscadet, Pinot Grigio, young Chablis |
| Deep gold, full viscosity | Age, oak, botrytis, or warm climate white |
| Copper/salmon (rosé depth) | Dry Provençal rosé (pale copper), orange wine (amber), or darker rosé |
| Amber/orange | Orange wine (skin contact white) or significant oxidative aging |

### Fruit Condition as Climate Map

This is one of the most reliable indicators available:

- **Tart green and citrus fruit** (lime, green apple, gooseberry, sour cherry) → Cool climate. The grapes ripened slowly, retaining high natural acidity and fresh primary fruit.
- **Ripe, round fruit** (peach, ripe plum, blackberry jam) → Warm to hot climate, or a particularly warm vintage in a moderate climate.
- **Dried, cooked, jammy fruit** (prune, raisin, stewed berry) → Very warm climate, late harvest, or significant bottle age. Also characteristic of fortified wines and some Mediterranean styles.

The key word is *condition*, not *type*. The difference between "fresh raspberry" and "dried raspberry/potpourri" on the nose of a Pinot Noir tells you as much about where the wine is from as any label.

## Section 4: Classic Variety Markers, The Arsenal

Memorize these. They are your core vocabulary for deductive tasting.

### Red Varieties

**Cabernet Sauvignon:**
Cassis/blackcurrant (primary), cedar, graphite/lead pencil, dried herb (tobacco leaf), green bell pepper when underripe. Structure: firm, grippy tannin; medium-high acid; full body. Oak signature: often evident (vanilla, toast). Age: tertiary leather, cigar box, dried herb.
*Trap: might be confused with Merlot if tannin is soft. Check for the graphite and green herb edge.*

**Merlot:**
Plum (red and dark), chocolate, bay leaf, coffee. Noticeably softer tannin than Cabernet. Rounder, plusher mouthfeel. Right Bank Bordeaux adds clay mineral and tobacco notes. New World: more fruit-forward and jammy.
*Trap: Pomerol-level Merlot can fool people into guessing Pinot Noir. Check the density and plum/chocolate signature.*

**Pinot Noir:**
Red cherry, strawberry, raspberry (cool climate); riper cherry and cranberry sauce (warmer or New World). Characteristic earthy, forest floor, undergrowth notes (especially Burgundy). Dried rose, violet with age. High acid. Silky, low tannin. Pale color that fades to brick early.
*Trap: Often confused with Grenache (both pale, low tannin). Check acid level (Pinot Noir = high; Grenache = low) and check for white pepper.*

**Syrah/Shiraz:**
Cool climate (Rhône/Northern Rhône): Pepper (white and black), bacon/smoked meat, olive, dark fruit. Warm climate (Barossa/McLaren Vale): Dark jam, chocolate, full body, high alcohol, sometimes coconut (American oak).
*Trap: Bacon/smoky note can confuse with aged wines. Check fruit condition and alcohol level.*

**Sangiovese:**
Sour red cherry, tomato leaf, dried herbs, leather. High acid, high tannin, medium body. Color goes orange-brick at the rim relatively quickly. Prominent savory/earthy character.
*Trap: Might seem tannic enough to be Cabernet. Check the sour cherry/tomato character and the orange rim.*

**Nebbiolo:**
Pale ruby to garnet color (despite the wine's power; don't let color fool you). Dried rose, tar, dried cherry, licorice. Sky-high tannin and acid when young, mellowing to leather, tobacco, and truffle with age (10–20 years). Long finish. Barolo/Barbaresco or Langhe.
*Trap: Pale color suggests Pinot Noir. But tannin and structure will be far more aggressive in a young Nebbiolo.*

**Tempranillo:**
Cherry, dried strawberry, leather, vanilla (especially with American oak in Rioja), dill, cedar. Medium acid and tannin. In Rioja Reserva/Gran Reserva: strong coconut/dill from American oak is a giveaway.
*Trap: Oak signature can mislead. Look for the specific leather and dried cherry fruit character.*

**Grenache:**
Pale color. Raspberry, white pepper, garrigue (Provençal herbs), orange peel with age. Low acid, high alcohol. Silky mouthfeel.
*Trap: Often confused with Pinot Noir. Check the low acid and white pepper/garrigue note.*

**Malbec:**
Violet/purple color (very deep). Dark plum, blackberry, dark chocolate, violet floral note. Velvety tannin, lower acid. Warm, rich finish. Argentine Malbec is the archetype.
*Trap: Deep color and dark fruit can suggest Syrah. Check for the violet note and the soft, round tannin.*

---

### White Varieties

**Chardonnay:**
The chameleon. Unoaked: green apple, lemon, chalk (Chablis), or peach and citrus (lighter). Oaked: butter, cream, vanilla, toast, hazelnut (Côte de Beaune). Warm climate: pineapple, ripe melon, low acid. Structure ranges enormously; this is one of the harder grapes to identify blind precisely because of winemaking variation.
*Key marker: If oaked, the butter/cream from malolactic fermentation (MLF) is usually unmistakable.*

**Sauvignon Blanc:**
Grass, grapefruit, gooseberry, cat pee (Loire), passion fruit, guava (New Zealand, warmer). High acid. Typically unoaked (except white Bordeaux/Pessac). Flinty/smoky mineral note in Sancerre (Sauvignon Fumé).
*Trap: Occasionally confused with aromatic whites. Check the pyrazine (green, grassy) and high acid.*

**Riesling:**
Lime, lemon, green apple (young); honey, beeswax, petrol/gasoline (TDN, the petroleum compound 1,1,6-trimethyl-1,2-dihydronaphthalene) (aged). High acid. Low alcohol in cool climates (Mosel = 7–9% ABV). Residual sugar level varies wildly; key to assess sweetness vs. acidity balance.
*Key marker: TDN petrol note is almost exclusively Riesling. When you smell it, you know.*

**Pinot Grigio vs. Pinot Gris:**
Same grape, opposite styles. Pinot Grigio (Italian): light body, citrus, crisp, neutral, low alcohol. Pinot Gris (Alsace): full body, spiced pear, honey, ginger, yellow apple, sometimes off-dry to sweet. Evaluate body and texture to distinguish.

**Viognier:**
Peach, apricot, orange blossom, jasmine (powerful floral nose). Full body. Low to medium acid; this is the tell. Rich, oily texture. Finishes slightly bitter.
*Trap: Confused with oaked Chardonnay due to richness and weight. The intense floral/apricot note and especially the low acid are the differentiators.*

**Gewürztraminer:**
Lychee, rose petal, Turkish delight, ginger spice, exotic. Low acid. Full body. High alcohol. One of the most recognizable grapes blind; if you smell lychee, it's almost always Gewürztraminer.

**Chenin Blanc:**
Quince, honey, beeswax, lanolin, green apple (dry); dried apricot, wax, toast (sweet/aged). High acid across all styles: the defining structural characteristic. Loire (Vouvray, Savennières) = classic. South Africa (Chenin/Steen) = more tropical, often richer.

**Assyrtiko:**
Saline minerality, volcanic mineral, lemon/lime, white grapefruit. High acid, full body (especially Smaragd-equivalent level). Very specific flavor; the salt-mineral note is almost unique to Santorini Assyrtiko.

## Section 5: Old World vs. New World Checklist

This is one of the most commonly tested deductive assessments. Use all the following indicators together; no single factor is definitive.

| Indicator | Old World | New World |
|---|---|---|
| **Acidity** | Higher (especially in whites and lighter reds) | Lower (fruit ripeness and warm climate reduce natural acid) |
| **Fruit Condition** | Fresh, primary, often tart or precise | Riper, rounder, often jammy or tropical |
| **Alcohol** | Generally lower (12–13.5% for most table wines) | Generally higher (13.5–15.5% common in reds) |
| **Tannin (reds)** | Often firmer, more drying, more integrated over time | Often softer, more immediately approachable |
| **Oak Style** | New French oak = vanilla, toast, subtle cedar; typically restrained use | American oak common in parts of South America and Spain = dill, coconut, vanilla bomb; also heavy new French oak in high-end Napa |
| **Earthiness/Savory Notes** | More common; terroir expression over fruit | Less common; fruit expression predominates |
| **Body** | Often medium; alcohol levels support lighter body | Often full; high alcohol supports full body |
| **Finish Character** | Mineral, savory, complex | Fruit-driven, spice, sometimes sweet-edged finish |

**Important:** These are tendencies, not rules. Warm vintages in Europe push Old World wines toward New World characteristics. Cool-climate New World regions (Hemel-en-Aarde, Mornington Peninsula, Central Otago) can produce wines of Old World restraint. Use the totality of the evidence.

> **Pro Tip:** When you're stuck between Old and New World, ask yourself: "Does this wine taste more like where the grapes grew, or more like what the winemaker did to it?" Old World = terroir-forward. New World = production-forward. Not always true, but it's a useful heuristic.

## Section 6: Common Deductive Traps

**Grenache vs. Pinot Noir (The Pale Red Trap)**
Both are pale in color. Both have silky, low tannin. Both show red fruit. Differentiators: *Grenache has low acid; Pinot Noir has high acid.* Grenache will also show white pepper, garrigue, and higher alcohol. Pinot Noir will show earthy forest floor and sour cherry. Check the salivation response; if your mouth is not flooded with acid, it's likely Grenache.

**Viognier vs. Oaked Chardonnay (The Rich White Trap)**
Both are full-bodied and golden. Differentiators: *Viognier has intensely aromatic floral nose (peach blossom, apricot, jasmine); Chardonnay with MLF has butter/cream.* Also: Viognier has significantly lower acid; it will feel flat and opulent, while Chardonnay (even buttery) retains more acidity. Viognier often has a slightly bitter finish.

**Malbec vs. Syrah (The Dark Fruit Trap)**
Both are deeply colored with dark fruit. Differentiators: *Syrah has pepper (white and black), bacon/savory meat notes, and firmer tannin.* Malbec has violet floral note, rounder softer tannin, and dark chocolate. Syrah finish is savory; Malbec finish is plush.

**Young Barolo vs. Young Cabernet Sauvignon (The Tannic Monster Trap)**
Both are grippy and tannic when young. Differentiators: *Barolo (Nebbiolo) has pale color and dried rose/tar characteristics.* Young Cabernet has deeper color and cassis/graphite. The color is your first clue; if a very tannic red is pale ruby, it's almost certainly Nebbiolo.

**Marlborough SB vs. Loire SB**
Both are high-acid, herbal Sauvignon Blanc. Differentiators: *Marlborough adds passion fruit, intense tropical fruit, and high aromatic intensity.* Loire (Sancerre/Pouilly-Fumé) is more restrained, flinty/smoky mineral-driven, and less obviously aromatic. Climate cues on the palate: Loire = lighter body, more mineral finish; Marlborough = more body, more persistent tropical.

## Section 7: Tasting Exercises and Building Your Aroma Library

**The Comparative Tasting Method:**
The fastest way to calibrate your palate is to taste two wines side by side that share one characteristic but differ on another. Examples:

- Two Pinot Noirs: one Burgundy, one Sonoma. Same grape; observe climate differences.
- Two Chardonnays: one unoaked (Chablis), one oaked (Puligny-Montrachet or California). Same grape; observe winemaking differences.
- Two reds of similar structure: Grenache vs. Pinot Noir. Observe acid/texture differences.

**The Aroma Library:**
Build a physical kit or mental library of non-wine reference aromas. Smelling actual: white peppercorns (for Syrah/Grüner Veltliner), graphite pencil shavings (for Cabernet), vanilla bean (for new oak), a leather wallet (for aged reds), lychee fruit (for Gewürztraminer), petroleum or rubber band (for TDN in Riesling), slate or wet stone (for Mosel Riesling terroir).

The more real-world anchors you build, the faster your pattern recognition becomes.

**The Tasting Grid:**
Use a structured written grid for every practice tasting. Resist writing free-form notes; the discipline of systematic categories prevents mental shortcuts and ensures you don't miss a step. A simple grid: Appearance / Nose (Clean? Fruit type? Oak? Secondary/Tertiary?) / Palate (Sweetness / Acid / Tannin / Alcohol / Body / Flavors / Finish) / Conclusion.

## Section 8: Communicating Tasting Notes to Guests

Technical tasting vocabulary is your internal tool. Guest communication is a translation exercise.

**What to avoid:**
- "This wine has high pyrazine expression with characteristic TDN on the retronasal olfaction.". This communicates nothing to a guest.
- Overly abstract poetic language: "A shimmering tapestry of dreams and wet rock.". Also useless.

**What works:**
- Reference familiar flavors and textures: "This smells like a just-cut lemon, some fresh herbs, and a stone driveway after rain, very refreshing."
- Use analogies to familiar experiences: "It has the same kind of bright, mouth-watering quality as a good lemonade, that's the acidity at work."
- Connect to the food: "The high acid here is going to cut right through the richness of the butter sauce; it acts like a squeeze of lemon."
- Signal quality markers without jargon: "Notice how the finish keeps going, that's a sign of how well-made this wine is."

**Translating technical terms:**

| Technical Language | Guest Language |
|---|---|
| High acidity | Bright, refreshing, mouth-watering, zesty |
| High tannin | Firm, grippy, structured, will age beautifully |
| Full body | Rich, full, substantial, fills your mouth |
| Low acid | Soft, smooth, round, easy-drinking |
| TDN/petrol note (Riesling) | "A distinctive mineral quality: some people describe it as a slight hint of flint or fuel; it's a sign of age and complexity" |
| Bretty/barnyard | "Earthy, a bit funky in an interesting way: it's intentional and adds complexity" |
| MLF/butter | "Creamy, rich, buttery texture: it's been through a secondary fermentation that adds that softness" |

> **Pro Tip:** The best guest-facing tasting note does three things: it describes what the guest will smell and taste, it explains why that's interesting or appropriate, and it connects to what they're eating. "This has a bright citrus and herb character, it's going to work beautifully with the salmon because the acidity will lift the richness of the fish." That's a complete communication.

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| Deductive tasting | A systematic tasting method that moves from observation to conclusion without prejudgment |
| TDN | 1,1,6-trimethyl-1,2-dihydronaphthalene; the compound responsible for the petrol/gasoline note in aged Riesling |
| Pyrazine | Organic compound responsible for green bell pepper and grass notes in Sauvignon Blanc and underripe Cabernet Sauvignon |
| TCA (Cork taint) | 2,4,6-trichloroanisole; the compound responsible for musty, damp cardboard off-flavors in corked wine |
| Volatile acidity (VA) | The portion of acidity detectable by smell; at high levels manifests as nail polish remover or sharp vinegar |
| Brett (Brettanomyces) | Wild yeast that produces barnyard, leather, horse stable aromas; a fault at high levels, complexity at low levels |
| Malolactic fermentation (MLF) | Secondary fermentation converting sharp malic acid to softer lactic acid; produces butter/cream aromas |
| Tannin polymerization | The chemical process by which tannin molecules link together during aging, producing softer, silkier texture |
| Garrigue | Provençal term for the wild herbs (lavender, thyme, rosemary, fennel) of the southern French scrubland; a classic aroma descriptor for Grenache and southern Rhône wines |
| Terroir-forward | A wine style in which the taste of the place (soil, climate, site) is the dominant flavor driver rather than winemaking intervention |
| Retronasal | The passage from the back of the mouth to the nasal cavity; responsible for aroma perception while the wine is in the mouth |
| Old World | Wines from traditional European wine regions (France, Italy, Spain, Germany, etc.) |
| New World | Wines from regions outside Europe (USA, Australia, NZ, South Africa, South America) |
