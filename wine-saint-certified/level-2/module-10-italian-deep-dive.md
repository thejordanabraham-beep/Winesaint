## Section 1: Italy's Classification System — The Full Picture

### DOCG, DOC, IGT, Vino da Tavola

Italy's wine classification is officially hierarchical but functionally more complex than any other major wine country.

**DOCG (Denominazione di Origine Controllata e Garantita):**
The highest official tier. There are currently 77 DOCGs. Wines must meet strict requirements: specific geographic origin, approved grape varieties, minimum aging, maximum yields, and government-tasting panel approval. The "G" (Garantita) means government-guaranteed authenticity. Examples: Barolo, Barbaresco, Brunello di Montalcino, Chianti Classico, Amarone della Valpolicella.

**DOC (Denominazione di Origine Controllata):**
The second tier, with over 340 designations. Similar rules to DOCG but without the final tasting guarantee. This tier contains both serious, historic appellations (Bolgheri, Montepulciano d'Abruzzo, Barbera d'Asti before its DOCG promotion) and many lesser-known ones.

**IGT (Indicazione Geografica Tipica):**
The third tier, broadly equivalent to French IGP (Vin de Pays). Geographic indication with relaxed grape variety and winemaking rules. This is where the critical paradox lives.

**Vino da Tavola:**
Table wine. No geographic or grape indication. The most basic category.

### The Super Tuscan Paradox — Why IGT Can Be More Prestigious Than DOCG

In the 1970s–1980s, innovative Tuscan producers began making wines that intentionally violated DOC/DOCG rules. The reasons:
- The Chianti DOC regulations at the time required a percentage of white grapes (Trebbiano, Malvasia) in the blend — diluting quality
- Producers wanted to use Cabernet Sauvignon, Merlot, and Syrah, which were not permitted in Chianti or Brunello DOCs
- Some wanted to make single-variety Sangiovese without geographic restriction

The result: wines like **Sassicaia** (Cabernet Sauvignon/Cabernet Franc), **Tignanello** (Sangiovese with Cabernet Sauvignon — created by Antinori, the first commercial use of Cabernet in Chianti), and **Ornellaia** (Cabernet Sauvignon/Merlot) were technically only "Vino da Tavola" or later IGT — yet they commanded prices far above any DOCG.

Sassicaia's success forced Italy's wine authorities to eventually create a dedicated DOC for it in 1994 (Bolgheri Sassicaia DOC — the only DOC named after a single estate). But most Super Tuscans remain IGT wines.

**The lesson:** In Italy, the label tier is a starting point, not a quality guarantee. An IGT from Tuscany made by a world-class producer from impeccably grown fruit can be demonstrably superior to a DOCG wine from an unmotivated co-operative. Classification tells you where the wine comes from and what rules it followed — the producer and their standards tell you what it tastes like.

> **Pro Tip:** When a guest looks puzzled by an IGT on your list at a premium price, use the Super Tuscan story: "Italian wine law is strict about what you can blend in their top appellations. Some of the most talented producers in Tuscany refused those restrictions — their wines are labeled 'IGT' by rule, not because they're lesser. It's the Italian equivalent of a Michelin-starred chef calling their dish 'comfort food.'"

## Section 2: Piedmont — Beyond Barolo and Barbaresco

Piedmont ("Piemonte") in northwest Italy is Italy's Burgundy — a fog-shrouded, hilly, truffle-rich region where indigenous varieties reign and quality and tradition are paramount. Level 1 covered Barolo and Barbaresco. Here we go deeper.

### The Langhé and Roero

The **Langhe** hills form the core of Piedmont winemaking — Barolo and Barbaresco sit here. The **Roero** is the area across the Tanaro River from the Langhe, with sandier soils and a more relaxed style.

**Langhe DOC:** A catch-all for wines that don't meet specific appellation criteria — often used by Barolo/Barbaresco producers for declassified Nebbiolo (labeled "Langhe Nebbiolo"), Chardonnay, or Freisa. A bottle of Langhe Nebbiolo from Giacomo Conterno or Bartolo Mascarello can be exceptional.

**Roero DOCG:** White Arneis (covered below) and red Nebbiolo from the Roero. Lighter, more approachable Nebbiolo than Barolo. Good value; undersold.

### Northern Piedmont — Gattinara and Ghemme

North of the Langhe, the **Alto Piemonte** (northern Piedmont) was once more prestigious than Barolo. Volcanic and glacial moraine soils produce Nebbiolo-based wines of remarkable elegance:

- **Gattinara DOCG:** Nebbiolo (locally called Spanna) with up to 10% Vespolina and Uva Rara. Lighter color and more aromatic than Barolo. Ages beautifully. Reference producer: **Antoniolo** (three single-crus: Osso San Grato, San Francesco, Castelle). Also Travaglini.
- **Ghemme DOCG:** Similar profile to Gattinara; slightly richer. Cantalupo is the key producer.
- **Boca, Bramaterra, Sizzano, Fara, Lessona:** Small DOC appellations with minuscule production. Collectible Nebbiolo-based wines for serious hunters.

### Barbera and Dolcetto

**Barbera d'Asti DOCG** and **Barbera d'Alba DOC:** Barbera is the most planted red grape in Piedmont. Naturally high acidity, deep color, low tannin. At its best: bright cherry, herbs, refreshing, food-versatile. In the hands of top producers (Giacomo Bologna's Braida pioneered single-vineyard Barbera, showing its serious aging potential), Barbera can age 10+ years. **Barbera d'Asti DOCG** (elevated from DOC in 2008) includes the prestigious sub-zones **Nizza** (further elevated to Nizza DOCG in 2014) and **Tinella**.

**Dolcetto d'Alba DOC:** Dolcetto is the everyday red of the Langhe — lower acid than Barbera, bitter almond finish, meant to be drunk young. "Dolcetto" means "little sweet one" — not because it's sweet (it's dry) but because of the early-ripening softer fruit compared to Nebbiolo. Paired with the rich, fatty food of Piedmont. Producers like Giacomo Conterno, Bruno Giacosa, and Vietti make serious Dolcettos.

### White Varieties of Piedmont

**Arneis (Roero Arneis DOCG / Langhe Arneis DOC):** Native white variety of the Roero. Once nearly extinct, revived by Bruno Giacosa and Vietti in the 1970s. Produces dry, aromatic, almond-scented whites. Best drunk within 2–3 years. Bruno Giacosa's Roero Arneis was the landmark bottling.

**Gavi / Gavi di Gavi DOCG (Cortese grape):** From southeastern Piedmont near the border of Liguria. 100% Cortese. When labeled "Gavi di Gavi" it comes from the town of Gavi itself (the inner core). Crisp, dry, mineral, citrus and almond notes. Should be drunk young and fresh. La Scolca (Black Label) is the iconic producer. Excellent restaurant white — guest-friendly, food-versatile.

### Sweet and Sparkling Piedmontese Wines

**Moscato d'Asti DOCG:** One of Italy's greatest sweet wines — and chronically undersold. Low alcohol (~5.5%), gently sparkling (frizzante rather than fully sparkling), intensely floral and peach-scented, low in sugar compared to typical dessert wines, refreshing acidity. From Canelli, Santo Stefano Belbo, and surrounding communes. Made by the Asti method (tank fermentation stopped early by chilling). Producers: La Spinetta, Paolo Saracco, Vietti.

**Asti Spumante DOCG:** Fully sparkling version of Moscato d'Asti. Higher pressure, sweeter. The bulk version is supermarket fare; quality versions from small producers are underrated.

**Brachetto d'Acqui DOCG:** Sweet, frizzante or spumante, from the Brachetto grape. Deep ruby-pink, intensely floral with wild strawberry and rose petal. One of the most seductive wines in Italy. Braida (Giacomo Bologna's family estate) makes the benchmark.

> **Pro Tip:** Moscato d'Asti is the perfect close to a tasting menu. It's not trying to be dessert wine — it's a fresh, aromatic, low-alcohol palate refresher. Guests who think they don't like sweet wines often love it when you frame it correctly: "It's more like a floral sorbet than a dessert wine — only five and a half percent alcohol, more refreshing than rich." La Spinetta or Paolo Saracco are easy sells.

## Section 3: Tuscany — Beyond Chianti Classico and Brunello

### The Super Tuscans of Bolgheri

**Bolgheri DOC** (Maremma coast, southwest Tuscany) is the birthplace of the Super Tuscan movement. The warm, coastal climate and diverse soils (sand, clay, rock) suit Cabernet Sauvignon, Merlot, and Syrah better than the cooler inland hills.

- **Sassicaia (Tenuta San Guido):** Created by Marchese Mario Incisa della Rocchetta using Cabernet Sauvignon/Cabernet Franc cuttings from Château Lafite Rothschild. First commercial vintage 1968. Now has its own DOC: **Bolgheri Sassicaia DOC** — the only Italian DOC named for a single estate.
- **Ornellaia (Tenuta dell'Ornellaia):** Cabernet Sauvignon dominant with Merlot, Cabernet Franc, Petit Verdot. Now owned by Frescobaldi. Makes **Masseto** — a 100% Merlot from a single clay-rich plot that has become one of Italy's most expensive wines.
- **Antinori's Guado al Tasso:** Major Bolgheri estate; excellent Cabernet Sauvignon/Merlot.
- **Ca' Marcanda (Gaja):** Angelo Gaja of Piedmont purchased this estate and makes three Bolgheri wines: Promis, Magari, Camarcanda.

**Maremma** is the broader coastal zone surrounding Bolgheri. Many excellent producers have moved in as Bolgheri prices and land values rose: Morisfarms, Brancaia, Le Macchiole.

### Other Key Tuscan Appellations

**Morellino di Scansano DOCG:** Sangiovese-based red from the coastal Maremma. "Morellino" is the local name for Sangiovese. Warmer climate than Chianti — wines are richer, rounder, more immediate. Good value alternative to Chianti Classico. Key producers: Moris Farms, Le Pupille, Fattoria di Magliano.

**Vino Nobile di Montepulciano DOCG:** Sangiovese (locally called Prugnolo Gentile) with optional blending components. From Montepulciano, a hill town in southern Tuscany (not to be confused with the Montepulciano grape from Abruzzo). Positioned between Chianti Classico and Brunello in style — structured, earthy, savory. Key producers: Avignonesi, Poliziano, Boscarelli.

**Vernaccia di San Gimignano DOCG:** Italy's first DOC (granted 1966), later elevated to DOCG. 100% Vernaccia di San Gimignano, a grape indigenous to this medieval hilltop town. Dry, crisp, slightly bitter finish, mineral and almond notes. Excellent with Tuscan food. Montenidoli and Teruzzi & Puthod are leading producers.

**Carmignano DOCG:** Located northwest of Florence, Carmignano has a remarkable distinction: it was the **first Italian appellation to officially permit Cabernet Sauvignon** in the blend — a tradition dating back to Cosimo de' Medici, who introduced "Uva Francesca" (French vines) to his estate in the 17th century. The DOC acknowledged this history in 1975. Sangiovese plus up to 20% Cabernet. Estate Capezzana is the reference producer.

## Section 4: Northeast Italy — Friuli, Alto Adige, Veneto in Depth

### Friuli-Venezia Giulia

Friuli (the short name) in the extreme northeast of Italy, bordering Slovenia, is one of Italy's most exciting regions for white wine. Its importance far exceeds its profile among most consumers.

**Key sub-zones:**
- **Collio DOC** (also spelled "Collio Goriziano"): The premium zone, immediately at the Slovenian border. Rolling hills with "ponca" — a distinctive local soil of marl and sandstone that promotes mineral, aromatic whites. The reference area for Friuli whites.
- **Colli Orientali del Friuli DOC** (COF): Larger zone to the north and east. Similar soils, also excellent whites; also the home of **Picolit** (a sweet wine from the Picolit grape — rare, expensive, and extraordinary).
- **Friuli Isonzo DOC and others:** More commercial zones in the plains.

**Key Friulian grape varieties:**
- **Friulano (formerly Tocai Friulano):** The flagship grape of Friuli. Renamed "Friulano" in 2007 due to confusion with Tokay wines. Dry, herbal, almond-bitter finish, full-bodied. Quintessential Friulian white. Marco Felluga, Vie di Romans, Livio Felluga (Terre Alte blend) are landmarks.
- **Ribolla Gialla:** Ancient indigenous grape. In a conventional style: crisp, floral, light. In the hands of Josko Gravner (who pioneered extended maceration on the skins): a deep amber, tannic, oxidative orange wine of extraordinary complexity. This is the epicenter of the modern orange wine movement.
- **Pinot Grigio Ramato:** The traditional Friulian style of Pinot Grigio — made with skin contact, producing a copper/amber "ramato" (copper-colored) wine. Completely different from the industrial pale Pinot Grigio of commercial wine. Gravner and Radikon helped revive this tradition. Friulano and Ramato are Friuli's claim to a pre-modern style of white winemaking that now drives the global natural wine market.

**The Orange Wine Tradition:** Extended maceration on white grape skins — sometimes weeks or months — produces wines with tannin, amber/orange color, oxidative notes, and fermented complexity. Josko Gravner (who ages wines in large Georgian clay amphorae) and Stanko Radikon are the founders of the modern orange wine movement. Both are from Oslavia in Collio, near the Slovenian border. Today orange wines are made globally, but they began here.

> **Pro Tip:** Orange wine is the most common "what is this?" question you'll get from curious guests. Have a clear, brief answer: "It's white wine made the way red wine is — left on the skins during fermentation, which extracts color and texture. It tastes more savory and tannic than conventional white wine. Think of it as the bridge between white and red." Then pour a small taste.

### Alto Adige / Südtirol

The northernmost wine region in Italy, bordering Austria and Switzerland in the Alps. This is wine's highest-altitude, German-speaking frontier — most residents speak German, the wine culture is Austrian, and the grape varieties reflect both Alpine and Mediterranean influences.

**Climate:** Cool-to-cold, with extreme diurnal temperature variation (hot days, cold nights at elevation). Soils vary: porphyry and granite in the north, limestone in the south.

**Key grapes:**
- **Gewürztraminer:** The village of **Tramin (Termeno)** in Alto Adige is believed to be the grape's origin. Alto Adige Gewürztraminer — especially from Cantina Tramin or Alois Lageder — can be extraordinary: lychee, rose petal, ginger, rich texture but with Alpine freshness rather than Alsace's oiliness.
- **Pinot Bianco (Weissburgunder):** Considered one of the finest expressions in the world here. Fresh, mineral, floral. Cantina Terlano produces an exceptional Pinot Bianco Vorberg Riserva that ages 20+ years.
- **Pinot Grigio (Rulander/Grauburgunder):** More mineral and structured than generic Italian Pinot Grigio. Worth the price premium.
- **Lagrein:** Indigenous red variety. Deep color, dark fruit, moderate tannin. The best expression of local red character. Under-known internationally. Excellent value.
- **Schiava (Vernatsch):** Light, red-fruited, low-tannin red. Local everyday wine. Not serious but charming in context.
- **Blauburgunder (Pinot Noir):** Alto Adige Pinot Noir from the best producers (Franz Haas, Haas Schweizer, Cantina Girlan) is among Italy's finest.

Key producers: Alois Lageder (large and consistently excellent across varieties), Cantina Terlano (co-operative; produces benchmark whites, especially Terlaner Cuvée), Franz Haas (Pinot Nero, Gewürztraminer), Elena Walch (premium single-vineyard wines), Tiefenbrunner.

### Veneto — Beyond Soave and Valpolicella

Level 1 covered Soave and Valpolicella/Amarone basics. Going deeper:

**Lugana DOC:** Southern end of Lake Garda, bordering Lombardy. Made from **Turbiana** (a biotype of Trebbiano di Lugana, genetically distinct from other Trebbiano). Rich, textured whites with mineral limestone character. Can age surprisingly well for 5–10 years. Ca' dei Frati is the reference producer.

**Bardolino DOC:** Light, fresh red from the eastern shore of Lake Garda. Corvina, Molinara, Rondinella (the Valpolicella blend). More similar to a rosé in weight than a serious red. Bardolino Chiaretto is the rosé version — one of Italy's finest.

**Custoza DOC:** White wine from southwest of Verona. Blend of Garganega, Trebbiano di Lugana, and others. Fresh, easy, food-friendly aperitif white. Underappreciated.

## Section 5: Central and Southern Italy — The Full Map

### Central Italy

**Lazio (Rome and surroundings):**
- **Frascati DOC:** Classic Roman white from the Castelli Romani hills southeast of Rome. Made primarily from Malvasia di Candia and Trebbiano Toscano. Historically important, often ordinary today. The best examples (Villa Simone, Castel de Paolis) show what Frascati can be with lower yields.
- **Est! Est!! Est!!! di Montefiascone DOC:** Famous more for its name (a medieval bishop's wine scout was so enthusiastic he wrote "Est! Est!! Est!!!" — "It is! It is!! It is!!!" — on the cellar door) than its quality today. Northern Lazio; Trebbiano and Malvasia blend. A story wine — fun to put on a list.

**Umbria (the green heart of Italy):**
- **Sagrantino di Montefalco DOCG:** One of the most distinctive red wines in Italy. Sagrantino is considered the most tannic grape variety in the world — massively structured, deeply colored, with a dense bitterness when young. It requires 10–20 years to show its best. Produced only in and around Montefalco in central Umbria. Key producers: **Arnaldo Caprai** (revived the variety in the 1980s; their Vigna Collepiano and 25 Anni are benchmarks), Tabarrini. Also a sweet, passito version exists.
- **Montefalco Rosso DOC:** A softer blend with Sangiovese, Sagrantino, and Merlot. More approachable than DOCG Sagrantino.
- **Orvieto DOC:** Historic white wine from the dramatic cliffside town. Trebbiano and Grechetto blend. Most commercial Orvieto is neutral and forgettable; the best examples (Palazzone, Barberani) show real character and even a stunning late-harvest "Muffa Nobile" (botrytized) version.

**Marche (Adriatic coast):**
- **Verdicchio dei Castelli di Jesi DOC/DOCG:** Italy's finest indigenous white grape from central Marche. Verdicchio has natural high acidity, a distinctive bitter almond finish, and lime/green herb character. It ages beautifully — top examples can go 10–15 years. The Classico sub-zone and Riserva category are the quality tier. Key producers: **Garofoli** (Podium is their benchmark Riserva), **Umani Ronchi**, **Sartarelli**.
- **Verdicchio di Matelica DOC:** Smaller production zone from the interior hills; denser soils, more structured Verdicchio with even more aging potential.
- **Rosso Conero DOC/DOCG:** 100% Montepulciano (the grape, not the town) from the Monte Conero coastal area near Ancona. Deep, rich red. Umani Ronchi (Pelago) and Moroder are key producers.
- **Rosso Piceno DOC:** Sangiovese and Montepulciano blend from southern Marche. Good value.

### Southern Italy — Campania and the "Barolo of the South"

Campania (Naples and surroundings) has an extraordinary wine heritage dating to ancient Greece. It was Greece's most prized wine region in antiquity; today it's Italy's most exciting story for indigenous white and red varieties.

**Taurasi DOCG — "the Barolo of the South":**
Made from **Aglianico** grown in the volcanic hills east of Naples. Aglianico is Italy's great southern red variety: high tannin, high acidity, deep color, earthy, smoky, and capable of extraordinary complexity and aging. Taurasi requires 3 years aging minimum (4 for Riserva), including at least 1 year in barrel. The result is austere when young, opening to leather, tar, dark cherry, and volcanic minerality over 10–20 years. Reference producer: **Mastroberardino** (the family that kept Aglianico alive during phylloxera and modernization; their Radici Riserva is the benchmark). Also **Feudi di San Gregorio** (large, quality-focused, international), **Luigi Tecce**, **Quintodecimo** (Luigi Moio).

**Fiano di Avellino DOCG:** Fiano is one of Italy's great indigenous white grapes. Grown in the volcanic limestone hills of Irpinia, Fiano produces wines of extraordinary aromatic complexity — hazelnut, tropical fruit, mineral, lanolin — with the acidity and structure to age 5–15 years. Mastroberardino, Feudi di San Gregorio, Pietracupa.

**Greco di Tufo DOCG:** Greco (believed to be one of the ancient Greek-introduced varieties) from the volcanic tufa soils around Tufo. Richer, more textural than Fiano, with apricot, mineral, and volcanic character. Mastroberardino, Feudi di San Gregorio, Villa Raiano.

> **Pro Tip:** The Campanian whites (Fiano di Avellino, Greco di Tufo) are among the most intellectually interesting wines on any Italian list. When a guest says they want Italian white wine but "not Pinot Grigio," these are your go-to answer. Frame them: "These were the wines the ancient Romans considered the greatest in their empire. They're indigenous to Campania's volcanic hills and they don't exist anywhere else in the world."

### Puglia

Italy's "heel" — one of the world's most prolific wine-producing regions by volume, now developing a serious quality identity.

- **Primitivo di Manduria DOC/DOCG:** Primitivo is genetically identical to Zinfandel (both descended from the Croatian grape Crljenak Kaštelanski). Grown in Puglia's hot, flat, limestone soils, it produces rich, jammy, high-alcohol reds. Best versions from old vines are genuinely complex and have a distinctive wild berry, dried fruit, and spice profile. Gianfranco Fino (ES — from a single centenarian-vine plot) is the benchmark.
- **Negroamaro:** Puglia's most widely planted indigenous variety. Dark color, bitter finish ("negro amaro" = black bitter), tobacco, dark fruits. Best in Salice Salentino DOC and as a component in many Puglian reds. Leone de Castris (producer of the legendary Rosé "Five Roses" in 1943) is the iconic house.
- **Salice Salentino DOC:** Negroamaro-based red from the Salento peninsula (the very tip of the heel). Deep, rich, earthy. Often overlooked value.

### Sicily

Once known as a source of anonymous, hot, high-alcohol blending wine, Sicily has undergone a complete transformation since the 1990s.

- **Nero d'Avola:** The flagship Sicilian red variety. Deep color, rich dark fruit, chocolate, tar. Best from the Noto area in southeast Sicily. Can be extraordinary from low-yielding old vines. Planeta, COS, Gulfi (Nerobufaleffj) are key.
- **Nerello Mascalese on Etna:** This is Sicily's most exciting wine story. Mount Etna is an active volcano; its black volcanic soils and high altitude (600–1,000m) produce Nerello Mascalese of extraordinary lightness, perfume, and minerality — often compared to Burgundy's Pinot Noir. Two designations: **Etna Rosso DOC** (Nerello Mascalese) and **Etna Bianco DOC** (Carricante, a white variety). Single-contrada (contrada = village/plot) wines from specific lava flows are highly sought. Key producers: **Benanti** (pioneered the Etna movement in the 1990s), **Cornelissen** (natural wine extremist; orange and red; iconic bottles), **Passopisaro**, **Terre Nere**, **Russo**. Etna wines are allocated and increasingly expensive — prices have risen dramatically since 2010.
- **Marsala DOC:** Fortified wine from western Sicily. A once-great wine reduced to cooking ingredient status in most markets. The serious version (Vecchio and Stravecchio, aged 5+ years) is extraordinary. Pellegrino and De Bartoli (Marco De Bartoli's Marsala Superiore and Vecchio Samperi — the latter a non-fortified solera wine) are the quality references.

### Sardinia

A world unto itself — ancient granite soils, strong winds, a wine culture closer to Spain than mainland Italy.

- **Cannonau di Sardegna DOC:** Cannonau is the local name for **Grenache** (Garnacha in Spanish). Sardinia likely exported Grenache to Spain centuries ago, not the other way around — though the debate continues. Cannonau is the island's flagship red: earthy, spicy, herbal, medium-bodied. Sardinian villages produce old-vine Cannonau from centenarian vines. This region is one of the world's Blue Zones (populations with unusual longevity) — Cannonau's high polyphenols are sometimes cited. Argiolas, Sella & Mosca, Gabbas are key producers.
- **Vermentino di Sardegna DOC / Vermentino di Gallura DOCG:** The DOCG of Gallura (northeastern Sardinia) is the quality peak. Vermentino produces dry, aromatic, full-bodied whites with bitter almond, citrus, and saline mineral character. The schist and granite soils of Gallura give it a distinctive rocky minerality. Capichera is the reference producer; also Sella & Mosca, CS Gallura.

## Section 6: Key Indigenous Grapes — Reference Guide

Italy has more indigenous grape varieties than any other country — over 2,000 are documented, with approximately 500 in commercial cultivation. These are the ones every Level 2 sommelier must know:

| Grape | Region(s) | Style Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Nebbiolo** | Piedmont, Alto Piemonte | High tannin, high acid, pale garnet; tar, roses, leather; Barolo, Barbaresco, Gattinara |
| **Sangiovese** | Tuscany (Chianti, Brunello, Morellino, Vino Nobile), Marche, Umbria, Emilia-Romagna | Italy's most widely planted red; tart cherry, earthy, leather; style ranges from light (Chianti) to massive (Brunello) |
| **Barbera** | Piedmont | High acid, low tannin, deep color; bright cherry, herbs; excellent acidity makes it food-versatile |
| **Dolcetto** | Piedmont | Low acid, low tannin, bitter almond finish; meant to be drunk young; food-friendly |
| **Montepulciano** | Abruzzo, Marche | (Not the town — the grape) Deep, dark, plummy, moderate tannin; not related to Sangiovese; Montepulciano d'Abruzzo DOC |
| **Aglianico** | Campania (Taurasi), Basilicata (Aglianico del Vulture) | High tannin, high acid, volcanic earthy character; requires aging; "the Barolo of the South" |
| **Nero d'Avola** | Sicily | Deep, dark, rich; chocolate, tar; warm climate power |
| **Nerello Mascalese** | Sicily (Etna) | Light, perfumed, high acid, volcanic mineral; resembles Pinot Noir in delicacy |
| **Corvina** | Veneto | Base of Valpolicella, Amarone, Ripasso; bright cherry, herbal; concentrated through appassimento |
| **Garganega** | Veneto (Soave) | Neutral to aromatic; almond, citrus, mineral; Soave Classico from volcanic basalt |
| **Verdicchio** | Marche | High acid, bitter almond finish, citrus; ages beautifully |
| **Fiano** | Campania | Hazelnut, tropical fruit, floral, lanolin; excellent aging potential |
| **Greco** | Campania (Greco di Tufo) | Rich, apricot, mineral, volcanic; ancient variety |
| **Trebbiano** | Throughout Italy | Neutral, high acid; workhouse white; Trebbiano Toscano (lesser), Trebbiano d'Abruzzo (can be excellent) |
| **Vermentino** | Sardinia, Liguria, Tuscany (Maremma) | Aromatic, bitter almond, saline mineral; full-bodied for a white |
| **Ribolla Gialla** | Friuli | Crisp and floral conventionally; amber and tannic under extended maceration (orange wine) |
| **Friulano** | Friuli | Herbal, almond-bitter, full-bodied; indigenous to Friuli |
| **Arneis** | Piedmont (Roero) | Dry, aromatic, almond-scented; drink young |
| **Moscato Bianco** | Piedmont | Aromatic, peach and apricot, floral; base of Moscato d'Asti |
| **Primitivo** | Puglia | Genetically identical to Zinfandel; jammy, high alcohol, dark fruit |
| **Negroamaro** | Puglia | Dark, earthy, bitter finish; tobacco, dark berry |
| **Cannonau** | Sardinia | Sardinian name for Grenache; earthy, spicy, herbal |
| **Sagrantino** | Umbria (Montefalco) | Most tannic grape in the world; requires decades of aging; dark, bitter, complex |

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| DOCG | Denominazione di Origine Controllata e Garantita; Italy's highest wine classification tier |
| DOC | Denominazione di Origine Controllata; second tier of Italian wine classification |
| IGT | Indicazione Geografica Tipica; third tier; geographic indication with flexible rules; home of Super Tuscans |
| Super Tuscan | Wines from Tuscany that use non-traditional varieties or blends, classified as IGT but often commanding premium prices |
| Appassimento | The process of drying grapes to concentrate sugar and flavor; used in Amarone, Recioto, and some Ripasso wines |
| Ripasso | Valpolicella re-fermented on the dried grape pomace from Amarone production, gaining body and complexity |
| Passito | Sweet wine made from dried (or botrytized) grapes |
| Contrada | A named vineyard plot on Mount Etna, equivalent to a Burgundy lieu-dit or lieux-dits |
| Ramato | Copper-colored Pinot Grigio made with skin contact; traditional in Friuli |
| Ponca | The distinctive marl-sandstone soil of Collio in Friuli |
| Crasse de fer | (Also relevant in Tuscany's Maremma) Iron-rich subsoil element |
| Schist | Metamorphic rock found in Gallura (Sardinia) and Etna; produces distinctive mineral wine character |
| Primitivo | Puglian red grape genetically identical to Zinfandel and Croatian Crljenak Kaštelanski |
| Solera | A fractional blending system where wine from multiple vintages is blended by drawing from progressively older casks; used in some Marsala production |
