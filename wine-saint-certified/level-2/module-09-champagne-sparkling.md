By the end of this module, you will be able to:

## Section 1: Champagne Geography, The Five Zones

Champagne is the northernmost major wine region in France, located approximately 90 miles northeast of Paris. Its climate sits at the very edge of viticultural possibility. Cold winters, cool summers, and the constant risk of frost create conditions that, paradoxically, produce the most refined and celebrated sparkling wines in the world. The cold limits ripeness, preserving acidity and delicacy; the chalky soils provide drainage and reflect heat back to the vines.

The region encompasses approximately 34,000 hectares of vineyards. Five distinct zones define the character of Champagne's raw material:

### 1. Montagne de Reims

The forested plateau south of Reims, with its escarpment facing north, east, and south. The chalky soils of the Côte de Noirs (the northern face) produce **Pinot Noir of extraordinary depth and structure**, despite the north-facing exposure (white chalk reflects sunlight and radiates heat stored during the day). Grand Cru villages: Verzenay, Verzy, Mailly-Champagne, Bouzy, Ambonnay.

- Verzenay and Verzy: North-facing, structured, high-acid Pinot Noir, the backbone of many prestige cuvées.
- Bouzy and Ambonnay: South-facing plots in the Montagne, producing richer, fuller Pinot Noir. Bouzy is also the source of a rare still red wine, Coteaux Champenois Rouge.

Dominant grape: **Pinot Noir**

### 2. Vallée de la Marne

The river valley running east-west through the heart of Champagne. Warmer, with clay-rich soils mixed with chalk. The Marne Valley is Champagne's warmest zone, ideal for **Pinot Meunier**, which ripens earlier than Pinot Noir and Chardonnay, making it frost-resistant. This valley produces wines with fruit-forward character, freshness, and accessibility. Historically overlooked for prestige, but grower producers in the Vallée (like Jérôme Prévost) have changed that perception.

Dominant grape: **Pinot Meunier**

### 3. Côte des Blancs

Running south from Épernay, the Côte des Blancs is the kingdom of **Chardonnay**. Pure chalk soils, deep Belemnita chalk, produce Chardonnay with extraordinary mineral precision, electric acidity, and citrus/floral character. Grand Cru villages: Cramant, Avize, Oger, Le Mesnil-sur-Oger.

- **Le Mesnil-sur-Oger:** The most celebrated Chardonnay village in Champagne. Produces wines of jaw-dropping precision and longevity. Pierre Péters is based here; Krug's Clos du Mesnil is the most famous single-vineyard Champagne in the world.
- **Cramant and Avize:** Equally prestigious. Agrapart (Avize), Larmandier-Bernier (Vertus, just south) are key grower references.

Dominant grape: **Chardonnay**

### 4. Côte de Sézanne

South of the Côte des Blancs, the Côte de Sézanne is a less-discussed extension of the chalky terrain. Chardonnay-dominant, producing wines with slightly more body and less racy acidity than Côte des Blancs. Few Grand or Premier Cru villages, but several quality grower producers. Increasingly relevant as climate change pushes ripeness.

Dominant grape: **Chardonnay**

### 5. Aube / Côte des Bar

The southernmost and most geographically separate zone, located ~100 km south of Épernay near the Burgundy border. Soils here are **Kimmeridgian limestone and clay**; the same as Chablis, rather than chalk. Dominant grape: **Pinot Noir**, producing fuller, earthier character. Long dismissed as a source of blending material, the Aube has dramatically raised its profile. Key area: Barséquanais sub-region. Rosé de Riceys (a rare still rosé from Pinot Noir) comes from here.

Dominant grape: **Pinot Noir**

### The Échelle des Crus

Champagne's village classification system, the **échelle des crus** ("ladder of growths"), rates villages on a percentage scale from 80–100%:
- **Grand Cru (100%):** 17 villages
- **Premier Cru (90–99%):** ~42 villages
- All other villages: 80–89%

Historically this scale determined the price paid per kilogram of grapes. While price negotiations are now more complex, the rating remains a quality indicator. The 17 Grand Cru villages span all major zones: Montagne (Verzenay, Ambonnay, Bouzy, Mailly, Verzy, Sillery, Puisieulx, Beaumont-sur-Vesle), Côte des Blancs (Cramant, Avize, Oger, Le Mesnil-sur-Oger, Oiry), and the Marne (Aÿ, Tours-sur-Marne, classified for both colors, and Louvois).

## Section 2: Champagne Grapes and the Traditional Method

### The Grapes

**Pinot Noir:** Accounts for approximately 38% of total Champagne plantings. The dominant variety in the Montagne de Reims and Aube. Provides **body, structure, red fruit character, and aging potential**. Paradoxically, Pinot Noir is a red grape that produces a white wine when pressed gently and quickly enough to avoid skin contact. The first pressings (cuvée) from Pinot Noir are often the most pale and delicate.

**Pinot Meunier:** Approximately 32% of plantings. The workhorse of the Vallée de la Marne. Bud-bursts and ripens earlier than Pinot Noir, making it more frost-resistant. Contributes **fruitiness, freshness, and early approachability** to blends, and was historically used to make NV Champagne accessible young. For decades dismissed as a "lesser" variety, Meunier is now recognized as a serious grape in its own right, especially in single-variety expressions from growers like Jérôme Prévost (La Closerie, Faîte) and Krug (who use significant percentages).

**Chardonnay:** Approximately 30% of plantings. Dominant in the Côte des Blancs. Provides **elegance, acidity, citrus, chalk mineral notes, and the capacity to age to extraordinary complexity**. The sole variety in Blanc de Blancs.

**Rare permitted varieties (combined <1%):**
- **Pinot Blanc:** Clean, neutral, used occasionally
- **Pinot Gris (Fromenteau):** Adds body and richness in very small amounts
- **Arbanne:** Ancient variety, highly aromatic; extremely rare; a few producers (Drappier) use it
- **Petit Meslier:** High-acid, late-ripening; extremely rare; used by Laherte Frères

### The Traditional Method, Step by Step

The **méthode champenoise** (called "méthode traditionnelle" outside Champagne) is the defining process that creates the finest sparkling wines in the world. Every step serves a purpose.

**1. Harvest:**
Grapes are hand-harvested (for all quality Champagne; machine harvesting is used only in the most basic production). Harvest in Champagne is extraordinarily labor-intensive, approximately 120,000 workers participate during the short window. Grapes must be picked intact; any crushing would release color from red grape skins.

**2. Pressing (Pressurage):**
Champagne uses specific press sizes. A traditional Coquard press holds **4,000 kg of whole clusters** (a "marc"). Strict rules govern extraction:
- The first 2,050 liters of juice are the **cuvée**; the finest, most delicate, most prized press fraction.
- The next 500 liters are the **taille**, slightly more extracted, darker, more phenolic; used in blending or sold off.
- Everything after is rejected. Pressing must be complete within approximately 4 hours.

Many prestige houses use only the cuvée for their finest wines.

**3. First Fermentation (Fermentation Alcoolique):**
The must is fermented in stainless steel or, for some traditionalist houses (Krug, Bollinger, Salon's partner Delamotte), in neutral oak barrels. Yeasts convert sugars to alcohol, producing a still, dry, high-acid base wine (vin clair). The base wines are tasted extensively during this period to identify the character of each press, vineyard, village, and variety.

**4. Assemblage (Blending):**
The most critical artistic step, particularly for non-vintage Champagne. The chef de cave (cellar master) blends wines from:
- Multiple villages and vineyards
- Multiple grape varieties
- Multiple vintages, reserve wines from previous years (for NV only)

The goal is to achieve the **house style**, a consistent flavor profile year after year, regardless of vintage variation. This is the skill that separates great NV Champagne from the rest. Houses like Krug, Bollinger, and Billecart-Salmon age reserve wines extensively; Krug uses wines going back 10+ years. NV Champagne may contain 20–50 component wines.

**5. Liqueur de Tirage (Tirage Dosage):**
Before bottling, a measured mixture of **sugar and yeast** (the liqueur de tirage) is added to the base wine. This triggers the second fermentation in bottle. Typically 24 g/L of sugar is added, producing approximately 6 atmospheres of pressure (about 3x a car tire) and ~1.2% additional alcohol.

**6. Second Fermentation in Bottle:**
Sealed with a temporary crown cap, the bottle is placed horizontally in cellar. Over 6–8 weeks, the yeast consumes all added sugar, converting it to CO₂ (which dissolves under pressure into the wine) and alcohol. The yeast then dies, producing **lees** (spent yeast cells) that remain in contact with the wine during aging.

**7. Aging on Lees:**
This is where the magic happens. As the dead yeast cells break down through **autolysis**, they release amino acids, fatty acids, and polysaccharides into the wine, producing the characteristic toasty, brioche, biscuity, creamy notes that define aged Champagne. Minimum legal aging requirements:
- NV (non-vintage): 15 months minimum (12 on lees)
- Vintage: 3 years minimum (on lees)
- Most quality houses exceed these minimums substantially. Krug NV ages 6+ years; vintage Champagnes from Pol Roger or Billecart-Salmon often age 7–10 years before release.

**8. Riddling (Remuage):**
The lees must be collected in the neck of the bottle for removal. In traditional riddling, bottles are placed in **pupitres** (A-frame wooden racks) and turned by hand, 1/8 turn per day, gradually tilting from horizontal to vertical (inverted) over 6–8 weeks, moving the lees plug toward the crown cap. This was the role of the **remueur**, a skilled artisan who could riddle 40,000 bottles per day.

Modern production uses **gyropalettes** (large wire cages that riddle mechanically). They accomplish in 3–7 days what pupitres achieve in weeks, and do so more consistently. All major houses use gyropalettes for NV; some still hand-riddle prestige cuvées.

**9. Disgorgement (Dégorgement):**
The neck of the inverted bottle is frozen in an ice-brine bath, creating a plug of frozen wine containing the lees. The crown cap is removed; the internal pressure ejects the frozen plug cleanly. The wine is immediately assessed and refilled. Modern disgorgement happens at very high speed (can be 20,000+ bottles per hour in large facilities).

**Late disgorgement / RD (Récemment Dégorgé):** Bollinger created the concept of keeping wine on its lees far longer than usual before disgorgement. Wines gain extraordinary depth, creaminess, and complexity. RD is disgorged to order (relatively). Billecart-Salmon, Gosset, and others now practice similar late disgorgement.

**10. Dosage:**
After disgorgement, a small amount of liquid (the **liqueur d'expédition**) is added to top up the bottle: a mixture of reserve wine and sugar. The quantity of sugar in this liqueur determines the final sweetness level of the Champagne. A winemaker who wants Brut Nature adds no sugar at all (or a tiny amount to stabilize). See sweetness levels below.

**11. Corking and Resting:**
The mushroom cork is inserted (under enormous pressure, it starts as a cylinder), wired down with the muselet, and the bottle rests for several more months to allow the dosage to integrate before release.

## Section 3: Styles, Sweetness Levels, and House Profiles

### Non-Vintage vs. Vintage Champagne

**Non-Vintage (NV):** The backbone of every Champagne house. No year is stated on the label. The chef de cave blends reserve wines from multiple vintages to achieve consistency of house style. NV Champagne is not "lesser"; it is a different goal. The NV is where the chef de cave demonstrates their art over time. Krug Grande Cuvée, Bollinger Special Cuvée, and Billecart-Salmon Brut Réserve are considered among the finest sparkling wines in the world and carry no vintage date.

**Vintage Champagne:** Produced only in exceptional years. 100% of the wine must come from the stated vintage. Vintage Champagne expresses a specific growing season rather than house consistency. It will generally be richer, more complex, and more age-worthy than NV, and considerably more expensive.

**Prestige Cuvée:** The top of each house's range, usually a vintage wine from the best parcels, in the most distinctive packaging. These are marketing flagships but also genuinely great wines. See below.

### Champagne Styles

**Blanc de Blancs:** 100% Chardonnay. Typically from the Côte des Blancs. Pale gold color, fine persistent bubbles, high acidity, citrus and white flower character. Ages to nutty, honeyed complexity. Salon, Billecart-Salmon BdB, Taittinger Comtes de Champagne (prestige BdB), Pierre Péters.

**Blanc de Noirs:** Made from Pinot Noir and/or Pinot Meunier only, pressed quickly to avoid skin contact. Richer, more golden color (trace pink is acceptable), fuller body, red fruit and spice character. Bollinger Vieilles Vignes Françaises (from ungrafted Pinot Noir vines), Ruinart Blanc de Noirs, Henri Goutorbe.

**Rosé Champagne:** Produced two ways:
- **Assemblage method:** Adding a small quantity (typically 10–15%) of still red Pinot Noir wine (from Bouzy or Ambonnay) to the NV base wine before tirage. This is the most common and most controlled method.
- **Saignée method:** Brief skin maceration of Pinot Noir or Meunier before pressing, extracting color and some tannin. Produces a richer, more structured rosé. Less common; considered more artisanal. Billecart-Salmon uses a distinctive cold-settling process for their NV Brut Rosé.

**Prestige Cuvées:**

| Prestige Cuvée | House | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Dom Pérignon | Moët & Chandon | Named for the legendary cellar master; always vintage; now made by Richard Geoffroy's successor; consistent and serious |
| Cristal | Louis Roederer | Created for Tsar Alexander II in 1876; clear flat-bottomed bottle; high Pinot Noir content; great aging potential |
| Krug Grande Cuvée | Krug | Multi-vintage (not NV in the traditional sense); 6+ years on lees; 20+ component wines; among the most complex Champagnes made |
| Belle Époque | Perrier-Jouët | Famous Art Nouveau floral bottle; vintage; Chardonnay-dominant; elegant and floral |
| Sir Winston Churchill | Pol Roger | Churchill drank Pol Roger daily; this cuvée is Pinot Noir-dominant, powerful, rich; vintage |
| Comtes de Champagne | Taittinger | Blanc de Blancs from Grand Cru Côte des Blancs; among the finest and longest-lived Blanc de Blancs |
| Cuvée Nicolas François Billecart | Billecart-Salmon | Vintage; elegant house style; Pinot Noir-driven; exceptional longevity |
| RD (Récemment Dégorgé) | Bollinger | Late disgorged vintage Champagne; extraordinary depth and creaminess; one of the great Champagne experiences |
| Blanc de Blancs Grand Cru | Salon | Single village (Le Mesnil), single variety, single vineyard; made only in the finest years; one of the rarest and most age-worthy Champagnes |

> **Pro Tip:** When a guest orders Dom Pérignon or Cristal by name, don't just fulfill the order. Add one sentence of context: "Cristal was originally created for the Russian Tsar in 1876. The flat glass bottom was a security measure against hidden explosives. It's now one of the most iconic bottles in wine." This is the kind of detail that earns tips and loyalty.

### Sweetness Levels

| Style | Residual Sugar (g/L) | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Brut Nature / Zero Dosage | 0–3 g/L | No sugar added at dosage; bone dry; shows terroir nakedly; requires very ripe, balanced base wine |
| Extra Brut | 0–6 g/L | Very dry; becoming increasingly popular with sommeliers and wine-focused consumers |
| Brut | 0–12 g/L | The most common style; the standard for most NV and vintage Champagne; perceived as dry |
| Extra Dry (Extra Sec) | 12–17 g/L | Counterintuitively, slightly sweeter than Brut; popular in cocktails |
| Sec | 17–32 g/L | Off-dry; aperitif style; rarely seen on serious wine lists |
| Demi-Sec | 32–50 g/L | Noticeably sweet; excellent with desserts and foie gras; Moët & Chandon Nectar Impérial |
| Doux | 50+ g/L | Very sweet; rarely made or found commercially |

> **Pro Tip:** Zero dosage is having a moment. If you have a wine-savvy guest who says they find Champagne "too sweet" or "cloying," Brut Nature from a great vintage and producer may convert them entirely. Suggest Bruno Paillard Extra Brut or Billecart-Salmon Extra Brut to start.

## Section 4: Grande Marque Houses and Grower Champagne

### The Grande Marque Houses

The term "Grande Marque" refers informally to the major Champagne houses, large-production négociant-manipulants (NM) who purchase grapes from growers, produce wine, and sell it under their label globally. They own some vineyards but primarily rely on contracts with vignerons throughout the region.

**Key houses and their style signatures:**

| House | Style Signature | Notable Cuvées |
|---|---|---|
| Moët & Chandon | Fresh, accessible, youthful; mass production at quality level | Brut Impérial NV, Dom Pérignon |
| Veuve Clicquot | Full-bodied, rich, Pinot Noir-driven; consistent yellow label NV | Yellow Label NV, La Grande Dame |
| Laurent-Perrier | Light, delicate; known especially for their Blanc de Blancs and Cuvée Rosé | Grand Siècle (multi-vintage blend of three finest years), Cuvée Rosé |
| Pol Roger | Classic, structured, Pinot Noir-dominant; famously Churchill's preferred Champagne | Brut Réserve NV, Sir Winston Churchill Cuvée |
| Bollinger | Rich, powerful, oak-influenced; some base wines fermented in small barrels; prestige in RD | Special Cuvée NV, RD, Vieilles Vignes Françaises |
| Krug | The artisan house of the grande marques; multi-vintage NV aged 6+ years; barrel fermentation | Grande Cuvée NV, Clos du Mesnil, Clos d'Ambonnay |
| Louis Roederer | Well-balanced, medium-full; significant owner of vineyards | Brut Premier NV (now Collection), Cristal |
| Taittinger | Chardonnay-forward, elegant, lighter style | Brut Réserve NV, Comtes de Champagne Blanc de Blancs |
| Billecart-Salmon | Precision, delicacy, exceptional rosé | Brut Réserve NV, Brut Rosé, Nicolas François Billecart |
| Perrier-Jouët | Floral, Chardonnay-forward, elegant | Blason Rosé, Belle Époque |

### Grower Champagne (Récoltant-Manipulant / RM)

**What it is:** A grower-producer makes Champagne entirely from their own vineyards. The "RM" designation appears on the label (next to the government license number in tiny print on the label base). Grande Marque houses are "NM" (Négociant-Manipulant).

**The movement:** Grower Champagne became fashionable internationally from the 2000s onward, driven by sommeliers interested in terroir-specific, artisanal alternatives to house-blended NV. The argument: since the greatest Champagnes (Krug Clos du Mesnil, Bollinger Vieilles Vignes, Salon) are essentially single-vineyard or village expressions, why should village-specific Champagne only come from big houses? A grower in Ambonnay or Le Mesnil with excellent vines can express that terroir directly.

**The challenge:** Grower Champagnes lack the reserve wine library and blending range of the grandes marques, so vintage variation is more pronounced, and not every release will be consistent.

**Key grower producers:**

| Producer | Village/Zone | Style |
|---|---|---|
| Jacques Selosse (Anselme Selosse) | Avize (Côte des Blancs) | Oxidative, barrel-fermented, zero-dosage philosophy; one of the most influential producers in Champagne; Substance (solera-method NV) is legendary |
| Egly-Ouriet | Ambonnay (Montagne de Reims) | Powerful, long-aged Pinot Noir-dominant; late disgorgement; among the finest growers in Champagne |
| Larmandier-Bernier | Vertus (Côte des Blancs) | Biodynamic; mineral, precise Chardonnay; zero-dosage focus |
| Pierre Péters | Le Mesnil-sur-Oger | Blanc de Blancs of extraordinary precision; Cuvée Spéciale Les Chétillons (single plot) is reference Blanc de Blancs |
| Agrapart & Fils | Avize | Terroir-focused; multiple single-village Blanc de Blancs; Vénus and Expérience are standouts |
| Jérôme Prévost | Gueux (Vallée de la Marne) | La Closerie Fac-similé; almost entirely Pinot Meunier; one of the most sought-after grower Champagnes |
| Chartogne-Taillet | Merfy (outside Grand Cru zones) | Alexandre Chartogne; low dosage; single-parcel wines from Kimmeridgian clay |

> **Pro Tip:** Grower Champagne is a sommelier's differentiator. Any beverage director can list Moët and Veuve; it takes knowledge and effort to list Egly-Ouriet or Pierre Péters. These bottles generate conversation, loyalty, and trust with serious wine guests. Put at least two or three on your list.

## Section 5: Other Major Sparkling Wines

Not every sparkling wine situation requires Champagne. A complete sommelier knows the world of sparkling wine in depth.

### Cava (Spain)

Produced primarily in the Penedès region of Catalonia (San Sadurní d'Anoia), with small production zones in other Spanish regions. Made by traditional method from native grapes: **Macabeo, Parellada, Xarel·lo** (white); **Garnacha, Monastrell, Trepat, Pinot Noir** (for rosé and the newer red-grape Cavas). Aging categories:
- **Cava:** 9 months minimum
- **Cava Reserva:** 15 months minimum
- **Cava Gran Reserva:** 30 months minimum, vintage
- **Cava de Paraje Calificado:** Single-vineyard designation; minimum 36 months; highest quality tier

Key producers: Raventós i Blanc (who left the Cava DO to form Conca del Riu Anoia, rejecting lower standards), Gramona, Recaredo (makes excellent long-aged Brut Nature). Freixenet and Codorníu dominate volume.

### Crémant (France)

"Crémant" is the designation for French traditional-method sparkling wines made outside Champagne. Made with the same method but from local grapes and under regional appellation rules. Key appellations:

- **Crémant d'Alsace:** From Alsace; Pinot Blanc, Auxerrois, Riesling, Pinot Gris, Pinot Noir, Chardonnay. France's second largest sparkling wine appellation after Champagne. Fresh, aromatic.
- **Crémant de Bourgogne:** Chardonnay and Pinot Noir from Burgundy. Often excellent value, same grapes, same method as Champagne, at a fraction of the price.
- **Crémant de Loire:** Chenin Blanc dominant. Can age well. Producers: Langlois-Château, Bouvet-Ladubay.
- **Crémant du Jura, Crémant de Limoux, Crémant de Bordeaux:** Lesser-known but worth exploring for a diverse sparkling program.

### Franciacorta (Italy)

Italy's answer to Champagne. Produced by traditional method in Franciacorta DOCG (Lombardy, near Brescia). Grapes: **Chardonnay, Pinot Nero (Pinot Noir), Pinot Bianco, Erbamat** (minor native variety). Among the most technically rigorous sparkling wine appellations outside Champagne. Aging minimums: non-vintage 18 months (25 months for Satèn and Rosé), vintage 30 months, Riserva 60 months.

Key producers: Ca' del Bosco (consistently excellent, especially Cuvée Annamaria Clementi Riserva), Bellavista (Vittorio Moretti Riserva), Berlucchi.

> **Pro Tip:** Franciacorta is chronically underrated outside Italy. If you have a Champagne-loving guest who is curious and open, Franciacorta is a compelling conversation. Ca' del Bosco Cuvée Prestige is an accessible starting point.

### Prosecco (Italy)

Made in the Veneto and Friuli from the **Glera** grape by the **Charmat (tank) method** (secondary fermentation in pressurized tanks, not in individual bottles). This is the fundamental difference from traditional method wines: tank method is faster, less expensive, and produces fresher, fruitier, less autolytic styles.

**DOC vs. DOCG:**
- **Prosecco DOC:** Broad zone; large production; basic quality
- **Prosecco di Conegliano-Valdobbiadene DOCG:** The heartland; higher quality; sub-zones: **Rive** (single-village) and the prestigious **Superiore di Cartizze** (~107 ha hillside vineyard, the Grand Cru of Prosecco)
- **Asolo Prosecco DOCG:** Second DOCG zone; less well-known

Prosecco is not Champagne and should not be positioned as such. It is a fresh, aromatic, light sparkling wine ideal for aperitivo and casual service. Key producers at quality level: Bisol, Ruggeri, Nino Franco.

### Sekt (Germany / Austria)

Germany and Austria produce traditional-method sparkling wines, though most commercial Sekt is tank-method. Key quality designations:
- **Winzersekt / Crémant d'Alsace equivalent:** Grower-produced, traditional method, estate fruit. Growing category.
- Quality German Sekt from Riesling (e.g., Griesel & Compagnie, Solter) can be extraordinary: high acid, mineral, with aged complexity not found elsewhere.

### English Sparkling Wine

The southeast of England, particularly Kent, Sussex, and Hampshire, has emerged as one of the most exciting sparkling wine regions in the world. The same **chalk soils** that underlie the Côte des Blancs extend under the English Channel. Cool climate preserves acidity; the traditional method grapes (Pinot Noir, Pinot Meunier, Chardonnay) thrive.

Key producers: **Nyetimber** (pioneer; consistently excellent), **Ridgeview** (South Downs chalk), **Hambledon Vineyard** (oldest commercial English wine estate on its current site), **Gusbourne**.

English sparkling wine regularly defeats Champagne in blind tastings and commands comparable prices. Its trajectory is one of the most compelling stories in the wine world.

### Pétillant Naturel (Pét-Nat)

Made by the **méthode ancestrale**: the wine is bottled before primary fermentation is complete, so the CO₂ produced by the fermenting yeasts is captured in the bottle. No second fermentation is induced; no dosage; often no disgorgement (resulting in cloudy wine). Lower pressure (1–2.5 atmospheres vs. 6 for Champagne). Can be made from any grape, anywhere.

Pét-Nat is the antithesis of Champagne's technological precision. It is rustic, variable, expressive, and often delicious. It has become a defining category for natural wine and younger wine drinkers. Leading examples: Domaine de la Taille aux Loups (Montlouis, Loire), Clai Sveti Jakov (Croatia), Donkey & Goat (California).

> **Pro Tip:** Pét-Nat is an excellent by-the-glass option for guests who find Champagne intimidating or "too formal." It's approachable, fun, usually well-priced, and tells a simple story. Keep one or two on your BTG list and use them to introduce sparkling wine to hesitant guests.

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| Méthode Champenoise / Méthode Traditionnelle | The traditional method: secondary fermentation in bottle, aging on lees, riddling, disgorgement, dosage |
| Assemblage | The blending of base wines from different villages, varieties, and vintages; the central art of NV Champagne production |
| Cuvée | The finest first-press fraction; also used as a generic term for a specific wine or batch |
| Taille | The second-press fraction in Champagne, slightly coarser than the cuvée |
| Liqueur de tirage | The sugar-yeast mixture added before bottling to trigger the second fermentation |
| Lees (lies) | Spent yeast cells that remain in contact with wine during aging; source of autolytic flavor compounds |
| Autolysis | The breakdown of dead yeast cells, releasing compounds that produce toasty, brioche, biscuity notes in Champagne |
| Remuage (Riddling) | The process of gradually inverting bottles to move lees toward the crown cap |
| Dégorgement (Disgorgement) | Removal of the frozen lees plug from the bottle |
| Dosage / Liqueur d'expédition | The sugar-wine mixture added after disgorgement to determine final sweetness |
| Blanc de Blancs | Champagne made exclusively from white grapes (Chardonnay) |
| Blanc de Noirs | Champagne made exclusively from black grapes (Pinot Noir, Meunier), pressed without skin contact |
| Récoltant-Manipulant (RM) | A grower who makes Champagne from their own vineyards |
| Négociant-Manipulant (NM) | A house that buys grapes or wine from growers |
| RD (Récemment Dégorgé) | Recently disgorged; wine held on lees for extended aging before final disgorgement |
| Charmat / Tank Method | Secondary fermentation in pressurized tanks rather than individual bottles; used for Prosecco |
| Pétillant Naturel (Pét-Nat) | Sparkling wine made by méthode ancestrale: bottled during primary fermentation, no second fermentation added |
| Échelle des crus | Champagne's village quality rating scale: 17 Grand Cru (100%), ~42 Premier Cru (90-99%) |
